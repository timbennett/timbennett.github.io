Kids Of Zoo

By Patrick 2012-03-01T00:00:00+00:00

Buried somewhere in the virtual reams of commentary devoted to the recent Labor Party leadership stoush was an irrefutable observation: both leadership contenders lacked the basic communications skills to convey their respective messages to both the ALP caucus, and the broader community. Drowning in the rhetorical sludge of modern management speak, the so-called narrative is a rancid rhetorical slurry masquerading as a clear story.

It’s in such times that the brutal honesty and glistening clarity of punk rock comes to the fore. The agenda is political in the most basic sense of the term; the confrontational narrative smacks you in the face like an aggrieved footy supporter still searching for answers for a season lost to the fickle hand of umpiring fate.

And so it is with Kids of Zoo. The band is led by Tim Wold, former Specimens guitarist, currently fulfilling bass duties in Russian Roulettes. Consistent with the contemporary punk rock world’s oxymoronic marriage of analogue and digital format, Kids of Zoo’s debut album, following a 2010 EP, is released on vinyl with digital download code. The album isn’t weighed down with academic or philosophical baggage: Wold shrieks plaintively about matters of the heart, the vagaries of life, even learning to eat meat in the suburbs (surely, a soon-to-be lost art in these days of suburban housing blocks and pre-cooked meals). The guitar licks are packed full of Friday night attitude and the rhythm section is up for a fight, but only if provoked. (You don’t need to wave your weapon around when you can stare down danger.)

Mechanical foetus by KIDS OF ZOO by ADAGIO830

The opening track, the delightfully titled ?Anal Pastry?, is a scene setter: swaggering into action on the back of a tough-as-nails backbeat, pierced with shards of guitar, as Wold pours his heart out in the time-honoured rock’n?roll fashion. The negativism of ?Blackscratch? is rendered in the distinctive shades of a young Kent Steedman taking a metaphorical sharp stick to the linear pub rock chords that characterised the northern beaches of Sydney in the early-1980s. ?Yabba Man? is laden with angst, and an angular attack that suggests new wave, dead on arrival.

Analpastry by KIDS OF ZOO by ADAGIO830

On ?Gang Warfare?, the band circles around its eventual target, like high school students mobilising at the local train station in expectation of some serious juvenile delinquency. Ninety seconds in, and the punches fly, the reverb and feedback providing the metaphorical blood running down the path. ?Prawn Off Shotgun? is infinitely more than a cute pun; ?Tommy Cooper’s Dead? is the proverbial wander into the night searching for meaning, with nothing but an arm full of Ty Segall records and a mind littered with archaic English comic reference points.

?Polish Underground? loiters on the punk edge of funk rock, saturated with carrier grade nihilism. ?When You’re Born in the Suburbs You Learn How to Eat Your Meat? offers a philosophical observation on the lasting significance of a solid suburban upbringing. The ghost of X looms in the background of ?Mechanical Foetus?. ?Saturated Fats? offers a threat of retribution with a guitar lick that sounds ominously like an evacuation siren.

The bare-fisted beauty of Kids of Zoo lies in its brazen attitude, its ability to talk straight and tough, neither a bull’s turd or a prisoner in sight. In a world where rhetorical precision has been captured by dark and evil semantic forces, Kids of Zoo is a beacon of punk rock honesty.
