Matt Banham: Why The Jade Monkey Should Live Forever

By Darren Levin 2012-03-04T13:00:00+00:00

Adelaide native MATT BANHAM pays tribute to The Jade Monkey, which will soon make way for a $65-million development.


My old band No Through Road played our first and last gigs at the Jade Monkey several years apart. After our first gig I had to go to the hospital with mild concussion after falling head first off a drum kit. After the last gig we all had to go home to nurse our old man hangovers.

Over the past nine or so years I have wandered along to countless shows at the Jade Monkey; some good, some bad. At least at the bad ones there was a nice beer garden you could escape to. I remember loudly daring my friend Ben Flippy to jump over the back fence of the beer garden one night, promising I would go with him. I didn’t, but he did, and was stuck in an alleyway that he couldn’t come back from. No one bothered to help him out. Great times. A lot of nights there have blurred into one, probably because Zac never refused me service. (It makes him one of the best bartenders in town.)


I started playing music in Adelaide properly around 1999; only seeing glimpses of its heyday when venues like The Tivoli, Producers and Mad Love Bar gave bands a lot of choice of places to play around the city. One by one they were all disappearing. The only choices left seemed to be The Rhino Room, The Crown & Sceptre and The Grace Emily and my friends and I would play these three venues as often as we could. Around 2001, The Crown & Sceptre was sold and became some sort of ?funky bar? and the Rhino Room began to focus on comedy and DJs. My friends and I were at a bit of a loss of what to do. Dark days for Adelaide.

Along came the Jade Monkey. Created by Zac Coligan and Naomi Thompson in late 2002, it was a godsend. It was a place for musicians made by musicians. It was like our own little FUBU venue. I had seen Zac around town since I was about 17 playing in a band called Bergerac. I thought they were pretty cool, and they all had really great sneakers. Zac was the super-friendly bass player who would remember your name and was crazy enthusiastic about everything; perfect personality to run a bar really. Naomi and Zac went onto play in the wonderful Sea Thieves and though a lot quieter a bartender than Zac, she always knew what I wanted to drink before I got to the bar. Just like in Cheers. I was Norm, she was Shelly Long, and Zac was Danson with pointier sideburns.


Pretty soon my pals and I were there all the time, either playing or watching or talking crap while pretending to be watching. Oh, how I scoffed at the people walking around with pots of tea as I drank lager after lager, and tried to convince Zac to let me behind the bar to ?help people out?. And the bands were pretty good too. I remember seeing great sets from Love Of Diagrams, Wolf & Cub, Wagons, Hit The Jackpot, Grand Salvo, Sweet Raxxx and many more. Colin (Adelaide’s biggest music fan) became their unofficial glassy and used to wander round the bar, carrying bottles and talking-up bands to anyone who would listen. The place was a loud, stinky, smoke-free bed of local musicians and their friends, only ever interrupted by the cocktail blender that always seemed to pipe up during bands? acoustic numbers.

Bands loved the huge stage covered in twinkling lights – even if there was a spot that dripped rain on you occasionally. Mixers loved the mixing pulpit where they could loom over people. I DJed there a couple of times and felt like a Golden God watching over my drunken minions. For years they even hung the ugly sign that my friend Cuchulain made for them; accommodating is a good word for Zac and Naomi.

?The place was a loud, stinky, smoke-free bed of local musicians and their friends.?

At the moment the Jade looks like it might be closing down or moving to another location which is an enormous pity. I’m certain many young Adelaide bands played some of their first gigs in that room. Some great, some awful but at least there was a place that bands had a chance to play that wasn’t just the back room of a bar – it’s a proper venue with foldback and everything! Like a real show that your parents could be proud of and say , I’m going to my daughter’s concert.? Just like those famous-type people.

The famous old Jade should live forever, if not on Twin Street let’s hope for an equally good location. And when they are old and deaf, I can visit Naomi and Zac and hassle them to let me behind the bar or at least for a free bag of Twisties.

+

####Matt Banham is a solo artist now based in Sydney.
