In The Days When The World Was Wide

By Aaron 2013-07-21T00:00:00+00:00

On their first album together in six years, Ron Peno and Kim Salmon reconnect over not just melancholy folk and bluegrass but hopeful Brill Building-esque pop, writes AARON CURRAN.


Listening to the new Darling Downs LP reminds me of the one time I met Ron S. Peno in person, more than 20 years ago, unbelievably on my very first evening out in Sydney.

My traveling companion James and I, eager to drink away the dusty monotony of a three-day train ride from Perth, bumped into Ron upstairs on the balcony of Selina’s in Coogee Bay during a Concrete Blonde concert. Both being big fans of Died Pretty we said our semi-starstruck g’days. Later that night in an infamous Kings Cross basement club called the Kardomah Caf? (a.k.a. the Dark Coma) we met up with Peno again, striking up enjoyably inebriated conversation. As we didn’t have much money but did have access to a catering-size barrel of bourbon at my cousin’s place around the corner, we invited him back to our temporary digs for a drink.

?In hindsight, piercing melancholy and yelping Appalachian inflections were ever-present in Peno’s vocals for Died Pretty.?

Peno’s only proviso in joining us was to check that we ?didn’t do hammer? (I’m not sure then we even knew what that meant). Back at the tiny borrowed flat we poured hefty measures from the plastic barrel and, finding an acoustic guitar in the corner, began playing old Rolling Stones songs with Ron, of course, on lead vocals. My strongest memories are the tracks we tried from Beggars Banquet: the lascivious stomp of ?Parachute Woman?; the irreverent country blues of ?Dear Doctor?; and, particularly, the forsaken balladry of ?No Expectations?, Ron’s keening croon digging deep at the dark truths of the lyric: ?Our love is like our music/It’s here and then it’s gone.?

In hindsight, piercing melancholy and yelping Appalachian inflections were ever-present in Peno’s vocals for Died Pretty, but it took his collaboration with Kim Salmon in The Darling Downs to unfetter the influences of country and folk music and bring them to the fore. The two albums they made as a duo were starkly affecting, particularly their last release from six years ago, From One to Another, a compelling if unsettling listen that appeared to draw the partnership to a close.

?If [this] was a Western it’d be ?Lonesome Dove?, not ?Unforgiven?.?

But, as Ron himself puts it, The Darling Downs ?never broke up; we just had a break.? They now return with In the Days When the World was Wide*, an LP titled after a line in one of his favourite Henry Lawson poems. That poem, like some of the songs on this record, harkens back to the lost opportunities of youth, but in a fond and measured way filled with peaceful resignation rather than caustic regret. There’s bright calm at the centre of this album, in marked contrast to the murky shadows of their last. If *In the Days When the World was Wide* was a Western it’d be *Lonesome Dove*, not *Unforgiven.

First track ?Saved? exemplifies this sanguine tone, with a warm arrangement of lilting banjo, bass and piano, and soaring melodies straight from the Brill Building, one of this album’s key but unexpected influences (Salmon has mentioned Carole King here on M+N*; the press release accurately references Burt Bacharach). Next is the hymnal ?Forever Night?, an assurance of love in the face of the inevitability of darkness. ?Down to the River? is spare and calm until a determined chorus rises up, filled with percussion, roughly-strummed chords and airborne harmonies. ?Wish You Were Her? taps into the same Stones-y spirit that Ron summoned when we drunkenly sang those songs from *Beggars Banquet all those years ago, a wild and gleeful dance ?through wildfire – head in flames.? ?There Were Tears? is stripped back to just banjo, vocals and a gently ebbing fiddle; along with ?I Don’t Care?, it continues the theme of gracious submission to unavoidable fate.

?Darling Downs? lyrics are chosen for their musicality, not just meaning.?

The rollicking ?Between the Forest and the Trees? is driven by whip-crack rhythms and a two-note twang, contrasting with the soft glow of ?Light of the World?, a song that reminds how Darling Downs? lyrics are chosen for their musicality, not just meaning: ?It’s the colder the shoulder and then/It’s the eyes in the staring of then/It’s the sharpening tongues so refined/and a place in the world so defined.? Contributions from guests like Mike Stranges (drums, bass, percussion) and Julitha Ryan (piano) are subtle. Salmon’s guitar playing is unsurprisingly excellent throughout yet remarkable too for its economy and nuance, such as the softly plucked high notes on closer ?Your Face? that become a tender bed for the rest of the song to fall back upon, relinquishing the pain of ?this world outside? to join ?all of these souls – laid to rest.?

Often the most moving country and folk music can be fairly accused of an inherent gloominess, and The Darling Downs – like Townes Van Zandt or Karen Dalton – make music that carries the very weight of life, too much for some to bear. But the wonderful songs on In the Days When the World was Wide hold tight to hopefulness, tempering the sadness with tranquility: ?No light ever shone oh so bright/Remember these times.?

+

##?In the Days When the World was Wide? comes out this Saturday (July 27) independently through Fuse. It launches the same night at The Toff in Town in Melbourne from 7:40pm, with support from Skyscraper Stan & The Commission Flats and Fraser A Gorman.
