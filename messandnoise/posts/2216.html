Pip

By Shaun 2010-03-01T00:00:00+00:00

Despite what most indie pop purveyors will have you believe, percussion doesn’t need to be a mere time keeper. Drums don’t need to be a subdued unadorned pulse tying the meatier components of a song together. Often, the most interesting – and probably enduring – modern pop groups realise that exploiting the possibilities of rhythm makes for more compelling music: it can be the difference between greatness and mediocrity. In the case of Otouto, it defines them.

Melbourne’s Otouto are very much saved by a signatory approach to time keeping. It’s a rather dreary selling point really, that Otouto’s percussionist (Kid Sam’s Kishore Ryan) is amazing, but let’s be honest here: it’s true. The three-piece write songs that are determinably pastel and playful, but they discard with the tiring notion that all good songs, irrespective of their studio adornments, should hold up well stripped of extraneous instrumentation (and judging by the uninspired plod of most indie rock/pop drummers – all percussion is extraneous). That’s bullshit – because Otouto is very much a sum of its parts: to remove any of these subtle elements would be to see them disappear.

There’s no sense of showiness here, though. Pip is a very spacious, whitewashed and pristine pop album with all elements – keys, guitars, vocals – prodded carefully into focus. To listen to Pip is to be welcomed into a world of meticulously minimal interior design – white, dull greys and the occasional splash of rich colour calculatedly placed to some subliminal end. It’s clinically focused but, with the aid of the Brown sisters? earthy vocals, ultimately warm and welcoming. The Browns – Martha and Hazel – are plaintive and non-committal, offering an occasional sentimental gesture quickly contained by a contrastingly naff assessment such as ?falling in love is like watching a long video?. A lot of Otouto’s lyrics work like that: profound non-sequiturs that have emerged from banter, or languidly realised observations with a ?little-book-of? style profundity. Sometimes the Browns convey in words what the music would do anyway, such as on ?Twelve-Ten?, which lyrically and musically is all bright light and right angles; a neurotically organised bliss, everything in its place.

But it’s the drums that create this spot-free, ironed-straight ambience. They enforce restraint on the other instrumentation with only a couple of exceptions in the loose strum of ?Autumn? and the tumbling central riff of ?Astronauts?. On tracks like ?W. Hillier? and ?Cartoon Shoes? though, the ornate percussion seems to breath the keys and guitars into life, with each component as distinct as the colours on a Rubik’s Cube. It’s a fresh and well-executed vision for a very young band: probably a debut they’ll struggle to live up to.
