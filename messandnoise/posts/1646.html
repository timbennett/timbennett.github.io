Red Spring

By emmy hennings 2007-08-12T00:00:00+00:00

There’s an undeniable twinge of gothic anxiety to Underlapper, not that you’d ever guess at it from the appearance of these six unassuming Sydney men. But it eats at the edges of their sound, a sense that the machines are thinking for themselves, whirrs and clunks scattered through the mix like the echoes of sinister dot-matrix printers. It’s in the way that the vocals are subsumed – melted and bent – beneath the instrumentation on early songs ?Meanderthal? and ?Little Tapioca?. Gothic anxiety is, partly, a fear of the human being joined irreversibly to the inhuman, a fusion that Underlapper play out via an atmosphere that’s part melancholic ?live? band and part delicate electronic processing.

It’s the former that wins out – just – on largely instrumental songs such as ?Werewolf Etiquette for Self Defence?, replete with cool piano, or ?Fool’s Cap?, where saxophone takes the lead melody. However, the phased vocals of ?Haunted Cornice? and a piercing guitar sound not heard since The Cure nearly drowned in their own decadence on Kiss Me, Kiss Me, Kiss Me* are two steps too far towards the Batcave. There’s promise to *Red Spring, though the track sequencing is jumbled: what’s here is an interesting collection of discrete songs, rather than a cohesive album. When Underlapper have settled the balance between menace and pleasant, minor key pop they could be very good – and the resolution of their sound does not necessarily lie in smoothing over the points where these two strands meet. I suspect the opposite.
