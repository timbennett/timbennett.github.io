By A Thread

By Tom Valcanis 2014-03-26T00:00:00+00:00

Poison City Records may one day hold the same prestige that Lookout Records once did if they keep fossicking to find punk rock gems such as The Smith Street Band, The Bennies and – now releasing their second record – Tasmania’s Luca Brasi. Yes, just like The Godfather bodyguard.

Luca Brasi routinely share backroom pubs with folk- and pop-punkers. Each two- to three-minute polemic they strum and kick out truthfully sounds like they’re doing it themselves, as best they can. Their punk leans toward tight two-beat and is unafraid of showy leads, like a latter-day Jawbreaker fired up by the promise of a spotlight. Even so, their punk is a strange animal. They don’t swan or bleed like a pack of bohemians with teeth. It’s inconceivable, as somewhere inside lurks some kind of a future. ?Even the clouds are your friends today,? rasps Tyler Richardson, Luca Brasi’s rough-and-tumble frontman, and each repetition in the buoyant opener ?KDR? weaves a fast and loose optimism.

Their future is buried under thickets of bright jangled leads and a rhythm section stepping up to mics in three-part harmony. A steady thump rocks the foundations of ?Benthos?, vibrant phrases colouring in shades of longing: ?I wanna build something tonight,? Richardson desperately implores, ?while there’s still a bright spark burning behind my eyes.? Rolling on, a shadow of melancholy lengthens over By a Thread*. ?I’m hearing a death rattle and I’m 25 years old,? sings Richardson, laying ?Death Rattle? bare. ?I don’t wanna know!? shouts a return, slapped on like a pair of hands over a frightened child’s eyes. It feels like a chill blowing through the summer and beer of *Sunshine & Technology.

?Here’s Looking at You Kid Rock? is a song sung about the heartland, capturing the isolating album cover: storm clouds gathering. ?Pupils dilate/calming my urge to evacuate,? staggers Richardson over bobs of rusty arpeggio. Pervasively feeling southbound and down, Luca Brasi’s sun still shines behind and through. ?Borders and Statelines? burns brighter, a crackling refrain conjuring visions of clambering moshers screaming and punching the air besides.

Choice cut ?Western Junction? starts folky and unplugged, with Richardson yawning that he’s ?built on goodbyes – sifting through decisions I never really made.? Luca Brasi don’t tumble into a hole to wallow and have a think. They crawl out on ?burnt-out legs,? swearing they’re ?set on this life,? complete with a stomp on the overdrive pedal.

Luca Brasi aren’t stranded so much as islanded in seas of mates and memories. Memories unvarnished and pure like thought bubbling into the mind’s eye. Memories of regret tempered with spirits in renewal. In By a Thread, Aussie punk rock has a real future, dislocated from flipping off the suburbs and its contents.
