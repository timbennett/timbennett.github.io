Feel The Pain

By Doug Wallen 2011-06-01T00:00:00+00:00

Acoustic guitar and drums might seem like a limiting set-up. But Perth duo The Painkillers coax so much out of it – from garage and country to blues and early rock. Passing references to those genres are dispensed as offhandedly as frontman Joe Bludge’s lyrics about women, drink, and other mixed blessings in life. The rock pedigree of drummer James Baker, meanwhile, has long been off the charts: his past bands include The Victims, The Scientists, The Hoodoo Gurus, Beasts of Bourbon, and The Dubrovniks. He falls committedly into step with Bludge’s cautionary rambles, four of which he co-wrote on this punchy third album.

Even if you want to simply call The Painkillers garage – and hell, they once won a WAMi Award for Best Punk Band – they don’t run on adrenaline but on a nervous looseness. It’s as if they’ve got to get through each song before it collapses beneath them like a spent horse. There’s a self-effacing, neurotic energy that’s welcome, as is the songwriting from the perspective of two road-tested older men rather than another faceless round of young guns. On ballads like ?Rodeo? and the organ-backlit ?Big Eyes Green?, Bludge’s foggy rasp has a vulnerable tug that speaks volumes.

Tomorrow – The Painkillers by Mess+Noise

But again, there’s a lot of ground covered here. ?Tomorrow? and ?Jump Your Ship? nail jangling proto-garage and recall The Modern Lovers and The Velvet Underground, respectively. ?On A Lyric By Robert Herrick? and ?Leave Me Alone? are the most rocking, while the pop-infused closer ?Memories? makes a well-aimed lunge for the heartstrings. Bludge has a Dylan-ish drawl in places, and ?Theodora? leans on Dylan’s legacy with a titular character that seems mythical, petty and badass all at once.
There are two covers: an 87-second take on ?Lipstick? by the Perth band The Homicides, which has a great mid-career White Stripes feel, and the Jimmie Rodgers tune ?Gamblin? Bar Room Blues?, a dark tale that’s remarkably lived in. Producer Lorenzo Sinagra brings a homey glow that’s perfect for these shop-worn songs.

Feel The Pain turns out to be as charmingly imperfect and unpredictable as The Painkillers are well-versed in music history and comfortable in their skins.
