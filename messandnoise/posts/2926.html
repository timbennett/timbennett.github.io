Aldous Harding

By Anthony Carew 2014-07-30T00:00:00+00:00

The opening track on Aldous Harding’s debut LP is called ?Stop Your Tears?. It could just as easily be called ?Stop Whatever It is That You’re Doing?. Once Harding’s voice arrives, 30 seconds in, it’s hard to remain in the digital age’s constant state of semi-distraction. The best adjective for her singing would be ?arresting?; her songbird’s warble – etched with the turn of the season, throaty with pain, yet ruffled with a twirl of theatricality – the kind that stops hearts, stops men in their tracks, stops chatty crowds silent.

Harding’s vocals are set over oft-fingerpicked guitar, echoes of Gothic English folk in her song’s spartan settings and shadowy sentiments. She’s equally as affecting when astride a full, fiddle-boasting backing band (?Hunter?), set against classical minimalism (?No Peace?) or murmuring mournful resignations, adrift amidst the eerie wail of a musical saw (?Two Bitten Hearts?). Harding’s music – and her voice – is keenly reminiscent of Marissa Nadler and Josephine Foster; having the same careful elegance and dark romance, the same turn of parlour despair.

?Feed these words to the wind,? Harding sings, voice a-tremor, in ?Small Bones of Courage?, the song so beautiful that it, in turn, lures listeners unto the breeze, inspires them to alight on flights of pure imagination. Hearing Harding trill of reading ?Baudelaire in the afternoon? or drinking mead or baring ?your naked breast into the sun,? romanticism will likely flower in the minds of many; those in thrall to her sinuous singing free to imagine not just the idyllic rural fantasias familiar to folksong, but the luminous phantasm of this sad-voiced, barefooted folkie frolicking through them.

Yet, far from the wild fantasies her voice summons, in the cold light of reality, Aldous Harding is Hanna Harding, a rough-?round-the-edges smartass who hails from Lyttelton, New Zealand, a port town just north of Christchurch. Those who saw her on a recent run of Australian gigs, opening for labelmate Tiny Ruins, came face-to-face with a genuine eccentric; a wry, awkward, self-flagellating figure unafraid of scuttling her own show, of letting between-song banter ruin the mood. And Harding’s drunken-car-crash conversation with Jason Moore on RRR is equal parts hilarious and horrifying, an interviewing disaster not for the fainthearted.

This reality doesn’t diminish the dizzying beauty of Harding’s music, doesn’t sharpen its soft-edge fogs into a less flattering focus. In fact, its incongruity adds layers of intrigue, creates contrast and contradiction; makes her wild, rough-cut carolling seem more remarkable. No bit of biographical baggage or quirk of personality can turn something so majestic mundane. This, as always, is music’s magic.
