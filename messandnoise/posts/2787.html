Free Time

By Anthony Carew 2013-07-15T00:00:00+00:00

Free Time is what Panel of Judges? Dion Nania found on his hands when he followed his girlfriend to New York. She had a job, he didn’t. Eventually that led to a band named after the idle, by-himself-in-a-foreign-city hours Nania had to burn. ?When I wake up/I will go downstairs/and I’ll make coffee for you,? he sings eagerly in ?It’s Alright?, playing the role of willing, available boyfriend – albeit a boyfriend who occasionally has to leave to go back to Australia. It’s a love song with a current of dissatisfaction, which means it feels like a relationship, not a fantasy; the path to true love a ?crooked road? winding towards an acceptance of how-things-are that borders on lyrical Zen koan: ?It’s alright/even when it’s not alright.?

?It’s Alright? plays out, as do the rest of Nania’s new songs, over melancholy indie jangle; the guitar notes chiming and lingering, the sentiments all sun-sleepy and warm to the touch. The debut Free Time album does disservice to ideas of musical architectural-psychology: its songwriter moves from Melbourne to New York yet makes music that sounds more stretched-out, suburban, and, to dip into the vernacular of Aussie clich?, ?laidback?. The boisterousness of Panel of Judges is nowhere to be heard on songs like ?Nature’s Cup?, whose sunny days and spiralling, semi-psychedelic guitar leads are relaxed and unhurried. The antidote to the anxiety and stress of New York living – and the way it manifests in so much music – is plentiful time to bum around.

This sound and spirit is instantly comparable to Twerps and Real Estate, two bands to whom Nania has an intimate connection. That each comes from either side of the Pacific suits the nature of Free Time, whose very makeup – Nania backed by New Yorkers Jonah Maurer, Adrienne Humblet and Michael Mimoun – is a reminder of its leader’s split life, and the cross-continental ?ping pong? behind its making. The spine of the songs were recorded in New York with Woods? Jarvis Taveniere and then brought by Nania back to Melbourne, where he enlisted various locals – like Julie Burleigh, who plays chirping recorder on the brittle, folky, somehow-storybook-ish ?World Without Love?; and Twerps? Marty Frawley and Julia MacFarlane, who add acoustic guitar and vocal harmonies, respectively, to ?Here and There? – for additional Jack Farley-tracked parts before taking it back to be finished off by the band in New York.

?How time flies/and so do I,? Nania sings on the to-ing-and-fro-ing ?Here and There?, the LP’s most literal tale of a life spent split between cities, never quite inhabiting either. He may, mid-?It Doesn’t Stop,? profess the dream of being ?inscrutable? in everything he says, yet Nania’s lyrics go the opposite direction: so clear and straightforward they seek wisdom in simplicity. ?Nothing ends well,? he sings in ?I Lost Again,? unintentionally bordering on the secret wisdoms of Tom Cruise in Cocktail; and in ?Here and There?, his laments at being an ?outside man? in New York are spelt out in the sing-song ?dispatches from home/they make me feel alone.?

Yet, as time flies, this divide becomes less clear. ?It won’t be long, I’m coming home,? Nania chirps in ?World Without Love.? Only, he neglects to specify where exactly home is these days.
