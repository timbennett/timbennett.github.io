We Are The Lost Loves

By DomAlessio 2010-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

Old habits die hard for Matthew Wright. He’s the former frontman for Melbourne rockers The Getaway Plan and a musician accustomed to radiating overwrought emotion. Though I’m sure they’d probably deny it, The Getaway Plan were undoubtedly one of Australia’s most efficacious proponents of ?emo? music, and evidently Wright’s finding it hard to pull away from the pained artist persona in his new outfit Young Heretics.

Whereas The Getaway Plan built their sound around stacks of guitar amps cranked to 11, Young Heretics opt for a far glossier patina, utilising keyboards and electronic drum loops. But on a micro level they’re quite similar acts, in that both believe bombast and drama is the best way to deliver passion. But it doesn’t really work like that. As a result, their debut long-player, We Are The Lost Loves, comes across as far too earnest. Indeed, everything about this project seems driven towards painting Wright and his musical collaborator – half-sister and vocalist Kitty Hart – as misunderstood artists, railing against a society that’s shunned them as outcasts. They are agitators – ?heretics?, if you will – in a world that won’t accept them.

So as you can tell, this is very serious music made by two musicians who take their art – and themselves – very seriously. Take ?Risk/Loss?, for example. Now, Hart has a striking and emotive voice when she wants to, but on this song she strips away her natural timbre by running her vocals through distortion. Then she sings naff, contrite lines like, ?Take care/We know that you want to break down?, which read like a tortured teenager’s diary. It’s emo synth-pop that sounds very much contrived.

For every song like ?Come Together?, which piles on layers of bombastic strings, synthesisers and something that sounds like Enya on helium, there are others that strip everything back, leaving nothing but a keyboard and Kitty Hart’s honeyed voice. But what could have been emotional ballads – like ?Dark Prince? and ?I Know I’m A Wolf? – are rendered limpid and saccharine with stock-standard lyricism and an overtly synthetic piano tone. For a band that employed such a large cast of guest musicians to play on their debut, you’d think someone could have rolled in an actual piano at some stage.

Young Heretics are haunted by their emo ghosts. Their anachronistic songwriting ideas can’t be transferred from a band like The Getaway Plan and plastered onto synth-pop just because it’s the genre du jour. There are salvageable moments, like ?The Lost Loves? which highlights how well Hart and Wright’s voices sit together, but these are far too fleeting. Ultimately, We Are The Lost Loves is awkward and overly earnest, like reading your teenage poetry 15 years after you’ve written it.
