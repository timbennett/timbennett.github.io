+DOME

By lawsonfletcher 2011-03-25T00:00:00+00:00

For all its charms, The Sound of Trees Falling on People was beset by an odd mood that haunts the foundations of electronica generally – that feeling of alienation emerging from the music’s supposedly inhuman features: absent vocals, unearthly instruments. Of course it’s a falsehood – all music is deeply human, and there are hands, hearts and minds behind even the glossiest sheen. Feelings about music are rarely rational though, and you sense Seekae felt the same way a little bit, trying to hold this spectral feeling at bay with the tacked-on warmth of glockenspiel, shoegazing guitars and vocal samples (most beautifully, Ivan Vizintin from Ghoul’s rich take for ?Wool?).

To turn +DOME against the question of the inhuman illuminates what has to be this album’s central achievement – it’s a synthesis of the organic and technical so that the two become gleefully indistinguishable. Consider the beats; you can never quite tell if they are the product of animal hide or circuitry. Or the way the band have expanded their instrumental palette, with the addition of strings and more developed guitar work, but still couched it in their shining strength – piercing synth lines and dizzying ionic warbles. It’s all showcased on the standout ?Gnor?, which moves from Qua-esque bubbles to a propulsive percussive buildup, before climaxing in a intricate, layered freak-out, leaving you both beguiled and exhausted. It seems apt ?Gnor? meets everything in the middle; a synecdoche for an album brimming with ideas brought to their fullest form.

Seekae: Gnor by gaga-digi

While The Sound of Trees* touched on a kind of loneliness, *+Dome is thoroughly embedded in the flesh of the world. Less fussily clean than their first album, these windswept textures rubbing up against one another on every track lend credence to the jokey pseudo-genre the band first described their music as: ?ghetto ambience?. It’s perfectly executed sound sculpting, with layers of crackle and rust suffused softly into the superior structures of each song.
