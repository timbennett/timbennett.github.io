Dirt Farmer

By Doug Wallen 2012-06-05T00:00:00+00:00

If their band name summons images of worn flannel and grunge, Dirt Farmer couldn’t be further from that. The Melbourne-via-Albury five-piece do have the occasional harmonica part hinting at a professed alt-country element, but really they specialise in the most clean-cut of guitar-pop. There’s no dirt whatsoever on their debut EP, which tucks five tunes into 15 minutes with slick efficiency.

First up is ?Kick It?, basically an understudy in the event that tragedy should ever befall Little Red’s ?Rock It?. It’s got a bit more Vampire Weekend guitar waggle, but the boyish harmonies and reverbed pulse are all there. And like ?Rock It?, it’s a perfectly executed single that’s all but impossible to appreciate.

Kick It by Dirt Farmer

Such breezy lightness can get dangerously closer to vacuousness, which is why it’s good that previous single ?Johnny Marble? is more of a character song and lifts its dark saunter from Gorillaz’s ?Clint Eastwood?. ?Real Young? finds frontman Stuart Barlow, who used to play guitar for Lisa Mitchell, sounding a bit like Dean Wareham vocally, doling out talk of photobooths and other token timelessness. ?Showgirl? is Strokes-ish in the distorted singing and snaky guitar at chorus time, whereas ?Honey? is much more British Invasion than the rest and not as fresh-sounding for it.

Like many a young band, then, Dirt Farmer are still nailing down a definitive voice. Yet even as just an exercise in penning singles both indie- and radio-friendly, this EP is decidedly winsome. It may be fleeting, but that’s part of the charm. These songs don’t waste our time: they get in and then get out, with a surplus of catchiness.
