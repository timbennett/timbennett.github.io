Critics Poll 2010: 50-21

By Darren Levin 2010-12-20T13:00:00+00:00

You’ve had the chance to tell us your favourite local albums of the year in our fifth annual Readers Poll. Now, it’s our critics? turn to weigh in. Here’s the first installment (50-21) of our selections for Album of the Year 2010. Tomorrow, it’s the Top 20.

50. Various Artists
Long Gone Whistle: The Songs Of Maurice Frawley (Liberation)
?Overseen by old bandmates Paul Kelly and Charlie Owen, this is a fitting tribute, wide ranging and beautifully presented.?

49. Straight Arrows
It’s Happening (Rice Is Nice)

48. Heirs
Fowl (Denovali Records)
?’Fowl’ is a great leap forward for Heirs, taking them beyond genres into a realm which is almost hermetic in its vision; intensely focused, yet also expansive. It’s technically complex, yet uncluttered and sparse in its execution.?

47. Great Earthquake
Drawings (Sensory Projects)
?It’s a stressed-out, wiry mess of metallic percussion, hand-claps, accordion, bass and guitar that manages to use this intensity to invoke joy as well as nervousness.?

46. Gin Club
Deathwish (Plus One Records)
?From the urgent ?Pennies? to the regal, conclusive ?Shake Hands?, ‘Deathwish’ finds them concentrating their efforts toward a singular goal of potent, resonant songwriting.?

45. Kes Trio
Black Brown Green Grey White (Mistletone)
?’Black Brown Green Grey White’ is the musical equivalent of a persistent mosquito bite – irritating, but pleasurable to scratch and impossible to leave alone.?.

44. Various Artists
Artefacts Of Australian Experimental Music, Volume 2 (Shame File Music)
?’Volume II’ illustrates the merging of the more cerebral conceptual strains of post-Cage composers, and their increasing use of electronic technology such as tape loops and synthesisers, with the ?anything goes? spirit of noise and improvisational musicians that arrived at similar practices from the punk movements of the late ?70s.?

43. Sianna Lee
Phoenix Propellor (Enchanted Recordings)
?Whereas Love Outside Andromeda exhumed their frustrations through distortion pedals and driving post-grunge salvos, the music on ‘Phoenix Propeller’ retains all the tension like a tightly wound coil.?

42. Matt Bailey
The Three I’s (Gold Records)
?As the title of this album implies, Bailey’s writing and performance is part of an ongoing inquiry into the nature of identity. It’s part of everyday existence and an extension of the his personality, but it’s also a fiction, an artificial construct.?.

41. Holidays
Post Paradise (Liberation)
?Somewhere between Cut Copy’s electro wonderland, Gypsy And The Cat’s soft rock, and Empire Of The Sun’s delusions of pop grandeur sits ‘Post Paradise’.?

40. Faux Pas
Noiseworks (Sensory Projects/Heroics)
?With an evidently deep affection for music that countless others have dismissed as dated, awkward or ironic, it would be difficult to accurately second guess Faux Pas’ own aesthetic benchmarks.?

39. Chris Altmann
Que Paso (Ridin? High Records)

38. The Boat People
Dear Darkly (independent)
?’Dear Darkly’ should be lauded for its adventurous spirit, intelligent arrangements, but most of all, for being a sublime, idiosyncratic pop record.?

37. Amaya Laucirica
Early Summer (Departed Sounds)
?It’s an album marked by understatement and restraint, no matter how many different instruments materialise.?

36. Naked on the Vague
Heaps of Nothing (Siltbreeze)
?Naked on the Vague exploit what most of their contemporaries probably consider too outre: the surreal and the obfuscating, the colours, textures and illuminations that ensue through the penetration of some other.?

35. Ninetynine
Bande Magnetique (Pasty Records)
?A rewarding listen from a band who are inexplicably still one of Melbourne’s lesser-known treasures.?

34. Mystery Twin
Mystery Twin (Sensory Projects)
?It’s apt the disc comes swaddled in – illustrations of loveable, acid-haze-derived creatures frolicking among magical fronds and plants, so too are his sonic creations quietly suggestive of whimsical netherworlds.?

33. The Break
Church Of The Open Sky (Bombora Records)
?The Break don’t deal in puritan authenticity, but blend the traditional surf trademarks with occasional excursions into electronic sounds.?

32. Paradise Motel
Australian Ghost Story (independent)
?’AGS’ feels like an altogether more human Paradise Motel, one less concerned with myth and fantasy, and more with trenchant reality as a more reliable guide to illuminate the immutable fact that, as they used to say, this can’t last forever.?

31. Sia
We Are Born (Monkey Puzzle/Inertia)
?It manifests itself in music that yokes mainstream pop melodies, disco tempos and glossy synth-pop patinas.?

30. Grinderman
Grinderman 2 (Mute Records)
?The problem with Grinderman is not so much that Cave has set out to demystify his legacy, but that he seems to have lost his inspiration and direction in the process, and now can’t decide which road to travel down.?

29. Budd
Jawa (independent)
?It might be missing the restless and excitable sex beat of pure rock’n?roll, but its throbbing intensity hits you somewhere below your pudgy gut and stays there for a while.?

28. White Woods
Bellplay (Sensory Projects)
?White Woods have committed themselves to a specific brand of underground rock that doesn’t dare clean itself up.?

27. Kim Salmon & The Surrealists
Grand Unifying Theory (Low Transit Industries)
?This is an artist defined by his very restlessness, his willingness to both embrace and redefine his own legacy and keep presenting us with work that is as original as it is commercially unviable.?

26. Fabulous Diamonds
Fabulous Diamonds II (Chapter Music)
?This is kosmische for a post-cosmic world, a peek through a view finder into a future once fetishised but now forgotten.?

25. Darren Hanlon
I Will Love You At All (Flippin Yeah)
?He may be distinctly inner-west, he may be distinctly Gympie, he may be distinctly Australian, but what makes Darren Hanlon such an amazing songwriter is that his songs are so distinctly universal.?

24. Seja
We Have Secrets But Nobody Cares (Rice Is Nice)
?An insular, reflective record bursting with breathtaking pop melodies, Secrets seems designed for dancing around the bedroom more than at the club.?

23. Bum Creek
AL (Chapter Music)
?’AL’ proves the ongoing relevance of noise music in an age where celebrated modern rock bands can uncritically remanufacture done-and-dusted rock motifs without question.?


22. Charles Jenkins and The Zhivagos
Walk This Ocean (Dust Devil Music)

21. Cloud Control
Bliss Release (The Ivy League)
?’Bliss Release’ sounds like its title. It’s an album that will take you to that mystical, wondrous land with the green grass and dappled sunlight. It’s pop music with heart and soul, and it’s a beautiful listen.?
