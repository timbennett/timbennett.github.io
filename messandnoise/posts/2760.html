Mind Maps

By Jules LeFevre 2013-04-25T00:00:00+00:00

Space is a beautiful thing. It gives room, stretches everything, and lays the music out in a way that’s digestible. Small droplets can become rivers, given enough space.

Great Earthquake doesn’t do space. On his second LP, Melbourne artist Noah Symons stitches together a patchwork of rhythms, sounds and ideas to create an utterly beguiling and somewhat disjointed record. The compositions are dense, meandering and at times completely immersive. In between the swathes of liquid-y guitar, glockenspiel and vocal doo-daa’s, Symons adds in bird calls, rain sounds and, on more than one occasion, an accordion. It’s an ambitious soundscape that gets only partially realised.

Kicking off proceedings with a punchy drum track, the opening title track then starts piling on the layers at every fourth beat, and soon the charming melody provided by the glockenspiel is swallowed by vocal chants, piano drones and accordion stutters. ?Do. Make? nearly breaks the mould with a quietly thumbed guitar riff over bird tweets, but falls back when the jingle bells make an appearance. When the polyrhythms emerge mid-track they clash instead of click; likewise on the following track, the dislocating ?Appropriate?. The tendency toward overcrowding eliminates the necessary breathing space, and these tracks lose their simple, intelligent ideas within the barrage of noise.

Amidst the cacophony, though, there’s some good material. ?Human Activity? flows with a simple guitar melody over nature sounds, but takes a bizarre turn with a raucous drum interlude that cracks the song in two. ?More Fun Less Tears? is a tranquil break, with vocal melodies riding a mesh of soft guitar and drums. Taken individually, the instrumental layers contain good ideas. There are catchy hooks, crafted by a knowledgeable ear. Lifting the weight of a million layers from them, they finally begin to lift off the ground. Standout ?In Nature (Song for Owen)? operates on a more traditional level, and it’s all the better for it, containing a nicely worked melody that the layers build around rather than attempt to drown.

Mind Maps may just be one of the most apt album titles ever. Ideas bounce and clash and are then cast aside while new tangents are explored and then ignored. It bursts with potential, wanting everything at the same time. But what it really needs is space to breathe.
