Picture Machine

By ReneSchaefer 2009-10-01T00:00:00+00:00

A sense of small town struggle pervades the debut outing for Hobart’s Ivy St, writes REN? SCHAEFER.


Great art often comes out of physical or intellectual isolation. It’s one of the reasons Hobart has produced so many bands with distinct visions and a sense of righteous fury at being stuck in a stifling environment that places little value on their creative efforts. Ivy St is the latest group in a lineage that includes the likes of Sea Scouts, 50 Million Clowns, The Frustrations and Little Ugly Girls; musicians who channel the experiences of growing up in small towns, among peers with closed minds and conservative attitudes, and communicate these feelings in stark sounds and language.

While still a young band, listening to Picture Machine conveys the sense of people who have lived through a lot. A sense of struggle pervades these 10 songs, a sense of striving to be heard, striving to live. This is not entertainment, but a reason to go on living.

The album begins with a guitar chord and a bass riff repeated over and over, establishing an austere tone from the start. The mood is one of gravity, of struggling for air, of trying to find release. But that release does not come. When Thomas Hyland’s vocals kick in during the second song, the sense of claustrophobia only increases. His guitar is a dense carpet of treble notes, deliberately flat and devoid of melody, while Patrick Hatch’s bass plays compact circular melodies and Chris Campbell’s drums refuse to follow rock’n?roll cliches.

Against this monochrome backdrop, Hyland spits out words that speak of the profound alienation of a thinker who can’t help but question everything he observes. His startling use of language emerges gradually throughout this album, eventually leading the listener to strain to decipher his lines and piece together oblique narratives.

?What emerges is a poignant chronicling of life in an isolated community where the only possible way out is through physical escape or death.?

By the fourth song, ?Families Form Gangs?, I found myself skipping back through the track repeatedly to catch all the words, something I have hardly done since my teenage years. What emerges is a poignant chronicling of life in an isolated community where the only possible way out is through physical escape or death. The protagonist of the song is degraded and humiliated by the casual violence, both mental and real, that pervades everyday life, and mocked by a hypocritical religious education he has to endure against his convictions. All he can do is smile quietly ?at a truth that was not mine?. This dissociation is summed up in the line, ?I grew up in space?, conveying an infinite distance from the rest of society.

The religious theme resurfaces in ?Drunk On Blood?. Here the guitar is reigned in to give the voice more prominence. By no means a straight narrative, the central metaphor of the song refers to the transubstantiation of wine into Jesus? blood in the Roman Catholic communion ritual. Being drunk on religion on one hand, and alcohol on the other, offer the same relief from the emptiness of existence and small town aimlessness. ?We’ll drink to pass our time, for everything we’re not,? Hyland mourns.

Elsewhere, literature and television are equally dismissed as being unable to offer solace or hope. Even music and writing offer little in the way of escape. In the final song, ?I Don’t Read My Words?, Hyland addresses his ambiguous attitude to music, as he recalls sitting in his car listening to a love song. ?The voice of another tempted me to be theirs?, but he finds it impossible to embrace the conceit of this romantic entreaty and refuses to employ similar devices in his own work: ?I’d rather trap things in words than try to meet some demand,? he sings.

This conflictedness about the compulsion to create in the face of popular and universal indifference is a theme many writers have had to grapple with. In its mixture of despair and unreasonable optimism it echoes Samuel Beckett’s conclusion to his novel The Unnamable: ?I can’t go on, I’ll go on.? The fact that this record exists in the first place means that Ivy St have already won.

+

Ivy St’s Picture Machine is out now on Wireless Records.
