Black Brown Green Grey White

By ReneSchaefer 2010-09-01T00:00:00+00:00

On Black Brown Green Grey White, Kes Trio make beautifully ugly music, ripping apart the guts of indie pop and setting them on fire. They do this with the utterly conventional tools of a rock band, but they apply a fierce intelligence and perverse spirit to their songs, which sets them apart from just about any other type of music being made right now.

From its opening salvo of abstract howls and heavy tub-thumping, to its mutant, quasi-psychedelic pastoral interludes, this is an utterly schizophrenic record. It documents a band cutting loose from all notions of genre, refusing to play any game other than their own, and delighting in confounding their listeners. There are remnants of twisted blues progressions in Karl Scullin’s guitar playing, which favours a glistening clean sound, that only occasionally breaks into distorted riffs. Lehmann Smith’s bass, in contrast, sounds like an implement used to scrape the barnacles off the bottom of a disintegrating Russian oil tanker, while Julian Patterson plays drums like he’s trying to kill imaginary cockroaches crawling all over his kit.

Like the greatest works by Syd Barrett, Brian Wilson, Pere Ubu and Captain Beefheart, this album delights in wild juxtapositions of mood. It contrasts jarring dissonance with sugary twee pop moments, primal scream therapy and John Lennon-on-helium warped classicism. It’s a rollercoaster ride through the history of post-Beatles pop that sideswipes the avant garde and summersaults into a no-man’s land of music yet to be pigeonholed.

In a recent M+N interview, Scullin proclaimed a dissatisfaction with traditional approaches to vocals and song structures. Listening to this record, his internal struggle to break free of constraints becomes utterly palpable. Individually, and as a group, these guys have been playing for long enough to feel comfortable ditching the compass, with no expectation of a safe return from whatever terra nullius they might discover. It’s what makes them exhilarating and utterly essential.

Australian underground music is littered with iconoclasts who commanded small, yet loyal followings, without ever demanding or receiving accolades. The Laughing Clowns, for all their inspired, groundbreaking melding of punk rock and jazz, remain the province of hermit-like aesthetes, far from the mainstream. Tactics are name-dropped by a few cognoscenti. Scullin, Smith and Patterson understand this dynamic. They couldn’t give a shit really whether they will be understood, feted, or ignored. This is their strength – to forge on regardless, to become more obscure with each release, to break templates, to revel in the power to confound.

Black Brown Green Grey White is the musical equivalent of a persistent mosquito bite – irritating, but pleasurable to scratch and impossible to leave alone.
