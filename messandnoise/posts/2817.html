Step Brothers

By Mitchell Judge 2013-10-01T00:00:00+00:00

It seems like an appropriate time to do a stocktake for Alex Grigg: his first innings with Red Riders returned two catchy yet rather bland, middle-of-the-road rock albums that seem so dated that, listening to Drown in Colour now, it’s hard to believe it was released just four years ago. Second time out, his partnership with Owen Penglis yielded catchy, endlessly replayable garage rock with Straight Arrows. The main difference between the two, apart from trading in a studio for a kitchen and adding a few fuzz pedals, was the fact that Al learned not to take it too seriously.

The debut album for his third band Palms is made up of 11 songs about unrequited love, and the theme is obvious from the album’s very first line: ?Lately I’ve been thinking about the things I can’t let go.? Now that’s an understatement. You could be forgiven for initially thinking that Al is starting to take himself a little too seriously again. However, this heart-on-sleeve approach has resulted in a personal and honest record. The album cover offers an awkward, unpolished glimpse into their everyday life, a stark contrast to Red Riders? brooding band photos and model-adorned album covers.

The album is built around the band’s three undeniably brilliant singles, and if you have spent any amount of time near a radio in the past 18 months, they will likely be familiar enough to feel like cuts in a best-of compilation rather than a new release. Making use of the quiet/loud dynamic, ?Love? and ?This Last Year? display the unrestrained emotion in their howling choruses that was always missing from Red Riders? songs.

?A Supposedly Fun Thing I’ll Never Do Again? and ?End of Forever? succeed by treading the same path: combining powerful hooks with an honest, relatable heartbreak story. These songs are elevated by their slacker charm as well as Grigg’s delivery, which has the ability to pack a great deal of passion into simple lyrics. Examples of this no-frills approach to lyrics are littered throughout the album, with easy rhymes like ?I don’t think that I should see your face again/How the hell did we even end up friends??

There are rare moments where Palms journey away from the game plan; a left-field guitar freakout turns the straightforward ?You Were Mine? into something that wouldn’t look out of place on an early Dinosaur Jr. record. ?Far Gone?, the album’s slow-burning closer, features dreamy guitar work as Grigg forces out all of the things he doesn’t want to do, stopping just short of saying that he wants to be your tugboat captain. The band naturally slides into a different sound in both cases, which hints at the possibility of exploring new territory for album #2.

A decade has passed since Red Riders formed and, while Grigg has certainly matured as a songwriter, he hasn’t forgotten to have fun. This album proves that he’s got the balance just about right.
