Black Rat

By Edward Sharp-Paul 2014-05-04T00:00:00+00:00

I like DZ Deathrays but I don’t know why. As a critic, this is a shitty feeling.

Bratty, thrashy punkoid bands are almost as old as rock ?n? roll, and as long as there are hormones, they will remain a part of the scenery. There’s almost nothing to it beyond spunky insolence, volume and a basic grasp of one’s instrument, so why is it that some strike a (figurative) chord while others don’t?

Well, in this case it’s the hooks. The frantic chant-along on ?Gina Works at Hearts?, the sinuous riff in ?Tonight Alright?, the plaintive falsetto on ?Keep Myself on Edge?, and about a dozen other moments that make you forget that you came here to lose a litre of sweat. Certainly Shane Parsons? innovative octave-doubling, modem-impersonating guitar work might be one reason as well: he makes a lie of the idea that simple equates to stupid, finding endless ways to make his guitar talk.

Another reason DZ Deathrays hit the mark might lie in the band’s ability to wring dynamic and rhythmic variation out of their limited means. This has been a barely-heralded strength of their work for a while, and Black Rat represents a consolidation and extension in that regard. The band don’t exactly genre-hop, but they are able to summon the ghosts of other genres through a variety of means. ?Reflective Skull? has the faintest hint of soul, but in tone rather than style; producer Burke Reid dials in an incredibly tight, compressed drum sound while Parsons gets as close as anyone will to making a guitar sound like a trumpet fanfare. ?Northern Lights?, on the other hand, does Arcade Fire-style grandeur on a frayed shoestring – and of course, the roughness of the approximation is where all the interest lies.

These are the digressions, though. Back on home turf, and there are plenty of garage stompers designed with the pit in mind. They do what they need to do (and they remind you what a monstrous hitter drummer Simon Ridley is), but it makes for a slightly splay-footed album, with one foot in the past and toes in several futures. As a result, Black Rat, while not all that long, is definitely too long. There was a time when bands like this would bang out EPs, with albums only an occasional indulgence. The standard doesn’t really tail off, but 11 tracks over 40 minutes simply isn’t justified.

Bloodstreams was a howl into the void, and it turns out the void digs DZ Deathrays? fresh sound. Do they keep howling? Of course they do, but with just enough new timbres and textures to suggest that there are a few more chapters in this particular book.
