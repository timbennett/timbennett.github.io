Curiouser

By DomAlessio 2008-10-18T00:00:00+00:00

The music of Kate Miller-Heidke is an acquired taste. It’s because a pop song is never simply just a ?pop song? for this former opera pixie. There are so many embellishments, both vocally and instrumentally, that her songs can be refreshingly quirky or cumbersomely overweight. The inconsistent quality of the 12 tracks included on her sophomore release Curioiser only serve to exacerbate the mixed reactions a Kate Miller-Heidke album will inevitably generate.

Curiouser* is an apt title. The 27-year-old has certainly broadened the palette employed on her 2007 debut *Little Eve. In reductionist terms, if Beck produced Kate Bush’s next album, it would sound something like Kate Miller-Heidke’s latest musical tome. Like any commercial pop artist, she’s vulnerable to bouts of banality, such as the saccharine piano ballad ?The Last Day On Earth? and the pedestrian, gossamer pop of ?The End Of School?. ?Caught In The Crowd? submits to platitudes in a song where KMH laments not standing up for a punk geek at school. It’s a lesson for the kiddies about acceptance.

Curiouser is filled with pop oddities like the ska-inflected ?God’s Gift to Women?, where KMH smoothly delivers with wit, ?If you’re God’s gift to women/ Then God got it wrong?, and ?Politics in Space?, which is part Appalachian folk, part Disney musical. The Neptunes-esque funk of ?The One Thing I Know? finds Miller-Heidke overreaching but the backbeat groove is insistent and propulsive, rescuing a song whose parts feel incongruous. The album’s first single and arguable highlight is the self-deprecating ?Can’t Shake It?, where Miller-Heidke’s confident cadence is superb above an intractably infectious pop-rock foundation. It’s the song Rogue Traders wish they were good enough to write.

It’s within Kate Miller-Heidke’s elliptical take on pop that Curiouser*?s strength lies. It occasionally succumbs to typical mainstream triteness but if more songwriters were as gallant as KMH, the Austereo network wouldn’t simply be a vortex of shit. If nothing else, hopefully *Curiouser will serve as a gateway drug for tweens to discover a higher plane of music.
