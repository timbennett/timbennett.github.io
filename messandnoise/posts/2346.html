Summer

By Doug Wallen 2010-08-01T00:00:00+00:00

Recorded at the same time as this year’s earlier Autumn* 7″, Erasers? *Summer cassette is distinct thematically rather than sonically. The two EPs even share a track in ?Autumn Trees?.

Summer is every bit as excellent as its predecessor while showing off a few different tricks from the Perth duo. Multi-instrumentalists Rupert Thomas and Rebecca Orchard have a knack for isolating beautifully tranquil guitar loops and finding new sparks of life in looped percussion and keyboard drones. What sets it apart is how clean and pure everything is, steering well clear of the self-conscious haze that’s flooding so much home-recorded music right now.

?Autumn Trees? remains bewitching months after first exposure, starting with a melody not far removed from Architecture in Helsinki’s old gem ?Souvenirs? before teasing out Clinic-like melodica and then Orchard’s detached vocals. None of that quite foretells ?Something That’s Nothing?, however. By far the longest song Erasers has released, it mines a ticking, tinny drum loop against a minimal buzz of organ. Orchard’s voice is more vague and ghostly than ever, clouding the lyrics and evoking a mantra-like trance against the song’s seven-minute drone. It’s one of those tracks where you can hear all the parts independently of each other and yet it all still adds up to something compelling and mysterious.

?Softly Spoken/Summers End? is indeed two songs that segue together, again with the vocals set on some otherworldly plane and the music as quiet and simple and perfect as Beach House’s recent album. A bit of rustling leads into the closing ?Warm Air?, which sees Orchard’s voice more earthbound, despite the exacts lyrics still obscured. There are more spacey washes of keyboard and tingling guitar lines, but in no way does it feel redundant. And though the ingredients are familiar, the results aren’t easy to pigeon-hole. It’s as much ambient as it is post-punk; all vibe and deconstruction.

Like the 7?, Summer was home-recorded and features cover art by Orchard. It was released by US tape label Solid Melts and limited to 50 copies, which includes a digital version. Track it down while you can: There’s much to admire in the band’s miniature creations, which drift by with all the airy lightness the title promises.
