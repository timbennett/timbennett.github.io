Fail Like You Mean It

By Doug Wallen 2010-09-03T00:00:00+00:00

It’s not enough to say Scott Spark plays keyboards. Rather, he plays every instrument involving keys. On this first album, he turns to celeste, grand piano, Wurlitzer and Farfisa organs, harpsichord, analogue synthesisers, and toy piano. Spark also reaps the musical potential of scissors and a Polaroid camera. Yes, there are proverbial bells and whistles all over Fail Like You Mean It, the Brisbane singer-songwriter’s splashy debut. There’s also a wide cast of players and guests, complete with the ubiquitous Megan Washington singing back-up. It’s 42 minutes of go-for-broke piano-pop bustle.

And so one has to strain at times to concentrate on the nuances of Spark’s lyrics and delivery. They’re definitely there – he proves a clear-eyed songwriter with a sharply melodic voice and no shortage of subject matter – but it’s doubtful so many layers of extravagance do him any real favours. Spark has talented friends, sure, and he reached out to American producer Tucker Martine (Decemberists, Death Cab) to mix the album, but all the fleeting instruments and ideas distract from Spark’s actual work. That’s a problem, considering he can recall songwriters like Josh Pyke and Ben Folds as often as he rightly distinguishes himself. There’s also an issue of momentum with the album, which never matches the bright, tumbling exuberance of its first three songs.

Namely, ?My Address Book? and the listless ?Elvis? lag behind that initial burst, and lyrics from there range from sentimental (?Last Christmas?) to whiny (?I’m Fighting!?) and bitter (?Delusions Of The Heart?). To keep our ears tickled all the while, Seja Vogel lends beats to one song, Melbourne writer Lorelei Vashti offers a spirited ?hooray? on another, and The Go-Betweens? Adele Pickvance often plays bass. Yet those elements contribute to a din that only finally fades on the last two songs. ?Half Formed Song? may be the first clear glimpse at Spark; it’s one of the few tracks where he simply plays piano and sings, although a three-piece brass section does comes in. And the closing ?Eat Your Heart Out? is stripped down but finds an interesting rustle in programmed beats, guitar and synth. It’s gorgeous, and what’s more, it’s not running itself ragged trying to use all the tricks at its disposal.

During that same song, Spark croons, ?Now I’m ready to be found?, and while it’s not what the song is about, he’s edging closer to finding himself as a musician and songwriter. When he does, it’ll have little to do with guest stars and novel instrumentation.
