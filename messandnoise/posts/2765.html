Solemns

By Anthony Carew 2013-05-12T00:00:00+00:00

It’s just three songs, but ANTHONY CAREW finds much to savour in the second pairing of Will Oldham and Dirty Three guitarist Mick Turner (and friends).


The last time The Marquis de Tren and Bonnie ?Prince? Billy came co-billed was back in 2000, with Get On Jolly. It found these twin figures of indie royalty – Mick Turner of Dirty Three, and Will Oldham, freshly graduated from Palace to ?Prince? – collaborating via traded tapes like some proto-Postal Service. Turner made the music in Melbourne, Oldham sung over it in Louisville (?I know you take pleasure in my singing,? he sung; oh and how) and an EP was born.

At the time, the pair were both frighteningly prolific – Turner playing in Dirty Three and the Dirty duo the Tren Brothers, and as a freshly-minted solo artist; Oldham being, well, Oldham – and rather than some one-off, the set had the feeling of a first instalment. Yet it’s taken them 13 years to collaborate again; the interim having found Turner slowing, playing and recording more sporadically (this is his first non-Dirty Three record since 2007?s semi-solo LP Blue Trees*), whilst Oldham has wandered into places far stranger (*Wonder Showzen*, *Trapped in the Closet*, *Jackass 3D, signature cologne, Bonnie ?Prince? Billy-branded condoms, etc.) than anyone could’ve expected.

??Solemn 10? is built around the Turner/White interplay and its quarter-century history.?

Solemns finds them billed again on equal footing, but it’s not just the two of them; not just a simple match of music to words; nor was it made over distance. Instead, the EP was recorded in Melbourne at Head Gap during Bonnie ?Prince? Billy’s 2012 Australian tour and piles a host of musicians into the one space. This includes Oldham’s whole live band – Emmett ?Cairo Gang? Kelly, Van Campbell, and, gladly, Angel Olsen, she of the heart-stilling warble – and both Turner and his fellow Tren Brother, Jim White.

Musically, Get On Jolly* had the feeling of Turner’s solo records – a hovering cloud of hot-breath melodicas and dewy guitars – but *Solemns, with White behind the kit, can’t help but be more rhythmic, more forceful, more driven-forward. His percussion, as ever, uses bursts of misdirection and irregularity to create a sense of momentum, picking up steam even if his time-keeping never comes close to locomotive chug.

Turner’s guitar, the definitive instrument of the previous EP – and once so memorably described by Bobby Gillespie as sounding ?the way that stars are spaced out in the sky? – withdraws a little more: ?Solemn 28? awash in churchy organ chords and ?Solemn 119? finding his playing parrying and pirouetting with Kelly’s. ?Solemn 10?, the centrepiece of this sadly brief EP, is, however, built around the Turner/White interplay and its quarter-century history, that way they can sound both stumbling and in-step, bumping into – and off of – each other beautifully.

?Solemn 10? uses the Tren Brothers? playing as framework to hang a slow-stirring epic on; even its peaks leaving a carved-out central space – the one usually flashed with Warren Ellis’s lightning jags of squalling violin – in which Oldham and Olsen can sweetly carol. On Get On Jolly, the Bonnie ?Prince? set the words of early-20th-century Bengali poet Rabindranath Tagore to song, but here he reaches back farther and sings Bible psalms (the ?solemns? of the title) aloud as if presiding over missa cantata.

Ever since he played a teenage preacher in John Sayles? 1987 movie Matewan*, Oldham has often been thought of in religious terms. He’s been staging a dialogue with God ever since – from Palace Brothers? 1993 debut *There is No One What Will Take Care of You* to 2011?s *Wolfroy Goes to Town – and thus feels at home singing of wickedness and sin, pleading for his Lord, emptying his heart in devotion. ?Arise, O Lord, strike at him!? Oldham and Olsen sing, in glorious harmony, over the mystical rattle and splatter of the Brothers Tren; ?Solemn 10? cresting, pushing into a place far beyond some between-albums EP, into those inarticulable airs where music brushes against the divine.

+

####?Solemns? is out now through Drag City.
