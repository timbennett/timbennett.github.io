Grinderman

By andyr 2007-02-26T12:00:00+00:00

Grinderman isn’t quite the monster its concept promised: a rough-and-tumble offshoot of Nick Cave and The Bad Seeds, on which four ageing rock idols stumble back into the gutters of their youth. Between them, Cave’s partners Martyn Casey, Jim Sclavunos and Warren Ellis have a shared resume that includes The Triffids, The Cramps and The Dirty Three (though Ellis’s violin is used sparingly). Apparently Cave, who tries his hand at lead guitar, ad-libbed many of the lyrics. He mythologises himself from starkly different angles. One song is about a rock star who ?drank panther piss and fucked the girls you’re probably married to,? the next a withered and pathetic 49-year-old who can’t pull groupies anymore.

It’s a return to Cave’s chaotic past only for those who can remember The Birthday Party first-hand, now too old to require much shock before declaring things ?noisy?. That doesn’t mean it’s bad, just that comparisons to Junkyard* would be an overstatement. Each time the band cut loose they balance it with a slower track that could have fit on the last few Bad Seeds albums, though the feeling here is less self-conscious. It’s more than a vanity project, but less than breathtaking. *Grinderman?s best song is its last, ?Love Bomb?, a sped-up tribute to The Velvet Underground’s ?Sister Ray? in which Cave’s guitar sounds like a pig yanked through an abattoir. It’s noteworthy that, having incorporated other artist’s lyrics into songs in the past, Cave leaves out the original’s junkie catch-cry. ?I’m searching for my mainline,? sang Lou Reed in 1967. Four decades later, Cave replaces the chorus with: ?She said she used to love me all night long/?But you’ve gotten so thin and sick?.
