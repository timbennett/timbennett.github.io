Euphoria

By Hannah Brooks 2012-03-23T00:00:00+00:00

Like the feeling it’s boldly named after, Love Connection’s second album is a slippery one. While there’s no question that the band – led by Michael Caterer on guitar/vocals and Kobi Simpson on keys, with drummer Dean Noble and bassist Nathan Burgess – has blossomed over a short period into a muscular and noteworthy Melbourne quartet, Euphoria is hard to get a hold on. It’s exuberant and rapturous, filled with layer upon layer of warm guitars, bewitching keyboards and volcanic drums. Yet even with persistent plays, the album has a way of leaving you unsure: stranded and polarised by your own reactions.

Running at 57 minutes, it’s slicker than the debut they released in 2010. Gone is the cartoon cover and youthful pep for something bigger and more well-oiled. Over the span of 11 tracks there are great moments. ?You Don’t Need Muscles To Get Love? and first single ?Nobody Knows? instantly stand head and shoulders above the others, with memorable melodies and a rich dreaminess. Yet bookended by flatter tracks, the promise of these songs, along with standout instrumental ?Forest?, is obscured. In the group’s eagerness to show their range, the stronger tracks lose their power.

Love Connection – Nobody Knows (edit) by sensoryprojects10

?Coasting? is a pretty homage to the leitmotif of choice for so many ?now? bands – summer, sand and shimmeriness – while ?Day by Day? grapples with the ?Peter Pan? blues. Lyrically, Euphoria struggles. While the title of ?Sex In The Cinema? indicates greatness, a glance at the lyrics – ?Sometimes I will be right there/Because you know I care/Oh fuck you/You know that I love you/You’re alright? – kills some of the magic. Most lyrics are indecipherable but the ones you can hear hint at Caterer struggling with frustration and confusion, as if he’s tugging at his own hair, trying to disengage the knots.

Unexpectedly the final, 20-minute title track is one of the finest. Sitting somewhere in the realm of Aphex Twin astral travelling to a kinesiology appointment, it sees Caterer at his least radio-friendly but comes closest to touching base with the record’s emotional namesake.

Euphoria signals a band with immense ideas, talent and ambition but one whose execution, at least on this album, is marred by meandering. With the announcement they have yet another album ready to release later this year, Love Connection are proving to be determined and driven. Whether this is an asset or a reckless impediment is debatable.
