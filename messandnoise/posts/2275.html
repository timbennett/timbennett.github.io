Conquer the Town, Easy as Cake

By Jodi Biddle 2010-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

Brisbane has got splendid, offbeat indie pop coming out the wazoo. Something about the sticky suburban sprawl and quiet night time streets must breed a desire to pick up a ukulele and get some male-female harmonies happening. It’s crowded for such a small scene, and dangerously close to becoming tiresome. But among all the would-be troubadours and indie bands vying for attention, Ball Park Music step aside and quietly hold their own.

They’re sort of earnest and fruity, like your dad when he’s dancing at a wedding and doesn’t care how embarrassing it is that he’s trying so hard: there’s absolutely nothing disaffected about them. Coupled with a burgeoning reputation for a blistering live show and what you have is some serious promise. Their second release, Conquer the Town, Easy as Cake doesn’t disappoint, bursting with enthusiasm and downright joie de vivre.

Breakout single ?iFly? is unapologetically twee, with the darling chorus of, ?I fucking love you, I think you’re pretty/I fucking love you all of time?, sure to melt the coldest hipster heart. It’s pop at its most basic, borrowing from ?90s hopeless-but-adorable-boy themes expounded by the likes of Weezer and Ben Kweller. ?The Ghost I Saw When I Was Dreaming? has shades of Beck and Eels with lonesome loudspeaker vocals, while ?Hello Anais? is a gentle, swaying ballad. ?Sea Strangers (I Don’t Know You)? picks up the pace again with some fun, bouncing piano and another ridiculously catchy chorus about all the sharp and witty one liners we wish we had thought of at the time (as well as the superiority of sea monkeys as companions). ?Western Whirl? is David McCormack’s solo stuff without the pervading sense of irony.

This is a band still finding their feet, wandering through folk, honky tonk, stopping briefly at alt-country and tipping a hat to mid ?90s pop punk. And yet despite these disparate elements, or perhaps because of them, Conquer the Town, Easy as Cake has a greater longevity than many debut EPs. More than anything else, however, it’s a promise of even better things to come.
