Hypomantra

By Tommy Faith 2014-01-15T00:00:00+00:00

Sydney duo Yon Yonson have wrung an ambitious, unpredictable album out of personal insecurities, writes TOMMY FAITH.


Were you to make an assessment of Yon Yonson the man, based purely on his musical output, the list of characteristics would be headed by a pair of words, underlined for emphasis: clinical depression. At first listen this music is dark and loose, and it pulls you in and down.

Having made that list, you’d have to throw it out because you are wrong. Firstly, Yon Yonson is not a singular entity and not a man at all. Rather, a burgeoning Sydney duo (Andrew Kuo and Nathan Saad) who took the long road to releasing their second record Hypomantra* via a mixtape and a spate of EPs and the hard-worn path of the critic’s darling. *Hypomantra is their first release to truly begin to extend out to those with less refined ears. Secondly, it would be an injustice to the duo to write them off as depressed pop, and although no record featuring Simo Soo is ever going to feel particularly level-headed, this is a highly refined collection of songs is flecked with the man/machine juxtaposition and the honesty of introspection.

?A post-tertiary pair of bedroom producers struggling with a sense of self.?

?Feeling pretty down since they gave me this degree,? opines Kuo. Many are they who would label this a first-world problem but it’s not really, is it? At least, not exclusively. The search for self and purpose is relevant to all humanity, whether or not our relative luxuries allow us time to indulge these base desires. I choose to fixate on the severe honesty of the record’s moments like these, for to pretend to be anything other than a young adult from western Sydney would be a false labour. We are what we are and for Yon Yonson that means a post-tertiary pair of bedroom producers struggling with a sense of self.

The notion is reiterated in ?Nothing to Be Done? (bleak enough, if only in title), which demonstrates the same deficient sense of self: ?Since I was young I wanted to be someone but I never got close to that.? This is the hypomantra reference on which the record title hinges. The same medical prefix that’s used to indicate a deficiency of heat in hypothermia here denotes a insufficiency of self, coupled with a repeated statement to create the Hypomantra, said over and over and over. Attention need also be paid to the more technical aspects of this song, namely that most gloriously rich bass line that prevents a full immersion in bleaker waters. Back on that conceptual tip, it’s interesting to see such self-doubt paralleled with the self-awareness of ?Orantes Lane? (?I never finish songs/I just glue all of my demos together?).

Hypomantra by Yon Yonson

The highlight of Hypomantra is ?Culver City?, which sees the duo at their most ambitious, extending geographically (Culver City sits in West LA) as well as technically within a rich, vast tableau of sound, starting on a bed of glitched vocal runs and affected acoustic guitar. Layers are added, vocals enter, staccato drums snaps increase in pace. More piano. More guitar. Stylised bass. Electric guitar, it keeps building. By 3:10 the vocals return and all things ascend, particularly that electric guitar line. Then it’s over. In my eyes this straddles two worlds the same as an artist like Bibio. It’s highly organic in its instrumentation but filtered heavily through synthesisers, the internet, computers and online digitising machines. Hell, I’ll come out and say it: this could be a Bibio track.

For all its quasi-exuberance and absurd samples, ?Intro (No Shade, No Shine, No Birds)? doesn’t entirely fit with the rest of the record, despite being completely engrossing. It’s a touch insane – between the chanting Children of the Corn, ?50s game-show brass and rollicking xylophone, it’s hard to find your feet in what is ostensibly a nauseating funhouse until that hip-hop beat drops, heralded by a psychotic, pitch-shifted sample. It’s the conclusion of this song that makes way for the bass line in ?Nothing to be Done? and a prime example of the strange transitioning across the release as a whole.

?Best imbibed in one sitting; from track one to the record’s end, the music effectively never stops.?

It almost feels as if the Yon Yons have organised the record through arbitrary dissections, with outros indistinguishable from the following tracks? intros. It lends itself to a record best imbibed in the one sitting, in that, from track one to the record’s end, the music effectively never stops. It falters and quietens but the sounds never cease and the cyclical mantra goes on, ending blithely with a song about wine.

Yon Yonson have spent quite a time in the shadows but with the dawn of Hypomantra that could be about to change. It’s a self-assured release that’s ironically underpinned by lyrical self-doubt. It’ll be interesting to see if they’ve managed to shed the insecurities by the time the next record is due, and just as interesting to see if they can produce anything as strong in the absence of those same insecurities.

+

##?Hypomantra? is out now as a free download.
