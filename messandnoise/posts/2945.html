Falling For You

By Anthony Carew 2014-09-28T00:00:00+00:00

There’s always a moment at any Harpoons show where people tend to lose their shit. It’s that instant when these four unassuming-looking kids let loose with something that doesn’t just sound like some old soul ballad, but summons that genuine spirit, stokes a fire that feels like it’s going to burn the whole place down. Hearing Bec Rigby belt out something like ?Ring Ring? – a heart-rending slow-jam steeped in mid-?60s Atlantic R&B singles – in a wild soulful holler is hardly what one expects at, say, Monday Night Mass. And the collective freakout of audiences, unsuspecting or in-the-know, has been a highlight of Harpoons shows since way back when these unassuming-looking kids were even younger, even fresher of face and surprising of sound.

The Harpoons? debut 2011 EP, Faith*, was a work of lo-fi pastiche whose rawness – and rough guitars – made it play like a garage-rock record. It was largely sung by brothers Jack and Henry Madin, which means that, three years on, it feels like formative sketchings; a demo relic of a band who now sound neither raw nor rockin?. By 2012?s single *Walk Away/Keep You Around, Rigby was at the forefront of their sound, and both songs have ended up on The Harpoons? long-in-the-making debut LP. The album finds the quartet moving beyond their simple beginnings, coming both into their own and into the moment: boasting more synths and drum machines, more contemporary R’n?B production leanings and more Martin King on the mic; the band’s one-time drummer moving to the front-of-stage for the suitably slinky ?Don’t Say My Name? and ?Can We Work This Out?.

The tracks are all variations on the love song: chronicling desire, daydreams and romance pined for, consummated and lost. The lyrics rarely veer anywhere near idiosyncrasy and quotability; instead, they sound like pop-song platitudes couched in the universal nature of heartbreak. (And speaking of universal: when King sings ?cell phone? on ?Can We Work This Out?, mid boy/girl back-and-forth with Rigby – la Oscar + Martin’s ?What I Know?, an Australian heart can only sink.) The band – and the Madins, especially – are students of pop, and they know that sing-song sentiments can come to life and create magic, if blessed with the right melody and sung with the right conviction.

And this is where Rigby comes in. Vocals may be divided between the four members, but there’s a real divide between those songs which employ her as a supporting character and those in which she takes centre stage. The divide is even greater between hearing her sing something like ?I will wait every night and every day/to know if you feel it? and seeing it typed out in black-and-white, bordering on banal. She sings it in a way that makes you believe it, and Falling For You, in turn, is an album with the songs to turn a listener into a believer.
