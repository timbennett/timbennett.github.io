Novo?s

By Mitchell Judge 2013-10-15T00:00:00+00:00

You don’t forget the first time you see The Gooch Palms live. Leroy Macqueen’s combination of nudity, blood and crowd interaction creates something much more shocking and entertaining than a show from an ordinary rock band. As they walk the fine line between a novelty band and a genuinely unique punk two-piece, it raises the question of whether the band’s songs could stand up without these ridiculous antics.

It takes less than four minutes for the band to prove that their songwriting can surpass their attention-grabbing live show with the album’s best song, opener ?We Get By?. The catchy ?me and you? refrain is the first of many repetitive yet irresistible choruses throughout the album. However, it is obvious that the couple’s attention has shifted for their proper debut: they have replaced the paranoia and absurdity of early singles ?Cucaracha? and ?Houston We Have a Problem? with stories from everyday life. Singing in a band, eating takeaway and watching reruns of Prisoner, it is all very normal by The Goochies? standards, and there’s no sign of a Theremin to be found.

After last year’s space-themed R U 4 Sirius?, the band has changed from being decidedly out of this world to pinpointing their existence within Newcastle. In a similar manner to The Fighting League?s Dominic ?King of the Capital? Death, Macqueen now relishes being the face, voice and gooch of the Steel City, fully equipped with a Newcastle Knights guitar strap. ?Hunter Street Mall? details the love-hate relationship they have with Newcastle: ?All the smackheads are lined up to pick up the dole – because there’s nothing going on at the mall!? ?Novo’s? hits the target far better, critiquing the mundaneness of the city and its inhabitants with the brilliant line ?9 to 5, that’s how a Novo lives their life/At 64 they come alive, buy the RV, ready to die.?

Sure, there is nothing new about airing the frustration of living in a boring small town, but this insightful commentary shows a level of maturity far beyond the tired jingoism that was suggested by the album title. Whether this approach was a result of the band growing out of their juvenile rebellious phase or simply following the dolewave trend is tough to decipher, when in truth it is probably a combination of the two.

NOVO’S by The Gooch Palms

In a rare reflection of their live show, Kat Friend steps out from her musical sidekick role to play a notable ?straight guy? role. Her simple bratty delivery on ?Loudest Mouth? and ?The Slide? bears a striking resemblance to MC and Sweetie from Bloods, and it provides a nice contrast to Macqueen’s vocal gymnastics. Album closer ?Don’t Cry? is easily the best example of Macqueen’s impressive range, as he jumps from a low-register croon to an utterly ridiculous falsetto. It is also the only song on Novo’s that is remotely shocking or divisive; instead they have delivered a pleasant album that does not need to rely on ludicrousness to be enjoyable.

However, it is this ordinary pleasantness that makes it almost easy to forget. And that’s something I thought I would never say about The Gooch Palms.
