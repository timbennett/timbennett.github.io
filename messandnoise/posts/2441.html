Baptism Of Uzi

By Patrick 2011-03-22T00:00:00+00:00

As a stand-alone statement, Baptism of Uzi doesn’t make a lot of sense. The two critical semantic terms – a verb that traces its origins back to the critical early events of the New Testament, and a noun describing a machine gun originally invented by Israeli officer Uziel Gal – do not sit together naturally. Its discursive intent is confusing, to say the least – at least one person I’ve encountered has mixed up Baptism of Uzi with Mission of Burma (and narrowly missed Nation of Ulysses) – is it a statement of spiritual enlightenment through the use of automatic machine guns, perhaps? Or is about the atonement of violence through the baptismal ritual?

One thing’s for certain: it’s not a name that leaves the memory – no matter how snowed down with the trivialities, inanities or chemical excess – in a hurry. And you can make the same observation in relation to Baptism of Uzi’s music.

Having released the rarely-sighted Dictaphonium 1 last year, Baptism of Uzi has now released a second record (actually, a fetching orange cassette). At its broadest categorisation, this is psychedelic music, in all that beaten and battered term’s laboured glory. The cassette is divided into two contrasting sides.

The first, epitomised by the instrumental ?Dead By Dawn?, is acid rock in its most confronting grinding, cerebral guise. A meandering beat is gradually encircled by spriralling space-rock synth noise and shards of guitar that pierce the atmosphere with the intensity of jolts of static electricity in a fledgling storm. ?Strange Cousin? is similarly constructed, a loosely-configured structure of freaked-out riffs and rolling drum beats dancing around each other like a couple of acid casualties exploring the boundaries of free-form existence. ?The Drought? is awash with progressive rock indulgence. Fighting for attention against deranged synth effects is a droning vocal that sounds like a person lost in a sea of psychological confusion, searching for something of cognitive resonance.

Dead By Dawn by Baptism Of Uzi

The second side is ushered in with ?Stray Current?. In stark opposition to the preceding tracks, this is simply a great pop song. Its opening moments invoke memories of Julian Cope’s ?Out of My Mind On Dope and Speed? (a song returned to its rightful glory by The Sand Pebbles, a band happy to champion Baptism of Uzi’s many psychedelic talents). Whereas Cope was on his way to a Lewis Carroll-inspired world of mycological-excitement, Baptism of Uzi unveils the oft-neglected pop beauty latent in 1970s West Coast rock. ?The Bat? continues the trajectory; if its predecessor was dosing the Doobie Brothers with Owsley acid, ?The Bat? is the razor-sharp psychedelic exploration that would have arrested legendary American DJ Casey Kasem from his turgid FM radio existence.

Strange Cousin by Baptism Of Uzi

?I Walk? is no less compelling. Its initial focus is on sparse structures and tones, an arid incarnation of the palm trees and boulevards of the previous two tracks. But just when you’re ready for a wander out to the Joshua Tree to imbibe some peyote, another golden riff with antecedents in The Grateful Dead, The Byrds, Spacemen 3 and Galaxy 500 appears to massage your brain. The CD version includes another (untitled) track that apparently didn’t fit on the cassette. Another instrumental, it’s replete with psychedelic space rock noise and a shift in tempo that yanks you out of your flaccid slumber and shoves your face up against a kaleidoscopic wall of sonic chaos.

Baptism of Uzi is less a band than a musical journey. As a former senior manager of mine remarked, we’re all on a journey in life, we just don’t know exactly where we’re going. I doubt if Baptism of Uzi have a precise musical destination in mind, but it’s within the journey that enlightenment is found.
