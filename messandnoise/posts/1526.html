Wonderland

By mathieson 2007-02-09T12:00:00+00:00

Here, once more, are the young men. The debut album from Melbourne’s Dead Frenchmen comes tagged with Joy Division/early 80s epaulettes, but if those influences are present, they’re certainly not overwhelming. The trio displays none of Interpol’s stentorian swagger, for example. If anything, they have the sound (although not the neo-psychedelic spaciousness) of mid-period Died Pretty. Singer and guitarist Michael Pledger has the husky, crystalline guitar tone Brett Myers favoured, while his vocals swell and stir in a manner reminiscent of Ron Peno’s bottomless register. Then again, Died Pretty never had a rhythm section as muscular and precise as (now departed) bassist Toby Dundas and drummer Greg Doig.

The songs cross between personal and public spaces, eroding the gaps between the two. Titles hint at the everyday conflict explored: ?Animal Behaviour?, ?Wrecking Machine?, ?Language of Bones?? But the execution adds an element of intrigue, from the way the guitar spirals outwards on ?Cross Fire? to the punchy vocal cadence of ?Passenger?. For a three-piece they swell ominously, with Lindsay Gravina’s production buttressing their bravura. The most impressive moment on Wonderland is ?If We Could Only Disappear?, but the sentiment is a misnomer. Dead Frenchmen leave a sturdy footprint and there’s a definable weight to their stern power trio aesthetic. They have an appetite for construction.
