Soft Focus

By Mitchell Judge 2014-11-17T00:00:00+00:00

Part of The Ocean Party’s appeal is the way that their songs elicit a clear setting. Listen close enough to their catalogue and you can hear their migration from Wagga Wagga to Melbourne. You can hear them sitting ?On the Hill?, walking through shopping centres and living in a social house. The band’s emphasis on geography is clearer than ever on Soft Focus, with two songs named after roads and one more named for the town Charters Towers. Knowledge of a song’s physical location is not required to understand its meaning, though; with lines like ?As I stroll down the night is leaking, coming down with the rain,? the street changes for each listener but the feeling remains the same.

By creating these environments, it becomes easier to relate to their lyrics of inner turmoil and insecurities, and Soft Focus* becomes a more inviting listen. As always, the band are primarily concerned with detailing the everyday struggles of early adulthood, but on this record they approach these subjects with a new level of maturity. On ?Wading In? Zac Denton sings, ?It’s all the same, nothing ever really changes,? and it is strikingly similar to the chorus of *Split standout ?Quarter Life Crisis? – ?Every weekend’s always the same – every weekend, every weekend.? Scratch below the surface and it is clear that the two songs convey very different sentiments, with oblique and often romantic lyricism. The Ocean Party have done some growing up in the short timeframe between albums.

Later, on ?Deluded?, Liam Halliwell sings, ?You told me I shouldn’t expect to always be right, but I still swear I’ve never said the wrong thing in my life? just to prove that they still carry the charm of youth. Where the band has truly stepped up is in the broader scope of instrumentation. The reluctant poster boys for dolewave have branched out from jangly guitars to include horns, saxophone, clarinet, harmonica and synths throughout the album. The influence of The Triffids can be seen all over the record, and at times the band channel shades of Eels, Beirut and Belle & Sebastian.

The warmth and cohesiveness of the album makes it easy to forget that it was the work of five different songwriters. This sameness is a minor drawback, as the pacing of the final three songs can make the album seem longer than it actually is. All three tracks have a quiet, unassuming beauty, but they all feel like they could be the album closer. I kept waiting for another moment like the amazing saxophone solo on ?Head Down? to inject a little energy into the final run, but to no avail.

While The Ocean Party’s albums change as their experiences and perspectives change, their ambition to create great music continues to grow stronger. It is incredible to see such a prolific band so determined to improve. This is a group of lifelong friends that are growing up together in front of an audience, maturing as people while honing their songwriting.
