The Wolves

By Anthony Carew 2013-04-16T00:00:00+00:00

Nicking your handle from Nick Drake’s foppy middle LP seems like a move out to tap into those folk-pop crossover dollars, but stripped of their dandy Ys and thus destylised, the echoing words of Melburnian duo Brighter Later feel more literal. Pick your low-light situation – dawn, dusk, barlight, streetlight, 2am under blankets – and this music seems like a sure enough soundtrack to it.

It’s certainly no piece of folk-pop crossover, but it’s hard to say what, stylistically, it is; neither Americana nor Australiana, country or urban. It’s quiet and minimal, but not to the point where it feels like some slowcore stylistic tic; The Wolves seems, somehow, like an album whose sound is a product of humility. Without even leaving town, Grand Salvo and Luluc are decent enough frames to view the music through, but mostly Brighter Later’s first effort feels like a portrait of songwriter Jaye Kranz. Promising debuts are oft heralded as announcing the arrival of a ?new voice?, and literally, vocally, Kranz lovingly drawls every slow-rolling syllable as if her hero is Victoria Williams, even if she’s too shy to let her singing go more than a half-measure beyond a whisper.

Come and Go by BRIGHTER LATER

Though there are lengthy instrumental stretches existing around Kranz’s singing, her vocals almost function like the rhythmic centre of the songs; they, as the music’s most tangible measures, used as an anchor for atmospherics that otherwise may just drift away. There are lots of instruments, but they’re almost all decay, no attack; guitars glinting as harmonics or trailing as echo, percussion a shimmer of mallet on cymbal, keyboards used as if a colour filter. It’s music of mood, shadow, suggestion, reservation; a handful of dangling notes bouncing around in the deconsecrated, converted church it was recorded in, an evocative studio space that also happens to be Kranz’s house. By very dint of the ground on which it was recorded, The Wolves has connections to the spiritual and the domestic, and intersections thereof. When Kranz sings ?I swing my hammer round? in closer ?Holy Water,? she’s not talking of nailing up a cross, but undertaking renovations.

It’s notable, too, that this converted church space isn’t in the far-flung countryside, but in Yaraville in Melbourne’s inner west; a pocket of upscale bohemia flowering in the shadow of industrial refineries. There’s plentiful references to nature: rain and snow, trees and lakes, wind and weather. And ?The Woods?, in its cello scrapes, organ droplets and sinuous modular-synth gusts, is a suggestive piece of landscape painting. But this isn’t – and here burn those folk-pop crossover dollars up in smoke – an attempt to author some musical rural idyll that serves as audio bushwalk for its cubicle-bound audience. Instead, Kranz sings of nature not as idealised manifestation of all that is good, but as the promise thereof – a place of rose-tinted holiday transience and indulged daydream.

The Woods by BRIGHTER LATER

?I promised you pine wood trees,? Kranz laments at the drop of the beautiful ?Magnolia?, ?I promised you that and a piece of the canyon.? It’s a song as apology, as if its titular blossoms are landing on a roof alongside the satellite dish, both elegantly beautiful and kind of pathetic. ?When I close my eyes, I can hear the horses run,? Kranz murmurs in ?Holy Water?, tripping into juxtapositions of city/country as memory/temporality, but when she opens them she just sees next door’s weatherboard.

It’s not a neighbourhood record. If anything, it’s a traveller’s one; songs written in an Israeli plastics factory and a New York apartment, set largely through a winter draped under snowfall and daubed with delicate references to calculating latitudes and distances. But it’s an album with a specific sense of place, both in the home in which it was made and the terrains unto which its songwriter has wandered.
