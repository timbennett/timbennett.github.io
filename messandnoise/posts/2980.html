About Time

By Krystal Maynard 2015-03-02T00:00:00+00:00

No blaring comparisons spring to mind when listening to Chook Race’s debut album, although the sound certainly sits within a ballpark. The jangly power-pop accompanied by non-committal vocals fits nicely with the likes of local luminaries Twerps, Boomgates and Dick Diver – Individuality within a defined genre. About Time opens with the quaint pop of ?Dentists?; peppered with slick and speedy guitar licks between vocal passages, it’s a kind of recluse’s anthem: ?You’re so inviting, but I just feel like hiding.? The following ?Time? is a more relaxed number, settling into a groove. A lyrical theme is already becoming apparent: one of confusion, discontent and the demise of relationships that were previously such a constant.

Drummer Carolyn Hawkins takes front row on ?Always on Time?, stripping back the earnestness and opting for lyrics a little more inconsequential. Her vocals give the song a more ?90s indie feel. ?Clever? is a meaty slice of almost instrumental pop that holds its own surprisingly well on the album. With a short burst of lyrics arriving just before the song reaches the three-minute mark, it’s a cheeky little number. ?Outbound? is the charming soundtrack to your next laidback adventure; crank the stereo and leave your worries behind as you drive into the unknown.

?Good for Me? is a sweet serenade to the perfect girl, replete with a purely driving instrumental outro that makes a revitalising end to what could have been a relatively humble song. ?Relax? bounces along, effortless harmonies juxtaposing with the underlying unease that laces the main vocal, sonically and lyrically. ?Everyday? bemoans the monotony of life and, while it lacks the character and dynamic of the majority of the songs on the album, its blandness is suited to the subject matter, making it seem intentional and logical. ?Tables Turned? references the ever-changing nature of human relationships and sums up the tone of the album appropriately.

About Time captures the headiness of youth: the insecurity, the excitement and the boredom. It displays strength of musical character and plays just how an album should: weaving, bobbing and keeping you interested every step of the way.
