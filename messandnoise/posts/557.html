Tracks Of The Year 2010

By Darren Levin 2010-12-22T13:00:00+00:00

10. Oscar + Martin
?Recognise?

The dissolution of Psuche has been a blessing in disguise for founding members Oscar Slorach-Thorne and Martin King. It’s allowed them to focus the pastiche of sounds that constituted their debut LP into a deceptively simple pop song like ?Recognise?, which sets some poignant lyrics about reincarnation (?I recognise you/Every life time?) against warm samples and J Dilla-inspired beats. It probably helps that its accompanying clip (directed by Josh Aylett and Mahmood Fazal) reminds me of Kids. – Darren Levin

9. Midnight Juggernauts
?Lara Vs The Savage Pack? (from The Crystal Axis)

Their absence in both our Readers and Critics polls is telling, but we haven’t exactly fallen out of love with the MJs – it’s just their new album The Crystal Axis* didn’t exactly melt our faces like 2007?s *Dystopia did. Still, it had a couple great singles in ?Vital Signs?, ?This New Technology? and ?Lara Vs The Savage Pack?, all perfect examples of their shift towards a more ?live? sound (with the addition of drummer Daniel Stricker a few years back). The groove here is key, and things really take off at around 2.30 when everything dissolves into a deeply hypnotic space jam that’d make the other MJ proud. – Darren Levin

8. Blank Realm
?Full Moon Door? (from D’j? What?)

As with those Shakespeare aspiring monkeys, odds are that if you were to lock 100 free-form, improvisational rock groups in a room eventually one would produce a song. The wait could be centuries, but Blank Realm have managed, after six years of playing together, to accidentally write a pop song – and in a timely fashion. It’s true that this was improvised – the vocals were added later, according to the group – but this strange confluence of the buoyant and the tranquil, the hazily psychedelic and the rhythmically assertive, results in something genuinely alchemical: a mongrel of ensemble moods that an orthodox approach to songwriting rarely achieves. ‘Full Moon Door’ is like two songs played together – with moods at odds with one another – coalescing and making sense. – Shaun Prescott

7. Bedroom Philosopher
?Northcote (So Hungover)? (from Songs From The 86 Tram)

Misconstrued as a ?joke song? upon its release, The Bedroom Philosopher’s ?Northcote (So Hungover)? will one day be regarded as a landmark release in rock’s evolution, just like ?Interstellar Overdrive? by Pink Floyd or The Velvets? ?Sister Ray?. Until then, it’s OK to laugh at the hipster jokes safe in the knowledge they have nothing to do with you or your collection of Coogi jumpers and Casio Data Banks. – Darren Levin

6. Otouto
?W.Hillier? (from Pip)

Otouto told M+N earlier this year of their love for dance music, and if ‘W.Hillier’ – the loveliest song on their debut Pip – is a hint of inclinations to come, then dancing without moving your hips will be more popular among white collegiate indie lovers than ever. I, for one, am swaying my torso back and forth rigidly in anticipation. – Shaun Prescott

5. Super Wild Horses
?Fifteen? (from Fifteen)

Not since Oasis? ?Wonderwall? has a drum beat been so intelligently placed. About 25 seconds into ?Fifteen? – the title track of Super Wild Horses? superb debut – a snare drum comes crashing in, perfectly coinciding with the line: ?footsteps tangle up your street?. A nod to the lost art of word painting isn’t all ?Fifteen? has going for it, however. Like The Shangri-La’s ?Leader of the Pack? and other songs from that golden era of ?60s girl groups, the song is about falling hard for a boy from the wrong side of the tracks: ?Your gang/Your gang/Is coming to town.? It’s a sentiment framed by the most minimal of furniture: piano overdubs in place of bass, stark percussion and a few notes struck on a fuzzed-up guitar. Rock’n?roll should never be anything more than this. – Darren Levin

4. Scott & Charlene’s Wedding
?Footscray Station? (from Para Vista Social Club)

There’s nothing like a forensically executed slice of inner-city boho ennui to make it feel as though your life means something. Craig Dermody’s comfortable with singing about himself despite how uninteresting he is: his songs relate grindingly inconsequential plights but he’s convinced of their importance. ‘Footscray Station’ feels medicinal compared to it’s abundant contemporaries because it’s casual anguish practices no restraint. It isn’t embarrassed of itself, it isn’t guilty, and there’s nothing vague or obfuscatory about it. It’s a story that Dermody felt needed telling in as blunt a fashion as possible, a story about moderate inner-city poverty, elusive inspiration and eventual salvation through ?rock ‘n roll?. Much, much better than it sounds on paper. – Shaun Prescott

3. Dan Kelly
?Hold On, I’m Coming On? (from *Dan Kelly’s Dream)

A track about peaking at ATP Mt Buller (geddit?) with a line that says more about the modern condition than anything Kanye West put out this year: ?I tumble slowly through the crowd/As groups of older guys discuss The Wire* versus *Mad Men, again and again.? Such was the strength of the album, we could’ve easily interchanged it for ?The Decommissioner?, ?Poisoned Estuary Jam? or ?Dan Kelly’s Dream?, but I like the way the guitars smash together like atoms in the LHC, and how the song – like its protagonist – gets progressively more fucked up until it climaxes in a whirl of phased piano and even more guitar histrionics. – Darren Levin

2. Total Control
?Paranoid Video?

It only took a little buffing around the edges for Total Control to evolve from a middling electro-pop side project into a group you want to set up a Google Alerts for. Imagine Scattered Order’s unfuckwithable ‘Contact European Repetition’, or any of the cold/minimal wave one-offs that were reissued on compilations earlier this year, and you’re close. Just because you’re from a punk rock background doesn’t mean you need distortion, and abandoning that impulse has helped Total Control birth their strongest song to date, complete with the type of micro-second snare hits that seem to rip a hole in the space-time continuum. Listen to it five times in a row. And then do it again. – Shaun Prescott

1. Catcall
?Swimming Pool?

Most listeners with a lot of embryonic exposure to ?80s chart pop, and a generational inkling that throughout the last decade we’ve been short changed of otherworldly, over-reaching, aspirational pop music, will immediately connect with ‘Swimming Pool’. Most will shrug off the connection, apportion it, slot it somewhere as a sensibility they’re not comfortable with and move on. But despite every (utterly objectionable) argument against ‘Swimming Pool’ you could possibly conjure, it’s the most resonant pop song to unexpectedly emerge in 2010.

Catherine Kelleher presents a retrospectively utopian vision of pop music – urbane, graceful, texturally luxurious, inherently joyous. She steals the reputedly facile textures of a reputedly facile decade (the ?80s) and forces them to bend to her own poetic whims, stirring moods and remembrances that tickle the skin, subtly rattle the nerves and beckon slowly the pay-off that most pop artists insist on beginning with. For Catcall, modern pop tropes are the Jaeger and Red Bull to her carefully savoured Belgian beer. This is a rare species of pop music that doesn’t press itself upon you. It bleeds and smears and evolves and gradually becomes alive. – Shaun Prescott
