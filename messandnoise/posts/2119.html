Dead Air

By ReneSchaefer 2009-10-01T00:00:00+00:00

I’m constantly amazed at the magpie-like ability of young musicians to find the treasures buried in the desert of pop culture’s bewildering past that set them off on their own unique creative path. The disingenuously named Melbourne quartet East Brunswick All Girls Choir have obviously picked over the musical detritus of late ?90s post-grunge slow-core and absorbed stylistic cues from the likes of Art Of Fighting, Seam, Codeine, Crow, 2 Litre Dolby and Come.

This is most evident in the reliance on a quiet/loud dynamic in the majority of songs on the band’s debut mini-album. It’s an old trick, but it’s one that hasn’t lost any of its appeal. There’s nothing quite like listening to the tension in a song slowly building, knowing full well that any second the guitars and drums will blast off orgasmically into the stratosphere and the vocals will go from Jeff Buckley-like crooning to full-throttled wailing. The guitar sound is absolutely perfect on this recording, with individual notes soaring one second, then submerged under sheets of massed chords as on the magnificently epic ?Thanks For The Fucking Job?.

As with all formulas, there is a danger of becoming predictable after a while, but EBAGC vary their approach enough to warrant many repeat listens. The stand-out track for me is ?Serralves?, which smothers its insistent Jim Woff-like bass riff under a healthy dose of white noise guitar angst, and doesn’t outstay its welcome. Indeed, all the numbers here are kept to standard pop song length, avoiding the excesses of many adherents to the post-rock genre. Never is the actual song sacrificed for gratuitous noodlings, tiresome repetition or jazz-rock instrumental wank.

It’s all quite concise, and the guitars are still crisp and gnarly, even with healthy doses of distortion in the loud sections. Down-strokes rule the day, and it’s hard to repress the desire to bang one’s head in slow motion once those big crescendos kick in. As with most of the bands of their ilk, EBAGC aren’t interested in rock star posturing or individual glory within the band, but prefer to hunker down and deliver a monumental artifact that is more than the sum of its parts.

How they fit into the current mood of indie rock is not quite clear to me, but somehow I’m glad this band exists in all its willingness to buck trends and embrace music that is both out of time and timeless.
