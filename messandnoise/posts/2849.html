Night Bus

By Anthony Carew 2013-12-04T00:00:00+00:00

?Not enough songs start with a guitar solo and then repeat the same solo later in the song,? Jonathan Michell recently lamented. He was, we’d assume, joking; the leader of Melbourne’s The Ancients having a sense of humour wry enough to have once played in a band called Mum Smokes. But beneath the smirk lay his musical sincerity: you could make a case that the whole of Night Bus – and not just ?Jenny Street?, the LP’s doubly-solo’d closing cut – is one long guitar solo.

These might sound like fighting words, but they need not. It’s not as if Michell is the Yngwie Malmsteen of Melburnian slackerdom; more that Night Bus* is an album built around its guitar-playing, which remains at its core even when Michell doesn’t have his foot on the proverbial foldback. With the nadir of hair-metal now a quarter century ago, and the old punk dogma having long ago lost its command, a guitar solo only makes you wince if it’s in the wrong hands. So: *Night Bus feels like one long six-string showcase, but only if your models for guitar soloists are, say, Stephen Malkmus, Ira Kaplan, Alasdair MacLean and Michio Kurihara.

With the electric guitar at its core, it’s unavoidable that the third Ancients album must touch upon some vestige of classic guitar-pop. With the scrappiness of 2010?s The Ancients 2 traded in for something resembling an elegant melancholy – the record recorded, pristinely, by John Lee (ex-Mountains in the Sky, and the guy behind the Lost Animal boards) and Neil Thomason (once of Ricaine, now with a long r’sum? of rolling-tape) – in turn ?House of Cards? and ?She Lives in a Tent? border on chamber-pop. The former plays things straight, and blue, with a solemn ode set to cleanly picked banjo and golden-age guitar chime. But the latter slaps the genre’s English idyll into Australian suburbia, all its peeling harmonies and plucks of harpsichord-preset (the latter played by Yuko Kuno of Tokyo psych-poppers My Pal Foot Foot) soon sloshed under washed-out guitar sonics, including one memorable sting that sounds like the strings are unwinding as Michell plays.

Fans of the band at their shortest and buzziest will be heartened by chorus-killin? single ?Hey Now? and the shoegaze-y ?Hamster?, on which bassist Georgie Ward takes a dreamy lead vocal under saturated starbursts of fluff-on-the-needle fuzz. The album, though, feels more expansive than this, having a daydreamy quality conveyed not by Michell’s blithely murmured vocals, but by the colour and swirl of the guitars, the way they rise high and bright, on beyond the everyday’s greys. The songs rarely break beyond three minutes, yet even in short spaces they feel like they’re stretching out. And when The Ancients do truly stretch out on the six-minute ?Molokai?, there’s solos ?til close.
