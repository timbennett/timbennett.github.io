Brat Farrar II

By Patrick 2014-02-12T00:00:00+00:00

Melbourne garage-rock fixture Sam Agostino shows off his more eclectic taste with his second album as Brat Farrar, writes PATRICK EMERY.


Some years ago Sam Agostino told me he wanted to record an album of covers of fucked-up tunes: songs of the ilk Charles Manson dropped on the LA music community in the latter part of the ?60s, before it became apparent that this aspiring songwriter was far more dangerous than the average doped-out hippie shaman.

Agostino’s latest album under his Brat Farrar moniker isn’t the freak-cover album subject of that idle promise of yore; it is, however, an album that provides a glimpse into the eclectic range of Agostino’s musical interests. It opens with ?I’m Dead Now?, a song that conjures up images of a bedroom-bound teenager exploring the existential depths of doom rock. ?Do You Really Wanna Know? is the garage-rock Agostino we’ve grown to know and love; a dirty riff pays homage to Greg Sage, spiked with the odd electronic effect that might have been ripped from Alien Sex Fiend.

?Good by Myself? could have been a Digger and the Pussycats track, if only the logistical stars were lined up. Agostino surveys the ruins of a failed relationship and declares his independence with the defiance of a Baltic republic offering a two-fingered salute to its former political masters. ?Johnny Sparrow? sounds like the title suggests it would: a bit of chirping guitar and plenty of billowing rhythms; Agostino hovers in the background, muttering like a grumpy old bloke displeased with contemporary social mores and predicting it’ll all go to hell in a hand basket.

?Wouldn’t Give You Up? is the electronic track: a subtle nod to Devo, or maybe Boney M’s ?Nightflight to Venus?. You wonder where Ausmuteants would take a track like this given the artistic licence, and then you start to shiver at the prospect. ?Nothing There? starts out like Ace Frehley truly fucked up on speed, only prevented from catapulting over the musical abyss by Agostino’s reassuring tones. ?There’s really nothing there,? Agostino intones, and he’s probably right – we’re all searching for meaning, but only some people believe it’s actually there.

?An early-morning stumble trying to make sense of Joy Division with a head full of bad acid.?

?Closing In? channels The Mark of Cain around the time the Scott brothers dragged in a drum machine when they couldn’t find a drummer prepared to tolerate their military discipline. The aesthetic of the song is disconcerting, an early-morning stumble trying to make sense of Joy Division with a head full of bad acid. It trails off and you’re safe, but only for now.

?Off to See the World? is the pop track and all is right with the world. You can move, you can dance, you can relax; yet there’s something almost condescending in Agostino’s lyrics. Is he celebrating the wonders of international travel or taking the piss – and does it really matter? ?Vapour Wave? is dangerous, viscous noise. If you’re wavering on the edge of psychiatric stability, you’re better off staying away.

?You’re a Mess? has a spark of Cosmic Psychos? psychological interrogation: midway through the song Agostino’s barbed commentary gives way to shards of guitar, and you wonder if the object of his ire will acknowledge the rhetorical slings thrown their way. Finally there’s ?No Redemption?, a bookend doom-and-gloom track to match the album’s opening moment. It’s that moment in a dream when you’re wandering without obvious purpose, trying to make sense of the shapes and sounds you see and feel around you. Maybe there’s something deeper on offer, and Agostino is tempting us to find out what it is. Then again, maybe it’s just a song, and Brat Farrar II is just an album – an intriguing album at that.

+
