Drown In Colour

By JP Hammond 2009-07-01T00:00:00+00:00

The ?Darlinghurst disease? takes hold early on the second Red Riders record Drown in Colour, which opens with an unnerving prophecy: ?In 10 more years I’ll walk into a bar/On my way home from some rag where I’ve been writing – and we’ll talk about my old band.? As far as confident, declaratory openings for a second album go, it’s hardly up there with, ?Teenage angst has paid off well/And now I’m bored and old.? For a band like Red Riders, who have a clear deficit in the goodwill stakes, it’s also a notably curious stratagem: too petulant and entitled to succeed as sympathy, too on-point about questionable commitment to convert detractors to the cause.

Even more troubling is the fact that the opening two tracks of Drown In Colour do nothing to defy singer Alex Grigg’s bland augury. ?Tomorrow/Today? is so melodically close to The Cure’s ?A Forest? in the verses that it’s bereft of anything even remotely memorable. ?Ordinary? is just that; a song that makes the fatal error of mistaking the Libertines benzodiazepam clash as jaunty, clean fun. In contrast, first single ?You’ve Got A Lot of Nerve? has the kind of verve and energy Red Riders desperately need to hold the future back, while ?Never Gonna Be Enough? repeats the formula at the back end. They’re choppy, dirty ditties elevated by the mostly dire company they keep.

Grigg’s easy assumption of the role of sole lead singer (after the departure of Adrian Deutsch) is a significant strike in the positive stakes. Even if he’s a tad too reliant on the nasal chewing of words favoured by Fat Bob Smith of The Cure, its precisely this narcoleptic, congested slur that creates the easy emotional connection of the most successful songs on the record. In ?All Mine?, Grigg stumbles repeatedly over the words with an increasingly lackadaisical approach to tune and pitch – and it all works perfectly.

One of the main problems for Drown In Colour, however, is a mix from Woody Annison that seems expertly designed to subsume all the crisp, clear guitar tones he’s recorded into a muddy mid-range wash. It’s a peculiarly nasty listen on the home stereo; a record where the snare has a kind of hollow deadening emphasis that sucks the very life from the songs. Part of the problem is that he’s pitching for a sterile ?80s sound that comes across better in abstract than reality. Annison’s better with the arctic synth tones he overlays on ?You’ve Got A Lot of Nerve? and ?Feels Like Grace?, conjuring space and clean lines against the Riders? pre-prepared chaotic confusion. Elsewhere, when he reverts to producing them like a straight modern rock band (on ?So Long? and ?Over Again?), the record almost shakes its insistent inertia. Almost, but not quite.

The other component that hangs like ballast from the record is, well, the Annandale thing. Grigg’s shooting for a paen to the restless urbanity of modern life; all decadent confusion and bruised glamour. It’s material that’s been fine fodder for songwriters for generations but it needs a keen eye for the minutiae of place, time and relationships. At best, Grigg’s got a nice line in cheap turns of phrase (the ?Darlinghurst disease? is his construct). At his worst, he offers this: ?Nothing that binds, like a ship with no guy, like a street with no sign, like a crim with no crime.? For a band so definitively aligning themselves with the malaise of inner-urban, peripatetic existence, they never even feel like they hit the grim mire of the streets of Sydney.

In the end, there’s little more than a dreary, depressing ineluctability to the record. For Alex Grigg and his Red Riders, the future may be closer than they think.
