Deep In A Dream: An Evening With The Songs Of David McComb

By Lauren 2009-11-01T00:00:00+00:00

David McComb’s songs swarm with life – delivered ferocious with emotion, written with a literary arc in considered language, his rich songbook by turns harrowing, archly funny, deliciously black. (Not featured here, but ?Enemy Mine? from the first Blackeyed Susans EP is a dazzling bit of enjoyably nasty songwriting). So it’s wonderful to hear McComb’s songs actually played live and brought to life on this album, recorded at The Corner Hotel in Melbourne earlier this year, as a tribute to McComb and a fundraiser for a documentary-in-progress called Love in Bright Landscapes.

Diving Bell pick up ?Red Pony? and give it a rough seas tumble and shudder. Vocalist Claudia Schneider really shines on their second contribution, a beautiful piano, violin and weepy/drunken group chorus version of ?Tender is the Night (The Long Fidelity)?. The Mime Set deliver a pretty faithful rendition, maybe a bit more rock’n?roll, of ?Lonely Stretch?, and they were probably the perfect band to plumb the depths of that song’s solitary madness.

For mine, I just don’t buy Charles Jenkins doing The Triffids? ?Raining Pleasure? – the shattered and bleak heart of that song doesn’t work as a journeyman singer/songwriter lament. The delicate emotional balance becomes wrung out and overplayed. Mick Thomas makes a hearty go of early Triffids single ?Spanish Blue? and gives it a pirate-y, salty campfire tang.

The Blackeyed Susans focus on later period McComb/early Susans era, with Robert Snarski’s occasional commentary in between giving context and background to the tracks. And I don’t mind admitting I have since gone scurrying back to revisit the early Susans records that feature ?Ocean of You? and ?A Curse on You?. The combination of McComb, co-songwriter Phil Kakulas and a cracking band was devastatingly brilliant.

The two final tracks are fairly obscure as far as I can tell. Co-written by Snarski/McComb in 1990, ?Jimmy? is a languid, sensual kind of song, bathed in late summer heat. The set ends with the sharply ironic ?The Good Life Never Ends?, perhaps the very last song written by McComb in his mid-?90s Co-Star band guise, and never before recorded. The heavy heart of Leonard Cohen hangs over lots of melting, honeyed pedal steel and Snarksi’s tenderly delivered lines such as, ?Every time I see one of those old girlfriends, tell her, the good life, it just never ends.?

There should probably be an annual David McComb celebration. We have been embarrassed by the riches of the all-star arts festival event Secret in the Shape of a Song* and now *Deep in a Dream, so why stop now? Let’s keep the fires burning.
