The Brae

By Anthony Carew 2014-02-27T00:00:00+00:00

With an inscrutable name, a scant lack of biography, a grainy Contra)*-esque cover, and a drowsy dream-pop sound, Yumi Zouma are a band born of – and, in many ways, for – the blogosphere. They’re the latest outfit to materialise from the throes of ones and zeroes, long before they’ve ever played a show, as a fully-formed Soundcloud account, members hiding behind hair and anonymity. To accuse them of being custom-built for *Gorilla vs. Bear would be presumptuous, but for those who dwell in the little corner of the online world reserved for the washed-out few, Yumi Zouma is an easy sell: if you liked the first Wild Nothing LP and those early DIANA files, you’re already there.

Yumi Zouma could be from anywhere – or, indeed, from nowhere – but they’re actually from Christchurch. At least, initially. Charlie Ryder and Josh Burgess both did stints (the former for longer than the latter) in NZ disco-punks Bang! Bang! Eche!, but Yumi Zouma weren’t formed as some side project in a Christchurch rehearsal room. Instead they were midwifed by Dropbox, the project coming to life as the pair shared files between Paris and Brooklyn (where Burgess works for Captured Tracks, presiding over the label’s careful resurrection of Flying Nun’s legendary catalogue). Kim Pflaum, another Kiwi, was brought in to add vocals; first in Paris, then on her return to Christchurch.

Of course, even if they’re actually from Christchurch, Yumi Zouma’s music has a placelessness to it, its airy sound and winsome ways floating in the collective cloud. They’re nominally a soft-pop band, but all those old AM sources seem refracted through references: pop bubblegum by way of the trebly cuties of late-?80s Sarah Records, sumptuous synth balladry via the mischievous misfits of mid-?00s Gothenburg. Suitably, their slow jams are pale and opaque, the dilution-of-influence played out in sounds less washed-out than thinned, The Brae?s aqueous jams spread wan as a runny watercolour.

Pflaum embodies these sentiments: her voice gentle, hesitant, sometimes even reticent; the verses on ?S’lka Gets Her Hopes Up? sounding nothing if not unsure. Ryder and Burgess have enough history that the na’vet? at play is a choice; this a project happily handmade, proudly wonky. As pop songs, the four tunes on this debut EP are pleasant but never forceful, as if only half-convinced of their own melody. But, though the three-piece wear their second-hand identity well, it’s still apparent that this is an entity finding its form; its members, worldly as they are, still finding their musical feet. Which makes Yumi Zouma very much a band of the blogosphere: their presentation, thus far, outpacing their music.
