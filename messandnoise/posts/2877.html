New Teen Angst Pt. 2

By Max Lavergne 2014-03-04T00:00:00+00:00

Over the last few years, Canberra’s Dream Damage label has done an impressive job curating a roster of artists who draw on the sounds of the past without ever coming across as self-consciously retro. Danger Beach’s debut album mined ?70s sci-fi synth soundtracks and TV Colours? 2013 LP assembled ?80s punk, speed metal and VHS distortion to fairly thrilling effect.

The latest addition to that hall of fame is Slow Violence. It’s the nom de plume* of Canberra’s Advait Thakur, who put together this debut EP from a dorm room at Rhode Island School of Design. It might be confirmation bias, but you can almost hear the New England chill pervading *New Teen Angst Pt. 2?s minimalist beats. It’s less jagged than earlier Dream Damage releases, using ?90s R&B as its source material. Over five songs, it dismantles and reassembles the components in a similar way to acts like How to Dress Well.

As with a lot of the recent crop of R&B reinventors, there’s a focus in Slow Violence’s songs on the way the textures and tempo of the genre can be used to create music that’s cold and impersonal rather than sensual. Opening track ?Time Trial? abridges its samples to the barest minimum, stitching itself together with brief blips of hi-hat and microscopic keyboard loops. Later, ?Crushin?? turns up the wooze in its woozy beats and overlays them with a disembodied soul warbling that verges occasionally into a scream.

The EP’s most memorable moment comes courtesy of closing track ?Kidz?, which in under two minutes morphs from folktronica-ish loop to subdued dream-pop reflection: ?I don’t know exactly where I’m goin?/But I’m right here right now,? Thakur sings over and over. As the song progresses, it makes a couple of tentative steps towards a (marginally) more contemporary sound palette, laying an ambient, narcotic glaze over its melody and producing a sound that brings to mind early-to-mid-?00s indie in the vein of Pinback.

It’s hard to avoid the feeling on New Teen Angst Pt. 2* that Slow Violence is being purposefully enigmatic; from the invented context of the EP’s title to the inscrutable lyrics of ?Kidz?, these songs don’t *want to be understood. If one thing’s clear, though, it’s that there’s still plenty of potency in those glossy R&B synths and drum machines.
