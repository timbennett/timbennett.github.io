No Guts. No Glory.

By Marcus Lester 2010-03-05T00:00:00+00:00

Man is condemned, according to the Vedic texts, to an endless cycle of birth, death and rebirth, with his behaviour in each reincarnation determining whether he turns up in cat dog, or human form at the next. At this point, imagine a patchouli-scented hippie, crushed by a Marshall stack falling like a Warner Bros cartoon anvil out of a clear blue sky. That’s Airbourne.

It’s not that the band have no time for repetition. Booze, women, freight trains, rock’n’roll itself; again and again across 13 tracks the same images recur, sketching in a vision of a tough, blue-singleted world of ultimate ?blokeitude? where men are men and sheilas are “a XXX movie just walking by” (?Blonde, Bad and Beautiful?). What Airbourne don’t have any time for is conscious thought. It’d be unfair to flat objects to call the band’s worldview two-dimensional. It’s more like a single point, a concentrated microdot of distilled boganity.

The tunes, written by the O’Keeffe brothers – Joel (vocals, guitar) and Ryan (drums) – feature protagonists who are “free and proud” (?Steel Town?), a “supercharged hot blooded sex machine” (?Armed and Dangerous?), “hotwired like a stolen car” (??Back on the Bottle?), who vows to “stand strong for the long haul” (?No Way But the Hard Way?) while “breaking out of hell” (?Bottom of the Well?). And fair enough. Rock has always been plagued by over-thinkers, chin-strokers and jazz fans keen to make something pretty simple into more than three chords and a “backbeat, you can’t lose it”.

There’s always going to be a place for an unrestrained bust of hedonism, a fuck-it celebration of right now, an explosion of glorious stupidity scrawling obscenities across the cultural edifices that want us to slow down, think about it, do the sensible thing. The problem is that this is not that record.

Anyone reading this is likely to have – implanted in their head like some kind of rock’n’roll Thetan that no amount of auditing could ever budge – the folk wisdom that Airbourne sound exactly like AC/DC. And it’s true enough. They sound mostly like AC/DC, especially in the hands of Chicago-based producer Johnny K (3 Doors Down, Soil, Machine Head). Mr K – John Karkazis to his friends – gives the whole thing the Back In Black treatment, all pounding drums and big bass.

Some of Acca Dacca’s rivals have obviously also stolen a bit of drinking time in the O’Keeffe household. Chucked in is a fair slab – perhaps 16 to 18 tinnies – of Rose Tattoo (although no sign of the tasty, stinging slide of the late Pete Wells). And is there perhaps a glass of (more) wine (waiter please) courtesy of The Poor?

Ultimately, however, any other influences are a thimbleful of spit dumped into a sweaty, seething sea of AC/DC-ness. Airbourne have the riffs, the single-entendre lyrics and the song structures and, best of all, they have what most Young brothers rip-off merchants can’t quite get right: the rhythm. How much effort, how much forensic listening, has gone into getting that boogie exactly right? It’s impossible to tell, except that it’s obviously a lot.

Getting that feel absolutely correct, slotting into that groove, is an enormous achievement. But it’s one that is a bit like learning to play guitar exactly like Django Reinhardt – as any number of dull jazz players have – or doing what just one nerd has achieved: building the biggest possible Sim City (six million residents! More than four years in the making! Based on the wheel of life and death!).

So yes, No Guts. No Glory. is mindless, right down to the boneheaded punctuation of the title. But it’s mindless in the same way as translating the phone directory into Sanskrit is, rather than in the way being 17, drinking a bottle of brown spirits at a party, copping a pash and throwing up in the bushes is.

No Guts. No Glory. Not as much fun as it should be.
