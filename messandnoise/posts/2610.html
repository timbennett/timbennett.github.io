Drinking From The Sun

By Shaun 2012-03-09T00:00:00+00:00

Drinking From The Sun kicks off with the Hilltop Hoods offering superfluous context for their sixth LP. A kind of lyrical continuity snippet for those unaware of who they are or where they came from, Hilltop Hoods regale us here with a pop star resume of sorts: radio playlists conquered – check. Foreign shores rocked – check. Drunk pride veiled in an old mate’s humility -check. Other stuff happens too: new relationships, lost friends, sprouting children. Once certain their audience is up to speed, Hilltop Hoods launch into business proper.

Their business is party rap, and this opening salvo is the first of a three-part song called ‘The Thirst’, the final of which closes the record on a similar note, serving as an assurance that while this is their most larger-than-life LP to date, the Hilltop Hoods are still the kind of guys you could probably have a beer with. The group labour the point that they can’t comprehend where they are and how they got there, thus achieving that air of throwabout approachability that’s basically mandatory in this country’s rap music.

It’s believable. The Hilltop Hoods are pretty relaxed, and so is their music. It’s an unashamedly unambitious collage of hip-hop sounds pulled in a myriad directions. Drinking from the Sun‘s ?hugeness? doesn’t derive from its stylistic adventurousness as much as it does from a clear reverence for hip-hop, and the desire to touch as many bases as possible in 44 minutes.

What results is serviceable but hard to engage with directly: tempos never change dramatically, and the tenor of the group’s two MCs remains mostly the same whether they’re rapping about Aus rap being persecuted during ‘Good For Nothing’ (?Problematic child from a god abandoned isle/Where Hip Hop is like a convict on a standing trial?) or the horror of real warfare in ‘Speaking in Tongues’, one of the few songs here that isn’t explicitly about themselves. Orchestral instruments spattered across the canvass don’t make much of an impression on DJ Debris’ colourful but staunchly old-school productions, serving as unconvincing reminders that Drinking From the Sun is meant to be epic.

Hilltop Hoods – Good for nothing by Pokerbeats

The group’s reverence is found everywhere on this record, and in many ways Drinking From the Sun is a record about hip-hop. ‘The Underground’ traces their beginnings as underground rappers in cramped rooms and their enduring respect for their roots, while ‘Rattling the Keys to the Kingdom’ has them promising naysayers a liberal serving of I-told-you-so. The Hilltop Hoods are all about the affirmational energy of hip-hop: here, the delivery is far more important than the content. Appearances by Sia and members of the Roots and Jurassic 5 blend in with the textbook party rap furniture, making for a record hard to recommend to anyone who hasn’t already beaten their naked chests to these sounds at a festival already. Given what they’ve set out to do here though, and the competence with which they achieve it, they’re kinda beyond reproach.
