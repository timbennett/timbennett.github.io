Rising

By Mitchell Judge 2014-06-23T00:00:00+00:00

Straight Arrows will probably never shake the Nuggets revival tag – and that’s not necessarily a bad thing. They didn’t set out to invent a new sound, and once again they have succeeded in writing catchy garage rock that would be worthy of inclusion on the seminal compilation if they weren’t 50 years too late. Their renowned live show sees them assaulting eardrums and regularly covering the audience in rolls of toilet paper. By popularising this vintage sound, frontman Owen Penglis has become one of the most sought-after producers in the country and Straight Arrows accidentally changed the Australian musical landscape.

It’s been four years since the release of It’s Happening*, which is the average lifespan of a garage rock band in Sydney, so what has changed? In the album’s promotion, much has been made of the weirder side that *Rising* showcases, and the opening two tracks make good on this promise, creating a slow-moving, rather tense introduction. It’s worth noting that their debut album also had psychedelic-leaning tracks in ?Magic Sceptre? and ?Haunted Out?, though, so this isn’t a complete reinvention. On *Rising, the grittier production elevates ?Fruit of the Forest? and ?Petrified? from potential filler tracks to interesting deviations. These songs are also helped by the band’s newfound comfort in stretching an idea out, at least by their standards: the six longest songs they’ve ever written are all on this record.

Straight Arrows are still at their best when they raise the tempo though, with album highlights ?Can’t Stand It? and ?Rotten Teeth?. The band haven’t lost their ability to craft unbelievably catchy songs, even while they have stepped up to more complex songwriting (again, by their standards) by mostly avoiding their standard repetitive two-word choruses. ?Without Ya? is a twisted take on ?60s bubblegum pop, easing up on the distortion to reveal a sweet and simple tale of love. Elsewhere there are a few rare occasions where the lyrics are clear enough to be understood, and almost always there is nothing to be understood but a confusing assortment of words.

In some ways it feels like we are approaching the end of an era: Royal Headache and Circle Pit are reportedly finished, while Eddy Current Suppression Ring have been dormant for years. Broadly speaking, it feels as if local interest is sliding away from the garage sound that Straight Arrows were so instrumental in reviving. Seven years on from their formation as nothing more than a joke, it is remarkable that the band are still together – yet better than ever both live and on record.

‘R I S I N G’ by Straight Arrows
