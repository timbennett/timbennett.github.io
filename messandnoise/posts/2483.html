Seeker Lover Keeper

By Doug Wallen 2011-06-03T00:00:00+00:00

We already know the voices behind Seeker Lover Keeper – Sarah Blasko, Holly Throsby, Sally Seltmann – but that doesn’t diminish its chamber-pop majesty. All three women write scrutinising songs that can leave even the listener feeling exposed, and together it’s like a communal therapy session. But their work here has both a solidarity and a sense of open space that’s new. Embracing sisterly harmonies and quite natural backing, the trio locates a real sturdiness amid the sparseness.

Blasko emerges as the default leader, singing lead most often. Seltmann brings the kind of boldly autobiographical show-shoppers that defined her last album, while Throsby contributes the intimate sense of sharing inherent to her breathy whisper. Yet they test and push each other: Throsby projects more than usual on the Seltmann-penned standout ?Even Though I’m a Woman?. Like Seltmann’s best songs of late, it finds strength in the very weakness it reveals in the lyrics; in this case, looking forward to being away from her husband just for the thrill of missing him.

While Throsby begins to retreat into herself again on her own ?Going to Sleep?, the music is decidedly rousing. And Seltmann sounds darker and hungrier than ever before on her own ?Every Time?, thanks to the nervous pace and hand percussion. Likewise, Blasko’s would-be title track ?Theme 1? is lent new shades by the bowed banjo of multi-instrumentalist Shahzad Ismaily (Jolie Holland, Bonnie ?Prince? Billy) and the usual quiet work of journeyman Dirty Three drummer Jim White. As friendly as this collaboration is, there’s a spooked vibe to tracks such as Seltmann’s ?If the Night is Dark? and Blasko’s ?Bring Me Back? that’s almost harrowing.

You might think you know exactly how this band is going to sound, but this album feels more like new ground than old. The songwriters are each at once gaining and sacrificing something, and they’re well matched not just by each other but by Ismaily, White, Throsby’s bandmate Bree van Reyk, and producer Victor Van Vugt. For such a pooling of resources, these songs feel individual and true.
