BARB

By Doug Wallen 2010-08-24T00:00:00+00:00

More shaggy pet project than swaggering supergroup, BARB is a cluster of giddy creativity from four itinerant New Zealanders and one Australian. Liam Finn is the marquee name. He’s joined by long-time collaborator Eliza-Jane Barnes (son of Jimmy); Connan Mockasin and Seamus Ebbs of the band Connan Mockasin; and James Milne, who performs as Lawrence Arabia. It’s often Finn and Barnes singing lead, but the others chime in and everyone shares a collective songwriting credit as BARB. The album was cut in Neil Finn’s Auckland studio Roundhead, where Liam recorded his similarly rough-hewn, often kaleidoscopic solo debut I’ll Be Lightning. The band’s concept was hatched three years before, while all members were stationed in London.

As you’d expect of any whimsical jamboree between friends, the results are scattershot. In keeping with the nature of this experiment, bright flashes of pop are sparked but then doused in studio trickery and divergent impulses. With the effects-thickened ode to Leonardo DiCaprio ?Leo? and the sloshing urgency of the great ?Alcoholic Darling?, the album sets out with a commitment to untidiness. Different voices, instruments, and even genres come and go: you’ll hear Stereolab keyboards permeating ?Nile?, half-funk chaos and near-rapping on the tossed-off ?Beatman?, a spirited chant starting ?Not A Bird?, casual dissonance on ?Time To Contemplate?, and a sort of country/R&B torch song in ?2004?.

Fans of Lawrence Arabia – myself included – will appreciate his classicist pop smarts via ?Martin XII? and ?Looking Out Through Barb’s Eyes?. And Mockasin imbues ?Please Don’t Interrupt? with his distinct squeak. This is no mere showcase of Finn and Barnes? fabulous friends, though: it’s a new entity sprung from five overactive brains. As a band, BARB is largely enjoyable but can veer too far into the arcane and opaque, pushing us away when the songs seem all too worthy of our attention.
