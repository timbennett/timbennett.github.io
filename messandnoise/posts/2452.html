I Want That You Are Always Happy

By Doug Wallen 2011-04-08T00:00:00+00:00

The first taste we got of The Middle East’s proper full-length debut was ?Jesus Came to My Birthday Party?, a rough-hewn single so plucky it breaks into whistling by the end. One listen of I Want That You Are Always Happy, however, positions that song as the exception that proves the rule: there’s no rushing the Middle East. The Townsville ensemble may have made its name with slinking folk ballads like ?Blood? and ?The Darkest Side?, but even by that standard this album requires a rare amount of patience. It’s an hour long, the pacing is mostly glacial, and the sequencing strands listeners in the wilderness of certain dirge-like tracks for awfully long spells.

Recorded in Queensland as well as in Midlake’s Texas studio, the album capitalises neither on the band’s unlikely radio success nor the recent global boom in young folk artists. It’s a difficult, demanding sprawl. Even compared to forbears like the Paradise Motel and Tindersticks, this is slow and dark and somewhat selfish in opening itself up. Primary singer Jordan Ireland may take cues from ?the old Southern Cross? and our ?land of the bloody unknown?, from a train connecting Sydney to Newcastle, and from the historic gold town of Mount Morgan – to say nothing of ?Black Death 1349? – but for all that specificity, there’s a blanketing vagueness that keeps these songs from always connecting. Ireland’s lyrics themselves can be hard to make out, and recurring themes of death, desolation, and Jesus can darken the path rather than illuminate it.

Black Death 1349 by tmemusic

That said – and whether or not this qualifies as a ?grower? – there’s no shortage of eerie beauty here, especially once you let go of expectations and simply ride out the storm. Piano and strings trickle in nearly as often as languid strumming and creaky vocals, making for 14 songs – counting the hidden, Dirty Three-ish instrumental – that tend far more towards mood pieces than festival-ready singles. ?Land of the Bloody Unknown? and ?Jesus Came to My Birthday Party? may be light of step, but other tracks are oppressive by comparison. It’s only with a stretch towards the end – ?Months?, ?Dan’s Silverleaf?, ?Hunger Song?, and ?Ninth Avenue Reverie? – that The Middle East hints at the crowd-pleasing album lurking within this shrouded, serpentine opus.

It’s frustrating but also somehow admirable. Instead of simply writing a record of first-listen love affairs like ?Blood?, the band is making us work. Making us actually listen hard, take our time, and scan the back pages for whatever meaning we may find.
