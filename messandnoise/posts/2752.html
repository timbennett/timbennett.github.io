You’re A Shadow

By Tim Fitzpatrick 2013-04-01T00:00:00+00:00

If you’ve listened to the radio at all lately, you’re going to pick up on certain contemporary influences on this album. It’s a given, but that shouldn’t matter. ?Sharp Shooter? is straight-up sugar for the Triple J airwaves. You can see them playing it now on a festival stage somewhere, mid-afternoon: the sun’s still up and the majority of people aren’t too rowdy yet, camera crew sliding back and forth across the stage.

But that’s just one song, and it’ll either be the clincher or the down-point on this album, depending on where you’re coming from. That seems to be the strength of You’re a Shadow: it doesn’t stick to the recipe that’s seen Hungry Kids of Hungary do alright these past few years.

Hungry Kids of Hungary – Sharp Shooter by MuchoBravado

First are the under-the-sea kind of blips that open the album on ?What in the World?. Maybe it’s after finding myself listening to the album through headphones on the couch on a rainy day, but when the harmonies of ?Someone Else? kicked in, it felt miles away from the expected weird-summer-day-out vibe. The lead vocals seem more distant, with the harmonies pushed right up front.

More chilled than the usual indie pop, the album largely takes a restrained approach. Like on ?Colours?, a mid-album track that wouldn’t feel out of place on a SpongeBob episode featuring Salmon and Garfunk-Eel (and if that episode doesn’t exist already, it should). The same effect happens again on the closing ?San Simeon?.

Hungry Kids of Hungary – Twin Cities by MuchoBravado

This kind of thing happens a fair bit on You’re a Shadow, moments where the band will stretch out a melody and turn away from the expected route of a song, creating a completely different mood. This gives hope that the band could do some interesting left-turns in the future. It also gives the impression that they have a lot more mellow and varied influences than their contemporaries, and a decent shot at some kind of longevity as a result.
