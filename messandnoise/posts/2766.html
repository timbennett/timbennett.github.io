Kieran Ryan

By Jules LeFevre 2013-05-14T00:00:00+00:00

Kieran Ryan doesn’t concern himself with the small things. Trivial things like day-to-day living don’t feature that much on his debut LP, and he doesn’t bother mining feelings of love or pain for material. No, instead Ryan goes big, and from the sweeping explorations of human history (?Out of Africa?) to an eight-minute autobiographical epic (?Building a Snowman?), Ryan wants you to know that he’s a serious guy now.

Formerly of the Melbourne duo Kid Sam, Ryan has traded in the clatter and distortion for wistfulness and washes of cellos. The simple acoustic arrangements fit snugly around his honeyed vocals, which deliver long-tapered lyrics with an earnestness that grinds instead of soothes.

It’s that overwhelming earnestness that eclipses everything else. The songs all have delicate foundations, often built around an acoustic melody that’s beautiful in its simplicity, but it’s nowhere near strong enough to hold up the heavy vocals, which drag instead of sway.

What’s impressive is his scope. The storytelling is unashamedly grand and ambitious. ?Out of Africa? attempts to tell the story of mankind in four minutes with a plethora of metaphors and similes. The closing track, deliriously titled ?The World is Ending?, is a morbid death ballad (of course) and contains the sobering line: ?All the world is ending, and everything’s insane/But soon for me there will be no joy and no pain.?

The long-lingering ballads might work if they got off the ground, but for all the talk of life and death, the album’s pulse is a little weak. The highlight, ?Cannibal Club?, shoves some drums and electric guitar into the mix and makes a satisfying folk stomp of it (plus it rhymes ?sitars? with ?abattoirs? for good measure).

Ryan also name-checks some big influences here. ?Are You a Conspirator?? has all the sepia tone of a Redgum track, and the expansive storytelling is a big nod to the bard Paul Kelly. What’s missing is the crucial humility and intimacy. These songs exist in Ryan’s head, and the distance is palpable.

There’s fine material here; the arrangements are thoughtful and beautiful in parts and Ryan is obviously a fine musician and songwriter. Everything is already in his grasp; he just doesn’t need to try so hard.
