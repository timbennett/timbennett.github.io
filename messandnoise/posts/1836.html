Easy Fever: A Tribute To The Easybeats

By DomAlessio 2008-10-01T00:00:00+00:00

The tribute album is a curious creature. Ostensibly a musical eulogy for a revered band, more often than not they exude the foul stench of capitalism. Corral together a slew of well-loved bands, get them each to cover a song from whichever legend you’re honouring and package it all together. Minimal outlay for maximum return. It’s an odour that lingers around the latest tribute album for one of the great pioneers of Australian rock’n’roll, The Easybeats. But whose hand is in the honey pot? Is it Sony BMG, the artists contributing or is it former Easybeats frontman Stevie Wright who needs the cash more than anyone else involved in this project? Whoever’s at the helm, in these times of belt-tightening and child-care collapses, Easy Fever offers few incentives for people to hand over their hard-earned cash.

Apathy and myopia are the twin powers of destruction that wreak havoc through the 20 Easybeats covers included here. (For those playing along at home, there are some tracks that are actually Stevie Wright solo pieces, most notably the ‘Evie’ trilogy.) The Skybombers merely resurrect ‘Sorry’ and Faker languishes throughout their version of ‘I’ll Make You Happy’. Dash & Will’s performance of ‘Somethin’ Wrong’ is flaccid at best, and Ben Lee all but butchers the Easybeats’ iconic breakthrough single ‘Friday On My Mind’. He does an (ahem) amazing job of turning a sexually-charged rock song into a preppy indie pop piece replete with glockenspiel, sleigh bells and empty, ethereal vocals. Speaking of impossible feats, the wraithlike Veronicas somehow manage to strip away any sense of rock’n’roll bravado from ‘Good Times’, leaving nothing but a gossamer, vacuous pop-rock tune. And Grinspoon’s lassitude on ‘St. Louis’ makes for an abominable performance. My skin’s crawling just thinking about it.

But my cynicism isn’t impervious. Thirsty Merc do a strangely honourable rendition of ‘She’s So Fine’ as well as Children Collide, who imbue some of their own personality into ‘Black Eyed Bruiser’. Always the consummate professional, Neil Finn does an excellent job with ‘Women (Make You Feel Alright)’ but let’s face it, he could sing A Brief History of Time and make it sound like a sonnet. The ever-imaginative Dappled Cities turn ‘For My Woman’ into a creeping post-punk tune and The Resin Dogs re-imagine ‘Wedding Ring’ as if it was a ’90s dance number. Wright himself appears with the king of pub rock Jimmy Barnes. Their duet on ‘Mother Finger’ is passable, but only just.

The Easy Fever* bricolage ends with the ‘Evie’ trilogy. While parts two and three are average, it’s part one – the omnipresent Nic Cester-led ‘Let Your Hair Hang Down’ – which packs the vivaciousness that most of this disc lacks. They might be doing a straight-up cover, but at least they sound like they’ve having fun. Not like the majority of the acts on *Easy Fever, who were probably after a quick buck. Save your money and hunt down the Easybeats’ inimitable back catalogue instead.
