Guest Column: Georgia Fields On Grants

By Darren Levin 2010-11-04T13:00:00+00:00

When it comes to grant applications, the devil is in the detail, writes Melbourne-based singer GEORGIA FIELDS.


As an independent artist, so much of what you’re able to do comes back to how well lined with cash your pockets are. When I started work on my album I knew I’d need extra financial support, because of the extent of the orchestral arrangements.

Someone told me Arts Victoria (specifically, Victoria Rocks) were just handing cash out to musicians, so I called them to get the grant application paperwork, thinking this would be a walk in the park. My first application was unsuccessful, as was my second. What a rude shock! I did a third application, thinking that it was unlikely I’d get it this time, and that I’d have to put my album plans on the shelf. I received the confirmation letter one morning when I was recovering from brutal food poisoning – a moment I will not forget for many reasons.

If you are reading this, then chances are you have something pretty special that needs to be recorded so people can hear it. This is my advice to you:

Grant applications are based on your administration and planning skills, as much as your musical or creative merit. No matter how beautifully crafted your songs are, if you can’t balance a budget or explain the short- and long-term benefits of your activity, you won’t get any money. If you’re not a detail-minded person, enlist a nerdy friend. Take a previously funded artist out for coffee and pick their brains. Get a teacher at uni to proofread your application. You can also make appointments with the awesome staff at Victoria Rocks, but make sure you don’t leave this too close to the submission date.

I know this sounds like stating the obvious, but I can’t say it enough: read ALL the guidelines. They are boring and annoying, but if you don’t read them, you’ll miss out like I did. In one of my failed applications, all I needed to include was my ABN and to state who would own the master tapes. D’oh!

Grant applications are based on your administration and planning skills, as much as your musical or creative merit.

Questions I found hardest on the application were things like: ?What are the benefits of this activity?? (Um – making an album?); and ?What are the next steps after this activity?? (I dunno, having an album I guess?). These questions sound airy-fairy, but they’re actually a very important platform for you to explain your long-term goals, and do further convincing that your project or activity is worth funding. Knowing exactly WHY you want to record an album will strengthen your argument. For example, ?I have been playing live for a few years and would like a recorded product to support my live performances. Also, a recorded product will help me reach new audiences and give me further opportunity to play on the national touring scene.?

One thing that kept holding me back was, I was looking too far ahead and asking for funding for things that weren’t ABSOLUTELY necessary for the next step (insanely expensive custom packaging, huge publicity costs related to the launch, etc). Keep it simple. Only ask for what you desperately need for the next (first) step. You can always apply for further funding down the track. For instance, once you have the album tracked and recorded, you might be able to enlist an indie label to pay for mixing/mastering, and then apply in the next funding round for manufacturing costs. It takes longer, but that’s the road of an indie artist.

I’m currently working on three more grant applications to other government funding bodies, still learning about how best to plan what it is I want to achieve with my musical goals, and how to convince funding bodies that my plans are worth their dosh.

Good luck with your applications.
