Fever In The Road

By Edward Sharp-Paul 2013-01-21T00:00:00+00:00

The Bamboos are a band with an easy narrative. It’s not exactly gripping, but it’s all right there. Crack soul revivalists get their timing right and graduate to sustained mid-level success off the back of a brilliant live show and some judiciously chosen guest spots from up-and-comers and established stars alike.

Fever in the Road, though, is an album resistant to easy narrative. Though Kylie Auldist and Ella Thompson split vocal duties in the absence of guests, it is not the story of two vocalists: both have fine moments and flat spots alike, and neither wrest control of the album’s heart from the other. Neither is it the story of stylistic metamorphosis, as the band would have you believe: it oscillates between soul revivalism, 5th Dimension-style ?60s pop and something a little less backward-looking – but only ever by increments.

If there is a common thread, it’s that both Auldist and Thompson invariably give the songs what they need, without giving any of themselves. Auldist possesses a rich, earthy instrument, and so works through the soul belter terrain of ?Leave Nothing Behind? and ?The Truth?. However, while she has a commanding presence, she doesn’t have a heap to say, generally falling back on dependable old tropes. Lighter and more mellifluous, Thompson is more versatile but less striking, finding herself performing made-to-measure jobs on the album’s more eclectic offerings.

If that sounds like a backhander, well, it is and it isn’t – but that’s the definition of a backhander, isn’t it? It takes no small skill to shape-shift between genres and emotional tenors, but to do so often requires the dispassionate air of the craftsman, something that runs counter to the spirit of the music The Bamboos are trying to make. That’s the paradox of the rock ?n? roll tradition: some of its most exhilarating moments come from practitioners who don’t really know what they are doing. By the time you master your craft, that strange frisson is lost.

All of which means that Fever in the Road isn’t one of the most exhilarating moments ever committed to record. Oh well. What it is, though, is a work of estimable craftsmanship. The sounds contained herein are pristine, and Lance Ferguson and co-producer John Castle deserve credit for the depth and variety they are able to smuggle into the album. ?Harbinger? is a dreamy, opulent chamber-pop effort with a full complement of Walker Brothers strings, while the groove on ?Helpless Blues? is so tight that Ferguson’s spring reverb is left hanging in the air after each choppy chop of the choppy verse rhythm.

Over the last decade The Bamboos have become something of an institution, and on Fever in the Road they do as institutions do, offering a reliable brand, the warm embrace of the familiar and a modest rate of interest.
