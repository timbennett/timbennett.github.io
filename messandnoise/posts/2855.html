Watch It Wharf

By Mitchell Judge 2013-12-19T00:00:00+00:00

When I sat down with Nathan Roche seven months ago, he was sure to distance himself from being labelled as a musician, stating that he was a writer and bartender that only made music to be social. It was an odd sentence, given that Roche is currently playing in half a dozen bands, but it was perfectly in line with his carefree underdog personality. Despite releasing two albums and launching two books this year alone, Roche stays true to his apathetic and self-deprecating demeanour.

Roche partially takes inspiration for the album from an incident on Finger Wharf where he was denied entry to one of the fashionable buildings due to his shabby appearance (and possibly intoxication). Always championing the underdog’s cause, he details in the liner notes how the experience caused him to reflect on how the once blue-collar area had tragically morphed. However, ?You Are What You Are? lacks any real insight into Sydney’s transformation, instead churning out simple rhymes like ?I can’t give you keys because I don’t have a car/I can’t be bothered so I’ll hit another bar.?

The wharf does carry a presence throughout the album though, most obviously on ?Sailors into Woolloomooloo,? which continues Roche’s tradition of pondering the activities that can be undertaken in the suburbs of Sydney. Once again the answer involves drinking booze. The simple organ lines that repeat through ?Sailors? and ?Serafina,? as well as the chugging rhythm of ?Nowhere You’ll Know? help add a nautical feel to the album.

Elsewhere, musical influences are borrowed from a wide array of sources: ?60s girl-group call and response, vocal looping experiments and unashamed ?80s rock guitar solos, all combining for a very intriguing listen. The downside to this approach, though, is just how obvious it is that the experiments are partitioned; they generally appear at the start or end of a traditional rock song and therefore come across as a little half-baked.

Watch It Wharf is an interesting addition to the Roche catalogue, but it is far from a great album. There is evidence of his ability to pen a great tune (?When I Come Around? and ?Maybe? are standouts), and there are moments that are instantly attention-grabbing. However, they are outnumbered by simple songs that travel along without any real energy or purpose. I can’t help but think that if Roche embraced his talent as a musician, he could produce a truly exciting album and we could watch the underdog morph into a champion.
