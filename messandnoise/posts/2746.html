Calendar Days

By Max Easton 2013-03-14T00:00:00+00:00

Dick Diver continue to ponder life’s minor details and disappointments in familiar settings on the Melbourne quartet’s second album, writes MAX EASTON.


Dick Diver’s New Start Again* was a wintry album of the beautifully mundane, its common themes and sounds lending it a cohesiveness that felt like shared stories from a singular point in time. In recording its follow-up, Dick Diver seem less inclined to flow seamlessly between events; where *New Start Again* gave you a long winter, the loosely assembled collection of tracks on *Calendar Days present four seasons in 35 minutes.

Summer features on ?Alice,? autumn on ?Gap Life,? winter and spring on ?Water Damage? and ?Two Year Lease?. As these songs swing from the optimistically sunny to the desperately cold, the album’s title becomes all the more appropriate. In the same way you would a discarded calendar, the album looks back at a varied year of a household’s life. Minor appointments are scrawled on the vertical that mean little to the outsider, but with the filled-in details added, link a night shift with a life crisis, a grocery list with an awkward encounter, exclamation marks with a wasted holiday. The simmering anxiety of youth that New Start Again* so ably recounted still remains on *Calendar Days, but now its ideas are thrown out in sprawling directions, reassembling on the hastily built foundations of distracted details and retrospect.

Alice by dickdiver

?These songs romanticise insignificance, their characters observing a world that carries on unaffected by their everyday problems.?

The band’s strength lies in the way they don’t over-tell a story. The wide-angle lens is discarded in favour of intimate close-ups that detail only the most specific thoughts of a character lost in his or her environment. These fleeting lines evoke a loneliness in company, lines that address hope lying in resigned fates (?Nothing ever happens here but it will?) or calm humour in the face of sadness (?Forget her man/Forget who??). The constantly traded vocals of Al McKay, Rupe Edwards, Steph Hughes and Al Montfort add to that sensation. Hesitantly adjusting to change on ?Two Year Lease?, Edwards and Hughes sing, ?You used to be able to smoke inside around here.? And, just off-time with each other, continue with: ?But that was when it was your ex’s house/and that’s different to now.? Later the characters overhear someone talking about their failed living plans at an overstocked IGA. On Calendar Days, the pondering of life’s minor disappointments always occurs in familiar territory.

Dick Diver draw you into this insular glance at the world around them by attaching significance to unimportant details. On ?Water Damage?, a line as seemingly meaningless as, ?A long walk, the sound of birds/A picture frame, a third floor Fitness First,? somehow stops you in your tracks. On ?Calendar Days? unused fragments of routine gather in piles, while on ?Bondi ?98? foreign nations take each other by force while you fall asleep in front of the TV. These songs romanticise insignificance, their characters observing a world that carries on unaffected by their everyday problems. By December 31st, no one would bat an eyelid when the curled-up calendar is tossed into the recycling with the empties.

Water Damage by Dick Diver Calendar Days

The varied nature of these songs can also lend the album a restless quality. Almost every track cuts off a verse or two short, abruptly ending one event and starting elsewhere before you’re quite ready. The two-minute glance at the streets of ?Blue & That?, for example, bumps straight into ?Alice?, which then jolts to ?Calendar Days,? itself ending in an abrupt finish where the bridge might have returned. It’s only ?Amber? and ?Two Year Lease? which take the time to run to their expected end points.

Throughout the album, you’re left waiting for more, and unsettled when it cuts off prematurely. Maybe that sequencing was designed to mirror the feeling induced by all the distracted observations, but it’s more likely that Dick Diver just end their songs once the words run out. It’s probably expecting too much to wish that they never did.

+

##?Calendar Days? is out now through Chapter Music.
