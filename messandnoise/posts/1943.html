Back To Zero

By Adam 2009-04-01T00:00:00+00:00

What the fuck is this? Something off the Lemmings soundtrack? It’s probably not the best initial reaction to an album, but what else was I to think upon hearing the MIDI-tastic keyboards of ?Human Hair?, the first track on The Night Terrors? long-awaited debut full-length? Everything sounded so cold, so calculated, so entirely without feeling; not at all like the band I remembered from a couple of pretty good EPs released earlier this decade. The image of little those green-haired men (or whatever they were supposed to be) plummeting mindlessly to their deaths was hard to shake.

But it’s not a feeling that held out, thankfully. Actually, it sank after about 30 seconds, when the ghostly strains of Miles Brown’s Theremin began snaking into the mix. It’s said of all instruments, that the cello is closest in timbre to the human voice. If that’s so, then the Theremin must be a close second – well, at least the way Brown plays it. More than once, it sounds like The Night Terrors have drafted in a female soprano to sing a few haunting notes for them.

Back To Zero feels a lot less gritty than its predecessors, which presumably is the result of a few significant line-up changes over the past five years. The prog-rock leanings of the group’s earlier material are given free reign here, with synth players Paul Kearney and Marika Bardin layering most every track in glistening, almost Rick Wakeman-esque tones.

But rather than let this side of their sound drag them down into a dull quagmire of sequined shirts and dodgy concept albums, The Night Terrors keep all their songs (except the 20-minute title track) at a reasonable length and anchor everything with Ianto Kelly’s drumming. While anything but a simplistic player, he’s not at all showy, something which could easily have tipped this album over the edge.

Back To Zero* isn’t going to delight everyone. If ?80s-style keyboard tones send shivers down your spine for all the wrong reasons, then by all means, avoid this record like the plague (or swine flu, or whatever). But again, it’s not as if this is *The Myths and Legends of King Arthur and the Knights of the Round Table – The Night Terrors are better than that, always have been.

Strangely human despite its synthetic origins, Back To Zero is a curious and ultimately triumphant rebirth.
