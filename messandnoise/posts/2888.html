Oddments

By Lachlan Kanoniuk 2014-04-03T00:00:00+00:00

King Gizzard’s fourth album in 18 months is a safe reflection on their canon to date, writes LACHLAN KANONIUK. It may never quite soar, but it never falters either.


It’s tough out there for a rock ?n? roll band. The long way to the top is almost impossibly so, bands dissipating before lift-off, on an ostensible high or sliding into ill-defined stasis (ECSR, Royal Headache). Longevity isn’t paramount to success, but it appears to be an increasingly elusive prospect for non-solo acts, especially in a spacious country with relatively limited touring prospects.

King Gizzard & The Lizard Wizard are a band doing it right in the 2010s, possessing tangible savvy and consistently shifting stylistic focus. Emerging from their formative stint as a collective – one with a loosely fluctuating roster – they consolidated their lineup of seven for their debut EP of adept garage belters, Willoughby’s Beach*. They’ve upheld a remarkably prolific output since, with *Oddments marking their fourth album proper.

The ambition isn’t limited to the King Gizzard umbrella, the group acting as spearhead for a surreptitious and steady Anglesea-region invasion into the nation’s rock capital. Gizz members have germinated into a handful of auxiliary projects – most notably the Ambrose Kenny-Smith-fronted Murlocs, plus Stu Mackenzie’s snare duties on rising folkie Fraser A Gorman’s Big Harvest. Percussionist Eric Moore helms the band’s nascent in-house label Flightless, cultivating a stable of likeminded young acts alongside King Gizzard’s ascension. It’s a studious ethic, belying the initial greened-out stoner shades of their debut EP and its bemoaning cries of ?They make me get up in the morning?.

Oddments*, like the conceptual full-length *Eyes Like the Sky before it, lands in a space outside the conventional boundaries of the contemporary record-promote-release-tour cycle. It doesn’t feel rushed, but there’s a levity that runs the risk of being interpreted as stopgap. It’s a mellow, washy kaleidoscope – the majority of which could have easily passed for Stu MacKenzie solo material, especially when compared to the all-in opus of the previous album’s ?Head On/Pill?.

?There’s a warm, vintage weirdness sprouting to the fore.?

Opener ?Alluda Majaka? serves as a Tollywood sequel to Eyes Like the Sky*, the sample-driven groove and soulful organ garnished with carryover neighing-horse soundbites. It’s a welcoming jam, though a strange introduction for *Oddments* – but incongruence could be the point. The album’s closing, 25-second theme song would have made more sense at the top of the tracklist, considering its offhand throwback to the garage scrap of *Willoughby’s Beach.

Those flashes of up-tempo jams are comically short, as if to indulge and expel Thee Oh Sees comparisons simultaneously. That John Dwyer-esque falsetto yelp is present on ?Hot Wax?, countered by a stoic, Fred Schneider-imitating vocal drone. There’s a warm, vintage weirdness sprouting to the fore. A sweet ode to a salty subject, ?Vegemite? beholds an irrepressible charm akin to The Beach Boys? ?Vegetables? or, more recently, Ariel Pink’s ?Schnitzel Boogie?, imbuing nostalgic rosy-cheeked Australiana into childlike foodstuff appreciation.

Oddments? strength lies in its tonal cohesiveness, tying together a jack-knifing summary of King Gizzard’s stylistic leaps to date with a permeating sense of warmth – it can be as simple as the raindrops drizzled on the arpeggio acoustic plucks found on ?Homeless Man in Adidas?. It’s a reductive honing of craft, stripping back to find lushness, a pruning more so than a razing of King Gizzard’s trademark frenetic energy.

There’s little in the way of spectacle within Oddments*, but it doesn’t establish the promise of spectacle. It does what it says on the tin (or Vegemite jar), never really soaring yet never really faltering. A safe and psychedelic reflection on the King Gizzard canon to date, acting as an ellipses following last year’s more fully realised *Float Along – Fill Your Lungs, or perhaps an immediate colon preceding what’s next. History indicates we won’t have to wait long.

+
