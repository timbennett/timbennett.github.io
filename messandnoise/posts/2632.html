Bloodstreams

By Matt Shea 2012-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

DZ Deathrays are custom-made for the internet era. In an industry environment where the perceived wisdom is to now tour first and record second, the Brisbane-based duo have been doing plenty of the former, playing their fingers off to packed houses across Australia, the UK, the US and Europe. And well they might: DZ’s rambunctious, angular take on punk rock is something to be witnessed, rather than merely listened to.

In that sense, Bloodstreams can at times feel like a gesture of conciliation to the old way of doing things; something substantial to stock up the merch desk for another round of sold-out shows. It turns out to be a debut album packed with plenty of promise, but one that ultimately falls a touch short in its delivery.

Not that you’d think so upon initial listens. DZ Deathrays have taken the Nevermind approach to making a punk record, working with producer Richard Pike (PVT) to clean up the band’s sound and give it a big, brassy finish worthy of world domination. The title track, a woozy soundscape frontend-loaded as an intro, perhaps exists to illustrate the point. It’s an inclusive artistic decision, one that’ll alienate the purists but nevertheless broaden the band’s popular appeal.

The danger, of course, is that when you clean up the sonic mess, people pay a lot more attention to the songwriting. In that sense, DZ have – for the most part – lifted their game substantially, and there’s a clutch of delirious belters that stand up to repeated listens. Lead single ?No Sleep? is arguably the best thing the band have done to date; guitar sirens sounding as frontman Shane Parsons wails, ?No sleep till you pass out, you gotta/Motherfuckers say, I don’t want it back now.? It’s a stunning moment – something to be witnessed on the dance floor in a mist of sweat and unholy liquor.

No Sleep by DZDEATHRAYS

Likewise, ?Cops Capacity? – first recorded at a house party for the Brutal Tapes EP – illustrates the growing nous of Parsons and drummer Simon Ridley at packing on muscle without losing definition. The song’s extended third-quarter build no doubt works best when packaged with an audience to spit the words back at you, but remains exhilarating in recorded form. Of the cuts that move furthest away from DZ Deathrays? established sound, ?Gebbie Street? is the best, a noodling bass and bongo jam-out building into a ferocious descending finale. Great stuff.

Not all of Parsons and Ridley’s experiments work so well, though. ?Play Dead Till Your Dead?, driven by its swollen organ riff, is engaging, but the extra sonic space eventually highlights a deficiency in the songwriting. Likewise, ?Dumb it Down? is an interesting diversion but nothing more. It’s fun to hear DZ stretch their legs like this, but the lack of impetus on both cuts robs Bloodstreams of much of its momentum. Thankfully, the band’s sequenced the record carefully to parcel out these moments and bookend them with the more cut and thrust material.

But then that’s arguably the biggest problem with Bloodstreams: everything is put together so deliberately that the record ends up being a touch prosaic. There’s more than a handful of brilliant moments here, and Parsons and Ridley’s attempts at broadening their sound should be welcomed, but this is more a shot across the bows than the direct hit you feel the band are capable of. It feels over-produced, yet creatively rushed.

Still, what is this record but a primer for DZ Deathrays? live show? In that sense it does its job, and does it well.
