Blue Star

By ReneSchaefer 2009-09-01T00:00:00+00:00

Melbourne guitarist Matt Church makes intriguing music. On his solo album, The Pre-Raphaelites*, he crafted delicate pastoral folk songs in the vein of Nick Drake. In contrast, on latest album *Blue Star, he has drafted in drummer Steve Linquist and his wife Kathy Church on bass to join him on a series of extended space-rock jams.

While inspired by the psychedelia of the late 1960s, Church and his cohorts steer well clear of bogus consciousness expansion and focus on the cinematic aspects of the genre. The sci-fi tinged song titles imply a journey outwards into the infinity of space, but in effect the largely instrumental music turns in on itself, conveying a sense of solipsism and insularity.

If Blue Star* were the soundtrack to a movie, it would accompany something like the desolate Russian masterpieces of Andrei Tarkovsky, such as *Stalker* or *Solaris, in which protagonists are subject to unseen forces as they make their way towards an ill-defined objective through a barren landscape that reflects their own state of mind.

In concrete terms, Blue Star achieves this by keeping the rhythms simple and almost monotonous. Emphasised by barebones production, the drums rattle and clank along with stoic determination, while the bass lends steadfast support. It’s not the teutonic kraut rock beat of Neu!, which celebrated speed and momentum for its own sake. Rather, there is the sense of cautiously advancing further into vaguely daunting territory while the way back is irretrievably cut off.

At the helm of this expedition to the centre of the mind is Captain Church himself. With admirable restraint, he confines his playing to very few, carefully chosen notes, which he continually bends and twists with a limited arsenal of guitar effects. Extended solos dominate these tracks, without ever developing into clear-cut songs or shifting into different dynamics. Like Neil Young on the Dead Man soundtrack, Church is interested in draping the sounds he produces over the most skeletal of structures and creating haunting atmospheres.

Almost inevitably, the further he and his crew go on, the more they get mired in the wastelands they have been traversing. By the penultimate song, ?Red Star?, proceedings have slowed down to a crawl. Time comes to a near standstill in the final number and the disoriented travelers succumb to their own grandiose hallucinations without realising they are hopelessly lost and will never reach their elusive and possibly non-existent goal.

Ultimately then, Blue Star is a dystopian, almost nihilistic work. Instead of expanding, its vision narrows further and further as it progresses, while revealing nothing at journey’s end. Whether this is something a listener can embrace is entirely subjective, but as a work of art it poses a definite challenge. An album worth investigating.
