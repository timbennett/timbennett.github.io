We Knew It Was Not Going To Be Like This

By Mitchell Judge 2013-09-04T00:00:00+00:00

Quite a bit has changed for Surf City since their 2010 debut album Kudos; founding member Josh Kennedy left to start a family and the band took a short hiatus before regrouping to record album two. ?It’s a Common Life? bolts out of the gates, at least by Surf City’s standards, directing f-bombs at kids and criticising politicians while maintaining their whimsical nah-nah-nah’s. It sets the album up perfectly by showing that while they have matured, there is no mistaking this for anything other than a Surf City record.

Each of the opening four tracks could stake a claim as the most immediately engaging pop song Surf City have crafted. The band have taken a significant step forward with songwriting; they have grown more confident and are far more direct than ever before. Previously Davin Stoddard’s voice was buried behind layers of fuzz guitar, at times taking several listens to even make out the words, let alone meaning, of their songs. With the vocals now brought to the front and centre, the sense of disillusionment that pervades the album is all too clear.

It has been two years since Kudos garnered praise from all corners of the globe and yet they remain a relatively unknown, or at least very underrated, band. This rather mundane reality of the modern rock ?n? roll lifestyle is explored on ?NYC?, with the band ?listening to the radio, checking out the stats? before lamenting that ?New York City is for every kitty, but I don’t think I’m one of those kitties.?

Stoddard’s travels to the Big Apple and later South Korea were clearly major influences, as was the homesickness these travels brought on. ?No Place to Go? features regretful lines like ?Lessons learned, but nothing’s gained,? concluding that the only option is to find a way back home. Despite this lyrical focus, Surf City never sound pissed off; the album’s sunny psych-pop is a natural progression from Kudos.

The structuring of each song comes across as formulaic; start with approximately two minutes of pop, move into a repetitive psychedelic jam, rinse and repeat. The band executes it well, though, and instead of making the album drag towards the end, it contributes to the effortless feel of each track. For those who enjoyed ?Icy Lakes?, the hypnotic, seemingly endless ?Oceanic Graphs of the Wilderness? is even better. Other highlights include the backwards guitar solo in ?No Place to Go?, and the amazing final six minutes of ?What Gets Me By?, which sounds like an extended sigh of relief, absolving anything left of the melancholy that the album’s lyrics had established.

For a band that have always been described using references to classic bands like The Clean and Spaceman 3, this album serves as the strongest evidence that Surf City are far more than referential. The influence of such bands are still there, but the relaxed psych vibes are much closer to contemporaries Jagwar Ma. And while We Knew It Was Not Going to Be Like This may not catapult them into that same world-conquering league, it will make an excellent soundtrack to the upcoming summer.
