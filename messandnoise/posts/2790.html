New Centre Of The Universe Vol. 2

By Doug Wallen 2013-07-23T00:00:00+00:00

Last year’s first New Centre compilation was a simple proposition: 18 mostly garage bands, split between Geelong and Melbourne. True to the title, a showcase of a swelling musical hotspot in Victoria. This second volume keeps the same dodgy and eroded spirit that made the first sound so perfect on cassette, but widens the net to include more states and cities. And while garage is still key, so too is punk, post-punk, psych, surf and bedroom pop.

So what unites these 20 tracks? A persistent rawness, for one, as well as a lively awareness of the past and a fondness for brevity that makes the total running time just 53 minutes. All the songs were recorded last year or in 2011, except for a Straight Arrows demo from way back in 2007. But primarily it’s a shared spirit that allows the vocal sweetness of Super Wild Horses? ?Some Things I Can’t Do Without You? to make sense next to Bits of Shit’s barked and slurred ?F? (recorded live at Brunswick’s The Penny Black) and that Straight Arrows demo ?Running Wild?, which stays just as robustly in-the-red as their ?proper? songs.

It’s all defiantly DIY, whether cut with a seasoned engineer like Jack Farley or Mikey Young or the self-recorded solo musings of Turtlenecjk (with a 1960s-via-Elephant 6 carnival vibe), Wonderboy (the work of The Clits frontman Lucas Heenan, making a live debut with a backing band at the compilation’s launch) or Councillor of Ageing (junkyard bleariness from The Bonniwells? Mark Dean). Bratty delivery is a recurring theme, from Raw Prawn’s strong opener ?Stitched Me Up? to Leather Towel’s 54-second blast ?Food Poisoning? and Housewives? shouty guy/girl tirade ?You’s Hate?. Terrible Truths are especially prickly on ?Diamond?, while The Sulphur Lights make a refrain of ?Kill, kill, kill everyone.?

A few tracks are more by-the-numbers, like Sewers? ?General Gore? and The Towelheads? token surf entry ?Hijacked?, but they still sound cool. The vintage aspect is carried most visibly by The Nugs, a Newcastle band fronted by US expat Tristian Bunning: their ?I’m Spending the Night? starts with a Spanish count-in a la ?Wooly Bully? and recalls a slower sibling to The Ramones? ?Judy is a Punk?. Tiny Migrants follow their standout turn on last year’s Nuggets comp with the Super Wild Horses-ish throwback ?Talk Shit?, complete with surf-leaning guitar and call-and-response harmonies. Cobwebbs? dark ?Twenty Twelve? takes a 13th Floor Elevators vibe to more sneering territory, opening with the line ?I don’t give a fuck about 1985.? And The Gooch Palms? self-recorded ?A Sun, A Moon? is a timeless attack of muddied vocals and two-piece garage-rock primitivism.

The sequencing somehow works, even when relentless energy is paired with wobbly shrugs. Like Dick Diver’s entry on the previous comp, a few songs go for more of a thoughtful guitar-pop thing. Besides Wonderboy’s heartstring-tugging ?Some Kinda Love?, The Otchkies? dissipated country tune ?Exiled on Highway Seven? is more Flying Burrito Brothers than Black Lips. The Fighting League’s ?Bad Surprises? packs a doozy of a power-pop hook (think The Go-Go’s), not to mention a surprisingly clean vocal turn from the often punkier Dominic Death.

And with a deadpan dryness to the vocals, a fantastic squealing guitar solo and mention of a Stevens tribute band, The Stevens? totally Kinks-worthy ?Elpho Beach? could be the best song here. But man, has it got some stiff competition.

New Centre Of The Universe Vol. 2 by ANTI FADE
