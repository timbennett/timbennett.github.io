Let Music & Bodies Unite

By Edward Sharp-Paul 2011-10-01T00:00:00+00:00

Rat Vs Possum were something brand new when they emerged in 2009. Indebted to rave, psych, pop, and the floor-tom manufacturers of the world, their tribal-primitive experiments caught on quick, both with fans and other bands alike. Of course, so many bands caught on that their sound quickly became a ghetto, as much a millstone as a calling card. Furthermore, something of their unique brew was lost in translation on 2010?s Daughter Of Sunshine.

On Let Music And Bodies Unite, one gets the sense that the band is making a concerted attempt to address the shortcomings of their previous album, and define themselves more clearly as a band. The thin gruel of toms, chants and looped guitar has been replaced by a less varied, but more streamlined sound.

Synths are the showcase element on LM&BU, but they are deployed in all sorts of different ways, to all sorts of different ends. First single and standout track ?Fat Monk? rides a on the momentum of beatific Moog riff, with a faux-theremin making occasional cameos. The song may lack compositional depth, but it’s hard to be cranky when it’s executed with such style. Late album highlight ?Home (No)? borrows Gary Numan’s ?Are Friends Electric?? synth tone and straps it to a warp-speed Holy Fuck-like beat. In between, there are pulses, pads, chimes and drones, creating some lovely textures.

Rat vs Possum – Never Die by sensoryprojects10

Stripped of Daughter Of Sunshine’s* massed-tom inertia, Andrew Noble’s kit sounds lighter and immeasurably groovier. In fact, the record’s sheer danceability – perhaps its greatest strength – is largely down to him. At the front of the mix, the vocals have received a makeover as well. This, however, has been less successful. A long-time issue for Rat Vs Possum has been the lack of a focal point, as well as a serious susceptibility to a dodgy lyric. On *LM&BU, they partially rectify the former, while frequently succumbing to the latter.

Daphne Shum steps forward to do the lead singer thing, and does so with aplomb. Her voice has sufficient character to justify her newfound prominence, and it holds the listener’s attention. However, lyrics that might be charmingly daggy when chanted en masse are flat-out awkward here. ?Never Die? is the lowest ebb, all tripper’s metaphysics from start to finish.

However, LM&BU is not a Leonard Cohen album. Rat Vs Possum do hedonism, and an album as fun as this is, by definition, a success. If a slight lack of depth and the odd dodgy lyric is the price the listener pays, then so be it.
