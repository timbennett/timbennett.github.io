The Best Tracks Of 2012 (So Far)

By Darren Levin 2012-07-02T14:00:00+00:00

##As we enter the second half of 2012, DOUG WALLEN* and *DARREN LEVIN look back at the best local tracks of 2012 – from Pond to Pony Face, Catcall to Chook Race.

The Laurels
?Changing the Timeline?
(From the album Plains)

Even with that groggy start, ?Changing the Timeline? is the most immediate tune on The Laurels? first album. And yet the band’s vocal sneer and altered-state verve are still decidedly intact. There’s also a cool call-and-response element to the chorus, but Conor Hannan’s thick, locomotive bass is the defining feature. – Doug Wallen

Collarbones
?Missing?
(From the forthcoming album Die Young)

Stranding bona fide R&B vocals in a desolate patch of fog and then low-level percussion, ?Missing? leans on nebulous production to reflect its absent subject. But it’s more than just an on-the-nose study of absence. It’s newly concentrated and less choppy than previous Collarbones songs. Trying their hand at a proper slow jam, the duo have toned down the glitch-y edits to lay themselves bare. – Doug Wallen

Missing by Collarbones

The Bamboos
?I Got Burned?
(From the LP Medicine Man)

Of all the singers on The Bamboos? new record, Tim Rogers is probably the least accomplished – and he’d be the first to admit it, too. But while he certainly can’t belt out, ?Too laaaaate!?, like he could in the ?90s, this old rock dog has happened upon a new trick: a distinct falsetto that’s imperfect and full of character, just like the protagonist of this song. – Darren Levin

Bushwalking
?Visual Jam Doughnut?
(From the album First Time)

Song title of the year? Coming from the fevered collective consciousness of Ela Stiles (Songs), Nisa Venerosa (Fabulous Diamonds) and Karl Scullin (Kes Band), this single is so addictive because it feels like we’re merely dropping in on part of a much longer piece. Instrumental save for chanted vocal harmonies, ?Visual Jam Doughnut? is a droning showcase of bleary keyboards, stiff rhythms and increasingly harried guitar. Rinse and repeat. – Doug Wallen

Bushwalking, “Visual Jam Doughnut” by The FADER

Galapagoose
?One Who Can’t Move?
(From the album Commitments)

A song about being inert that actually moves. Trent Gill’s clipped sample of Sinatra singing a line from ?Send In The Clowns? is repeated like a mantra over rolling piano and kitchen-sink percussion (for that DIY feel), before briefly breaking down into something akin to The Beach Boys? ?Our Prayer?. It’s one of the most engaging and fully formed moments from debut Commitments. – Darren Levin

Galapagoose – One Who Can’t Move by twobrightlakes

Pond
?You Broke My Cool?
(From the album *Beard, Wives, Denim

Despite originally being the kid brother in the equation, Pond have just about rivalled Tame Impala for global buzz this year. Credit that to songs like the glam-mussed ?You Broke My Cool?, which refracts ?60s psych and ?70s prog colours into a summery new spectrum.

You Broke My Cool by POND

Ned Collette
?Il Futuro Fantastico?
(From the LP 2)

If you’ve ever found yourself as a foreigner in a strange land in a rat-infested apartment with a leaky roof and broken furniture in the dead of winter DO NOT LISTEN TO THIS SONG. In his ?Storytellers? piece, Ned Collette describes the opening cut of his second album 2 as a song about ?hopelessness?. The coda is the clincher, however: 90 seconds of musique concrete meets muzak from the genius mind of collaborator Joe Talia. – Darren Levin

Ned Collette + Wirewalker – Il Futuro Fantastico by 00remotecontrol

Woollen Kits
?University Narcolepsy?
(From the album Woollen Kits)

You needn’t have attended a day of uni to understand the temptation to fall asleep in class. With their usual spontaneous charisma, Woollen Kits turn an everyday occurrence into a mantra that takes up immediate residence in one’s head. Along the way, it achieves the kind of jangling drive that has launched a thousand power-pop tunes. – Doug Wallen

Emma Russack
?Tonight?
(From the album Sounds of Our City)

The nakedly confessional opener of a nakedly confessional album, ?Tonight? is about looking to one-night-stands to fill the emptiness. (Spoiler alert: they don’t.) Accompanied by Alec Marshall’s knockout guitar lead, its pitch-perfect narrative advertises a stubborn freedom (?I can do what I want now?) that’s bitterly cut through with apathy (?No great expectations/Anyone will do?). – Doug Wallen

Joe McKee
?Lunar Sea?
(From the album Burning Boy)

If you repeat the title seven times fast, you get a sense of what kind of headspace Joe McKee was in when he wrote the tone-setting opener of his first album post-Snowman. Opening with a string part recalling the theme from Psycho (later reprised by Busta Rhymes on ?Gimme Some More?), the song unfolds like a lucid dream, before dissolving into madness. – Darren Levin

Lunar Sea by Joe McKee

The Dirty Three
?Rising Below?
(From the album Toward the Low Sun)

It may not go for the scorched cacophony of opener ?Furnace Skies?, but this is the centrepiece of Dirty Three’s first album in seven years. ?Rising Below? is loose yet layered, showing ever more tangles as it nears completion. For the longest while, though, it simply glides along as if inhabiting well-worn grooves. Which, of course, it is. – Doug Wallen

Dirty Three – Rising Below by Bella Union

Catcall
?The World Is Ours?
(From the LP The Warmest Place)

This giddy love song from Catcall’s widely discussed debut is a cynicism-free zone where you get to indulge in your love of late-?80s Belinda Carlisle and John Hughes montage music without worrying what your friends think. They’re probably listening to it, too. – Darren Levin

Chet Faker
?Terms and Conditions?
(From the EP Terms and *Thinking In Textures)

Applying the fine print that none of us ever read to relationships, Chet Faker shuffles through the ruins with a smouldering warmth. This track proved there’s more to Nick Murphy’s hotly tipped R&B project than just a clever cover of ?No Diggity?. – Doug Wallen

Terms and Conditions by Chet Faker

Chook Race
?Pop Song?
(From the Chook Race 7?)

Does exactly what it says on the tin with doo-wop harmonies, indecipherable lyrics, an ocker accent, the kind of jangly guitars that are so in vogue in Melbourne’s underground right now, and a DIY film clip shot on location at Mordialloc pier. More proof we’re getting over the cultural cringe. – Darren Levin

Dune Rats
?Fuck It?
(From an upcoming EP)

While it’s not as flat-out infectious as their previous single ?Pogo?, Brisbane’s Dune Rats keep things light-hearted with this self-explanatory two-minute gem. Produced by Last Dinosaurs? Sean Caskey, it’s lo-fi in an oddly robust way. It also makes its title not the chorus but just a repeated aside – the perfect illustration of a band whose slacker traits run deep. – Doug Wallen

FUCK IT by Dune Rats

Hunting Grounds
?Flaws?
(From the forthcoming album In Hindsight)

Announcing the newly dreamy horizons of the regional Victoria band formerly known as Howl!, ?Flaws? is a foreboding march of a rock single (think latter-day Modest Mouse) that crams in a lot of ideas. It’s a bratty, poppy, slow-motion admission of imperfection, ever threatening to splinter at the next crossroads. – Doug Wallen

Pony Face
?Silver Tongue?
(From forthcoming album Hypnotised)

Not anything at all like the skate-punk band their name implies, Melbourne’s Pony Face recall Sparklehorse in their fuzzy execution. This track builds nicely from a fractured guitar line, gradually incorporating yet more fuzzy guitar, a male-female counterpart in the chorus and some Warren Ellis-esque violin. And speaking of The Dirty Three, the track was produced by Casey Rice, who recorded their most recent album, Toward The Low Sun. – Darren Levin

SILVER TONGUE by pony face

Lower Plenty
?Nullarbor?
(From the album Hard Rubbish)

This song always makes me stop what I’m doing and take a moment to appreciate it. Even before Al Montfort’s weedy vocals kick in – before we get to lines like, ?Out on the Nullarbor in Jack’s Commodore? and, ?She said, ?Do what you must, ?cause soon you’ll be dust?? – there’s something super special about this. Maybe it’s because, after one listen, we know that all that stuff is coming up. Or because Lower Plenty do the travelogue thing better than just about anyone. – Doug Wallen

World’s End Press
?Second Day Uptown?
(From the EP Second Day Uptown)

They’re at it again. These guys move through the funky best of 20th- century music so smoothly that it’s easy to forget they’re acolytes, not originators. If there’s a uniform feeling to the Melbourne band’s singles, it’s because they’re riffing on the same danceable themes. Still, ?Second Day Uptown? is a bit more considered, stretching the bubblegum bass lines and constant percolation to fit John Parkinson’s maturing vocals. – Doug Wallen

Summer Flake
?No More?
(From the EP Summer Flake)

Adelaide mainstay Stephanie Crase has played in more bands than you ever will. Roll call: Hit the Jackpot, No Through Road, Batrider, Birth Glow, Fair Maiden, Avant Gardeners. She impresses yet again with the solo outing Sumer Flake, inhabiting the melancholy guitar curvature of J Mascis on more tracks than one. That makes ?No More? a standout simply because it adds a spoonful of girl-group sugar and winds up closer to timeless pop than heavy-lidded grunge. – Doug Wallen

On The River by Summer Flake
