Great Barrier Grief

By Darren Levin 2011-03-01T00:00:00+00:00

Oh Mercy’s Alexander Gow is a young man in no rush to settle down. ?I love my kids, but I hate my wife,? he sings on ?On The Run?, a charming pop song, which – on paper at least – reads like a warning from his future self. Of the 11 songs on Oh Mercy’s second album Great Barrier Grief, the vast majority are about relationships – but there’s little room for anything more than a fleeting connection. As he reveals throughout the course of the record, Gow is more likely to love and leave.

On ?Let Me Go?, he confesses, ?I’m a single man/Don’t fuck on my plans?, over an elegant ?60s lounge backdrop; while the jaunty ?Confessions? speaks of the kind of transitory lifestyle that comes with fronting a touring band (?Round the world/Now I’m stationary/Lost a girl voluntarily?). Gow’s not immune from heartbreak, however. On ?Stay, Please Stay?, one of the most laconically cool pop songs you’ll hear all year, he plays the part of the jilted lover, desperately pleading, ?How do you smile and say goodbye/With a bag on your back/And not a tear in your eye?? It’s this perpetual heartache – both inflicted and sustained – that fuels Gow as a songwriter. It’s why he’s called this album Great Barrier Grief.

Recorded in Los Angeles with Mitchell Froom, the American producer behind Crowded House’s first two albums, Great Barrier Grief* is a deceptively simple pop record; a deliberate exercise in restraint. In some ways it’s a reaction against the lush arrangements that characterised the band’s 2008 debut *Privileged Woes*. In others, it reflects what Gow was listening to at the time, namely Serge Gainsbourg’s 2007 compilation *Comic Strip* and John Cale’s 1974 album *Fear. Both artists used instrumentation and arrangement to conjure a particular mood, but they also knew when to pare things back. It’s something Gow – a self-confessed pop classicist – has done well to emulate here.

With his acoustic guitar as the foundation – he switches to electric in a live context – the songs have been given room to simmer and breathe. ?Stay Please Stay? is little more than a neat descending/ascending riff nicely punctuated by marimba. ?Let Me Go? has a great ?Band On The Run? (part one) feel to it, with former bandmate Thom Savage’s slinky jazz guitar plugging the gaps. ?What You Do? opens with a ringing 12-string that conjures the expansiveness of the Australian outback much like The Triffids and The Go-Betweens (bands Oh Mercy have been compared to them in the past, but this time it really sticks), while the vibes in the verses create a sense of ambience and space akin to Cale’s ?Ship Of Fools?.

Some may misconstrue Gow’s laconic way of singing as indifference; like he’s not truly engaged with the material, or unsure about the direction his group should be taking in the absence of Savage, his songwriting foil on Privileged Woes. I’d say it’s the complete opposite. Far from an overwrought, overcrowded pitch to the festival market – the kind where song craft is sacrificed for grandiose statements – this is an assured album of simple pop pleasures. Such a rare thing to find these days.
