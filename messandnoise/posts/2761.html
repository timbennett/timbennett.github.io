FOUR (Acts Of Love)

By JodyMacgregor 2013-04-29T00:00:00+00:00

Mick Harvey’s previous solo album, Sketches from the Book of the Dead*, was a cycle of songs about the departed. What comes after death? Harvey says it’s love, and *FOUR (Acts of Love) is a concept album about romance.

He’s not following an album of loss, abandonment and dread with one of cheesy love songs, though. These aren’t songs named after women the songwriter has slept with or would like to sleep with, and they don’t exist to facilitate and then soundtrack love. Mick Harvey is not going to make love to you, woman; he is not going to lay you down by the fire. Instead, these songs treat love as something worth considering on its own.

FOUR (Acts of Love) is perversely divided into three. The first act opens with Harvey, accompanied by piano and guitar, praising the sun and the moon in flowery language of ?garlands gay? and a ?silver shield.? Don’t be afraid he’s gone all hippie-period Ben Lee on us, however. Before long he turns all that nature worship into an opportunity to ask God what happens to our dreams and achievements when we go, and why they’re not hanging in the sky like the Lord’s own trophy shelf is. Even though love is what it’s all about, Harvey opens up a darker space pretty quickly. He then fills it with ?Glorious?, a song that’s written by PJ Harvey and sounds like it, with echoing electric guitar and lyrics that celebrate love while describing it in terms that maybe aren’t so healthy – ?so deep it will not heal? – before ending in a threatening buzz.

The high point of this first act, maybe of the whole album, is ?Summertime in New York?. A cover of an Exuma song, Harvey’s version adds creepy whispering, a constantly repeating guitar phrase, double bass, Tom Waits murmuring, mumbles and moans – so many voices it’s like a bustling city street all caterwauling together in a wonderful cacophony. There are a few other covers on the album, but this is the one that Harvey most aggressively makes his own. Later he takes ?The Way Young Lovers Do?, which was a weird cross between folk music and big band in Van Morrison’s hands, and turns it into something like an incantation, talk-singing the lines over organ atmospherics. He also tackles The Saints? ?The Story of Love? and Roy Orbison’s ?Wild Hearts? in slightly more typical versions.

His own contributions seem less optimistic about the subject at hand than those covers, including a couple of droning snippets called ?Where There’s Smoke (Before)? and ?Where There’s Smoke (After)? that are condensed heartbreak in a tube. In ?I Wish That I Were a Stone? Harvey fantasises about being a gargoyle so he doesn’t have to deal with all this emotional nonsense. Sometimes the music is sparse and mostly guitar; other times it’s overblown and orchestral. Love is a big topic and he takes it on in a variety of different ways, with the closest thing to a defining statement coming in ?Fairy Dust?, which concludes that love is all there is ?for better or worse.?

Mick Harvey – FOUR (Acts of Love) – I Wish That I Were Stone by Mute UK

That’s followed by a reprise of the opening with different drums, an added vocal and a picked lead guitar bit – just enough changed to turn it into a rousing finisher for the third act. On the subject of those acts, I still don’t know why there are three of them on an album called FOUR (Acts of Love). I also haven’t got a clue why the songs are arranged in the seemingly careful way they are, or what message is hidden between them. What I can tell you is that even if you only engage with this album on a surface level, you’ll still enjoy it.

And seriously, listen to ?Summertime in New York? first chance you get.
