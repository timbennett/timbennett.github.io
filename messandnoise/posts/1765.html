Things to Learn

By Matt 2008-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

There is a trend towards ?60s and ?70s rock in Perth that I am, to varying degrees depending on my mood, annoyed by. It’s as if, not finding undiluted rock masculinity present in any of the pastiche musical forms of the 21st century, bands of young men have turned their focus backward upon a simpler epoch with simpler songs and an even simpler image of manhood. For this reason I approached the Silents? first album, Things to Learn*, with scepticism. There were other reasons. Their two other releases – the *23* and *Flicker and Flames EPs – were solid but unexciting, and singer Lloyd Stowe sounds a bit like The Vines? Craig Nicholls, but mainly I just wanted the pendulum in west coast rock to swing forward rather than backward.

So it was with a mixture of surprise and delight that I discovered that Things to Learn transcends the Silents? normally untrammelled ?60s nostalgia to become a seething storm; melody and drama coursing lightning forks through its angry folds. True, this is in large part due to the atmosphere set down by the first song, the title track, which is also the album’s best moment. But, though the following songs aren’t as exhilaratingly disturbed, they are suitably coloured by ?Things to Learn? – in which Kinks-style melodic gestures are applied to muscular swathes of string noise – and supported by their own special tics.

?Tune for a Nymph? builds to a leisurely chorus of impassioned but melancholy ?ahs?, the album’s oldest song ?Nightcrawl? still has its bouncing rhythm and killer hook, and acoustic ballad closer ?Bruised Sky? provides the necessary come-down. Despite the album losing momentum in its last third, its songs are constructed and sequenced so that one is never bored. Things to Learn always commands attention.

Like their coastmates the Bank Holidays, the Silents not only avoid the brainless imitation of their sleeve-worn inspirations but strike upon a wholly contemporary emotional resonance. Where the ?Banks? have accomplished this via the exultation of beatific pop, the Silents do it using the darker depths of rock. A scene of vacant Canned Heat impersonators still annoys me, but if it throws up the odd worthwhile album amid the donning of cravats then it’s not all bad.
