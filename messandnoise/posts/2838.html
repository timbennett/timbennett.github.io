Duets

By Anthony Carew 2013-11-12T00:00:00+00:00

When Karl Edwin Scullin talks of being ?disillusioned with vocals,? or wanting to transcend the limitations of singing, a cynic might wonder if it’s couched in disillusionment with his own voice, the limitations of his own singing. The esoteric Melburnian is a process-oriented conceptualist, a musician-as-artist who conceives his work in the big picture; each micro act contributing to the macro canon of his ongoing, ever-changing Kes canon. It’s no surprise, then, that his thoughts on the literalism of lyrics echoes sermons that the great boffin of pop, Brian Eno, has been preaching for decades. Yet, given that there’s a whole comment-thread history, on this very website, of punters posting ?love-the-music-pity-about-the-vocals? opinions regaring Kes, is Scullin’s recurring – and sometimes total – embrace of instrumentalism about preserving his vanity?

Scullin’s oddly compelling voice is the epitome of an ?acquired taste?: his singing sounding something like an elfin bogan impersonating Dylan; or, perhaps, a weedier, wonkier, more-country-Victoria take on Tom Rapp. And, just as on Kes Band’s 2009 LP Kes Band II, his latest Kes record has none of it. A wholly instrumental affair, Duets is titled with a smirk. There’s no ?Islands in the Stream?, but a set of improvised, incidental-feeling mood pieces made in co-helmed collaboration; the simple idea of ?two people in the same room playing off each other? a germinating seed from which the whole grew.

Duets by Kes

Reading the tracklist may fill you with dread, titles like ?Daddylonglegs and the Cockroach? evoking the childhood traumas of Prokofiev’s noxious Peter and the Wolf, in which instrumental timbre is turned into typecast caricature. And, even if an album this low-key could never border on that kind of lurid cartoon, the titles operate with the same playbill logic: ?Sparrow and the Inchworm? a brief, tiny piece where nylon-string guitar crawls beneath a fluttering clarinet; ?Electricity and the Boulder? a howl of washed-out distortion clanging into clunks of heavily echoed woodblock. Of course, the form doesn’t always hold: ?Chole and Dhal? sees Scullin and Kes Trio drummer Julian Patterson sit down with acoustic guitars and have a (classical) gas whilst waiting for their Indian takeout.

The idea is, freed from the yoke of pop-song format, the tonal qualities of the instrument and the expressiveness in the playing will come out unencumbered. But the response this provokes is: ?And?? Here, the process-artist has produced a set of compositions that say a lot about that process, but produce a whole that feels disconnected, and essentially directionless. The great clich? of post-rock’s eternal instrumentalism was that the music functioned as each listener’s soundtrack to a movie (never made), but Duets summons no such glorious, singular vision. Instead, it feels more like the storyboard to 16 disconnected scenes (never shot).

Scullin has long lamented the lag between recording and releasing; every Kes album coming out long after he’s already moved into some next, new phase. He almost never tours, and wishes he could just turn out albums like canvases. In this continuum, Duets makes sense as a conceptual piece, and the limited pressing it’s receiving is so light on investment that Scullin is free to forge ahead, unburdened by the promotional grind. Which is to say: this is undoubtedly minor Kes, a series of between-albums sound experiments more satisfying for artist than audience. Making an LP on which folksingin? brothers Oliver and Paddy Mann and songwriter-as-memoirist Laura Jean contribute only melodica, ambient noise and piano/guitar-scrawl may be liberating for the guests, but it’s a disappointment at conception: what draws listeners to those artists is the striking individualism of their singing, not their playing.

Maybe Scullin’s creaky, croaky voice isn’t, in contrast, the lure in Kes, but his determination to avoid singing feels like he’s shirking the most personal – and perhaps painful – part of his craft. Duets builds a shrine to instrumental idiosyncrasy, yet leaves Scullin’s most idiosyncratic instrument on the shelf.
