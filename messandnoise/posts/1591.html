Dream Days At The Hotel Existence

By andyr 2007-05-08T12:00:00+00:00

A few weeks before the release of this album, Queensland’s Parliament House started hanging the Aboriginal flag inside the members? chamber for the first time in 147 years. The symbolism of the event, designed to ?remind? members that the Parliament existed for the benefit of black people too, overshadowed another story that day. The country’s peak medical body announced that indigenous Australians could expect to die almost two decades before everybody else. Their life expectancy hadn’t risen since the 1920s.

Powderfinger copped some flak for omitting the second verse of ?Black Tears? on their sixth studio album after pressure from the legal team of Senior Sergeant Chris Hurley, the officer charged with manslaughter in connection with the death of Mulrunji Doomoodgee while in police custody in 2004. But the message hasn’t been diluted. ?There’s blood on all our hands/And blood on our boots,? Bernard Fanning sings in a raw voice, without the sheen of a Los Angeles studio that covers the rest of the album. As the song fades, he repeats the lines ?Island watchhouse bed/Black man’s lying dead?.

The song’s theme seems to resonate through the album as a whole. Tracks like ?Nobody Sees? and ?Ballad Of A Dead Man? that would otherwise seem like the usual lovelorn crap can’t help but take on a different meaning in the shadow of ?Black Tears?. Critics might not see the song as the sharpest of political comments, but when the country’s most popular band tackles the unpopular issue of injustice against blacks it is noteworthy; especially when, in Fanning’s words, most of their fans ?only buy two records a year?.

Musically, there’s nothing as punchy as the band’s best moments on Dream Days*. The album has a grand and overproduced 1970s feel to it, as if the five-piece had listened to equal parts Pink Floyd and their usual muse Neil Young while recording. Sometimes it pays off, as in centrepiece ?Who Really Cares? where psych-gospel organ and wailing guitar billow around Fanning’s vocals like a lost track from *Dark Side Of The Moon. But for the most part, the bonus ?best of? DVD rudely highlights the energy lacking on this release. There are some beautiful moments, but fans of rock & roll will be disappointed.
