II

By A.H. 2013-02-05T00:00:00+00:00

The Mint Chicks’ final gig descended into chaos after only four songs. Playing at the Bacco Room in Auckland to promote the aptly titled Bad Buzz EP at a fundraiser for label start-up MusicHy.pe, singer Kody Nielson reportedly picked up a second drum set, threw it at drummer Paul Roper, and a struggle ensued. The stage set-up was ferociously dismantled, and in the midst of the action a punter yelled out a song request. ?Start your own fucking band,? Nielson retorted. It was a suitable end for a band so regarded on the basis of the energy and friction of their music. In turn, the label sent out a presser denying the band had broken up, then sold footage of the bust-up for $3. Authentic.

Most members did start their own band. Kody Nielson formed Opossum with bass player Michael Logie and his partner Bic Runga. Logie formed F in Math and later joined Die! Die! Die!. But it was Ruban Nielson, Kody’s brother and the band’s guitarist, who found the biggest success, with Unknown Mortal Orchestra.

Unknown Mortal Orchestra- “Swim & Sleep (Like a Shark)” (Official Audio) by Jagjaguwar

Two albums on, the fire and bravado of The Mint Chicks seems to have left this Ruban Nielson. Following on from 2011’s self-titled album, II is a lackadaisical, hazy piece of work. It’s easy on first listens to write it off as boring, lacking as it does the extent of its predecessor’s pop hooks, each track seeming to segue without ceremony into the next; an album as one immense, fluid work. It comes across on first impressions as just textbook lethargic, sunny soul-psych – pleasant, but not immediately grabbing. But once the words sink in and the tunes stick in the mind, that’s exactly what makes it an intriguing and involving work. There’s a discrepancy between the sound and the message, and it’s fascinating.

Nielson is tired. He’s hurting, but seems to have given up trying to do anything about it. He sings, often delicately and calmly, of isolation, of putting a gun in one’s hand, of needing to get away from the sun, of throwing away the only one. He ruminates on being able to sink to the bottom like a shark to sleep forever and float away, of chasing his own dreams away. He’s so lonely, but says he can never quite reach the phone. But, he insists, he’s only lonely through the night.

?Nielson is tired. He’s hurting, but seems to have given up trying to do anything about it.?

II evokes the emptiness of loss once the shock and sting goes away. Still raw but having given way to numbness and inaction, Nielson’s lyrics sit among the murky psych, vocals layered and mostly pulled back into the music, lending an air of resignation to each devastatingly understated confession. The self-loathing is confronting. He concerns himself – and you – with fantasies of his own destruction, torturing himself with reminisces in the still hours between the sun. The occasional anguished, wailing chorus (see ‘Faded In The Morning’) breaks through, but even then it’s brought back in the mix, serving to mute and bind the force of the emotional outburst.

Perhaps he’s just burnt out. The album was written in pieces on tour – a schedule described in the press release as ?punishing and debauched? – though the album itself sounds seamless. Each track leads confidently, blurrily, into the next, a continuation of theme and sound that makes for one immersive experience. The occasional misstep – perhaps intentional – only adds to the sense of vulnerability, as on ‘The Opposite of Afternoon’, which seems to end half a beat too soon before intro-ing hazily into the defiant ‘No Need for a Leader’. The drive behind these songs constantly changes, up in tempo, down in mood, but they never seem disparate or out of place.

Unknown Mortal Orchestra – So Good At Being In Trouble by All Tomorrows Parties

Nielson’s come a long way since The Mint Chicks’ implosion, and it seems a lot of that has been spent inside his head. The aching that permeates every note on II* is disturbing and unsettling, but it’s also incredibly beautiful, and becomes more relatable with each listen. What originally feels distant and dense gradually grows closer; the intimacy of the work is overwhelming, and it all makes sense. Listen to *II long enough, and all that pain – paralysing, suffocating pain – gradually becomes yours, and makes you numb.
