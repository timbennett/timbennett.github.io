Echoes in the Aviary

By Jules LeFevre 2014-11-26T00:00:00+00:00

You might not expect a member of The Herd and Urthboy’s right-hand woman to release an album of twisting, dark pop – but that’s exactly what Jane Tyrrell has done. The fact that it’s also an excellent record just makes it all the better. The Melbourne singer’s debut is a cavernous expanse of sounds – crashing and clanging drums, shimmering synths, layered and iced harmonies – that’s knotted around Tyrrell’s lilting, opaque voice. There’s greater deception here as well: the record is multi-layered yet sounds sparse, huge but undeniably intimate, electronic but human.

The production is similarly complex. Helmed by a team ranging from Pip Norman (Sparkadia) to Dustin McLean (Axolotl), with extra co-writing credits from Laurence Pike (PVT), it’s busy and constantly moving, but with the lightest of touches. Pike’s influence is palpable: the frenetic percussion that permeates on ?Ships? and opener ?Wild Waters? is his personal blueprint. It can get lost sometimes; closer ?Transcendent? is uncharacteristically muddled with cheesy laptop synths. Mostly though, it’s clean and thoughtful, a precise chaos.

So when Tyrrell sings, ?Everything else is spinning out/I’ve got a line to the centre? on ?Wild Waters?, surrounded by big droning keyboards and garage percussion, it makes a nice kind of sense. It’s a cold opener as well – a heartbeat drum and clipped piano chords flitter under Tyrrell’s vocals in the verses. It’s an atmosphere that is cultivated further over the record: ?The Rush? is a call for help from doomed lovers, ?Raven? contains deathly imagery, ?The First Stand? is darkly triumphant.

What’s telling is Tyrrell’s ability to touch these dark lyrical corners and not lose propulsion. Whenever she tumbles into a lyrical abyss, there’s always a melodic hook to guide her back out. That’s another string in her bow: the melodies here are sharp and often surprising in terms of their placement (in ?Ships? the chorus seemingly springs out of nowhere), but always with the intent of keeping it moving, of forward motion. ?Raven? is mind-numbingly steady, thick tom drums underlining proceedings; ?Among The Bells? is built on a jazz vocal line with crispy and eerie harmonies. ?Stolen Apples? is an exception to the rule – there’s a lack of movement that renders it flat.

When it’s up, it’s even better. ?Ships? is built on complex, trip-hop-y percussion that blooms into an airy chorus. ?The First Stand? has similar foundations – a shifting bed for Tyrrell’s vocal line. There’s a slight flatness towards the end: as the closer, ?Transcendant? is too wandering, complete with a droning synth outro that extends for minutes. But the missteps are few and far between on Echoes in the Avairy. For the most part it’s a tight and assured record, and it makes for a solid establishment of a solo career.
