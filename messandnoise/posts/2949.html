Slow Release

By Mitchell Judge 2014-10-13T00:00:00+00:00

Slow Release may be the least apt album title of the year. After a clanging riff played in unison kicks off ?One?, frontman Billy Burke repeats ?Now? Now? Now?? and, as if it were an order, the band elevates to a chaotic jam that blows the door off the hinges. The album is less than a minute old when Yes I’m Leaving begin the assault of noise that remains present for the majority of the album. We are given two, maybe three, seconds of reprieve between songs before being blasted once again, and it is not until the sixth track, ?Salt?, begins with a lone bass riff that we are allowed to catch our breath. It is clear that Yes I’m Leaving have not lost their intensity.

As many have said before me, Yes I’m Leaving’s live show is incredible. Like Die! Die! Die! and Dead Farmers, they launch a power trio attack that hits from all angles. However, they have always had a bit of difficulty capturing the same ferocity on record. Last year’s Mission Bulb saw the band take a significant step forward with a professional recording and mixing that gave their sound more depth, and Slow Release has continued in the same direction.

With Burke’s voice front and centre, the dark lyrical matter that the band have always focused on has become more potent. No longer distorted or hidden behind the noise, the anguish in Burke’s shrieks is alarmingly personal, tapping into the listener’s fears and issues at an intimate level. Whether it is exploring social anxiety on ?Manic? and ?Alchemy? or raging against hopelessness on ?Care Less? and ?Secret?, Burke is always direct and honest.

The final track ?Husk? is further evidence of the band’s growing confidence – pushing their songwriting boundaries to create a slow-moving, distressing track that stands among their very best. Using the quiet/loud extreme to devastating effect, Burke’s vocals are left to stand on their own as he sings, ?You’re a liar/You’re a husk.? Burke sounds genuinely haunting as his pitch shifts upon pronouncing ?liar?. After an album that paints the rest of the world as a source of confusion and paranoia, for the first time he sounds like the nightmare to be feared.

Yes I’m Leaving are not a particularly cool band. Beginning with their peculiar name, along with off-kilter album covers and an introverted stage presence, there is nothing about them that screams rock and roll. They are without a clich’d image or backstory to market: their most fascinating fact is that they are just polite, ordinary guys. Of course, when you make music as good as this, none of that is of the slightest importance.
