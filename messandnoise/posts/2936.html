The Reprobettes

By Doug Wallen 2014-08-31T00:00:00+00:00

The Reprobettes have a lot going for them right off the bat. They’ve got a witty (and fitting) name, they know their girl groups, they have their own theme song and – unlike the waves (pun intended) of current garage bands shoehorning surf into their genre tag without the faintest trace of actual surf guitar – they dose their combination of old-school garage, pop and soul with genuine surf twang.

Their own girl-group factor is no accident: The Reprobettes formed after bassist Jemma Ives posted an online ad seeking bandmates in a ?60s-style all-girl garage outfit. But they’re not just five females paying tribute to cult sounds of the past, as this debut album reveals in spades. The Reprobettes load their songs with tales of sexual frustration, taking aim at disappointing men as much as their own bad behaviour where men are involved. ?I knew you had a girlfriend but I just didn’t care/?Cause I was young and stupid and you had gorgeous hair,? goes ?Young and Stupid?, describing one particular moment of weakness, while ?Man Drought? has fun with its title scenario: ?All the nice guys are taken and the handsome boys are gay.? ?Rebel Heather? includes this bit of ?Leader of the Pack?-esque spoken word: ?When Johnny came along, that’s when it all went wrong.?

Even ?Suicide Tuesday?, which is quite sprightly (save the intro) for a song about the worst day imaginable, quips: ?Never trust a guy that says he’s here to stay.? Most biting, and effective, of all is ?Hysterical?, on which Sally Balhorn’s Corin Tucker-esque vocals are trained on men dismissing female emotion: ?You say that I’m hysterical – irrational – emotional.? More organ-and-rhythm-based than other songs, it’s a lean and mean standout that saves Shannon Driscoll’s jabbering, badass guitar solo for the final minute, after Balhorn reaches her limit and announces, ?Well, I’ll show you hysterical, baby.? From there the band burn the house down with a tinny, punky minute of garage-roughened jamming.

Balhorn plays guitar and alternates lead vocal duties with keyboardist Kat Karamitros, while Ives sings lead on ?Rebel Heather?. But all five sing, to some degree. Completed by Carol Micallef’s loose, unfussed drumming, The Reprobettes intersperse their amusing venting and anthemic outbursts with more laidback cruising that doubles as a musical history lesson. Instrumental save for vocal harmonies, the opening ?Reprobettes Theme? seems to nod to both ?Rumble? and ?Tequila?, while the cover of Continental Co-ets? ?I Don’t Love You No More? packs in ?Green Onions?-esque guitar waggle and a playful wolf howl. ?Killer Bee? plays up the dark, distorted side of surf rock, and the closing cover of ?Stengun? by Linda Van Dyck With Boo & The Booboos is the album’s noisiest moment, although even it makes the time and space to let the sounds reverberate.

Produced by Off the Hip Records? Mick Baty, who co-produced the first Frowning Clouds album, this debut bristles with tongue-in-cheek, self-aware brattiness. Recalling the rickety, no-frills garage-pop of late-?90s band The Rondelles, The Reprobettes start off with a promising retro shtick but proceed to take their songs much further by airing real-life annoyances and burning through their angst. Their sound may skip around the decades, but their concerns are just as vital today as they would have been in the ?60s – Johnny or no Johnny.
