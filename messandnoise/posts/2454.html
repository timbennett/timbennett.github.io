Rock?n?Roll Is Where I Hide

By Patrick 2011-04-15T00:00:00+00:00

On one level, the career of Dave Graney is one of perpetual re-invention: from small-town kid to post-punk expatriate, Wild Bill Hickok impersonator to velvet suit-wearing King of Pop and his contemporary guise as the irreverent and iconoclastic elder statesman of Australian rock’n?roll. But Graney is no Bowie-style chameleon. Graney’s shtick, he reminded us in a song some years ago, weighs a tonne. There’s no irony in being Dave Graney – he just is who he is. Period.

Graney’s last ?new? release (2010?s Supermodified* was an album of remixes) was the super-excellent *Knock Yourself Out* in 2009. The album didn’t restore Graney’s King of Pop crown, but it did remind anyone who cared to listen that he remains one of Australia’s most significant songwriters. For his latest album, *Rock’n?Roll Is Where I Hide, Graney and his current band The Lurid Yellow Mist have released what he describes as his ?third debut album?.

Like the earlier Supermodified* and *We Wuz Curious* albums, *Rock’n?Roll Is Where I Hide finds Graney revisiting many of his earlier tunes. With the exception of the final track, ?We Don’t Belong to Anybody?, each of the songs featured will be familiar to anyone who’s followed Graney’s career over the past 10 to 15 years: the title track, ?Night of the Wolverine?, ?Feelin? Kinda Sporty?, ?I’m Gonna Release Your Soul?, ?The Stars?, ?Baby?, ?Apollo 69? and ?I’m Not Afraid to Be Heavy?.

Graney’s approach to the versions on Rock’n?Roll Is Where I Hide is to unleash the proven talents of The Lurid Yellow Mist: drummer, wife and long-time collaborator Clare Moore; guitarist Stu Perera; bass player Stu Thomas; and keyboardist Mark Fitzgibbon.

In that context, the title track assumes a rugged exterior like the prize-fighter aesthetic loitering on the sidelines of Knock Yourself Out*, while ?Apollo 69? is stripped back to its constituent parts and re-assembled into a dirty cocktail blues narrative. ?Feelin? Kinda Sporty? is as powerful as ever – Clare Moore’s drum beats are to die for, again and again – and ?Night of the Wolverine? is Graney in his ideal-type soft-focus, crooning mode, replete with the odd squawk for sharp effect (a subsequent variation on that theme can be found with *Birds ?n? Goats).

With Perera’s guitar taking centre stage, ?A Man on the Make? re-appears as a classic Australian pop song, its lyrics the ideal foil for the mythology of the Australian male constructed, sanitised and exploited by the dominant discourse of parochial cultural institutions. On ?I’m Gonna Release Your Soul?, the Lurid Yellow Mist explodes into action, with Graney assuming the persona of funk-laden self-help performer; ?Three Dead Passengers? is refined to exploit its dark and sombre tone.

The final, and previously unreleased track ?We Don’t Belong to Anybody?, is the latest in Graney’s occasional semi-autobiographical canon (the album has been timed with the release of Graney’s memoir 1001 Australia Nights*). Led by his falsetto cry, it’s a narrative of Graney and Moore’s sojourn through the main roads and dark alleys of the music community (?Coral Snakes – deadly, but beautiful/White Buffaloes – demons and omens and signs/*Hashish and Liquor/I speak through a Lurid Yellow Mist?). Like so much of Graney’s work, the faint whiff of self-indulgence in the background is overpowered by wry lyrics and an idiosyncratic pop sensibility.

Where Graney’s 40-plus year journey will take him next is anybody’s guess – but you can expect the postcards to be just as impressive and as colourful as ever.
