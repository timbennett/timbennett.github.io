Martyr Privates

By Samson McDougall 2014-10-01T00:00:00+00:00

Martyr Privates? 2012 7? was like that unknown out-of-towner shredding your local skate bowl so hard it made you want to hang up your rollerblades. ?Bless? in particular is an earwormy, tormented explosion of psychedelic bliss. The good news is you’ll also find the song on the band’s self-titled debut LP.

Album opener ?Someone’s Head? cuts a similar trajectory – all pulsing repetition and grinding guitars – but its lyrical dullness sits a little too high in the mix. The song outstays its welcome, labouring away without the intent you’d expect from a band finally opening the door on an album release. Follow-up ?Gold Chew? doesn’t suffer the same blankness. When this tune swaggers in with its far more conventional song construction (i.e. it’s got, like, a ?chorus? and ?solo? and shit), it’s easy to forgive the opening-track letdown and just groove along to the bass. And when singer Cameron Hawes snaps his vocals out of drone mode, he blows the thing into the stratosphere. ?You Can’t Stop Progress? comes next and the band break the chains even further in a sunshower of bright guitars with almost buried vocals glimmering through.

?Toe the Plank? struggles to really get going and the next couple drag the record back towards monotone, bottoming out completely during the aptly titled ?Yawnin War?. The brilliant ?Bless? brings it back in an instant and via ?Rope and Tarp? the band finally hit the mark with a softer number, giving the thing the balance it’s been lacking to this point. Final tune ?Sores? works as an album closer, though it would struggle to shine amongst the greyscale of the record’s midsection. Again it’s Hawes’s variation in vocal tone that lifts the song out of a dirge – reinforcing what a powerful weapon his voice can be when he chooses to use it.

This is solid rock music, but the superior quality of three or four of the tracks here, and definitely their earlier tunes, suggest Martyr Privates could’ve done a bit more. Stripping out the middle ground (both literally and figuratively) would’ve made for one hell of an EP rather than an album that feels a bit thin in parts. Is the only problem that they set the bar too high from the outset? Probably. The two-three punch of ?Gold Chew? and ?You Can’t Stop Progress? – plus a substantial third act – prove Martyr Privates have some super songs behind the big riffs and that they’re capable of pushing it much further.
