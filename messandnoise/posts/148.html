A Load Of Hot AIR?

By Trevor 2008-11-25T00:00:00+00:00

TREVOR BLOCK reports from the annual awards of the Australian Independent Record Labels Association, where the performances overshadowed the gongs themselves – but not the canapes.


There’s an unusual vibe at Melbourne’s Corner Hotel tonight (November 24). The whole place has been booked out for the AIR Awards, and despite the solid indie cred of the event, there’s a distinct whiff of industry here. People rarely seen at live shows mingle at the (free) bar and jostle for the (excellent) canapes. BlackBerrys are tickled, cards and numbers exchanged, while the medicinal smell of Jagermeister fills the air.

It’s a plush set-up, with one stage as the awards podium and the main showcase stage flanked by video screens showing clips of the various nominees. The selection and running order of the acts chosen to play turns out to be a good form guide for winners.

Of the awards themselves, well, Theatre Of Disco were robbed, and Eddy Current Supression Ring’s actual award (for Primary Colours as best hard rock/punk album) was smashed before it could be handed over, so one of Geoffrey Gurrumul Yunupingu’s three statuettes is substituted for the formalities. It’s odd to see all (seven? eight?) members of the Herd pile up to collect their trophy and surprising that Little Red leave empty handed. The balance between talk, presentations and performance is well handled, though, and surely this evening is mainly about performance.

The live sets are short and focused. The Getaway Plan get to play two tracks, which no doubt you will be hearing on Triple J very soon. Lone country girl Felicity Urquhart’s presence explains the cowboy hats in the crowd, though with a solo voice and an acoustic guitar she battles to be heard above the chatter. The Drones take the stage with Martha Wainwright and deliver two covers: ?How Do You Mend A Broken Heart?? by the Bee Gees and an early Nick Cave number, the title of which eludes me.

There’s an air of anticipation around Eddy Current’s appearance. Closing the night, they are greeted by a wall of photographers two-deep across the width of the stage, instead of the usual moshpit. From a cold start, they raise the pace of proceedings several notches with the opening chords of ?Sunday’s Coming?, and race through a 20-minute set, which runs out a bit longer than planned. Singer Brendan defies the celebrity thing, spending most of the time with his back to the crowd. It’s a rare treat to be able to watch and listen to them properly, without being mauled. They end with a razor sharp rendition of ?Which Way to Go?, a quick thanks, and are gone.

It might be possible to question the value of these awards as an end in themselves, but as an event, they work well.
