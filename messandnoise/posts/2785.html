Embassy Caf?

By Anthony Carew 2013-07-09T00:00:00+00:00

Given Andras Fox was barely out of the womb when Dream 2 Science dropped the classic deep-house minimalism of his 1990 self-titled LP, his obsessive love of the electronic sounds of a quarter-century ago has very little to do with cultural nostalgia – that I-love-the-?90s retromania that’s currently turning every outdoor rock festival into a parade of reformations and albums-played-in-order. (As someone who suffered through the ?90s, through corporate grunge, Britpop, nu-metal, $30 jewel cases, dial-up and Australia-as-isolated-backwater, this nostalgia feels borderline reprehensible.) Instead, Fox identifies with the essential melancholy that lingers in proto-house recordings, an airy looseness and casual contentedness that makes their stripped-down, live-sound productions seem a world away from the sporty machismo of dubstep’s bass-revving, car-culture competitiveness.

Embassy Caf?* marks Fox’s first release under the iconic-singular, after a sample-collagist solo album as Andras Fox, *Daydreaming*, and another record, *Taboo, as half of the duo Fox + Sui (which sets his loopy productions against the vocals of Sui Zhen). The LP is released on Dutch funk imprint Dopeness Galore, giving it casual internationalism, and Fox openly credits Larry Heard, Frankie Knuckles and Kenny Dixon Jr. as inspirations for its live beatmaking and analogue sound.

Whilst it may be more meaningful, in both spiritual and trainspotting ways, to someone who knows Mr. Fingers from Fingers Inc., all this talk of the house of yore shouldn’t suggest that this is some musical iteration of inside baseball; there an accessibility to Embassy Caf??s tasteful minimalism that crests on the recurring cuts that feature Oscar Slorach-Thorn (Oscar + Martin, Oscar Key Sung, Brothers Hand Mirror, etc.) on vocals.

On ?What They Say? Slorach-Thorn is there to offer the soulfully sung hook (and evoke what Fox calls house’s ?marginalised, black, queer? past), Fox whittling down his repeated refrain from ?You’ve got to know what they say? to a simple trigger of ?say? as, contrastingly, the spartan programming picks up jazzy organ runs and phased horn stabs as it progresses. ?Running Late? is the big standout, however: its minimalist boom-tish the bed for a buoyant pop song made melancholy by a transient one-finger piano motif and some heart-laid-bare, love-turned-tenuous vocals. Slorach-Thorn’s sentiments (?Though we are suffering/we sure fit together?) consecrate the dancefloor as intimate space, a forum for singing the things the things you otherwise can’t quite say (?Who’s to say/there’s not someone else out there/for you and me??).

?Dance music? is often understood – and pursued – with dumb functionality, but getting feet on the floor is clearly low on Fox’s list of ambitions. This comes not just in a song like ?Running Late,? where the confessional sadness stands at odds with casting inhibitions off, but in productions like ?Slow Dope? and ?Rhythm Ace?, whose BPM barely bumps a resting pulse. The former is a slippery, sinuous slink through synth squiggles; the latter assembles synthesised birdcalls, doorbell chimes and ringing phones like an ersatz take on Matthew Herbert’s realist John Cage-ism – Around the House?s literalised house music reduced to plastic diorama.

There, and elsewhere, Embassy Caf? is an album with a careful ear for space and gentle sensibility. A house 12-inch with not a single hard-pounding beat.
