The Sunset Park

By Doug Wallen 2011-07-01T00:00:00+00:00

There’s a fine line between iconic and cliche, and the Aerial Maps rip it to shreds on this second album. Leaning heavily on an overarching narrative, The Sunset Park takes us through pub violence, endless chain stores, desolate caravan parks, and other grim features of the Australian landscape. Ambitious though it is, it also indulges in stubborn colloquialisms and mythologises the mundane to the point of caricature. As if that weren’t risky enough in a nation known for its cultural cringe, the band runs through eight- and nine-minute songs, restless musical scene changes, and the portentous spoken-word storytelling of poet/frontman Adam Gibson.

A challenging listen, then, and one that doesn’t seem to invite repeated visits. But once the shock of the approach – both the lack of traditional singing and the bleak portrayal of Australia’s underclass – is behind us, the strengths of the band rise to the surface. Supplying the music to Gibson’s narrative are Simon Holmes (Hummingbirds, Fragile) and Sean Kennedy – each juggling about a dozen instruments – and a wide cast of guest players. (Holmes also produces.) The music leaps from folky and poppy to eerie and cinematic to kitschy and flat-out rocking.

The Aerial Maps – The Sunset Park by popboomerang

As for Gibson, he has a great voice, razor-sharp and urgent in its humanity. But there’s something smarmy about him playing the voice of the neglected, especially when he can’t help but romanticise the very harsh reality he exposes. Following the path of beer-swilling, woman-beating lead character Scotty Bennett (among others), Gibson delves into his own conflicted thoughts on Aussie life and veers all over in terms of tone. As the sprawling album nears its finish, ?Beer Glorious Beer? is partly a film noir-style punishment of its villain and partly a jaunty pub-folk punch line.

The Aerial Maps – Salvation Road (Single) – 01 Salvation Road by popboomerang

Flaws and all, though, there’s an incredible boldness to the Aerial Maps. Applying intense social commentary to the mode of a concept album, the band doubles down on earnestness and ambition. If the words can induce cringes and the story can seem contrived, the music is brightly catchy and wildly experimental. Everything is up for grabs – from the keyboards and programmed beats of the instrumental centrepiece ?Kamilaroi Highway? to the dance-punk itch of ?The Border Towns?. Gibson can inevitably come off preachy and his observations pedestrian, but the music mostly stands strong while the album gels better than it has any right to.

Literally traveling from one side of the country to the other, The Sunset Park surveys an Australia neglected by so many bands. (Most don’t even embrace their accents, let alone their culture). Whether the years ahead prove it a cult classic or a mere curiosity, it grabs at the potential of music and stories with both hands. Rather than aspire to scene credibility or radio rotation, the Aerial Maps are out to capture the land around them and share it – even if that means vastly limiting their audience.
