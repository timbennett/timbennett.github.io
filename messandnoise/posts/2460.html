Winters Blade

By Shaun 2011-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

Unless you’re intimately familiar with Lakes and/or really blunted, most of it will probably sound the same. Even on Winters Blade, where the hermetically sealed world of Melbourne’s Sean Bailey is slit open a fraction to allow Jon Michell to enter and clean the mix up a bit, he’s still sounding as uncompromisingly tapped into his cryptic world as ever. The tom-happy drums still trace a line among tangential eastern-scale riffs, the lyrics trade in charred fairy-dust mysticism (where you can understand them), and the resulting mood never lifts from a prevailing sense of impending conflict, like the rallying cries before a war.

Still, not many bands in Australia have as distinctive a sound as this, and it’d be remiss to expect Lakes to take a left-turn into … well, anything else. But along with the clearer production, with its sharp yet reverberated percussion and less gravelly electric guitar sound, comes a greater emphasis on Lakes? song structures: You won’t have to fight hard to identify where a verse becomes a chorus, for example. The added clarity also offers a sense of sonic space previously missing from Lakes? records: ?Waves and Glyphs? plumbs guttural bass-depths that might otherwise be levelled by 4-track grit. When Bailey sings of ?falling over the edge of time? it’s like you’re tumbling into a gorge as deep and dismal as the bass line.

Similarly, the title track is accompanied by an arctic keyboard line reminiscent of the more folksy, Tolkien-inspired fringes of black metal: It never deviates from the melody set out by the guitar, but instead imparts a sense of spaciousness, like you’re trapped in a frostbitten blizzard. It’s these small cosmetic touches that become important upon repeated listens. They’re the components that eventually emerge to define one song from another.

And it’s important to closely listen for these moments, because whether you’re a Lakes tragic or not it must be acknowledged that most Lakes songs do kinda sound the same. Working with the material, inhabiting the world and letting your imagination unravel the initially impenetrable songs is a crucial and enjoyable investment you have to undertake. But despite the welcome cleanliness of its production, Winters Blade will do nothing to convince non-fans. Lakes is just doing what Lakes does, take it or leave it.
