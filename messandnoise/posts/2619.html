Taedium Vitae

By Doug Wallen 2012-04-01T00:00:00+00:00

Lindsay Phillips? second album opens with the packing of a casket and closes with a dance over graves and a toast to the end of days. Two years on from Varning*, the Melbourne songwriter has lost none of his gallows gloom. Or hasn’t he? Phillips may meditate again and again on the passing of all things (?Moments grow over moments as moss to stone?) and *Taedium Vitae may mean ?the weariness of life? in Latin, but this may also be the sweetest album about death you’ll hear anytime soon.

Yes, Phillips appears somehow happy, even as he evokes Leonard Cohen with his grave baritone and poetic, almost Biblical lyrics. It’d be easy to credit producer Myles Mumford and a newfound cast of guest musicians – Varning was notably barebones – but it’s Phillips himself that seems optimistic. He grounds ?Sans Light? in lovely guitar picking, while his voice turns more to comforting than cautioning on ?Only You (And the Lord)?. You would think a song called ?The Stitches Come Undone? could only go one way, but it’s warmed with Mellotron and harmonica as Phillips reminds, ?If they’d held together tightly/There’d be no purpose for a song.?

Only You (And The Lord) by showoffservices

The instrumentation is understated in the best way, manifesting in whatever minimal or layered strokes are needed for each song. Sousaphone adds a creepy note to opener ?Of Abandonment?, but it’s also strangely cheering on the memorable single ?Exile?, as is Phillip’s trademark vocal chirrup. Hammond organ sooths amid such lines as ?Like the maker I give life and I take away? on ???But a Sacrifice Away??, and Andrew Keese’s vocals echo Phillips? for stirring effect on ?The Wolves Close In?, a fatherly devotional that’s got crime and punishment in its margins.

Aside from a few mentions of highways and other means of modern transport, these songs could pass for medieval standards or equally aged fables. ?We’re a Mystery? opens with a vocal call that’s more pure sound than language (at least English), as if Phillips is breaking in his voice for the tale to come. That voice, by the way, is more nimble than you’d expect on ?Choking on the Ashes of Bridges You’ve Burned? and other shadowy terrain. It’s not bogged down in the troubles it details, but more observing and empathising. Maybe that’s why the closing ?Martyrs & Saints?, with its instruction ?Let us drink to decay and the end of all days?, remains a plucky waltz.

Alas, Phillips is headed to Sweden indefinitely after the launch of this album. (Varning* is Swedish for ?warning? and ?Covenant? opens with spoken word by Emelie Pettersson in that language.) But just as he teased *Taedium Vitae* as *Varning was being released, the sleeve here promises a third album called Necropolis. So Phillips won’t be too far afield, from us or his favourite subject.
