Kites

By Doug Wallen 2013-11-05T00:00:00+00:00

We’ve heard Karl Smith’s songs stripped to minimal backing as well as bolstered with full-band vigour, thanks to the back-from-the-dead Sodastream and short-lived Lee Memorial, respectively. The Melbourne-based songwriter’s first album under his own name dwells somewhere in between, spilling over into other terrain along the way. It’s identifiably Smith every time he opens his mouth to sing, but it may still surprise longtime fans all the same.

The most important thing to know is that this isn’t just an offloading of solo material in between other projects. The way Kites* starts and finishes and progresses, it’s a thought-out *album more than a scattershot collection. It sees Smith more confident than ever, both in lyrics and arrangements, and even more expressive with his sharp, leaping voice than usual. It also sees him turn more to keyboard instruments, from the piano hook of the stormy ?Little Lucy? to the claggy chords of ?I Know Julia? to the jaunty procession opening ?Crotchets?.

Whether it’s his newfound fatherhood or simply working under his name that spurred these developments, it’s an admirable step forward. Look no further than the opening ?After Mr Morrison? to observe the ambition on display here: Smith gulps like Hank Williams as violin, cello, trumpet and trombone gather around him and the song transforms into discordant rock partway through. As lovely and lullaby-ish as the single ?I Want You? is in that familiar Sodastream way, that first song is bigger and more troubled. ?I’ve made big mistakes/I’ve swum with rattlesnakes,? Smith sings, ?And tumbled out the other side.?

Despite the solo guise being a new setting for him, he feels free on Kites. Free to get all sloshing and thunderous with distortion on the Lee Memorial-esque ?Hang Our Bodies?, free to entwine with the fireside drawl of Teeth & Tongue’s Jess Cornelius on ?Slow Short Story?, free to stretch out in a patient instrumental start for the closing ?Everything?. Free to dabble in arrangements that do a lot with a little, as on ?Glass Eye?, and, well, arrangements that do a lot with a lot.

Freest of all are the words. Whether he’s purging himself of ?ghosts and fears and doubts? on that last song or riffing on ?reruns and sitcoms and Westerns and rayguns? on ?Crotchets?, there’s a stream-of-consciousness outpour to it all, even as the craftsmanship stands as sturdy and studied as ever. Recording and mixing the songs himself, Smith sits in direct control here – and flourishes in the role.
