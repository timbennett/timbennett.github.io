Outlands

By Doug Wallen 2012-03-01T00:00:00+00:00

There’s no shortage of young Australian bands wearing ?60s influences on their sleeves. Sydney quintet Deep Sea Arcade are especially unabashed, name-checking a long list of forebears in the press kit for their debut album. One song alone tips its hat to The Doors, The Zombies, The Monkees and the title song from a Peter Sellers film.
This particular revisiting of the past – part British Invasion, part Britpop – is spot-on in and of itself, but what elevates Outlands above mere pastiche is the songs? basic hardiness and even more so the wonderfully cloudy production.

These may be head-bobbing, jangle-friendly tunes steeped in familiarity, but they’re given rich, woozy depth. There’s a great unity of feel to it all, despite piecemeal origins that include tracking in Sydney by Simon Berkfinger (Philadelphia Grand Jury) and Tim Whitten (Augie March, The Go-Betweens); sessions at home; and mixing in the UK by Dan Grech-Marguerat (The Vaccines, The Kooks) and in the US by Doug Boehm (Girls, The Vines).

Steam by DeepSeaArcade

Amid the band’s ever-shuffling reference points, maybe we can chalk up the consistency here to co-writers, singer Nic Mckenzie and bassist Nick Weaver, who’ve been collaborating since they were teens. No matter what the inspirations – whether Portishead, DJ Shadow and French New Wave sci-fi gems Alphaville* and *Fahrenheit 451 on the Air-esque title track, or the bygone Manchester scene and The Smiths elsewhere – it always ends up sounding like the same band.

Granted, if flashes of Leisure*-era Blur (?Girls?), The Turtles (?Don’t Be Sorry?) and a slightly warped, John Lennon-like delivery (?Together?) don’t appeal to you, *Outlands may not either. But there’s a lot of charm in how Deep Sea Arcade bubble-wrap their anachronistic guitar-pop for soft impact, be it with echo-y effects or handclaps and harmonies. The super catchy ?Granite City? and ?Lonely in Your Arms? are exuberant rave-ups delivered from a dreamy remove; ?Ride? achieves narcotic slowness; while wispy closer ?Airbulance? drifts off with the question, ?Am I wasting my time??

File Deep Sea Arcade alongside likeminded contemporaries Papercuts and Richard in Your Mind; bands that offset the potential kitsch of their ?60s fix with sterling arrangements and a shuffling sureness.
