Twerps

By Steph Kretowicz 2011-10-14T00:00:00+00:00

To some, the dream of a slacker utopia for the unrefined, flannel-toting stoner disappeared with the ?90s. Still, there are places – surrounded by suburbs and untouched by the rest of the world – where pockets devoted to the life of the loafer exist. Melbourne’s Twerps and those like them are part of that disheveled niche singing songs about getting drunk, high and stoned. Whether that’s a fact is yours to ponder, but those are the key words that make up most of the chorus for slacker manifesto ?Who Are You? (note the veteran distinction between ?uppers? and ?downers?) on Twerps? self-titled album debut. That song’s closing chorale makes up the crux of the band’s lethargic mythology.

After years of local and international releases on tape and vinyl since forming in 2008, Twerps have undergone the limp version of an overhaul – making cuts in places, additions in others. Most notably, they’ve dropped the ?the? and stuck with the one syllable for their band name, while lifting the low fidelity haze of recording and allowing their songs to breathe. Guitarist Julia McFarlane was a standout fixture during her Batrider days, even if her stiff, barefooted onstage presence seemed at odds with the thrashing intensity of the New Zealand-born nomads. With Twerps, though, she’s been thriving, taking a penchant for the jangling post-punk guitar rhythms of her Kiwi forebears The Clean and applying them to the evocative amble of Twerps opening track ?Dreamin?. Meanwhile, improved comfort levels see her take the lead in vocals and songwriting for the self-deprecating cheek of ?This Guy?.

Where producer Jack Farley (Beaches, St Helens) has driven away the dulling over-saturation of previous reverb-heavy recordings, the band have compensated with organs from Panel of Judges? Dion Nania and a progressive standout track in the heavy effects of ?Grow Old?. That song resembles a backyard version of Brian Eno’s ?Deep Blue Day?, while front man Martin Frawley’s usually overwrought man-boy vocal reveals its baritone side.

For all the tidiness, though, some things never scrub off and you can bet Twerps like to stay loose. There’s the crude vulgarity that features in Frawley’s chosen self-expression as he whines, ?You give me the shits and you bore me to bits?, in the ghostly trundle of ?Anything New? – sharing a similar sentiment to the ska-inspired Tenants song, ?You Shit Me To Tears?.

But while Twerps come across as a band with preoccupations that appear quintessentially Australian, there’s a universality of themes and timeless melody that resonate. The unearthly grunge of Kurt Vile surfaces in a grand breakdown in ?Don’t Be Surprised?, while global comparisons run from US surf pop labelmates Real Estate (both are signed to Underwater Peoples in the US) to the folk contemplation of Gentle Despite from Leeds.

Ultimately, Twerps are a band with the ability to split opinion evenly. Unstable compositions – reflecting unstable lives, perhaps – and thoroughly lived-in songs could be both a source of love or resentment. It probably just depends which side of the tracks you’re sitting on.
