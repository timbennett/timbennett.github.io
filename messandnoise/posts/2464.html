Grit Aglow

By Hannah Brooks 2011-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

Exactly how long it took Sydney’s Megastick Fanfare to complete their debut is unclear. Work began about two years ago, maybe longer. What’s clear is that it wasn’t supposed to take this long: the intention was to record over a few months at most, in the band’s rehearsal space with friend Frey Lindsay co-producing. Yet at some point outside factors such as parents, university and a manager moving to Mongolia all became part of the story.

With the ever-elusive album not ready until now, MF have built a profile based on live shows and a split 7? with fellow Sydney-siders ky?. Championed by triple j, the band has moved in a few short years from playing Of Montreal covers in their bedroom to being hailed as the Next Big Thing to come out of Sydney’s ?burgeoning psych-pop scene?.

As an album, Grit Aglow is a strong release, brimming with ideas and enthusiasm that mostly the band pull off. That said, another side-effect of over-stretching an album’s release is that sometimes the end result can cease to be relevant to the band themselves. At worst, it can come off sounding slightly stale; at best, they can find themselves represented by a sound they have grown out of. Mixed by Jonathan Boulet, the 10 songs, which were written somewhere between mid-2008 and mid-2009, succeed as a document of a time in the band’s early life. Yet the album as a whole shows its fractures and through that their progression and evolution as a band.

teething by megastickfanfare

The slow-building opener ‘Meconium’ stretches its arms, warms up and takes a deep breath before flexing its muscles, Zumba-style, around the two-minute mark. From there the album fluctuates between percussive, tribal pop and indie incantations topped off with a whole lot of delay. The songs are climactic, stretching their pop confines to the limit before pressing pulse on the blender. Overlapping harmonies are boyish and earnest while as a whole the vocals rely on repetition of sounds instead of words to convey meaning, giving it an esoteric rather than lyrical quality.

It’s difficult not to mention two bands of obvious influence, consciously or not. Sounding distinctly Vampire Weekend-ish at points, such as on ‘Good ?er’, and Animal Collective at their weirdest, it’s obvious – given Megastick’s median age – they belong to a generation raised on Strawberry Jam as much as Weet-Bix. And while that’s not a bad thing, the band does sound strongest when these influences are subsumed. When they find their own groove and stick to it, as on the moody ‘Do What You Like’ and ‘T is a P’, it works. Quieter moments like ‘Piano Song/Next Stop ~~~~~’ provide respite from the desperate, almost vomitous vocals of the earlier tracks, giving a glimpse into what could be if this quintet calmed it down a notch.

megastick fanfare – june strangelets by othertongues

What emerges most strongly is a sense of Megastick Fanfare as a band, in the proper sense of the word, with a collective identity. There are no dominant personalities present or individual idiosyncrasies leaking through: it simply sounds like five guys enjoying the collaborative experience of making music. In that sense Grit Aglow is a party record, albeit an odd one, and what it lacks in heart it makes up for in fun. As age and experience darken their abandon as individuals and a group it will be interesting to hear what becomes of them. For now, Megastick Fanfare have produced an ambitious debut but one that ultimately sounds like a band looking in the mirror and still seeing someone else.
