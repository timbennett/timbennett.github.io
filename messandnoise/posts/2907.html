Loopholes

By Mitchell Judge 2014-06-02T00:00:00+00:00

For better or worse, The Murlocs have been living in the shadow of their big brother King Gizzard & The Lizard Wizard since their existence. In almost every promotion, review or news piece about the band, King Gizzard are mentioned as a way of adding familiarity or reminding us that they share a harmonica player (Ambrose Kenny-Smith). It means that The Murlocs have probably gained fans through this association, but they also struggle to be recognised as their own entity. It is this intrinsic link between the bands, similar to Pond’s relationship with Tame Impala, which will continue to define them.

Naturally there will be comparisons between the two groups, and right now the differences could not be more pronounced. Since each band?s debut EP, which saw them playing a similar style of psych blues, Gizzard have shifted through an array of genres while The Murlocs have honed in on their singular sound. I doubt it is a conscious reaction to the constantly changing mindset of King Gizzard, as The Murlocs would be paying the least attention to these comparisons, but it highlights the different purposes of the two bands.

The deciding factor in enjoying this album is ultimately Ambrose Kenny-Smith’s voice. While no doubt providing a passionate performance across the 10 tracks, his tendency to sing every* line in a faux-American pained yell can be a little grating. The wailing harmonica, which has become a trademark, acts merely as a break between his next howl. *Loopholes succeeds when the band are given more of the spotlight, as on ?Evolve & Absorb? and ?Serial Imposter?.

What is most remarkable about Loopholes* is how cohesive it sounds, given that it was recorded over a period of three years, with the finished product lost twice along the way. No song deviates too far from the standard format they establish, even the longer title track that closes the album. If you’re a fan of The Murlocs, you know what to expect across the full 10 tracks. *Loopholes won’t surprise, and it doesn’t quite satisfy either. It leaves you wanting something a bit more.
