Homosapien

By Barnaby Smith 2013-02-13T00:00:00+00:00

As brilliant as PVT’s previous three albums were, they have until now sounded somewhat faceless, particularly on 2010?s Church With No Magic. It’s not that the music was cold (Warp signed them after all – a vindication of the seismic depth of their sound), but the human touch has been hard to locate. The futuristic, metallic production made everything somewhat robotic and mathematical, with Richard Pike’s vocals serving as a non-essential tool in that mix rather than an expressive instrument.

It’s different now, of course. As the new album’s name suggests, the Sydney trio have opted to open up and ally their crunching synth-rock with vulnerability and emotion. The familiar dark tones and thudding bass are not entirely sacrificed, it should be said – they just play second fiddle this time. This new concoction is encapsulated magnificently on ?Love & Defeat?, with Pike’s triumphant melody keeping an uncompromisingly gruff electronic track in check. Coming with this softer approach is some very fine songwriting and exceptional hooks, producing the highly disconcerting fact that it is possible to actually sing along with some of these songs.

Love & Defeat by PVT

Predictably, the subject matter has turned introspective and to matters of the heart. In its directness and apparent romantic sincerity, Homosapien is reminiscent of the evolution between Yeasayer’s first and second albums. Perhaps it’s the fact they are no longer under the Warp banner, and all the baggage of expectation that comes with that, but on ?Cold Romance? and the exceptional ?Nightfall? they explore well-worn romantic lyrical tropes, with the innovation of the percussive, textured tracks beneath preventing any clich? or sentiment.

If this album is ultimately about one thing, it’s Pike’s singing. His voice complements the overwhelmingly dark electronic bass that is PVT’s foundation – the influence of Depeche Mode is not slackening off. Yet Pike exhibits a smart, articulate and poppy manner of singing his band’s straightforward yet poetic words that veers him towards, in all seriousness, Duran Duran’s Simon Le Bon. The vocals feel like they have been laboured over much more than on previous albums, meaning Homosapien is full of clarity. It is accessible and memorable.

Cold Romance by PVT

But it is no masterpiece. Lag comes with the woeful and rather juvenile ?Electric?, sounding like a pastiche from The Mighty Boosh, while the pulsating smash-and-grab of ?Casual Success? jars with the deliberateness and wide open spaces of the rest of the record.

Those who have followed PVT from their beginnings and through the Warp years may deem that the trio have lost something, that this new openness detracts from their mystique. But free from the shackles of that iconic label, they have arrived at a sound that feels more honest and less like they are scratching about in some murky sonic apocalypse. By far their most fleshy and rounded LP to date, it’s almost as if they’re saying goodbye to an old identity and forging a breathy, sensuous new band.
