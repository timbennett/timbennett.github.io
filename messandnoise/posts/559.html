2010: The Year In News Pt 1

By Darren Levin 2011-01-04T13:00:00+00:00

Pub closures, protests, tragic deaths and a little band called Circle Pit – we look back on an eventful first half of 2010. Tomorrow, the last six months of the year.

January


St Kilda Farewells Favourite Son Howard: Five-hundred mourners pay tribute to Rowland S Howard at his funeral at the Sacred Heart Mission in St Kilda, just up the street from where he played his last ever gig. Howard is eulogised by long-time friend and partner Genevieve McGuckin, brother and bandmate Harry Howard, filmmaker Paul Goldman and collaborator Mick Harvey. In the weeks following his death, M+N?s Trevor Block approaches many of his friends and former bandmates for written tributes.

Pyramid’s Stormy Weather: A severe electrical storm forces the closure of the main stage at the Pyramid Rock Festival in Phillip Island, Victoria, on New Year’s Eve. Empire Of The Sun, Grinspoon, The Butterfly Effect and Van She stay in their caravans, and people totally lose their shit over the lack of a countdown. Organisers respond with, ?It could’ve been worse.?

All Bets Off At The Tote: Proprietor Bruce Milne issues a press release announcing the sudden closure of iconic Melbourne venue The Tote. In an interview with M+N, Milne blames ?high risk? licensing conditions, including increased fees and security requirements. More than 1000 people stage an impromptu protest, and the pub closes with a final flurry of bands on January 18 including an unforgettable performance of GOD’s ?My Pal? by Joel Silbersher and The Drones.

From Arthouse To Outhouse: Still reeling from the closure of The Tote, news breaks that The Arthouse, which has hosted punk and indie shows in North Melbourne for the past 19 years, would be closing its doors in May 2011. Speaking to M+N, management blames
liquor licensing regulations and a dispute with the landlord.

Perth’s Hydey Goes Quietly Into The Night: Not to be outdone by events on the east coast, Perth’s Hyde Park Hotel stops hosting gigs following a large-scale renovation. There are fears that when the front bar eventually re-opens, live music will not be a feature.

Laneway On The Move: Following 2009?s year to forget, the St Jerome’s Laneway Festival successfully shifts to Footscray in Melbourne and Rozelle in Sydney. UK headliners Echo & The Bunnymen do their best to throw a spanner in the works by missing a flight, but they’re replaced by The Dirty Three in Brisbane and The Midnight Juggernauts in Melbourne.

Splitting Up: After two albums, three EPs and more than 260 shows in 13 countries, Brisbane’s I Heart Hiroshima announce they’re taking a break; Melbourne experimental rock trio Grey Daturas call it quits after nine years; and short-lived quartet The Diamond Sea play their final two shows in Sydney.

February


Melbourne’s SLAM Dunk: A reported 20,000 people march from the State Library of Victoria to Parliament House in protest of Melbourne’s liquor licensing regime. An accord is signed on the eve of the rally, which promises to find a solution to the problem within six months.

Greed At Work In ?Kookaburra? Case: The Federal Court rules that Men At Work’s 1982 hit ?Down Under? reproduced a substantial part of folk standard ?Kookaburra Sits in the Old Gum Tree?. Songwriter Colin Hay describes the claim as ?opportunistic greed?.

We Need A New Proxy!: Following the closure of The Tote, the Victorian Government meets with representatives from Melbourne’s live music scene to try and nip the ?live music and alcohol-related violence? issue in the bud. Despite some ?quiet optimism?, little is resolved. Occasional M+N columnist and participant Dave Graney takes minutes for us.

Comeback Kings: Melbourne’s The Paradise Motel return after a 10-year hiatus with concept album Australian Ghost Story*; while Sydney’s Crow announce details of a new album, *Arcane*, their first since 1993?s Steve Albini-produced *My Kind of Pain.

Melbourne’s Birmy – Too Darn Loud?: The thorny issue of first amenity rights resurfaces after The Birmingham Hotel is rocked by noise complaints from a neighbouring property. An online petition circulates casting doubt on the future of gigs at the pub. Owners decide to renovate.

Lunchbox Launches: M+N launches its Lunchbox gig series at 1000 – Bend in Melbourne’s CBD with a free performance by Denim Owl.

In Memoriam: Acclaimed singer, songwriter and Black Arm Band instigator Ruby Hunter dies suddenly at her home in southwest Victoria, aged 54.

Splitting Up: Sydney’s Ghosts of Television meet an ?organic end? (obituary here); and Melbourne outfit Deloris call it a day after 11 years and four albums of ?varying degrees of worth?.

March


The AMP Takes Gender Bias Seriously: Lisa Mitchell wins the $30,000 Australian Music Prize, prompting claims she won by ?by default? and because of an article bemoaning the lack of female nominees. Patron and sometime M+N writer Chris Johnston issues a riposte.

Lost Weekend Lives Up To Its Name: After shifting venues and losing a day, Brisbane’s inaugural Lost Weekend festival eventually succumbs to poor tickets sales. It’s a surprising development given the strength of the line-up (Dinosaur Jr, The Brian Jonestown Massacre, The Drones).

Philly Jays Decamp To London: After inking a deal with Too Pure, Philadelphia Grand Jury follow in the footsteps of The Temper Trap, Snowman and pretty much every other Australian band with ambition when they announce their relocation to the UK. Singer Berkfinger later laments the decision in an interview, describing Britain as ?broken?.

Brisbane Says ?No? To Lockout: OK, so it was no SLAM Rally, but 600 Brisbane music lovers gather outside Queensland Parliament House to protest a proposed 12am lockout. Organisers claim the reduced trading hours would have significant financial implications for venues and tarnish Brisbane’s reputation as destination for touring bands.

In Memoriam: The music community mourns the death of HTRK bassist Sean Stewart, who took his own life at the age of 29. In a statement issued to M+N, band members Jonnine Standish and Nigel Yang pay tribute to his ?strange and beautiful? character. Uncompromisingly unique Melbourne songwriter Pip Proud dies after a long battle with throat cancer. Dave Nichols pays tribute

Splitting Up: St Helens call time on their one-album career after frontman Jarrod Quarrell decides to devote his energies to solo project Lost Animal. In an interview with M+N he says the decision wasn’t made lightly: ?It was a gut feeling that wouldn’t go away.?

April


Finger Pulled: Powderfinger, Australia’s greatest proponents of middle-of-the-road rock, announce their final tour at a press conference in Sydney. The band describe their seventh album Golden Rule* as their most ?complete and satisfying?; *M+N?s JP Hammond vigorously disagrees.

Petition Signed, Sealed, Delivered: A petition calling for the delinking of live music and alcohol-related violence is delivered to the Victorian Government. Drafted in response to the Tote’s closure, the petition is signed by more than 22,000 people.

GFC Claims LTI: After seven years and 95 releases, beloved Melbourne indie Low Transit Industries bows out on a high note with the release of Kim Salmon & The Surrealists first LP in over a decade, Grand Unifying Theory. Founders Darren Smallman and Simon Baird blame the changing nature of the industry and global financial pressures.

Tote Lives Again: After some behind-the-scenes negotiations, Bar Open/Yah Yah’s/Pony proprietors Jon Perring, Andrew Portokallis and Sam Crupi announce the imminent reopening of The Tote at a low-key press conference. Victorian premier John Brumby is there, presumably for a photo op.

The Shame Cauldron Ignites: Sydney’s Circle Pit stoke the fires with a provocative interview; Whitley keeps them burning by saying Melbourne pisses him off; and Violet Soho toast marshmallows on it by hitting out against claims they’re little more than grunge revivalists.

Rubber Says Sayonara To The CD: Melbourne-based indie label Rubber Records stops selling CDs in retail stores, shifting their operations online. ?Physical retail distribution is dictated by a business model that no longer works for either the customer, the artist or the label,? Rubber MD David Vodicka says in a statement.

May


Pivot Is Dead, Long Live PVT: Sydney’s Pivot relent to pressures from a US nu-metal act and change their name to PVT. ?The potential legal expense of fighting this was far too great and really not worth the risk for us,? the band says in a statement.

Another Festival Apology: Following Pyramid’s weather troubles, Blueprint’s very existence and the disappearance of Brisbane’s Lost Weekend, Groovin? The Moo organisers issue an apology for reported three-hour delays at their Bendigo event.

Tame Impala Go Shopping For Synths: Just days away from the release of their debut, Innerspeaker, Tame Impala’s Kevin Parker tells us he’s more excited about its synth-based follow-up. ?I’ve been buying a couple of analog synthesisers on eBay,? he says. ?They’re so much fun, they’re really taking the music to another level.?

Sianna Lee Tells All: The former Love Outside Andromeda frontwoman sits down on the therapist’s couch with Craig Mathieson, baring all about her band’s dissolution, her battle with an eating disorder and her debut solo album Phoenix Propeller.

Baily And Kuepper Reunite: One of rock’s most combustible couples – or so we’re lead to believe – reunite for a series of residencies in Brisbane, Sydney and Melbourne.

Splitting Up: Deaf Wish announce a ?final? show at Yah Yah’s in Melbourne (it proves to be a ruse: they reunite for third album Mercy in October); and Sydney’s Talons ?amicably? call time on their one-album career.

June


Vale Adam D Mills: The M+N community is rocked by the death of contributor and Sound&Fury records founder Adam D Mills, who tragically passes away aged 29. Mills is feted by a written tribute pieced together by fellow writers Shaun Prescott and Anwyn Crawford.

A Decade For The Dale: Sydney’s Annandale Hotel celebrates 10 years since the Rule brothers replaced pokies with live music with a series of performances by the likes of The Vines, Cloud Control, Gareth Liddiard and Jimmy Barnes. Andrew Ramadge gets sentimental in the back of a taxi, while Dan Rule tells us about his plans for the pub’s future.

Big In Spain: A placement in a popular beer commercial lands Melbourne indie-pop outfit The Triangles an unlikely #1 hit in Spain. Their song ?Applejack? charts ahead of Ke$ha, Lady Gaga and Eurovision song contest winner Lena.

Shock Goes Under: One of Australia’s largest independent music publishers, Sydney-based Shock Music Publishing, goes into liquidation after 17 years of operation. The company represented a sizeable catalogue of local and overseas acts including Kings of Leon, Grizzly Bear, Cosmic Psychos, Steve Lucas from X and Dave McCormack.

The Tote Re-Opens: M+N gets a sneak peak at the new-look pub when it opens its doors to the public for the first time in six months. New additions include a taco truck and PA, with former proprietor Bruce Milne graciously handing over the rights to the name. One for the history books: Eagle & The Worm are the first act to play the venue’s revamped stage.

A Bridge Over Brissie: A mishmash of artists from Brisbane and beyond help open the historic Go Between bridge, which links South Brisbane to the Inner City Bypass and Riverside Expressway. Robert Forster, Yves Klein Blue and The John Steel Singers perform.

Not So Gaga About Gaga: Melbourne’s Cut Copy inexplicably turn down a chance to tour with one of the world’s biggest pop stars Lady Gaga. ?We’re more focused on our own music and our own fan base,? Dan Whitford tells US website Pitchfork.

OAF Under Attack: A group of residents attempt to scupper the Oxford Art Factory’s plans to have their trading hours increased. The Sydney venue hits back, waging an online campaign against detractors blaming them for ?all of the ills of Oxford Street?.

Splitting Up: Sydney’s The Scare announce a final run of shows in NSW and Victoria.
