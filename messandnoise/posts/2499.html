Moods on the Move

By Steph Kretowicz 2011-07-22T00:00:00+00:00

If Bad Vibrations was Melbourne’s Panel of Judges most ?fully realised?, then this fourth album is the nebulous indie four-piece at its zenith. Even as the band appears to be reaching a tentative end, with founders and core songwriters Dion Nania and Alison Bolger diverging – one to New York, the other into motherhood – they couldn’t have done so more appropriately.

Like most bands of their nature, their sound is very much an organic initiation, creation and retention: they’re friends first, artists after. Nania and Bolger worked through the pressures of a romantic connection at its end with the aforementioned 2008 release, while Moods on the Move* has them re-establish their personal and creative relationship. There is a sense of peace that *Bad Vibrations – even going by the title alone – didn’t have. One couldn’t have imagined Panel topping the pining insecurity of ?All this Could Be Yours?, or extend on the sparse introspection of a sound they’ve remained perversely devoted to for nearly 15 years. However they’ve managed to build on it while staying true to the pondering temperament it evokes.

Panel no longer lingers in the nervous haze of being half asleep. They consciously meditate over their words and sounds, exploring a rich inner-world in all its complexity. Standout evolution ?Rainy? meanders through a flute-led procession that harks back to the exotic influences of ?70s psychedelia, while Nania’s ascension into the realms of poetic self-reflection comes in the brilliantly lyrical narrative of ?Dainty Vagabond?: ?There’s plenty to go around/Are we not all labourers in the vineyard of sound??

Panel of Judges – Dainty Vagabond by Mistletone

The cadenced billow of ?Drifting Away? – penned and sung by new guitar addition Martin Frawley (The Twerps) – not only welcomes another level of guitar layering (plus some playful whistles and shouts), but recognises the band’s egalitarian approach to creative input. Welcoming a sunnier disposition and doo-wop harmonies in album opener ?Bugles on the Moon?, Frawley’s influence is unmistakable. In the similarly ?60s inspired surf pop of ?Spirit?, Nania sings his praises to the charm of a seaside summer, while qualifying, ?everybody’s blue and alone?.

Panel of Judges – Coconut Trees by Mistletone

Amid the melancholic sentimentality and tender-footed vocal delivery of their shtick, album closer ?Take the Long Way? ends on a distinctly unusual swell beyond conventional song structure: a nudge towards where Panel of Judges could still go but due to circumstance, might not.
