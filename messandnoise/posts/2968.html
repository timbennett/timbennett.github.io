Orion

By Max Easton 2014-12-14T00:00:00+00:00

Orion don’t have much of a profile, but they mean more to people than is noticeable at first glance. At a show played to a shitty turnout in another average Sydney venue, there will be a sense of yearning among those gathered to see them. Even if the band flounder, Orion command a patience from their small audiences that few others have, and the eventual delivery – even if clicking for just one or two songs – yields an unparalleled surge of emotion. When watching Orion play, it feels oddly like they’re needed.

Orion have an unassuming character that comes out of the backgrounds of the people involved. Consisting two parts of Oily Boys (Dizzy and Yuta Matsumura, also of M.O.B.) and two of Whores (Sarah Davis and Chris Colla), their take on pop music brings with it a subtly aggressive air. Every uplifting melody is tugged at by an undertow, and each outburst is followed by a comedown. No song is left to swim comfortably in the light-hearted, making for a disarming effect that seems to let it infiltrate deeper into the psyche. Listening to this recording on repeat for a week, I can’t ever seem to shake it.

The songs of Orion veer from vague everyman reveals to dramatic overstatements. On ?Turbulence?, Matsumura nervously obsesses over the intentions behind someone’s return to town, and a few tracks later on ?Red Light District?, he’s declaring the sacrifice of his own blood. But even when swinging from reasonable sentiments to the melodramatic, it feels like they’ve led to each other. Like releasing a long-suppressed emotion that has had time to gestate unknowingly, it can be felt with a larger scope than is probably reasonable.

On ?Sexy Alien? (a clear contender for Sydney’s favourite track since it was first played on a stage) Matsumura distils these nameless anxieties into one line moaned bitterly through a leather glove: ?Why do I care?? Sung through a half dozen different intonations, it comes across like he’s trying to land on an answer before he’s even finished asking the question. In turn, it’s a question that can land uncomfortably on whatever anxiety you carry into the song. Sometimes, the greatest pop lyrics are the ones you’re forced to read into.

Regardless of its worth, Orion’s tape will be ignored primarily because it’s presented on a supposedly dead medium (a limited run of cassettes through new label Paradise Daily) that doesn’t fit the narrative that people seem to want from their music. It’s a great hypocrisy of the modern age that ?the album? is declared dead due to the proliferation of free music, yet music that gets discussed in any detail tends to be via formal full-length releases.

So Orion’s throwaway demo cassette (and free download) will likely float unnoticed, but that doesn’t make it irrelevant. For the small crowds who have pined for this since Orion arrived unexpectedly on the concrete floor of a Tempe warehouse 12 months ago, it’s one of the year’s most valued recordings. But why should anyone care?
