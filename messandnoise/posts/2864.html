Many Roads

By JodyMacgregor 2014-01-27T00:00:00+00:00

There is a hole in the heart of the world that can only be filled by a certain kind of guitar-pop, by boys who sing falsetto, by guitars and drums and maybe some keyboard, and Dappled Cities continue doing the laudable work of filling that hole. But they take their time and can only make so many albums, so Many Roads is a gap-plugging rarities compilation, selected from what are apparently hundreds of leftovers they had and one new song, the title track, placed on top like a bow.

Dappled Cities have often had some pretty oblique lyrics – the bit about timing bombs to blow on the backs of bears in ?Run With the Wind? is a favourite of mine – but the songs on Many Roads are some of their most impressionistic and vague. ?Don’t dissolve the time it takes to be found/There’s a science in disguise, though the carnival’s caving in at the sides,? according to ?Steven?. ?Steven? also repeats its name so often it could lead to semantic satiation, like somebody named Steven really pissed the band off and this is their elaborate scheme for revenge.

Themes do emerge even in this collection of leftover songs, however. In ?Many Roads? Dave Rennick compares his own philosophy regarding the roads he’s travelled in life with that of his father, while the jaunty ?Father & Son? finds the band’s other frontman Tim Derricourt doing much the same but in a less brash and more vulnerable way, and with way fewer Casio stabs. ?Easy Money? and ?Save Your Money? also obviously share a subject. The themes that emerge on this album are the themes you might expect to naturally pour out of men of a certain age: fatherhood and finances. (There are also a handful of references to caves and seeds, but let’s not go there.)

Some of these songs don’t benefit as much from the context they’ve been put into. ?Battlewon? is a lilting psychedelic nursery rhyme that’s lovely taken on its own but, put next to a forceful dance-rock song that sounds as close as they’ve ever come to Franz Ferdinand (?The Sleeper?), its buzzy echoing seems out of place and a bit cheap. ?Cave Country? is one of those Dappled Cities songs where they sound like a more upbeat version of The Smiths, but Derricourt’s vocal yelping doesn’t quite hit the marks he’s capable of elsewhere; maybe it was just an off day. Of course, the imperfections will turn out to be somebody else’s favourite thing about Many Roads, because for the serious obsessive these kind of albums exist as much to chart a band’s evolution as anything else. To state the bloody obvious, this is for the fans.

For me, though, it’s mostly been an excuse to go back and listen to Lake Air again, and remember how good an album that was.
