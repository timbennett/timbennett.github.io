Stories Of Towns and Cities

By JP Hammond 2009-07-01T00:00:00+00:00

Late one Thursday a month or so ago, I stumbled onto Sooners via their MySpace page. Some brief advice: if you’re a young Sydney band and you ply your trade in the field of ambient, moody rock and you list your influences with the checklist from cred central – everybody from The Dirty Three to Low to TV On The Radio – and you call one of your songs ?In Earnest?, hell, you better be bringing your A-game, lest your carcass rot in the exposing glare of the Sin City sun.

Sooners bring that and more on Stories Of Towns and Cities, their debut EP.

Opener ?Rover Boys Express? pulls off the deft trick of shifting from bleary-eyed mourning to major-chord harmonising seamlessly. ?In Earnest? – the highlight here – is a track of simple, shuddering intensity. Singer Robert Irish, backed by Annette Fortescue, ploughs into his vocals with the kind of deep, baleful baritone of which both a Manchester epileptic and a certain Caulfield Grammar graduate would be proud.

The five tracks here suggest nothing more than a band blessed with a complete uniformity of vision and a surfeit of exceptional riffs. There is an overarching, binding narrative to the record, but the extent that matters at first blush is marginal. Here’s what’s more important: ?For Me, For You? is simply blinding with its sheer number of virtuosic ideas. Irish responds to guitarist Alister Hill’s melodic sleights of hand by tracing a narrative of romantic disillusionment through the sheer dramatic force of his voice alone, repeatedly intoning the titular incantation. From 3.05, Hill and Irish introduce a joyous refrain that functions as a coda for the preceding despair. In those simple, short moments the twin guitars sketch the painful clarity that comes with rejection with a disarming, non-verbal force.

?Song for Moab?, probably the most obvious single here, rounds the package out with the kind of twinkling, incandescent despair that suggests a band like The National are contemporaries, rather than an inspiration, despite the many years separating them. To top it all off, the EP was recorded and produced by the band at The Mattress Factory, a factory they run as a rehearsal studio.

?Stories Of Towns and Cities? suggests one thing insistently – all that Sin City sun only helps Sooners see the decay more clearly. So colour me as eagerly anticipating their next move. There are repeat signs of greatness here.
