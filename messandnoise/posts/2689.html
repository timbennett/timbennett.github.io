Dark Before Blonde Dawn

By Doug Wallen 2012-10-08T00:00:00+00:00

There’s something very Melbourne about Pageants, and not just in the lackadaisical jangle and genial sprawl of guitars. There’s a certain mingling of surreal imagination and deadpan social observation that fits in nicely between The Ancients and Aleks and the Ramps. Head singer/songwriter Ben Pell has a weary strain to his vocals that fits this record’s slacker undertone yet belies just how well conceived the lyrics are. Luckily for us, they’re printed on the sleeve.

Pageants – Persian Fairy Floss by ghostrecords

As for the music, it plays around with a lot of familiar ingredients. You could trace a line pretty easily from The Velvet Underground to Pavement to the shambling likes of Woods and get quite close to Pageants. But while it’s saturated with reverb and boasts many indie-rock identifiers, there are also twinges of surf, garage and noise-pop bleeding through that saturation. The Jack Farley-helmed Dark Before Blonde Dawn sees the six-piece follow up their debut EP with winding pathways and sleepy layers. Nothing’s clear-cut here: it’s an album to inspire double-takes and dreamy musings alike.

Specific songs? The hooky and rough-hewn ?Pleasure Perched in the Parlour Palms? unspools like an ideal single, while ?Coconut Ice? channels ?Pale Blue Eyes? and even mentions a Nico. Cosy distortion and strumming define guitarist Ian Wallace’s ?Acidic Cruise?, and every element of the closing ?And Then Billie Started to Cry? feels warm and welcoming. The dirge-y eight-minute ?Footprints in the Sand? is beautifully meandering and a bit more psychedelic, made even more impressive for tucking a catchy little chorus into its drawn-out languor.

Pageants – Pleasure perched in the parlour palms by sensoryprojects10

Pell’s lyrics flow forth to great effect throughout, even when the refrains aren’t especially meaningful: ?There is no ?I? in together/The universe goes on forever,? goes ?Cannibal Twilight?. But for all the unfussed, beachside airs, there’s a poetic quality to every song, obvious in tossed-off descriptions of ?an air-conditioned nightmare at home? and ?eyes lit up like fireworks indoors.? There’s humour at play (?She lets her boyfriend play Kyuss tapes in the car?; ?I put myself to bed and I live in a first-world country?), and yet always an obscure threat of disaster too.

There’s a sustained mood across the record, with recurring themes and a lot of similar musical elements. That means the songs can sound a lot alike, but also like part of something bigger and more palpable. They all drink from the same sedately subversive well and, taking them all in, the effect is contagious.
