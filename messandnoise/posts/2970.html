Buddy Glass

By Doug Wallen 2014-12-21T00:00:00+00:00

Peabody?s Bruno Brayovic spent 2012 attending uni in France. While there he wrote most of the songs for his solo debut under the guise of Buddy Glass. But despite the geographic separation and solo nature, this material isn’t so far off from his regular band. In fact, a lot of it feels like a continuation of ?This Empty Road?, the more mellow opener of Peabody’s often rollicking 2010 LP Loose Manifesto*. That song’s raga-like wag and mood-shifting violin foreshadows many moments on *Buddy Glass, which isn’t strictly solo but ropes in a cast of 10 guest players and singers.

That slow and droning raga vibe resurfaces on this album’s ?The Plan? and the seven-minute ?Khloris & I?, among others, but there’s also the folky bent of a singer-songwriter. The propulsive acoustic guitar and punchy vocal insistence of ?Goodbye Goodbye? recalls Ben Salter – it’s almost like unplugged power-pop – while the clear-eyed verses and rousing chorus of ?Ain’t You Glad It’s Not You? evoke Cat Stevens. ?Tar Me Up? starts off with a troubadour ramble worthy of Darren Hanlon but quickly grows more harried; there’s some of Peabody’s volatility, only rendered mostly acoustically.

So far, so solid, even if most appealing primarily to curious Peabody fans. (Or to Infinity Broke fans, considering that band’s departing drummer Jared Harrison plays on 80% of the songs and frontman Jamie Hutchings lends reverbed guitar to the closing ?Stranger Waiting at My Front Door?.) But things start to get more interesting when Brayovic spikes his gentlemanly approach with a knotty blend of bitterness and romance. It’s there on ?This Strange Life?, roughened by fiddle and with Skye Knight singing backup. It’s there too on the arch standout ?Time is a Serpent?, which could pass for a less acidic Father John Misty. ?No one gets out of here alive,? Brayovic sings, paraphrasing Clive James, while his quip about ?the arsehole that clicks his fingers to call the waiter? belies the chiming, bejewelled sweetness of the music.

The best place to start, handily enough, happens to be the first song. ?The Spinning Titanic? repeats the mantra-styled lament ?I treated your love like a religion/The only thing in my field of vision? over a chugging guitar line, making for anxious, stripped-down pop carrying dark shades of post-punk. (Think XTC’s ?Making Plans for Nigel?.) Soon arrives a well-placed guest turn from singer Lauren Crew (Sister Jane, Belles Will Ring), feeding into the song’s apocalyptic tone and unravelling, dirge-y structure.

It’s the first of many harbingers of doom on this album, and those are the moments that make Buddy Glass most gripping. Brayovic’s songs are best when his palpable uncertainty and grim wit boil up and over the mannered exterior.

Buddy Glass by Buddy Glass
