Seven Drummers

By Edward Sharp-Paul 2014-04-21T00:00:00+00:00

Named for their Spinal Tap-esque run of bad luck with drummers, the title of East Brunswick All Girls Choir’s long-awaited debut album carries an explanatory, almost apologetic tone. Yeah, it’s been a while, soz, we’ve got a good excuse, honest.

Maybe it’s just me, but I was happy for them to take their time. Their debut mini-album, Dead Air, came out of nowhere, a cohesive, fully-formed piece of the highest quality. If they could do that from a standing start, how good could they be with a little time to find themselves, misplace themselves and then find themselves again?

Well, thanks to five years, a demob to Berlin, several returns and seven drummers, we have an answer: pretty bloody good. Where Dead Air* was seven successive slow-breaking thunderstorms, *Seven Drummers is something altogether less linear. At eight tracks, it highlights the blurry line between formats in the digital age, but it certainly does feel more like an album. Unlike the mini-album, its moments of brilliance are achieved not by simple surges of intensity, but by a variety of means.

?West Brunswick? kicks things off in slightly surprising fashion, with some Last Waltz country ribaldry. This rambling vibe recurs, but first they revert to familiar Australian gothic terrain with the outstanding, guttural ?Dirty Bird?. Nods to slowcore (?14 Clay Gully Crt?) and post-rock (?Aeroflot?) expand the palette even further. These tags are just tags, though: they are as relevant as a silly name, which is to say not really relevant at all. What matters is that EBAGC are able to consistently nail a feeling, and an elusive, crushingly familiar feeling at that. It’s enduring crushing setbacks and drudgery alike, and not knowing why you bother, but bothering all the same; it’s frayed ends, grotesquery, darkness and futility. It’s not the easiest atmosphere to evoke, but EBAGC manage it just fine.

Not everyone sees this stuff when they look out the window, but bands like EBACG mean a lot to the ones that do. Thankfully, though, they are careful to leaven the atmosphere a little. The sad, serene arpeggios of ?14 Clay Gully Crt? and the rambling ?Darius? offer uncharacteristic but welcome hints of sunlight.

The highlights of Seven Drummers*, though, are familiar. Hobbs and Rob Wrigley once again manage to pull some stellar guitar tones, maintaining a crystalline clarity that somehow withstands the generous drifts of distortion that are piled up throughout. Once again, Hobbs? trebly, just-holding-it-together delivery dominates the songs, even more so than before. Across *Seven Drummers, his delivery is more stylised and more deranged. This is great, but so are Hobbs? potted histories and cracked character sketches, and while plenty of the performances are spellbinding (Hobbs? opening contribution to ?Dirty Bird? the most notable example), their impact comes at the expense of the lyrics, which are often rendered indecipherable. Given the crushing eloquence of the music, though, this is a minor quibble.

Is it better than Dead Air*? Is a waterfall more beautiful than a rainbow? Is a shit analogy better than no analogy at all? Well, it’s not that simple. *Seven Drummers* doesn’t hit like *Dead Air did but, short of a radical makeover, a band can only really surprise you once. Given time, though, these songs cut just as deep, and in different ways.
