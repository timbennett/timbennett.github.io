Grass Openings

By Shaun 2009-09-08T00:00:00+00:00

The music of Brothers of the Occult Sisterhood is misunderstood. Once the name for Kyogle-based duo Michael and Kristina Donnelly, it’s now a fluid collective with Michael as its central component. This is their first release on Donnelly’s new non-CD-R label Pacific Soma.

If Donnelly’s documented mode of operation still stands, then Grass Openings is a collection of clippings from long-form improvisations that channel the peculiar space in which the group improvise: normally a small village near Kyogle, NSW, called Green Pigeon. That, at least, is what this sounds like, because despite their long hiatus (nearly 12 months is a long time for the Donnellys) not much in the world of Brothers of the Occult Sisterhood has changed.

BotOS’s earthy missives sound like a cumbersome creature lurching through rough foliage with its feet chained together, its lumbering progress eliciting the songs of foreign birds and insect life. Shuffling, barely rhythmic chimes and scrapes trace erratically through an ever-shifting world of glaze-eyed acoustic fumbling and malnourished vocal contortions. The effect is always one of open-air claustrophobia; an escape through dense scrub after waking up on the wrong side of the bed with Charles Manson.

Because it’s true enough: Brothers of the Occult Sisterhood do sound like a troupe of bent over hippies, stoned to their eyeballs, running their fingers up and down their instruments with a slightly pissed off Ouija board miked up in the corner. That’s not to say this is a failure, as such a description might imply. On the contrary, Grass Openings succeeds where most music of this ilk doesn’t in that it’s flat out weird and blissfully confounding. Thanks to the subtlety with which the ensemble operates, a furtive stillness pervades these recordings – like sitting in a glass dome while a livid nature runs its brutal course all around.

Of course, banging all the instruments at your disposal until the speakers blaringly clip might not constitute subtlety to most listeners, but it’s the intricate uniformity of Grass Openings* that proves hypnotic: the way oncoming textures carefully insinuate themselves into the larger whole, shifting the canvas’s colour palette slowly but surely until the destination resembles an inverted starting point. Brothers of the Occult Sisterhood create soundworlds that dare you to pick them apart and get inside, which is why this group is often misunderstood: a work rate like Donnelly’s sends collectors into frenzy, leaving little time to properly engage with his work. Take the time to dismantle the thick tangled exterior of *Grass Openings though, and you’ll be surprised by the immensity of colour that lies within.
