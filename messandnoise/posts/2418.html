Natural Disasters

By Doug Wallen 2011-02-01T00:00:00+00:00

If you’re going to make your voice and lyrics the utter focus of an album, you’d better have the songs for it. Dave Rogers does. Bringing to mind the more popular acts of the old Candle Records stable – say, the Lucksmiths and Darren Hanlon – Rogers? fourth album is all about crisp enunciation; fleeting pop embellishment; a playful folk lilt; and witty, poignant words. The importance of lyrics doesn’t mean this record is demanding: most songs hover around two minutes, and only a few are more than three. And even when Rogers resorts to barbs, his delivery is defined by an airy calm.

Backing harmonies come from Emma Heeney and drummer Dave Kleynjans, while Amy Bennett plays viola on three songs. There’s also a chorus of friends sending off ?This Part of Town?, but mostly Rogers strips things as bare as possible. That works well for the way he dispenses compact stories punctuated by striking details. ?There’s nothing that you own except initials on a loan,? he sings, lamenting the subject’s self-inflicted money worries on ?Pay to Pay?. In a more romantic mood, he cites unwashed dishes and unpaid bills to demonstrate the absorption of love on ?Best Case Scenario?. And ?I’d Cave? shows just how neatly he can package sentiments.

It’s a more upbeat album than its title would have you think, although Rogers does bristle at the too-cool posturing rampant in both the music industry and his native Melbourne. The album opens with the line, ?Drunk at the awards ceremony/Staring at the glittering prize?, and ?Your Heart’s an Only Child? mentions ?taste driven by committee? and ?a world driven by looks?. In perhaps a straighter version of the Bedroom Philosopher’s ?Northcote (So Hungover)?, he contrasts the perky music of ?Westgarth Talking? with withering bon mots like, ?You can’t get an accent like that from anywhere else?, and ?Looks like we’ll have to let the street press decide.?

Still, those touches don’t distract from the writerly gifts on display here. And Rogers doesn’t let negativity consume him. The honeyed licks of ?Breaking Bones? offset its dark themes, while ?Going Through the Motions? bridges power-pop and alt-country with a freshness worthy of Teenage Fanclub. Well-observed frustration may drive his songs, but Rogers resolves those conflicts by building heavenly tunes around them.
