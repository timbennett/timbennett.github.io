Paddington Workers Club

By Jake Cleland 2013-02-09T00:00:00+00:00

At the risk of setting your eyes rolling right off the bat with a trite analogy, the club of the title might as well be the nutshell which unfolds to reveal the core of Dollar Bar’s second record. A working-class institution built for 9-to-5ers to ease the daily drudgery turned, in so many cases, into a place-to-be for musical yoofs. Dollar Bar’s Patrick McCabe calls the Paddington Workers Club ?a shitty old barroom? where underage inner-city kids were always ?sneaking in and drinking goon.?

Sounds romantic, doesn’t it? Dollar Bar’s performances there apparently inspired a bunch of those kids to go off and start rock bands of their own. Like it or not, the Paddington Workers Club was the centre of a scene, and while the album named after it comes off pretty working-class with its four-piece setup, it’s the scenesters who occupy most of the songs.

Paddington Workers Club puts the personalities and sensibilities of its three songwriters right up front, each singing the songs they wrote (with the exception of ?Animal Defences?, written by Chris Yates but sung by Dale Peachey). The result is a record which sounds like three-in-one. You’ve got the Big Star-y jawns ?(You’re) Blind Baby? and ?State of Decay?, the David Berman drawl of ?Diff’rent 4 Gurls? and even the scratched-voice spectre of Bob Pollard lingering about in the abstract poetics of ?Animal Defences? and ?Hipster Mental Ward?. There’s kind of a Liz Phair thing on ?State of Decay? too. This is a record for people whose idea of the ?90s was formed ?91-to-?95, not ?98-to-?02.

But it’s not three identities fighting for dominance. Rather, it sounds like a group of mates passing around a guitar, conversing through song. The topic of conversation revolves mostly around the trendies, coolsies, indies, hipsters, self-regarding youths – whatever you wanna call ?em – and the foibles of those Kids Today. McCabe reckons they’re ?dressin? for aviation? and ?fallin? in love with the hate online?; Peachey sees ?em ?moving to Melbourne to hook up with people from Brisbane?; and Yates pegs them taking drugs and drinking lemonade and spending the rent on anything but. The examination is self-directed (or inflicted) as much as it’s extended outwards. ?How can we blame one another when we’re all so freakin? attractive?? Peachey mocks on ?Everyone’s Everyone?. It sounds nearly spiteful but half guilty, too.

Ten years is a long time between albums. ?We’d gotten a lot of Triple J rotation and started to get sweet support slots for touring bands,? McCabe tells me, but then ?I think we all just needed a break really. Basically we all just went our separate ways for a while.? The story, then, might be of a band who killed their momentum stone-dead only to return a decade later with a record cheekily ribbing their peers and themselves and the qualities they see echoed in the kids coming up from behind. If so, then ?Everyone’s Everyone? is the perfect closer, couching its scene-skewering in wry humour. ?Everyone’s fanning your flames/But that doesn’t make it okay.?

Incestuous cliques aren’t without their benefits, but they’re just as much fun to hate – even when you’re at the heart of ?em.

Paddington Workers Club by Dollar Bar
