The Vasco Era

By Max Easton 2011-10-14T00:00:00+00:00

After running from expectations on their first two studio albums to face life crises, short-lived break-ups and critical condemnation, Apollo Bay natives The Vasco Era have finally made peace with their identity to record the most true-to-self release of their eight-year lifespan. Shirking the pop hooks and commercial sheen of last year’s Lucille (produced by Scott Horscroft of Presets and My Disco fame) The Vasco Era have found residence in a new, rougher territory. Self-titled, it steps away from the nervous misses of their debut and academic posturing of their second, culminating in their most successful record to date.

After ?Nah Nah Nah Nah’s confused ramble and the buoyant, but stilted ?All The Pretty Lies?, the album abruptly reaches its darkest point. With frontman Sid O’Neil?s downcast vocal languishing among a wall-of-sound drone, ?Avatar Blues? runs from self-pitying jibes and judgmental cracks (?Why was a naturopath smoking rolled-up cigarettes??) to a violent aside featuring a three-month coma and a feature film, all with thinly-veiled references to depression and ostracism. It’s an eerily bleak moment that’s only returned to on the misunderstood dejection of ?Not Everyone is From New York City?, where vocals are obscured under a reverbed sea of drums and bass while O’Neil?s razor-edge wail courses through the matted wall of sound.

It may be lazy to call this record a coming of age, but it’s hard not to identify a retrospective voice of reason running throughout. On ?Every Boy’s the Same,? O’Neil mumbles, ?I went and saw Doctor Leeroy/He specialises in heartbreak and disappointment/He looked in my eye/He gave me a diagnosis/I was a hypochondriac? in almost an acceptance of his past insecurities. Indeed, beneath the off-handed cracks and flippant choruses (?Do they like boys with child-bearing hips??) is genuine emotional catharsis.

Child Bearing Hips by thevascoera

O’Neil drops the sense of over-reaching that plagued ?Lucille? for off handed admissions and truths. The cyclical river of doubt in ?Politenessman? (?I drowned my future in that river, and I drowned my past/Stopped being anxious for a while/But then a while did pass?) is as much an opening into O’Neil?s self-doubt as the ?delusions of significance? his character admits to in ?Chemicals Run Out?. None of which, however, are as explicit as the album’s centerpiece, ?Rock n Roll Is The Only Thing That Makes Me Feel Good?.

The track paints a laundry list of failed coping mechanisms, dropping that teaser of a line, ?I tried transcending criticism/But songs sound better in your head than when you’re done with them.? By the time O’Neil?s done with meditation, psytrance and William Blake’s The Marriage of Heaven and Hell (?I never did understand it too well?), he finds that his crutch is his music, replete with the obligatory guttural squeals. If anything, it’s the album’s theme song; an epiphany reached after a return from rock bottom.

By the time the record finishes on the drug-fucked meander ?Conversation at Revolver?, the band has chartered a see-sawing emotional battle. Swinging from the depths of depression to the careless highs of ignorance, the album is The Vasco Era at their most revealing. They’re not screaming themselves out of youth as on ?Oh We Do Like to Be By the Seaside?, nor are they trying to obscure feelings under characters ala ?Lucille?. With The Vasco Era, they’re allowing whatever comes to the table find its way onto record. Ironically, by putting together an album without trying to appease critical opinion, they’ve crafted their most likely to garner acclaim. It makes you wonder: when there’s a band this good underneath all the shit they left in their wake, what were they ever running from in the first place?
