Monument

By Ian Rogers 2012-06-30T00:00:00+00:00

Full disclosure: I was surprised when I turned over my copy of Monument by Children Collide and noticed the Universal Music Group logo on the back. My apologies to the band but I didn’t think anyone in rock music released anything worth hearing on those things any more. Is this the product of some deal signed forever ago, on the last gasp of commercial promise for Australian alternative rock? Releasing an album, on compact disc, on a major label, in 2012, doesn’t even seem efficient any more. These are weird times, I suppose.

In a way, this is how I’ve often felt about the band. In my mind, they’re an interesting group stuck inside all sorts of bad ideas, the worst of which is aspiring to last century’s idea of a music career in Australia. They have a tinge of rock star swagger and fashion glamour to them as well, and usually this stuff speaks to me (I hate boring things), but with Children Collide it’s felt like a bad fit. I’m sure they live it but I flat out don’t give a fuck about authenticity or genuine performance or any of that junk, that’s not it. It’s something else, something mildly off putting. What I’m trying to say is this: I’m listening to Monument right now and I feel as though I should like the band a lot more than I do.

In context, they’ve made a really good record. Big on choruses and hooks and beats. Everyone’s brought something to the table, it’s 12 songs long and it doesn’t lag. They still ply a particularly English-sounding indie rock but it’s tempered in places with flourishes of ?contemporary psychedelia? (think: delay, songs about the sun) and one song (?Sphere of Influence?) that might – if you’re a teenager – sound like Suicide. It’s dancing music, essentially, and they do it well. Johnny is in good voice, and he still manages to sneak all sorts of less conventional guitar sounds under the pop veneer. And he’s aptly supported by Heath Crawley, a bass player who has always worked that weird tipping point between smart post-punk and crass disco. There’s no sign of trouble or tension in outgoing drummer Ryan Caesar’s performance either, in fact he seems particularly on beat in places. The lyrics are, obviously, nonsense but they make me laugh (with them). Nonsense isn’t the easiest thing in the world to get right. Neither is making pop music.

It all comes to a sharp point for me in the second track. Even though the song is called ?Sword To A Gunfight?, I ignored the title as I listened to the track repeatedly on the bus. I could have sworn that the lyrics were: ?Egg, Child, Man/Never bring a soul to a gunfight, alright/?Cos they shoot from the hip while you slash from the wrist.? But I was wrong. I misheard it. The lyric really is that old chestnut, sword to a gunfight. So maybe I was listening for the wrong things all along? Either way, it’s a catchy song.
