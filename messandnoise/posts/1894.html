The Man Is Dead

By JP Hammond 2009-02-01T00:00:00+00:00

What to make of a band that thanks Virginia Woolf in the liner notes and has a (Very Serious and Important) song called ‘Disco Biscuit Love’?

Sydney’s The Jezabels have been touted as a Next Big Thing for a few years now, and their new EP The Man is Dead brooks no opposition in the ambition stakes. That said, it’s a truly bewildering release.

‘Disco Biscuit Love’ aligns a perfectly listenable Coldplay/Arcade Fire rip (?Rebellion? off the Arcade Fire’s Funeral may yet prove to be this decade’s Eddie Vedder growl) with some of the most blindingly god-awful lyrics I’ve ever heard (?I know he’s groovy but he only loves you when he’s on pills,? anyone?).

Things don’t improve quickly. ‘Be A Star’ starts with an appreciably murky guitar line before piling on hand-claps, military-shuffle drums and an excessively histrionic vocal tracking the narrative of one ?who was never meant to be a star?. Repeat listens aren’t positive, with the cumulative hysteria provoking unfavourable comparisons to Bon Jovi circa the Young Guns II soundtrack.

Contrastingly, ‘Electric Lover’ is a bleak little moody number that succeeds. Simple and downbeat, it suggests they’ve broadened their palette to include some records by Stars and some Australian contemporaries (Youth Group, The Panics) amid the Funeral repetitions.

Similarly, ‘Old Little Girls’ is good, strong stuff. It plays perfectly to vocalist Hayley M’s penchant for dramatic overstatement by pitching the arrangement in noisy competition, rather than accompaniment, with her.

It all concludes with ‘Unmarked Helicopters’, a smart, staccato, tempo-shifting pop song that, at times, recalls the more melodically focused Yeah Yeah Yeahs of Show Yr Bones and suggests the raves they’ve received weren’t just the result of excessive ?disco biscuit love? on the part of desperate Sydney record execs.

God only knows where they’re heading with all this. If there is a unifying positive across this release, it’s the fact that they sound so wild, wide-eyed, ready and open to finding something new. Here’s hoping what they find is grime and grit. Some dirt on these songs would serve them well.

Vaulting ambition, which overleaps itself and falls on the other side – it’s Shakespeare, not their buddy Virginia, but they’d do well to heed the reference.
