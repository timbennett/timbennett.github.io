Long Gone Whistle: The Songs Of Maurice Frawley

By Trevor 2010-08-01T00:00:00+00:00

Maurice Frawley died of cancer in May 2009. He was one of those people that helped make Melbourne’s music community what it is today, being both a player and a stayer. He was a part of the fabric of the local scene for more than 30 years, and something of a key figure within a certain sector of it: that area where older, seasoned hands would relax and play with friends, for friends, although all-comers were welcome. He’d often perform on the south side of town, often on a Saturday or, better yet, a Sunday afternoon, in a place like the Greyhound in St Kilda, where he would stand directly on the sticky carpet not on stage. I watched him play on and off since the mid ?80s when he was a member of The Olympic Sideburns, and always found him friendly and passionate about his music without being obsessed or overbearing.

Overseen by old bandmates Paul Kelly and Charlie Owen, this is a fitting tribute, wide ranging and beautifully presented. There’s some initially surprising contributions from the likes of Luke and Katy Steele and Megan Washington, who acquit themselves well, as well as a who’s who of Australian music represented. Aside from Kelly and Owen, you get offerings from artists such as Adalita, Spencer P Jones, Tex Perkins, Don Walker, Chrissie Amphlett and Tim Rogers; an impressive list of those who knew either the man or his music and were moved to contribute.

There’s real value in the inclusion of a third disc, containing the now not-so-easy-to-find originals of every tune that’s been covered, so you can hear how they sounded when Frawley played them. Recorded between 1992 and 2005, there are 21 tracks by his bands The Working Class Ringos and The Yardhands, which is enough to give a real feel for what Frawley’s own music and voice sounded like. And, by reversal, you get a sense of what the participants brought to the songs featured, too. This is a tribute, not a museum, and plenty of the artists put some of themselves into the tunes.

It seems beside the point of this collection to highlight any contribution over another, given they were all offered in the same spirit and are part of a wider whole, but it’s hard to go past the character-laden vocals of Spencer Jones and Renee Geyer’s duet on ?You I Want?; the way Don Walker’s laconic, semi-spoken take on ?Goodnight Matron, Hello Jesus? (backed by the Working Class Ringos) fits the wordplay so perfectly; and the horn section that gives Dan Kelly’s ?Cool Water? a white soul tinge.

I reckon Frawley would’ve been touched and very pleased with this, although slightly embarrassed by the fuss. But he deserves it.
