Sun Sets On A New Tomorrow

By ReneSchaefer 2010-08-01T00:00:00+00:00

Consensus from friends and acquaintances has it that living in Perth in summer is akin to spending a season in hell. They conjur up visions of vast shopping malls, treeless suburbs, endless strips of fast food franchises, beaten into submission by the harsh glare of a relentlessly vicious sun, heat reflecting off suppurating tarmac and scorched earth. According to these hardy souls, for months on end life retreats into hidden corners and festers away, producing surprising new mutations.

Such is the case with musical collective smRts, who not only call Perth home, but chose to record their debut album at the peak of a heatwave in December 2009. Obviously they are made of strong stuff, voluntarily submitting themselves to temperatures in the mid-40s to lay down the tracks for this album of twisted and beguiling hybrids of surf instrumentals, post-rock and Eastern European folk songs.

In text, this amalgam may not make much sense, but there is something undeniably absorbing about a band that references Russian polka rhythms as much as the restrained rock sounds of Slint or Bedhead. Being a multicultural group has its advantages, as guitarist Predrag Delibasich lends a decidedly non-Anglo bent to what essentially is an indie rock permutation. This kind of thing can work a treat when done well – witness British band The Wedding Present’s forays into Ukrainian folk styles in the late ?80s – but it can also come off as incredibly try-hard and wanky, depending on the sensibilities of its creators.

On ?Sun Sets On A New Tomorrow?, three guitars interlace and percolate away over restless rhythms, which approximates the lurching dance beat of a drunken Shadows cover band at your uncle Dmitri’s wedding reception, while you sneak sips of vodka from the grown-ups? table and try to feel up your pubescent cousin in the cloakroom. Yep, it’s that kind of record, but it also isn’t. The post-rock influence comes through at all times, meaning there is an elegance and intricacy to these songs, which lifts them far above mere goodtime music.

Recorded live in the studio, without overdubs or embellishing technology, the main attraction lies in the way the guitar lines run rings around each other, flirt shamelessly with strangers, start yelling at each other and then stomp off into opposite corners, kicking a chair or two before slamming the door in a huff. And before too long they are back at it, making goo-goo eyes and trying to outdo each other in a dexterous flurry of fingers and jazzy notes.

Shellac’s Bob Weston has done a fine job mastering the finished album to bring out this captivating drama. On ?Curious Bird? somebody imitates an oompah tuba with a fuzz guitar, while aunt Olga takes surf lessons from Dick Dale on ?Monsters Are Just What We Need?. The traditional number ?I’m Sick With Love (So Far Away)? is the song the Cossack Shadows pull out when some maudlin drunk picks a fight on the dancefloor, while ?Lenin’s Funeral March? is a re-interpretation of a composition by Shostakovich. I’m not making this up, it really is!

What we have here is quite a surreal listening experience, but one that is worth returning to over and over. The fact that there are no vocals whatsoever leaves the music to speak for itself and the listener’s mind to conjure the scenarios that are being soundtracked. Like one of those quirky Eastern European road movies, shot through with equal parts comedy and heartbreak, this album keeps surprising you. Just as you think you’ve got a handle on what’s going on, things take an unexpected turn and you find yourself sitting in the dirt by the side of the road, with your hat and wallet gone, and clutching an empty vodka bottle.
