The Cat

By Matt Shea 2011-07-01T00:00:00+00:00

Ben Salter has always been a slightly frustrating musician to a follow – not because he’s inconsistent with his art, but because he often seems so interminably restless. Salter has performed with at least four different bands of reasonable profile over the years – Giants of Science, The Wilson Pickers, The Gin Club and The Young Liberals – and all are going concerns when it comes to his involvement, even if the Pickers played on without him while he hammered out this solo record.

The Brisbane-based songsmith takes the lead on most of his projects, but the juggling makes him a hard man to pin down. In that sense, The Cat*, his debut solo release, is long overdue. It’s overdue in a more practical definition too, Salter having tooled away on some of these cuts for well over 10 years. Indeed, *The Cat almost feels like a purge, Salter and his hired hands moving through many of these songs at what feels like breakneck speed.

Not that you should in any way underestimate this record. There’s been a slew of fantastic solo releases already this year – including Adalita’s self-titled debut and Mike Noga’s The Balladeer Hunter – but I’d feel comfortable in arguing that this is the best of the lot. The songs themselves are excellent, but true to Salter’s ambitions it’s in the treatment that they truly shine.

Something like the icy title track layers its scaling melody with a detailing that encourages listeners to explore on a much deeper level. Julien Wilson’s ferocious saxophone is the obvious touch point, but the track bustles with brilliant, percussive half-breaks and guitars that pluck and chime organically. It’s a technique that runs through the entire album – from the propulsive, desperate ?The Coward? to flat-out gorgeous closer, ?Last Night I Dreamt I Was Chasing You? – and one that comes from experience. If Salter had recorded these deceptively simple songs 10 years ago they probably wouldn’t be nearly as interesting.

Ben Salter – The Coward by Mess+Noise

With such a need to experiment, The Cat could have easily become over-egged. But Salter, along with co-producers Gareth Liddiard (The Drones) and Robert F Cranny (Sarah Blasko), knows when to create space rather than just noise. Nowhere is this better demonstrated than on the album’s plaintive, echoing centrepiece, ?West End Girls?, in which Salter details the lurid side of Brisbane’s lamp-lit streets. The seven-minute cut sits slap-bang in the middle of the album and it says a lot about the careful, considered treatment that it doesn’t bring everything to a grinding halt.

Salter plans on supporting The Cat* over the next couple of months, before no doubt returning to one of his many other projects for the summer. But despite his enthusiasm for this solo release you wonder sometimes if he’s underestimated its importance. *The Cat is fantastic in its own right, but more tellingly it allows Salter to place a peg in the ground on which to build his own legacy.
