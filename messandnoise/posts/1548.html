Standing On The Outside: The Songs of Cold Chisel

By Lauren 2007-02-28T12:00:00+00:00

It begs the question – an album of Chisels covers? why? Once the tribute album seemed like a novel idea, but experience has proven that entire discs of cover versions are just plain annoying. Restraint is all – throw a well-chosen, slightly unexpected cover into your live set and fans will only love you more. Additionally, what point is there in replicating the style of the original? Surely the joy is in personalising and making the original almost unrecognisable? who could forget Frente’s bare bones version of ?Bizarre Love Triangle?, fr’instance? Whereas, guaranteed, no one will ever remember Grinspoon’s attempt here at ?Saturday Night?. Especially as the bonus disc here contains the originals, rendering the pale imitations only more obsolete, as time has definitely not dimmed these iconic songs.

On this basis, I would actually enjoy hearing these tunes in the live sets of the following artists, in order of appearance: Pete Murray’s gentle, touching ?Forever Now?; Ben Lee’s ?No Sense?, which has a hugely enjoyable, slightly crazed-indie feel; Troy Cassar-Daley, who exudes a surprising amount of grit and passion into ?Bow River?. I also appreciate the ladies – Katie Noonan’s hushed and spare, piano-based ?Choir Girl? and, equally, The Waifs? banjo ?n? harmonica textured ?Four Walls? is a gentle delight, while Sarah Blasko brings a believable emotion to her woozy take on ?Flame Trees?. And praise also to Augie March for a soaring, aching ?Janelle?, they can do no wrong at the moment.

Everyone else is sleepwalking, but I didn’t see this coming – Paul Kelly has somehow managed to make the mighty, urgent desperation of ?Khe Sanh? sound, of all things, twee. Which is just bad in ways previously thought unimaginable.
