Where Were You At Lunch

By Doug Wallen 2012-06-07T00:00:00+00:00

Without the burden of vocals, instrumental music can signify anything. And on their self-titled debut, Where Were You at Lunch do. They also assert the range of their erratic compositions while lurching between tight repetition and corroded noise. It’s a startling outlet for the Melbourne trio of drummer Kishore Ryan (Kid Sam, Otouto), guitarist Samaan Fieck (ex-Psuche) and bassist Peter Emptage (also a singer-songwriter), and it shows off another side of producer Nick Huggins as well. A world away from the spotless contours of Huggins? usual production, this album feels caked with the accumulated rust and grit of the band’s many admitted influences.

Those include Captain Beefheart, Glenn Branca, Can and Harry Pussy, for starters. More to the point, there’s something of The Necks in WWYAL, who are sinewy on one hand and flat-out unnerving on the other. Opener ?Don’t Be Funny? tries out tentative guitar notes before a sudden crash launches a rollicking, jazz-like groove that’s right in the wheelhouse of Tortoise. It’s got a controlled hunger and wildness to it that fits the animal agility on display, before mostly relinquishing that control in favour of a frayed, stop-start ending.

Don’t Be Funny by Where Were You At Lunch

?Geelong #4? has the same dragging quality to Ryan’s drum work as Jim White, but this album really isn’t hung up on other people’s records. It’s more an elemental outpouring of three instrumental voices, with Huggins there to fully realise it all. He gates and distorts sounds in post-production for the crackling outro of ?Toshio? and lends acoustic guitar early on in the queasy centrepiece ?Wife?. Mostly, though, he lets these guys muck around with rhythm and saturated, inter-bled tones.

From the creepy double-bass drone that defines the first two minutes of ?Geelong #2? to the overdriven grit that finishes off the closing ?Don’t Do Anything You Wouldn’t Eat? (in more ways than one), this record thrives when painting outside the lines. There’s even some fleeting, punk-ish whoa-oh vocals on ?Milwaukee?, a U.S. Maple-ish track that’s like a conversation with itself where one side is screaming its head off and the other remains softly consoling. Most telling of all is the nine-minute near-suite ?Wife?, as likely to mine percussive clatter as get a hot gut full of bass feedback and take an unexpected turn for delicate melody with some prepared piano.

Wife by Where Were You At Lunch

Some of WWYAL’s songs reportedly came from generating intervals and rhythms from sport statistics. (That could also be bullshit.) Others are improvisatory and others more plotted. Whatever the approach, this name-your-price debut plays more like an excited live set than something safely defined by the regimentation of an album.
