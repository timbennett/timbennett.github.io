2011: The Year In News Pt 1

By Darren Levin 2012-01-02T13:00:00+00:00

Venue closures, Nirvana tribute gigs and rising flood waters dominate an eventful first half of 2011. Part two here.

January


Brisbane floods: As rising flood waters devastate Queensland, several venues within Brisbane’s CBD and Fortitude Valley entertainment precinct are forced to close and cancel gigs. Benefit shows are held across the country, however newly retired Powderfinger decline calls from local MPs to ?dust off? their guitars for a once-off benefit show, choosing to release a single for victims instead. M+N contributor Andrew Stafford writes a moving column about what records he’d save, while Custard and Regurgitator sign on for a February benefit gig. (Photo by Elleni Toumpas)

Lofly flies no more: M+N contributor Andrew McMillen laments the loss of Brisbane’s Lofly Hangar, which shut its doors in late 2010.

In memoriam: A dark weekend for Australian pub rock with the death of Cold Chisel drummer Steve Prestwich and Sherbet guitarist Harvey James. Meanwhile, local tributes flow for Broadcast’s Trish Keenan, who dies after reportedly contracting the H1N1 flu after a visit to Australia for a series of shows in early December

Angus & Julia top the Hottest 100: A good showing by Australian acts in triple j’s Hottest 100 is overshadowed by Angus & Julia Stone’s insipid ?Big Jet Plane? taking out the top spot. It’s later named the second biggest controversy of 2011 in our annual Readers Poll. Meanwhile, Dave Graney weighs in.

Signings, splits: Modular snaps up Architecture In Helsinki for their fourth (and best) album *Moment Bends; Brisbane’s Yves Klein Blue call it a day after three short years; and Alana Skyring leaves The Grates for a career in cooking. (She’s later courted by Neil and Sharon Finn for their Pajama Club project.)

Lunchbox makes Sydney bow: After seven shows in Melbourne, the M+N* Lunchbox series finally makes its way up the Hume for a debut Sydney performance as part Sydney Festival. Drones frontman Gareth Liddiard performs songs from his debut solo outing *Strange Tourist.

February


Tote back on track: More than a year on from its brief closure, Melbourne’s iconic Tote Hotel celebrates a new licence, which winds back the ?high risk? licensing conditions that brought the venue to its knees in 2010. Meanwhile, Melbourne’s SLAM rally celebrates its one-year anniversary with a night of one-off collaborations at – yep, you guessed it – The Tote. We ask whether the battle to save Melbourne’s live music scene was only just beginning. (Photo by Robert Carbone)

Venue woes in Melbourne and Sydney: Melbourne’s Pure Pop Records battles for the right to hold gigs in their courtyard following noise complaints, while Justin Hemmes? Merivale Group purchases Sydney’s Excelsior Hotel, turning it into a Mexican restaurant.

What’s in a name: A clash with an American dubstep DJ prompts Brisbane duo DZ to change their name to DZ Deathrays, while Ballarat’s Howl become Hunting Grounds.

Splitting up: London-via-Perth outfit Snowman call it a day following drummer Olga Hermanniusson and Ross DiBlasio’s relocation to Iceland (third album Absence is released posthumously), while country-rock legends The Dingoes? short-lived reunion comes to an end. ?It was an adventure we wanted to go on at the time, and the adventure is just about complete,? songwriter-guitarist Kerryn Tolhurst told M+N at the time.

Nirvana tribute stirs mean spirit: The Shame Cauldron? fires up for the first time in ?11 with the ill-conceived announcement of a Nirvana tribute gig in celebration of Nevermind*?s 20th anniversary. The Vasco Era’s Sid O’Neil logs onto *M+N to deny his involvement and while he’s later replaced by Brock Tengstrom (ex-Regular John), the gig eventually (and expectedly) falls over. In reference to the ?scandal?, O’Neil cheekily opens The Vasco Era’s Lunchbox gig with Nirvana’s ?Scentless Apprentice?.

March


Institutions on the market: Long-time Missing Link owner Nigel Rennard puts his iconic Melbourne record store up for sale. He says he’s looking to pass it on to ?someone with extensive knowledge of local and overseas independent music, preferably with retail knowledge?. Meanwhile, the Public Bar in North Melbourne is listed for sale with ?vacant possession?. The building is being spruiked as having ?potential for ten levels residential development?. Not to be outdone, the Annandale’s Rule brothers announce they’re looking for investors that ?share their passion for live music?. One of these will survive the year. (Photo by Leah Robertson)

More venue woes: Following The Excelsior’s sale, Surry Hills loses another venue, with Raval ?unable to sustain itself with live music?. The venue had been hosting gigs since August 2009. Over in Melbourne, The Arthouse announces its final run of gigs featuring acts that have made the venue ?their home? over the past 20 years.

My Disco derailed: Melbourne’s My Disco are forced to cancel gigs after bass player Liam Andrews ruptures his spleen in a road accident. ?The next thing I remember was waking up in ICU where I was told I required several blood transfusions as a result of internal bleeding,? he told M+N in an interview.

Vivid line-up leaked: Some super sleuthing by an M+N poster unveils the Vivid Live line-up a day early. When its officially announced, the bill is dominated by acts on curator Stephen Pavlovic’s Modular label. He later defends his curatorial style in a promotional video posted to YouTube. It’s brilliantly parodied by Half a Cow boss Nic Dalton.

April

The Arthouse farwelled: After 20 years of operation, North Melbourne punk institution The Arthouse hosts an emotional final night of bands, including a reformed H-Block 101. We pay tribute, as does the Nation Blue’s Tom Lyngcoln and Yidcore’s Bram Presser. (Photo by Greg Bricknell)

New kid on the festival block: The inaugural Sugar Mountain Festival makes its debut at Melbourne’s Forum Theatre, with performances by Otouto, Qua, Virgo Four, Aa and Twerps. Our reviewer describes it as a ?well-funded Steiner school concert only with more floor toms?.

Splitting up: Sydney’s much-hyped class of ?03 loses another graduate, with Red Riders calling it quits for goods. Writing on their website, the band said that after eight ?immensely fun years? together, they’ve decided to bow out.

Buskers change tune: The Shame Cauldron? strikes again, forcing serial buskers Set Sail to change their act following a discussion thread, which lambasted their ?godawful tunes, insistent nappy-brain positive street vibes and four hour ‘greatest hits’ medleys on the street?.

Cure for Vivid rumours begin: The M+N rumour mill swings into overdrive with strong indications that The Cure are set to be announced as the ?secret act? for Sydney’s Vivid Live. Pav spinning ?A Forest? on FBi Radio, among a clutch of tracks by Vivid LIVE acts, further whets appetites.

Middle East diss own album: In a revealing interview with M+N* contributor Doug Wallen, Middle East singer Rohin Jones says he’s not a ?massive fan? of the band’s protracted first album, *I Want That You Are Always Happy. He also foreshadows a break-up: ?I still want to do other things with my life, if you know what I mean.?

In the studio: Witch Hats spend seven days in Melbourne’s Head Gap studios recording tracks for a more ?song-focused? second album, while Adelaide’s Leader Cheetah offer a glimpse into the process behind album number two, Lotus Skies, via YouTube.

May

Splendour ticketing woes: For the first time in ?many years? Splendour In The Grass fails to sell out on the same day tickets go on sale. Event producers blame a bank glitch, which leads to an unprecedented 12-hour suspension of sales. While other blogs speculate on the ?real? story behind the ticketing woes, M+N reveals the ?truth? in graph and pie chart form. It eventually sells out, and we publish an apology. (Photo by Justin Edwards)

Silverchair split: A joint statement from Daniel Johns, Chris Joannou and Ben Gillies confirms silverchair’s lapse into a ?deep sleep? after 20 years. Apparently they want to do some ?more inspiring? things.

Cure rumour confirmed: One of the worst kept secrets in festival history is confirmed with The Cure announcing two shows at Sydney Opera House as part of Vivid Live. The band plays three albums in their entirety: Three Imaginary Boys*, *Seventeen Seconds* and *Faith.

Libs love/hate live music: While newly established peak body Music Victoria has its funding secured until 2013, the Victorian government shelves the $2.4-million FReeZACentral program and cuts funding for the contemporary music sector by an estimated 56 percent.

Rock folklore for sale: The 115-year-old Yass mansion where Gareth Liddiard and Jack Ladder recorded Strange Tourist and *Hurtsville, respectively, is put up for sale for a lazy $2.4-million.

Kasey Chambers writes world’s best song: A panel comprised of Tom Waits, The Pixies? Black Francis, Timbaland and Peter Gabriel selects Kasey Chambers? ?Beautiful Mess? as the overall winner of the International Songwriting Competition for 2010. She pockets $US25,000.

Regional pub re-opens: After seven months of renovations, the historic Palais in Central Victoria announces its reopening under new management. Tim Rogers plays the official launch in June.

Reformations: Ratcat, The Falling Joys and Melbourne’s Baseball.

Music quiz show kaput: ABC announces the demise of long-running music quiz show Spicks & Specks after seven years. Music captain Myf Warhurst describes working on the show as ?a wonderful privilege?.

June


Avalanches top triple j poll: As a precursor to the publically voted Hottest 100 Australian Albums of All Time, The Avalanches? Since I Left You tops a poll of 175 musicians, industry ?professionals? and triple j presenters. Those who thought it may be indicative of how the public will vote are later proven wrong. As for The Avalanches? protracted follow-up, band members Robbie Chater and Tony Diblasi tell triple j it’s currently sitting unmixed on a ?rickety beige Mac with software people stopped using in 1997?. Oh, and they play a ?perplexing set? at VIVID.

More ?90s reformations: Melbourne’s Underground Lovers announce they’re recording their first album since 1999?s Cold Feeling*, while Sydney’s Clouds regroup after 14 years for shows with the UK’s Jesus Jones and The Wonder Stuff. Singer Trish Young flags the possibility of more shows in an interview with *M+N.

Tyler The Creator vs Brisbane The Racist: Headline-grabbing mainman of LA’s Odd Future vents his spleen on Twitter following an alleged encounter with a ?racist asshole? at a Brisbane KFC. The story gets picked up The Brisbane Times, who cobble together his Twitter rants, including his claim ?They Hate Niggas Out Here?.

NME hype for Aus acts: Queensland outfits Bleeding Knees Club and DZ Deathrays (formerly DZ, see above) crack NME?s ?50 Best New Bands of 2011? list, coming in at an impressive #21 and #8, respectively.

M+N goes ?viral?: We launch a campaign to get elusive Tasmanian metal legend Striborg to the top of triple j’s Hottest Australian Albums of All Time. Why? Because polls like this are inane.

Luke Steele collabs with Beyonce: Shit gets real for the Sleepy Jackson/Empire of the Sun frontman when he co-writes a ?sensual R&B jam? for Beyonce’s new album 4.

More rock ?folklore? for sale: The ocean-side mansion where Tame Impala recorded their ?debutt? album Innerspeaker goes up for sale for a tick under $6-million.

Hudson’s Tassie ?king hit?: Faker’s Nathan Hudson claims he was punched in a Tassie venue following an alleged homophobic assault. The venue issues a statement saying he was drunk. Hudson later laments the fact he took the whole thing public.

Jack Ladder is not Nick Cave: In an interview with M+N, Sydney singer Jack Ladder refutes claims he sounds like a certain singer from Warracknabeal. Reviewer Shaun Prescott disagrees.

Ding Dong on fire: Melbourne’s Ding Dong Lounge forced to ?close indefinitely? following a blaze in an adjoining German restaurant.

Rocking Horse on the brink: The rise of digital downloading, high rent and the effects of Brisbane’s floods on the retail trade nearly brings Brisbane’s favourite record stores to its knees. A Facebook group is set up in protest.

Stonefield at Glasto: Despite technical difficulties, four sisters from rural Victoria play a well-received set at Glastonbury including a Led Zep cover that sparks the crowd into life.
