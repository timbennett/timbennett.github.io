Real Pain Supernova

By Everett True 2011-05-09T00:00:00+00:00

It starts as it means to finish. Full-on, heavy, a miasma of distorted guitars and thudding dreams and strangled vocals. Relentless, dependable. Push and strain. Push and strain. Plateau. Peak. Push and strain. The opening track of the third album (heavy-duty vinyl: double LP) from Brisbane’s beloved intellectuals and doom metal aficionados No Anchor is called ‘The End’ and it feels like it could be.

The second track is forgettable. ‘Wolves Bite And Disappear’. It sounds like an entire section of Brisbane is so in thrall to the tail-end of Seattle grunge circa 1993 that they still believe this to be a good and fair and relevant thing. You can hear the argument now: “Earth had to come from somewhere, you know.” Indeed. As did Boris and Sunn O))) and all those other bands that like to make, paint, vibrate and hang signs up saying ?Girls Allowed?.

The fourth track is ferocious, fantastic. ‘Dead Pony’. All off-mic screaming and incomprehensible words and guitars that blister fingers just by hearing them and drums that duplicate the DOOF DOOF DOOF of exceedingly loud music that fucks the shit out of you because you only hear it for two seconds before it’s passed by. Except that this track is three minutes long, and just keeps going.

Dead Pony by No Anchor

The final track is the one that should make grown men stumble in gutters. Gallivant, gurn, gallant … sorry, where were we? The voice box echo is set to FULL, YOU DUMB ASS. The bass is so slow and ponderous you fear it will trip over its own notes as it starts into the next line. The guitars … well, you can call them guitars but I prefer to call them amplifiers. Wrench, you fucking wench! Wrench! There’s 17 minutes, 28 seconds of this brawling, bruised, surly beauty and it ain’t enough: it’s the one time that No Anchor totally let themselves go and lose themselves in the moment. You want more. Much more. It pummels and smashes your ears into an oblivion that borders upon the pure bliss. ‘Gatton Bohemia’ it’s called, and no I don’t know why Ian Rogers and Alex Gillies and Donovan Miller need to be so oblique. It feels like it’s going to stop. It feels like it’s going to fall apart. It feels like it’s going to break out in a full-on Bear Love Fest. It fucking rules.

It also probably goes on a little too long but … well, that’s the point. It becomes so monolithic … wrong word … it transcends time.

‘Shut My Mouth’ sounds like a bunch of Tad fans got together for a 20-year reunion. Nothing wrong with that, of course.

Shut My Mouth by No Anchor

‘The Perils Of Small Town Living’ seems an odd title for a pair – sorry, triplet – of fellows who profess to enjoy living in their hometown so much. The music attempts to be ambient, softened. It’s tricksy, messes around with sequencing and stuff. It’s filler.

‘Come Again’ features – gasp! – a female on lead vocals (Jo Nilson of the Butcher Birds). In the context of music like this it feels like near heresy. Sadly, in structure it’s something approaching a song, and something approaching a song is something we really don’t need at the end of Side One. And wait … not Black Sabbath, again? Well fuck. It’s a great riff. Still, in Sub Pop 200 terminology which I’m sure we can all understand, this is Blood Circus to the full-blown wind-streaming-through-hair gorge-fest of ‘Gatton Bohemia”s Screaming Trees.

‘Key Cutter’ finishes a little like Rose Kemp’s awesome ‘Blood Medik II’ track starts. Damn, but I wish the rest of the track merited comparison.

A fine album, spoiled only by the fact half the tracks aren’t up to the standard of the other half.
