Oh Boy

By Nick Argyriou 2013-10-03T00:00:00+00:00

After working to an album-a-year system after their 2008 reformation, Oh Boy is The Paradise Motel’s first opus since 2011. Coupling angst-riddled narratives – both sonically and within Charles Bickford’s words and voice – with plenty of jocularity regarding mid-life assessments of the human condition, it hits hard. Emotional connections are easy to achieve with any Paradise Motel material, and nothing changes here. Recorded predominantly live with Casey Rice in 2011 and 2012, the band have assembled an album of rare splendour.

Generally brash and audacious when Bickford controls the mic, then tender and forlorn when vocalist Merida Sussex takes her turn, ?King of the Past? brings the pair together in seamless unison. With breathy and controlled vocals, slicing violin and jungle drums complementing the Matthew Aulich guitars, the added harmony of bassist Esme McDonald sets the song up in that trademark ,operatic-styled vein.

Lined up against 2010?s conceptually themed Australian Ghost Story* and 2011 record *I Still Hear Your Voice at Night*, *Oh Boy is a heftier release by way of musicality, but the songwriting heart remains true. Relying significantly less on the atmospheric tweaks, desolate strings and the Sussex vocal of the past, it stands alone.

From odes to Sydney (?City of Fortune?), and coping with loss (?East of Early Losses?), third track ?The Spider? is the album’s epic, coming in at just over seven minutes and finding Bickford riffing on the dreaded male midlife. ?The middle is a trembling place, set between hope and disgrace,? he offers as he launches into one effective and rather obscure rant after the next – a common house spider used as voyeur to the happenings. Commenting on daily minutiae, he delivers a series of prophetic statements: ?Wipe your arse, fill the car – fuck your wife, love your child – don’t be tired – live[s] on dust and free porn? in one of the more absorbing, Nietzsche-esque sonnets of 2013.

Bickford has previously admitted to the media (back in late 2011) that Oh Boy was influenced by the ensemble’s live shows, which had become ?wilder? and ?harder-rocking.? The record was also due out in March 2012, but that’s another story.

Theoretical writings expressed in gothic and noir scenes have forever defined the band yet have never limited their ambition. Album closer ?He was Alive? is perhaps a sign of a genre they’ve yet to explore, with its gyratory pop movements setting them apart from much of what has been dished up to date. Invest the time into Oh Boy and survey the Paradise Motel mindset, as it rousingly exposes itself verse upon metaphysical verse. It’s quite the liberating exercise.
