Surface Paradise

By Patrick 2009-08-01T00:00:00+00:00

If you listen to the apparently prophetic words of Clay Shirky, Mark Pesce or other self-appointed gurus of futuristic discourse, the world is in a state of flux; a permanent white water of cultural change where the power relationship between state and citizen is being undermined and subverted in the wake of social networking sites, blogging and whatever other socio-media-technological creature is nominated as flavour of the week.

That you can head out to the suburbs and see row upon row of replica housing replete with modern conveniences churning out greenhouse gases faster than the bullshit spewing from Kyle Sandilands? moronic mouth is, apparently, part of the paradigm threatening the world we live in. The more we’re empowered by technology, the more we’re beholden to the bland rhetoric of the specious service sector and the idiocy of the entertainment industry.

It’s this contemporary cultural paradox that Root!, led by DC Root (widely acknowledged these days to be the artist previously known as TISM’s Humphrey B Flaubert), continues to explore. On their second full-length album Surface Paradise, Root’s sociological focus is littered with the kind of satire, sarcasm and insights that Hugh Mackay would do well to ponder. There’s the suburban dystopia of shopping malls, gargantuan houses and plasma TV screens (?Surface Paradise?, ?(Sort of) Emo?, ?My Other Bumper Sticker is Intelligent?, ?Crown Tower Blues?, ?Home??). Elsewhere, Root! explore the latent narcissism that underpins the modern media environment, where Andy Warhol’s dream of 15 minutes of fame has been distorted into mindless, self-indulgent observations on popular culture, politics, food and household appliances (?Joe Blogs?, ?Famous For Being Famous For Being Famous?, ?I Hang Out With the Guys in Jet (?s Uncle)?, ?Thank God You’re Here?). Squeezed in for good measure are plenty of caustic observations on the pseudo-religious adoration afforded to management consultants (?Orange People?) and the entertainment industry’s institutional bias toward juvenile pop stars (?I Fought the Groove Police?).

The music is at its best when it reverts to the country twang first embraced by Root! and propelled by guitarist Henry Root. The band’s electronic pop dalliances, on the other hand, don’t always work as well as planned. With lyrics as scathing as this, only the sharpest licks will suffice.

As someone raised in the suburbs, a professional life plagued by facile change management discourse and an irrational disdain for social networking sites and blogging, the philosophical ranting of DC Root! resonates like an Anthony Robbins seminar on ?Completely Obvious Rules For Your Life?. With Kevin Rudd hell bent on reducing language to the quality and consistency of half-set jelly, thank fuck for Root!.
