Readers Poll 2010: The Shame Cauldron Decides Pt 1

By Darren Levin 2010-12-17T00:00:00+00:00

The polls have officially closed for this year’s Readers Poll, and we’d like to thank everyone for making this the biggest in ‘M+N’ history. Without further ado, we present the winners of Best Controversy (is ?winner? the right word here?), Best Comeback, Best New-ish Act and Best Live Act. On Monday, we reveal your top 50 selections for Album of the Year.

Biggest Controversy

What are the odds of 2010?s biggest controversy happening the day before we closed the polls? Short Stack-gate may’ve ignited the M+N* cauldron of shame? like no other controversy before – it makes the Muscles saga of 2009 look like a comparative cakewalk – but it just missed out on being eligible. Now, we know what you’re thinking – ?thats why there are rubberes on pencils?, right? – but Andy Clemmenson (or his friend) will have to wait until next year to receive his winning copy of *The 100 Best Australian Albums (Hardie Grant, RRP $59.95).


1. Victoria’s Liquor Licensing regime/The Tote calls last drinks

It started with a press release and ended up with 20,000 people screaming ?It’s A Long Way To The Top? in full voice on Parliament steps. The closure of Melbourne’s iconic The Tote was nothing short of cataclysmic. It brought to light a flawed system that linked live music to alcohol-related violence (sanity prevailed, and it’s since been de-linked); planted the seed for Music Victoria; and reminded Melburnians just how fucking great it is that places like The Tote actually exist so we can go back to being apathetic about them again. Even though it re-opened under new ownership in June with a taco van and shiny PA, former proprietor Bruce Milne ended up losing stacks of money and the nine years he poured into the venue – but it wasn’t in vain. Let’s just hope the Liberals don’t stuff things up again.

2. The ARIAs debacle

Where do we start? Ricki-Lee’s homophobic ?wristies?? Jessica Mauboy saying ?de-butt? twice (and also, reportedly, in rehearsal)? A disgraceful 10-second tribute to James Freud? Bob Katter mispronouncing ARIA (see below)? Jessica Mauboy calling Tame Impala’s album ?Innerspeak? – twice? Cutbacks to people’s shoes? Steve Kilbey being creepy? In short: Network Ten tried a format change and ended up with a face full of failcake.

3. BAM! festival

The 2010 controversy that just kept on giving. It began with a parody announcement, got a whole lot more serious when we sent a mole to an artist meeting, prompted a please explain from its founder and ended up getting canned after a last-minute ticketing plea failed to convince punters to spend $200 on 80 bands they’ve never heard of.

4. ?Down Under? copyright claim

In which a Federal Court judge deemed Men At Work’s 1982 hit ?Down Under? to have reproduced a substantial part of folk standard ?Kookaburra Sits in the Old Gum Tree?. Cut Copy – you’re next.

5. Bob Katter presents ARIA for Best Indie Release

Best Comeback


1. Kim Salmon & The Surrealists

Kim Salmon has never gone away, but his Surrealists have. Formed in 1985, with The Scientists teetering on the brink of implosion, the band released six albums before Salmon decided to retire the moniker following You Gotta Let Me Do My Own Thing* in 1997. A one-off reformation show in Spain in 2006 heralded a new-look line-up – Salmon, Phil Collings (drums) and Stu Thomas (bass) – and planted the seed for a full-scale release. Out in March, The Surrealists? first album in 13 years *Grand Unifying Theory is full of ?venomous energy, cynicism and ironic observation? and capped off by a 25-minute, two-part instrumental. It was backed up with rare shows in Melbourne and Sydney, and ended up being the final release on much-loved Melbourne label Low Transit Industries.

2. Jebediah

Nineties indie-pop stalwarts Jebediah returned with a new label and a ?breakneck two-minute punk jam? that’s about a million miles away from The Basement Birds. Thank fuck for that!

3. Smudge

While they played the odd gig in 2009, Smudge were particularly active as they entered their 19th year. They embarked on their first European tour in 16 years in May; played headline shows in Melbourne, Sydney and Adelaide and Brisbane; recently supported The Lemonheads; and released a career-spanning retrospective through Half A Cow. Even Everett True liked it. They’re also working on new material, as drummer Alison Galloway told us in a recent interview.

4. Hoodoo Gurus

Twenty-nine years after their conception, The Gurus returned with their ninth studio album Purity of Essence*, their first since 2004?s *Mach Schau. ?With its catchy licks and Faulkner’s trademark sardonic humour, it confirms there’s life in the Gurus yet,? wrote Patrick Emery in an ?Icons? feature around the time of its release.

5. Muscles

Unperturbed by the internet hate machine and with a positive new outlook, Muscles dropped a cheekily titled EP, Younger and Immature*, a collection of ?thumping rave tunes? released in place of much anticipated second album *Manhood*. It was his first recording since 2007 debut LP *Guns, Babes, Lemonade and, surprisingly, it was put out through Modular.

Best New-Ish Act


1. Eagle & The Worm

A nine-piece Melbourne collective that burst out of the blocks with a ?boisterous double A-side? filled with multi-part harmonies, horn sections, cartoonish vocals and an unmistakable love of The Stones. Turns out the whole thing was conceptualised by Jarrad Brown – who splits his time between other Melbourne outfits Joe Neptune, Custom Kings and Your Animal – in his bedroom. For second single ?All I Know?, Brown mined Brazil’s tropicalia movement for inspiration. It bodes well for the band’s debut album, which was reportedly cut in three days with Brown’s favourite musician friends at the start of the year. Look out for it sometime in 2011.

2. kyu

Sydney vocal duo. Released debut, self-titled album through Popfrenzy in 2010. We called it ?immensely fulfilling?.

3. World’s End Press

Dancey Melbourne four-piece, who released one of the songs of the year in ?Only The Brave?.

4. Seja

Touring member of Regurgiator and The Mess Hall stepped out on her own for the finest synth-based pop record of the year.

5. Cabins

Ivy League’s smouldering new poster boys. Their debut Bright Victory recalls The Walkmen, Bob Dylan and The Velvet Underground.

6. Blank Realm

Around since 2004, but really coming into their own with pop-oriented single ?Full Moon Door?.

7. Bed Wettin? Bad Boys

Yet another great band off the Sydney garage-rock conveyor belt. Debut 7? ?Best Band In Sydney?/?Worst Band In Sydney? is ?magnifique?.

8. White Woods

Hobart transplants shift to Melbourne and record a ?staggering? debut, Bellplay.

9. 6s & 7s

New Perth outfit for The Stickfigures? Josh Fontaine. Debut album Choose The Sentinel Blooze has more echo than the Grand Canyon.

T10. New War

Mel and Chris from Seattle’s Shoplifting move to Melbourne and form a menacing, synth-laced band.

T10. Total Control

Another quality Mikey Young side project. One of the best bands at the recent Gonerfest in Memphis.

Best Live Act


1. The Drones

They lost out to Wagons in last year’s poll, so it was about time The Drones made amends. This year, they won the Best Live Act category by a landslide – not that they had to try very hard. An unusually unproductive year for Australia’s hardest working band – Gareth Liddiard was busy recording his debut solo record and playing shows in support – The Drones made infrequent live appearances, but when they did it was almost uniformly special. Their set at The Tote’s last drinks, which included a cover of GOD’s ?My Pal? with Joel Silbersher on vocals, was one of the greatest things we’ve ever seen.
