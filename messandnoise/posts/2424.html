Alps Of New South Wails

By ReneSchaefer 2011-02-11T00:00:00+00:00

Chris Hearn, the man who is Alps, refuses to stand still. While the cover of his latest record links it to his previous EP Cycles, he has ditched the more overtly intellectual and hermetic approach which defined that recording. On this final part of a loosely themed album trilogy he embraces a more dynamic sound, adding occasional contributions from members of fellow Newcastle band Bare Grillz to his guitar and keyboard-driven compositions.

This is pop music of a kind, albeit a kind of home-spun, rough-as-guts interpretation of what pop can be. The format of short bursts of melodic vocals, dirty guitar and dinky electronics works a treat, as each snippet presents a very distinct picture. Ideas are stated in the most simple and straightforward way possible and then make way for the next burst of energy. As always, succinctness works in Alps? favour.

Pop pickers will have a field day with this album, as Hearn compiles a virtual post-punk jukebox that cherry-picks the best of British and international bedsit pop of the early 1980s. Rather than creating a pastiche of the past though, looking back is merely a starting point for songs that are firmly rooted in the present.

Maybe it’s down to the good taste with which reference points have been chosen: There is the distorted thrash of ?A Wailing and a Gnashing of Teeth?, reminiscent of Warsaw’s earliest punk fumblings; ?Calling God on the Telephone? evokes the nervous dance beat of The Fire Engines; while ?Life is Taking? recalls the dramatically melancholic synth pop of Fad Gadget and Soft Cell.

Elsewhere, there are nods to the Dunedin Sound and the Scottish Postcard label (?Sail Away?), the minimalism of Young Marble Giants, and the darkly sweet malice of Legendary Pink Dots. ?No Condition? strips The Ramones of their buzzsaw guitar, while maintaining their signature three-chord sugar rush. ?Novocastrian Hunger? could be The Dead C jamming in the room next door while someone in the kitchen is pushing beer cans down the In-Sink-Erator.

In lesser hands, this magpie approach could easily fail to enthrall, but at the heart of Alps? method is a disarming playfulness. It’s summed up by the cover image – a child-like joy at putting on disguises while dispensing little nuggets of wisdom. Each song could be the starting point for a whole other band, but becoming the latest hip retro act is not what Hearn has in mind. There is conceptual thought behind his eclecticism, a toying with signifiers that provides a clever context for a body of work that concerns itself with the ?strange nostalgia for the future? that is so prevalent in popular music these days.

Alps Of New South Wails is aimed at an audience as conversant in the history of post-punk as the artist himself. It’s the antithesis of bands that cynically pilfer from the vaults in order to appear novel to virgin ears in search of exciting new sounds. If this makes this album sound like an academic exercise, nothing could be further from the truth. Songs like ?I Get So High? and ?Sail Away? are perfectly capable of making people bounce around the living room and grin maniacally at their simple perfection, which just goes to show that you can have it both ways: smart shit can also be fun.
