Syndrome Syndrome

By lawsonfletcher 2013-02-03T00:00:00+00:00

Sometimes it’s hard not to think of the Australian bush as a giant, psychosis-inducing crime scene that swallows up lives and provides cover for all sorts of weird and malignant proclivities. From Burke and Wills to Picnic at Hanging Rock, the curse of the coloniser seems inscribed into every journey inland.

On first blush, Gardland’s Syndrome Syndrome is another slice of abstract techno, maybe produced anywhere between Detroit and Berlin, but listen closely and all you’ll begin to hear is a very local landscape. Along with acts like Dro Carey, Horse Macgyver and Kangaroo Skull – not to mention the roster of Hunter Gatherer, a Sydney label run by the duo – Gardland are leading a techno renaissance down under. It’s a movement fascinated, in the words of Shaun Prescott, ?with sonic coldness and gridded, infinite space,? but also, one senses, with the bleakness at the heart of our country.

Shuddering with sandblasted, metallic textures, these brutalist analogue synth workouts are, like the best techno, at once immense and sparse. It’s the perfect evocation of a monstrous landscape both agoraphobically empty and deathly dense. Atmospherically, we’re talking The Hunter*, *Wolf Creek, all those films about people getting lost in the bush and doing weird things, or about those people who choose to live an outsider’s existence beneath the eye of the southern sky.

Perhaps the uncanny sounds of wildlife and rustling branches in parts of ?Ode to Ode? and ?Trepan Heke? were what Malcolm Naden heard when he ran from deserted bush hut to rundown cabin to dried-up creek in his long, lonely seven years in hiding; some kind of bizarre psycho-geography, the paranoia of the fugitive slowly eating away at him.

In other words, this is a pretty heavy album – establishing and maintaining this haunting, suspended mood almost solely through hardware-based jams, telling an Australian ghost story not by fireside but through repeating machines in the flash of the club. Spiralling and, at times, unsettlingly austere, Syndrome Syndrome evades capture but compels a return to the scene of the crime. ?I dreamt I was a footstep in the trail of a murderer.?
