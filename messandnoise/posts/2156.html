She?s So Hard

By DomAlessio 2009-11-06T00:00:00+00:00

There’s something almost Brechtian about Sydney quartet The Jezabels. Waifish vocalist Hayley Mary evokes as much carnage as a Bret Easton Ellis novel, yet she does it in a slightly more enigmatic way. It’s a murderous skinfest on ?Hurt Me?, which opens the band’s second EP She’s So Hard, but it’s not necessarily a sadistic bloody fantasy on her behalf. Mary’s allusions to Abel in the lyrics could make this a reverse Cain and Abel story, but it could just as easily be her reflections on a tumultuous past relationship. But hey, the way her narratives jump between tenses, veil their metaphors and commingle their characters, ?Hurt Me? might also be about some cannibalistic pre-gig ritual that The Jezabels perform before they go on stage. Then again, probably not.

Still, it’s a pretty confronting way to open The Jezabels? second recording, with Mary wailing, ?Hurt me, hurt me?, and, ?Cut me, serve me on the dinner table.? In truth the Sydney four-piece have always been fairly liberal with drama in their music, and their first EP The Man Is Dead* perhaps suffered from a little too much of it. On that disc, Mary’s Kate Bush-like yelping grated on me far too often and it felt incongruent with what the rest of the band were doing, which was essentially a left-field take on indie disco. But this time each member of The Jezabels has found their place. Pianist Heather Shannon channels The Jezabels? natural pomp and circumstance into melody lines that lead the music, while Sam Lockwood opts for atmospheric guitar lines. He evokes The Edge throughout *She’s So Hard*, saturating his trilled guitar lines in reverb like something from *The Joshua Tree.

The Jezabels are now learning to play as a unit. With The Man Is Dead it sounded like Shannon, Lockwood and Mary were all vying to be the centre of attention. Now it’s much more structured: Mary’s pared her vocals back, singing in her natural range (and, thankfully, not about ?disco biscuits?); Lockwood builds tension and atmosphere; and Shannon is the melodic driver. And I don’t want to downplay the role of drummer Nik Kaloper, but he’s an easy member to forget, mainly because he never does anything wrong. His rhythmic patterns are subtly in sync with the mood of the music.

It’s hard to ignore Hayley Mary though, both on- and off-stage. Live, she gyrates like Peter Garrett is her choreographer, but on record, and particularly on She’s So Hard, it’s her dramatic narratives, fuelled by neuroses, that make Mary the focal point. I say The Jezabels are Brechtian because Mary’s lyrics are so dark – they have songs like ?Violent Dream? and ?The Man Is Dead? – but the music and the clean, off-white liner notes project ethereality.

Bloody, lovesick, heartbroken, amputated characters aside, She’s So Hard is a much more palatable EP by The Jezabels. They still lack some sonic variety, but the steps they’ve taken since their debut EP is indicative of a band cognisant of their own strengths and weaknesses. Or maybe the instrumentalists are all scared that Mary’s bloodthirsty words will manifest if they don’t sharpen up and fly straight? Then again, probably not.
