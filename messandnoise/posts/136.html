One-day Wonders

By Darren Levin 2008-09-16T00:00:00+00:00

There’s nothing like a day in the studio to keep the creative fires burning, The Vandas Chris Altmann tells DARREN LEVIN.


How does a record two years in the making – four, if you start at day dot – take one day to complete?

?We just had a really great first day,? explains The Vandas singer/guitarist Chris Altmann, referring to a summer’s afternoon well spent at Soundpark studio in Northcote, Victoria.

?On the first day, we were really happy with the takes we got, so we figured there wasn’t really much point re-doing them. It’d be better spending our time mixing it and getting the guitars sounding good.?

While the band spent a further two days at Soundpark followed by two weeks of mixing in ?glorious analogue? at Newmarket Studios, it was that initial session that provided the raw material for Slow Burn. Even the vocals, says Altmann, were cut live.

?We wanted the album to have a live feel to it, so the most important thing was to get really good takes.?

What it lacks in sheen, Slow Burn more than makes up for in visceral energy. With their live mixer Justin Hermes manning the desk along with producer Joel Silbersher (GOD, Hoss), the band cut loose, lending a sense of desperation and vitality to the album’s 10 tracks. The ragged, dual guitar interplay of bands like Crazy Horse and the MC5, says Altmann, were the inspiration behind the rough-and-ready approach.

?As long as the overall energy of the song was right, we didn’t mind having a few squeaks and squeals here and there. I’ve always liked that about a lot of older recordings. Most recordings now are way too sterile, the guitars are layered two or three times, everything is all flattened out and shaped. I like to hear that it’s [been recorded] live.?

The album’s sense of urgency also belies how it came into being. When the band entered the studio in January this year, it was off the back of a two-year studio absence. Their last release – the acclaimed EP, In The Morning – came out in late 2006.

As long as the overall energy of the song was right, we didn’t mind having a few squeaks and squeals here and there.

?We had about a year or two when we didn’t have a manager. We thought we’d self manage — and it was hard to manage!? jokes Altmann, adding that new manager Dan Craddock has been a godsend. ?We got slack and we were all involved doing other stuff and everything slips by quickly. Before we knew it, it’d been a year or two gone.?

A chance meeting with Silbersher at Altmann and guitarist Mikey Madden’s former workplace was the catalyst for their long-awaited debut. The trio bonded over a record player set up on the factory floor.

?We used to bring in vinyl all the time,? recalls Altmann. ?Joel’s into a lot of cool music and he has a really extensive knowledge, so when you’re talking about sounds, he knows exactly what you’re looking for.?

Silbersher, who Altmann first saw play with Tex Perkins and The Dark Horses in Adelaide, dutifully played the role of taskmaster, bringing a sense of perfectionism to proceedings.

?He made sure everyone put in 100 per cent effort,? Altmann says. ?We can be slack sometimes and it’s easy to think near enough is good enough, but Joel is very quick to point out the shortfalls. Everyone made sure they were doing the best they could because they didn’t want to get told off by Joel.?

While the album has a distinctly harder edge to their earlier country-tinged material (Silbersher’s garage-rock pedigree perhaps?), Altmann insists that Slow Burn wasn’t meant to mark an aesthetic shift.

?We didn’t want the album to be a bold new experiment for the band, or to explore new territories,? he says.
?We liked the sound of our live shows so we wanted the album to be a good representation of us live. It’s probably a good thing we waited so long, because it’s taken us that long to get the confidence to do that.?

Slow Burn is out now through Liberation Music.
