Track By Track: Four Hours Sleep

By Mess+Noise 2014-05-04T14:00:00+00:00

Eight years after the last Four Hours Sleep album, BILL McDONALD reconnects with longtime collaborators Peter Luscombe (Paul Kelly), Dan Luscombe (The Drones), Angie Hart (Frente) and Stephen Cummings (The Sports), plus many guests on top of that. The band are also making a video for every track on the record.

?Think I’m Ready?

We tried a few approaches to this song, but couldn’t quite get the right feel. The version on the album captures the mood, i.e. that no matter how long you wait wisdom will never come, certainly not through any age-related revelation. Angie and Stephen’s voices sound so good together, a combination that might not appear obvious at first. A nice baritone backing vocal from my old friend Patrick Williams.

?Sing (For Better Times)?

A song dedicated to the great drummer, and good friend to us all, Peter Jones. He’s not gone, he’s just dead. Vika and Linda Bull give a great uplifting power to the idea of carrying on, and trying to learn something along the way. Ray Pereira, who played with PJ for years in Dianna Kiss, plays some great African percussion, and there’s even a drum solo!

Four Hours Sleep (brand new album) by Four Hours Sleep

?Early in the Morning?

Stephen Cummings is a great singer – that is well known. Here he sings, in a very soulful way, about the pleasures of other people taking drugs and staying up late. Linda Bull appears again with slinky backing vocals, and Arne Hanna plays some very funky guitar.

?Green Arrow?

This song is built around a long 16-bar bass loop and Lilith Lane’s vocal stylings. The reverbs and effects on her voice are the result of some lucky mistakes in my studio. I try to use Pro Tools like a tape recorder where tracks bleed into one another and react to the interference. The problem comes when trying to take those results into another studio for mixing ; they never sound the same, so for this track I ended up doing a sub mix at home and bringing it to Shane [O’Mara]?s studio for the final mix. Worth the hassle, I think. Peter Luscombe added real drums in the studio in one complete take, so that at least saved some time.

?Test Your Love?

This is actually a run-through where we were experimenting with various ideas to best work with Angie’s brilliant lyrics. The great thing about working with experienced engineers like Tim Johnston is that they know when to record. We played the song through, improvising an arrangement on the fly. Sometimes you do 20 takes of a song to get it right, sometimes one is enough. Dan overdubbed the organ and synth melody against his own guitar.

?Marie Antoinette?

I stole a chord progression from a weird cabaret song-and-dance man that I backed many years ago in one of the strangest gigs I’ve ever done. His name was Movin? Melvin Brown. The structure is a simple eight-bar, two-chord progression, but divided into a group of three and a group of five instead of the normal two groups of four. I always look for the small variations that can make a regular something different. We used the cut-up technique for the lyrics and melody. Who knows what it means: something angry about buildings and food.

?You Left Your Mark on Me?

I first saw Angie sing in about 1992. There is no one who sings or writes like her; she is unique. It’s like she has direct unfettered access to her own heart. Geoff O’Connor from The Crayon Fields once said something about not needing to be playing loud minor chords to express angst or melancholy and I completely agree; sometimes that is the easy way. Dan makes the old upright piano weep, and Stefanie Thom plays some beautiful high strings in the style of ?Walk on the Wild Side?.

?A Multitude of Sins?

I take a lot of song titles from clich’s used in the news media, and I had a tiny tape loop of a church bell and a bird chirping I recorded on tour somewhere, and I really liked the soundtrack) to the movie The Social Network, and I had a nice outtake loop from Lilith Lane, and I really like having instrumental tracks on albums, so there you are.

?Wriggle On?

I like how old blues players change chords when they need to and don’t stick to the format. This boogie started with that in mind, with a steal from ?What a Wonderful World? by Louis Armstrong, which was a big hit in the ?60s. As you can see on our video clip, it is live in the studio in one complete take with a couple of overdubs added later, but no edits. Dan plays some great angular melodic guitar, an element of his style that I really enjoy. I love playing with Pete and Dan; we’re all different from each other, but manage to meet somewhere at the crossroads.
