Eyes Like The Sky

By Aaron 2013-04-08T00:00:00+00:00

King Gizzard and the Lizard Wizard’s exuberant 2012 debut LP, 12 Bar Bruise, was generally well-regarded by critics and listeners, an opinion this writer shared. What criticisms it received mainly focused on two points: a perceived lack of originality, that this was garage-psych-by-numbers too deeply in thrall to bands like Thee Oh Sees; or discomfort with the ockerisms and juvenile lyrics of spirited romps like ?Footy Footy? and ?Uh Oh, I Called Mum?.

The Victoria seven-piece neatly sidestep both of those criticisms, as well as the pressures of the ?second album blues?, with the release of Eyes Like the Sky mere months later. Rather than more noisy guitar pop with similar lyrical themes, they’ve made the unusual move of recording a self-described ?spaghetti western audio book? instead, all twanging riffs, thunderous drums and ghostly harmonica, evocatively written and raspingly narrated by Broderick Smith (ex-The Dingoes), father of the band’s harmonica player Ambrose Kenny-Smith.

The God Mans Goat Lust by flightless

This direction isn’t a total surprise if you’ve heard their earlier collaboration, ?Sam Cherry’s Last Shot?, a spoken-word standout on 12 Bar Bruise* that sat oddly yet at ease alongside the band’s self-written material. *Eyes Like the Sky was recorded quickly in ?comfortable conditions at home – narrated directly into a MacBook microphone,? resulting in a densely plotted half-hour narrative set along the unforgiving border of America and Mexico in the latter half of the 19th century, accompanied by atmospheric music and explosive sound effects.

Eyes Like the Sky* is a blood-soaked *bildungsroman* of slavery and revenge, which sounds like Tarantino and *Django Unchained* but plays out more like *Little Big Man rewritten by Cormac McCarthy as a screenplay for John Hillcoat or Andrew Dominik. The protagonist, a Mexican-Irish boy kidnapped by Native Americans and renamed Eyes Like the Sky, negotiates a path through lethal challenges while those around him fall to a swift and gory end.

?I love Western films,? enthuses guitarist and songwriter Stu MacKenzie. ?I love bad guys, Red Dead Redemption* – and evil guitars?. You can tell, and the results on tracks like the swaggering ?Year of Our Lord? and the predatorily eerie ?The God Man’s Goat Lust? are convincing. The language and plotting may not be as powerful or original as the stories that inspired them – say, the scripts of *Deadwood* or novels like *Blood Meridian – but that’s a pretty tall order. What the band have produced here is engaging and compelling, revealing a deep affection for the genre and its traits, whether the ringing melodies and percussive gunshots of Morricone’s soundtracks or the downbeat tone of cinema by Sam Peckinpah or Anthony Mann, all burdened with the inevitability of violence.

Year Of Our Lord by flightless

How many times can one hear the same story? This album may not have the shelf life of its predecessor, but it’s an inventive and entertaining diversion on the way to wherever reverb-soaked place The Gizzards end up next. More than that, they’ve bought themselves time enough to deliver something extra special with their next release, and Eyes Like the Sky ably reinforces that Mackenzie and company have the imagination and nous to do exactly that.
