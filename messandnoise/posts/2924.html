Lowtide

By Edward Sharp-Paul 2014-07-23T00:00:00+00:00

Lowtide have had an unassuming existence to this point, refining their sound across the same half-dozen Melbourne venues as everyone else. As good as they’ve become, though, there’s always been something self-effacing about them, a shadow-dwelling modesty that keeps them at a safe distance from the hushed platitudes that some of their peers enjoy. Their debut album is good enough to change all that, and while one suspects that Lowtide will slip out into the world in customarily quiet, beautiful fashion, it deserves to spend a little time in the aggregated hearts and ear canals of the marginal culture.

They offer something different, but not radically so. Their two-bass setup certainly sets them apart, but they don’t beat you over the head with it – the second low-end instrument is generally deployed like a rhythm guitar, or in near-unison with the more ?bass? of the two basses, creating a woolly chorus effect. Really, though, the album’s most arresting action is happening elsewhere, in the upper reaches of their expansive sound.

Reverb is the breakout star of this album. Forming great billowing drifts, hooking itself to guitars, keys, snares and voices alike: the enveloping reverb glues everything together, burying individual elements a bit, but also creating such a beguiling mood that it hardly matters. If that doesn’t sound like your thing, maybe look elsewhere for your kicks. For everyone else, Lowtide is an album to be luxuriated in.

They are unmistakably inspired by shoegaze, but it’s more of the airy, poppy Ride variety than the more abrasive strains, right down to the twinned vocals (boy/girl in this case) and persistent echoes of jangle-pop. The best of it is very good. ?Blue Movie? gracefully drifts and then surges, and ?Wedding Ring? has the raw, giddy naivet? of any number of C86)-spawned bands.

Like Ride, they’re sometimes guilty of over-sweetening the mix. The instrumental ?Maxill? Leaving, Seaward?, for instance, exposes shortcomings that are only hinted at elsewhere. As long as they keep cracking along with sufficient momentum and foreboding, the simplicity of Lowtide’s songcraft is no problem. When you take that away you have the harmonic na’vet?, but none of the grandeur that they’re obviously reaching for. They’re better when they keep things simple, propulsive and laced with foreboding, and most of Lowtide hits that mark effortlessly.

It’s not an album that will change lives, or direct us along the road to Damascus. That’s not their game. But neither are Lowtide undercutting or underselling themselves. They make beautiful sounds. That’s it, really.
