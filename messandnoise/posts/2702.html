Beyond Light

By Barnaby Smith 2012-11-06T00:00:00+00:00

Big, huge, miserable and aggressive analogue synths are a sure-fire way of evoking a certain disarming mood that is a prerequisite of excellent pop music. Even in inept hands, such a booming assault can sweep a listener up like the so-called “jungle rhythms” of rock ?n? roll that once so infected young people. It is not even really a matter of artistic merit, but purely a sensory, physical thing.

Considering that, it is to the current generation’s credit that many of today’s most important artists are able to take this emotional banker and twist it in ways that are genuinely a step forward – the likes of Factory Floor and, of course, a good deal of metal. Perhaps it’s all a question of balancing the momentous with the more skittish and superficial. It’s something Factory Floor do expertly, as did early Aphex Twin. Both artists are comparable with Tom Hall of AXXONN, a man who has left Australia for Los Angeles – a move which seems to have broadened his palette and allowed his signature electronic drone, such a definitive feature of his earlier work, to take on other flavours.

AXXONN – Sequel to Acid by AXXONN

Opener ?Sky’s One Kite? contains riffs, refrains, melodies even, yet against a blurry screech that still gives an impression of the abrasiveness to come on the album. ?Sequel to Acid?, too, is a mixture of fairly random electronic noise and a rhythmic, extremely clubby main theme. It is arguably the high point of the album, a combination of Hall’s old ways and new horizons.

Beyond Light may be open to being called “experimental,” or even veering on occasion into sound art, but one thing it is not is minimalist. Hall has chosen to, frankly, layer on everything that his machines allow him to. On none of these tracks does he offer space or pause for breath. But despite this richness, when the vocals of JZH emerge on ?Oceans Clear? it is hugely affecting, her wispy tones at the top of the mix bringing humanity back to an album that at various points feels like tipping over entirely into the artificial and mechanical. That respite doesn’t last long, though, the quite majestic ?Holda? re-establishing the relentless and nocturnal heartbeat of the album.

AXXONN – Dark Cut by AXXONN

Indeed, despite the fact that there are varying levels of energy and the odd drop of euphoria, Beyond Light is, to point out the obvious, an album of night music best experienced in a half-aware state. It is certainly not pretty. Midnight Juggernauts said their music, and their name, was designed to convey the idea of an “unstoppable force in the middle of the night.” Their innate tendency towards the tuneful meant they never quite managed it, but AXXONN fits that description to perfection.
