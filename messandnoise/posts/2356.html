D?j? What?

By Shaun 2010-09-01T00:00:00+00:00

This is probably the end of an era for Blank Realm. First released in July this year, D’j? What?* was originally a limited run CD-R compiled to promote shows the Brisbane four-piece played in Melbourne. It’s an important release because it captures the band in a transitional phase: Blank Realm’s pop inclinations are no longer a peripheral concern, and here they’ve moved further away from the thickets of feedback and free form hubris they started to shake off on their previous 2010 release *Heatless Ark*. *D’j? What?‘s inevitable consignment to obscurity needed to be stopped, and thanks to Brisbane label Bedroom Suck the crisis has been averted in the form of a vinyl reissue.

Blank Realm’s transformation has been a slow and natural one. Evidence that a pop band was lurking beneath the fug emerged on their two Not Not Fun cassettes and blossomed with their first 2010 LP Heatless Ark. This is pop of the spiky and loose-of-limb variety, with flailing melodies and zigzag vapour-trails, all submerged in a smeared, edge-of-sleep reverie. Blank Realm’s music has always sparkled: not with polish but as if thickly glazed in syrup, all the sharper edges pressed and smudged. Mikey Young’s treatment of this reissue hasn’t sharpened their signature approach – Blank Realm’s components still self-cannibalise until what’s left is a swell of strange sound whose origins seem remote. It sounds untraceable, with no scene or geographical watermarks, freer than most ?free? music and willing to follow tangents and traditionally ?bad? ideas.

‘Full Moon Door’ is in keeping with this aesthetic, but it’s their first truly effective pop song. Whole ensembles worth of technicolour driftwood linger just beneath the surface, hidden from cursory ears, like a phantom song keeping time in the shadows. Sarah and Daniel Spencer’s vocals are oddly detached from the synth and bass guided groove beneath: they levitate unsteadily in the mix like some serendipitous transmission, oblivious to the see-saw bassline dipping and rising beyond. The song’s most assertive elements are haunted by a slow-to-approach piano and acoustic guitar arrangement, which transforms the initially buoyant tune into something graceful and beautiful, all shadows and reflections.

Nothing else is as good as ‘Full Moon Door’, but only because it’s the most developed Blank Realm song to date, leaving the rest of the record feeling a bit like old news. But it’s still good news, with the hysterical tumble and fold of ‘My Nativity?, a more extroverted example of Blank Realm’s pop smarts. Their jams here are as narcotic and illusory as ever, with Luke Spencer’s ceaseless lead spills exploding against the mix into hundreds of tiny fluorescent dust mites. He’s a very assertive player who – despite his tendencies to get stuck in attack-mode (particularly on ‘Trained Creep’) – always helps to elucidate the glowering planet Blank Realm have happened upon. At times he sounds like he’s burning up in its atmosphere.

All this finds Blank Realm in an unusual position for a one-time jam band: there’s now something at stake. A future blueprint has been forwarded here, and there’s the feeling that if they don’t decide to run with it the whole enterprise will lose its value. Of course, the band will do what they want, but if the next Blank Realm record comprises even slightly inferior versions of ‘Full Moon Door’ they’ll prove themselves to be a very special group indeed. Their current live show is full of songs of this calibre, so it seems inevitable that Australian rock music will be getting a very potent shot in the arm shortly.
