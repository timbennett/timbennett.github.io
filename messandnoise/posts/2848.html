Don?t Tell The Driver

By Andrew P Street 2013-12-02T00:00:00+00:00

A spoonful of sugar helps the poison go down.

There’s a book described in Martin Amis’s The Information, a manuscript that’s physically unfinishable because every publisher who reads it gets increasingly ill the further they get into it.

Now, I’m not about to claim that the rhythmically complex and musically atmospheric new album from Mick Turner has a similar effect, but both myself and my editor fell ill while spinning this album for review. And yes, correlation is not causation, but maybe it’s worth eating your greens before listening to Don’t Tell the Driver. You know, just in case.

The much-cited big development here is the addition of vocals – mainly from the throat of one Caroline Kennedy McCracken, late of The Plums and Deadstar. And as someone who’s played ?Au Revoir Sex Kitten? pretty much every day since he was about 15, might I just make clear how good it is to hear that voice again.

However, even when the melodies are gorgeously sweet there’ll be something there to keep you off-balance: a slightly out-of-tune piano, a bass just behind the beat, a discordant melodica burst. This might be slow-tempo, largely acoustic and often instrumental music, but there’s little soothing about Don’t Tell the Driver.

The opening ?All Gone? sounds like it’s about to collapse in a welter of out-of-time instruments that somehow remain locked together. It’s like an endless loop of a drunken jam, interrupted with backwards guitars and keyboard overtones, and the first indication that this is going to be a woozy ride.

The title track has the fragile beauty of NYC neo-classical ensemble Rachel’s, contrasting with ?Long Way Gone?, which rests on slow-building brass from Peggy Frew and Dave Henry, with former Laughing Clown and regular Ed Kuepper right-hand Jeffrey Wegener keeping the off-kilter rhythms going.

But the genius of this album is the way it keeps you on your toes: Oliver Mann’s resonant operatic bass appears on ?Over Waves?, but just in case you think that’s going to be a stirring listen, you’ll be distracted by the existential terror of the just-in-range-of-hearing howls in the background, sounding like someone desperately calling for help as the song drifts past without stopping. Similarly, the repetitive melodicas of ?Here’s a Way? sounds like out-of-sync hurdy-gurdies in a nightmarish amusement park, while ?The Navigator? is effectively one long introduction for a song that never appears.

The result is a hypnotic, disorientating album that manages to be consistently beautiful and sinister. And now, I might just grab a Berocca and have a little lie down. You know, I’m really not feeling so crash hot?
