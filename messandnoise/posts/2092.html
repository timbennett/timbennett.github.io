For Bread & Circuses

By JodyMacgregor 2009-09-01T00:00:00+00:00

Does Timothy Carroll really have monumentally bad luck with women? His songs are full of ladies leaving and sometimes dying into the bargain,
but for all I know that’s just how his imagination works and he’s really cheerfully coupled. As well as all the women who done gone and went away, nature is another ever-present in his melancholy folk songs, but he manages the trick of using motifs without becoming repetitive. While most of the songs are just Carroll’s voice and his vintage guitar, strings and the backing harmonies of Corinna Scanlon pop up to haunt moments like the chorus of ?Danshyttan? (which is pronounced ?dunce-who-tonne? as the liner notes helpfully explain in case it comes out as the less romantic ?Dan shittin?? in your head).

The songs on For Bread And Circuses are full of cleverly observed
details and wry wit. ?Something Else? is a duet with Texas Tea’s Kate Jacobson, the two of them singing about a breakup, nostalgically reminiscing about sharing the late fees on movies while deciding who keeps which friends. ?Song For The Darling? is a farmer’s prayer for rain that angrily threatens God as much as it begs him. It sounds like it could have come straight from the M. Ward songbook, which is not a bad thing. There’s a great, bluesy clarinet solo in the middle as well and a gospel singalong chorus. The chorus of ?Sad Man? confesses, ?I’m a sad man I know?, as if to apologise for his heavy heart, but it’s hard to be depressed over plinky-plonky ukulele and later nods to ?cheap women, cheap whiskey, cheap bars/Champion Ruby and a deck of cards?.

The album’s closer is a cover of Low’s ?When I Go Deaf?. All of the little sounds that are normally produced away, like guitar strings buzzing and a chair creaking, are left in as if to remind you of the things you’ll lose when your own hearing goes. Meanwhile Carroll sings about how he’s going to enjoy his inevitable deafness, especially the fact that he won’t have to write any more of these damn songs. Just like his tales of sad men and lost women, he makes contemplating your own decrepitude downright enjoyable.
