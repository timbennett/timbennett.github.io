The Journey is Long

By Aaron 2012-03-01T00:00:00+00:00

What was it about Jeffrey Lee Pierce that forged such strong connections with Australia? Records by his influential band, The Gun Club, were released locally and sold healthily, particularly debut Fire of Love*, belying the band’s relative-obscurity overseas. The Gun Club toured Australia too, in September and October 1983, with Spencer P Jones and Billy Hood Jr from The Johnnys actually joining the band after two members refused to board the plane. Most significantly, Pierce had close ties with a lot of Australian musicians, many of whom appear on this compilation, *The Journey is Long, credited to The Jeffrey Lee Pierce Sessions Project.

The Project assembles over a dozen of Pierce’s ?old friends, collaborators and acolytes? to have a collaborative joust at completing a cache of demos and works-in-progress, discovered on an old cassette by Cypress Grove, bluesman and erstwhile partner of JLP. While The Journey is Long* is clearly a homage to Pierce, who died of a brain hemorrhage in 1996, it is less a typical tribute album and more a creative dialogue, situated somewhere between *Mermaid Avenue*, when Billy Bragg and Wilco took a crack at adding music to unfinished Woody Guthrie lyrics, and Josh Homme’s guest-fuelled *Desert Sessions.

It seems a labour of love for all concerned, not just Grove, the album’s musical director, but executive producer (and Gun Club biographer) Gene Temesey, journalist Kris Needs, who wrote the wonderful liner notes, and of course the performers, including Mick Harvey, Nick Cave, Hugo Race, Warren Ellis, and Tex Perkins, alongside internationals like Tav Falco, Steve Wynn, Barry Adamson and the Jim Jones Revue. There is a sense of relaxed collaboration at work, with many appearing on each others? tracks.

If this is all sounding a little familiar, well it might, for The Journey is Long* is the second volume for the JLP Sessions Project, after 2009?s *We Are Only Riders*, and features many of the same artists. It won’t stop here either: a third and final set, *The Past Has Overwhelmed Us, is just months away from release.

So has the past overwhelmed them? Well, tempting as it is to review this volume so pithily, the truth is no. The album is a definite grower that succeeds on its own terms, with more than enough dependable performances to warrant your time and interest. Lydia Lunch’s grating croak on ?The Brink? is the only contribution I outright detested, and even that had capable accompaniment.

Nick Cave offers two of the strongest songs, album opener ?City in Pain? a swaggering funk/soul hybrid with a stabbing blues guitar solo, and ?The Breaking Hands, a spare and haunting duet with Deborah Harry (though you’d be hard pushed to identify it was Harry if you came to the track blind). ?The Breaking Hands? is also solidly covered here by Mark Lanegan and Isobel Campbell but it is Cave’s version that I’ll be replaying.

Nick Cave & Deborah Harry -The Breaking Hands by Mess+Noise

?In My Room? from Tex Perkins and Lydia Lunch sounds more like the Beasts of Bourbon than anything we’ve heard from Perkins in years, so it wasn’t a total surprise to learn that ex-Beast Brian Hooper was on bass for this one. Warren Ellis? spectral violin overdubs are a highlight of ?Rose’s Blues? by Bertrand Cantat and Pascal Humbert, making the track unsettling but compelling.

The earthy, roots-fuelled performances work best though, like Hugo Race’s dobro-laced ?I’m Going Upstairs?, Mick Harvey’s sparkling ?Sonny Boy? and ?St Marks Place?, and Cypress Grove’s bottle-neck driven ?L.A. County Jail Blues?. The noisier, more frantic moments like Steve Wynn’s ?From Death to Texas? and Book of Love’s ?Vertical Smile? are less immediate, but they too get under your skin in time.

JLP purists may disagree but, for the rest of us, this is a record worth hearing. As a concept, the Jeffrey Lee Pierce Sessions Project might even last the three-volume stretch.
