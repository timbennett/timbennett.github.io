Research

By Edward Sharp-Paul 2011-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

Mountain Static is the post-TTT project of prime mover and sometime vocalist Simon Gibbs. By the evidence of Research, he has happened upon an apt moniker; usually strong and suave, here Gibbs? voice sounds spectral and naked, stripped of hubris. In light of his old band’s demise, it’s hard not to hear the classic tropes of the breakup record, albeit not the customary romantic breakup; themes of dislocation and isolation abound.

Gibbs seems to have retreat on his mind; into the landscape, into solitude, into some sort of primal, elemental state. This is borne out in his spare, repeated lyrics, and in the instruments that click and drone around him, coming on like a Black Mass broadcast on ham radio.

Research* sounds almost upside-down, full of the sort of incidental elements that would usually flit about at the outskirts of a song. ?Tataka Farewell?, for example, comprises a distant harmony of ?ooohs?, an egg-shaker and a tambourine over a muted marimba motif. And that’s it. Early highlight ?Valhalla? features no more than immense toms, a persistent electronic tick and Gibbs? voice harmonising with itself. Even stranger, as these elements multiply, and the song surges to its climax, it never even occurs to you that something might be missing. Even on ?Warning Song?, *Research?s first single, Gibb’s voice has only a distorted, droning organ to support it, and even this periodically drops out. There is an immense space where a lead vocal, or a guitar, or a conventional drumbeat might have been; that space is the star of the show, the missing element that haunts these songs.

Mountain Static – Warning Song by Mess+Noise

If it hasn’t yet become clear, this is not music for the pop charts. In its perversely claustrophobic minimalism, it resembles a backwoods take on the raga-like electronica of Melbourne’s Tantrums, and others of the genre-melding/genre-unto-themselves Two Bright Lakes/Sensory Projects set. Though TTT were by no means straight-shooting genre puritans themselves, it’s clear that Gibbs is relishing the freedom to explore and defy even the loosest definitions of form and genre.

Indeed it’s no great surprise to hear that Rat vs Possum’s Daphne Shum and O+O’s Oliver Hunter, among others, have been enlisted to bring Research to life in a live setting. (Shum also contributes to the album proper.) The highly atypical approach to percussion across the album – the variation runs from ?Elements? echo-laden gamelan drums, and tambourines and handclaps on ?Returning Song?, through to a sonorous ?When The Levee Breaks?-esque groove on ?Migrations Part 1? – suggests that Rat VS Possum’s kitchen-sink approach to percussion may have been an influence, too.

If the album has a weakness though, it’s that this will to experiment sometimes leads Gibbs astray into the sort of tribal-jungle schtick that represents the more half-baked, indulgent side of Melbourne’s experimental pop scene. The everybody-bang-a-drum-and-holler trick is a pretty good one, but Research comes perilously close to overdoing it in search of climactic moments that feel a little forced at times. Restraint is the most notable quality of this album, so when it lapses, so too does the quality.

It is testament to the strength of Gibbs? craft, though, that this aesthetic appropriation for the most part adds colour to the songs, rather than obscuring them under a welter of tricks. Perhaps that’s because there is so little to be obscured; these songs seem to radiate from somewhere beneath the crackle and buzz.

Research dispenses with such luxury items as hooks and choruses, in favour of stark, rhythm-heavy repetitions. Many will be put off by this uncompromising approach, but those who persevere will start to find beauty in the cacophony; a meditative calm in Mountain Static’s strange bulletins.
