A Trophy

By Andy Hazel 2010-09-01T00:00:00+00:00

Occupying a strange space as a singer and writer of solemn yet widescreen rock, while being an artist on a small label, Tobias Cummings is a fascinating prospect. The kind Melbourne specialises in producing; writers of tear-inducing potential million-sellers playing to 20 people on a Saturday night, songs that don’t seek gushing hyperbole or national radio airplay.

It’s unlikely any of these other songwriters would throw a line like, ?We’re the apotheosis of respectability as we sit here fucking each others eyes?, into the otherwise darkly melodic pop song ?Exegesis?, but it’s the sort of thing British music magazines would adore. If Cummings changed his name to Neville Stellar and adopted a British accent, it would likely be the last Melbourne heard of him until he did his ?Comet Returning? tour in 2020. As it is, A Trophy is an album that betrays Cummings? humbleness, and formidable skill as a vocalist who has no intention of world domination at all. In fact, it sounds like he’s struggling to exert domination over his own feelings for the best part of this remarkable album. It’s a type of humility that’s all the more pronounced coming from Melbourne’s knowingly cool indie music scene.

The whole album is redolent of hard-won achievement of either a creation of self via a relationship or deeply questioning the value of what he holds dear. Moving from the opening, questioning ?Goldmine? to the spiralling redemptive refrain of ?The Golden Dream?, it’s an evocative album that grows in quality with repeated listens. Sonically, A Trophy is perfect late-night/long-drive music, but, unlike so many of these sensitive well-read singer-songwriters working away from the harsh light of publicity, Cummings? is depicting a bright dawn.

With a voice that can only be described as blessed (and very much of the ?I listened to a lot of Radiohead in my teens and actually quite like early Coldplay? variety), he narrates a journey from a wedding through a series of darkly personal places before crashing the redemptive pleas that make a dream become real. It sounds like heavy going, but with the careful hands of Tim Whitten and Scott Horscroft at the mixing desk, and Cummings? innate sense of melody, use of dynamics, masterful musicianship and deft sensitivity, it never feels anything less than buoyant, and leaves those earlier reference points behind.

It’s a hidden gem of a release that will likely earn him another Australian Music Prize nomination and – unfortunately and unfairly given his considered approach and utter lack of urgency – little else.
