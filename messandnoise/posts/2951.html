About Face

By Jules LeFevre 2014-10-20T00:00:00+00:00

Big Scary’s evolution is a fairly calm and logical one. From the soft, acoustic nature of The Big Scary Four Seasons* and their earlier EPs to the electronic-tinged, AMP-winning 2013 record *Not Art*, there certainly wasn’t a rush forward into new musical areas, but there was a gentle stroll nonetheless. When Tom Iansek’s first solo record arrived in 2011, packaged as #1 Dads, it was clear where he was at in his own stage of evolution. *Man of Leisure recalled the earlier EPs – gentle alt-rock tracks that dealt heavily in poetics and rested on his easy, clear voice. On About Face, Iansek is definitely still in the same place, and he’s only getting better at being there.

About Face has dropped down a gear – the tracks lie happily in mid-tempo, and the big drums that dotted Man of Leisure are nowhere to be seen. In fact, apart from a brief, muted drum-machine explosion on ?Nominal?, or the intro to the curious closer ?Homeward Found?, this is pretty much an all-acoustic effort. More than just acoustic, it’s also sparse – at times, the songs hang only together by the soft picking of guitar and Iansek’s hushed vocals. There are times of richness: the iced-up, Bon Iver vocal harmonies that sweep across standout ?Nominal?, and the swell and release of opening track ?My Rush?. Even when the songs crack open, they rarely sound forced or rushed, such is the sense of relaxation and hanging back that Iansek has dispersed over the songs.

Within Big Scary, Tom Iansek’s poetic gloom had always been tempered by the presence of drummer and fellow vocalist Joanna Syme. In solo form, his lyrics range wide and display a stunning lyricism that turns the everyday (?Camberwell?) into scenes of simple elegance: ?Walked down to the supermarket/Milk and bread in my basket/Chocolate on the shelf and my mind on the docket/I’m a slave to the change that’s in my pocket.? Spoken in a dreary, spoken-word melody, it’s hypnotic. Elsewhere it’s more emotionally charged: he opened the record with ?You are my rush/You are my tortured dreams/You are my fear? and then goes on to drown in some murky depths over a bruising alt-rock chorus.

Melbourne singer Ainslie Wills guests on ?So Soldier?, a plodding ?80s slumber of a track that sees Iansek disappear into the background completely, apart from some shadowy backing vocals. Without the anchorage of his voice, the song quickly becomes listless. Better is the quiet ballad ?God Can Promise?, featuring Brisbane electronic artist Airling. Trading lines over a heartbeat slow piano, it’s hymn-like in delivery.

About Face (LP | 2014) by #1 Dads

Iansek previously stated that About Face was written during the exhaustive touring cycle that Big Scary endured after the release of Not Art, as a way of flexing his creative brain. Perhaps that goes some way to explaining why About Face feels like clusters of intimate murmurings. There’s beauty in the quiet, and elegance in the simple. This is both of those things.
