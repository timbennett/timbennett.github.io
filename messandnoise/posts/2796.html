Hobo Rocket

By Tim Fitzpatrick 2013-08-07T00:00:00+00:00

Hobo Rocket sees the pop of previous Pond albums kept to a minimum in favour of more sprawling structures, which is all the better. It’s still there on ?Xanman? and ?Giant Tortoise? – that tightly written pop, hooks and all – but it’s more an invitation to draw in the unassuming. A bit of a psych 7? for the digital age.

The most solid indication of how Pond have shaped their latest album is the sense that Sabbath’s ?Sweet Leaf? has been used as a reference point at least a couple of times throughout. There’s the riff midway through opener ?Whatever Happened to the Million Head Collide?, the cough/cackle at the start of ?Odarma? and other dirge-y turns. A bit like the story of how Ron Asheton based The Stooges? ?No Fun? on The Byrds? ?Tribal Gathering?, but in the case of Hobo Rocket the reference is spaced out across the album.

This reuse of different references is kept up for much of the album, with multiple songs or similar parts/structures patched together and surfacing on various songs. Not so much in the traditional psych-collage way (jarred and noticeable turns at moments of elevation, musique concrete leanings of the classics, etc.), but coherently in the extended jam sense. ?Midnight Mass? is probably the best example of this. A Mercury Rev-type yearning before that Sabbath crash comes back around and drops off before the kind of swirling and melancholic fade-out to an album you’d expect from a band that’s propelled the likes of Damo Suzuki.

Hobo Rocket sounds like a band that could just as easily drop out and release albums full of these jams. It’s not too farfetched to imagine them morphing into some old travelling jam band, slowly departing from structure into a world of sound, but those songwriting skills probably put that idea to rest whenever it’s brought up in band practice.

The band’s Psychedelic Mango* piss-taking/absurdist origins are also still there (if the title *Hobo Rocket didn’t give it away). Not saying Cowboy John’s the first cosmic cowboy, but he comes across as authentic while guesting on the title track, a sitar-backed space-country funk. Willie Nelson was called one because he smoked weed and played country music, even had long hair, but this makes more sense. Maybe it’s a mixture of Cowboy John’s mumbling and me going deaf but, from what I could pick up, the song’s about him not being able to find his ?horse with wings? or ?horse’s wings? and so he went to find a motorcycle, couldn’t and took off on his bicycle across the sky instead. Brilliant.

Maybe that’s what a hobo rocket is, or maybe it’s a shiny bottle of booze flying upwards against the backdrop of a starry night on its way to a dry mouth on a homeless night. A tallie with wings. Either way, bizarre scenes and images and some tight musicianship all make Hobo Rocket a pretty solid release.
