Bats And Birds

By ReneSchaefer 2009-01-10T00:00:00+00:00

Gareth Edwards, aka The Holy Rose, is a true original. Living in self-imposed exile with his young family in a suburb of Brisbane, he genuinely does not care to be part of pop culture or the music industry. Instead he goes about the simple business of life, observing the minutiae of everyday existence with the sharpest of eyes and then he retreats to the shed to craft his musings into the most hauntingly delicate songs imaginable.

Every once in a while, Edwards will put some of these recordings up on his MySpace page, iTunes, or burn a few CD-Rs for his friends and a small group of die-hard fans, who have followed the songwriter’s progress since his days as leader of Melbourne slow-core band Sandro in the mid-?90s.
This willful obscurity is part of his appeal of course, as he has become the melancholy poet of suburbia. His songs speak of domesticity, the beauty in the simple repetition of mundane existence. Accompanied by his guitar and the sparsest of home-recorded backing instrumentation, Edwards? voice is both intimately close and melodious. Over the past 13 years and 10 albums by Sandro and The Holy Rose, this voice has become an instrument of pure expression, lingering over simple phrases, or even a single word, until they are imbued with both lightness and absolute gravity.

A simple description of his young daughter on the swings in the local playground turns into a heartrending image with the addition of a single word: ?I’m pushing, I’m pushing, I’m pushing you – away.?

In a way, Edwards has more in common with painters like Jeffrey Smart, Eric Fischl and Edward Hopper, or the short stories of Raymond Carver, possessing a similarly acute eye for strange detail in a world that should be utterly familiar to us. There is even a touch of David Lynch at his best, as Edwards exposes the surreal, and sometimes dark world which lies hidden beneath the brightly lit surface of domestic life.

Unlike Lynch though, he never employs heavy-handed symbolism, but merely describes what he sees. In that, he is a realist, pure and simple. Another of his recurring themes is the changeable relationship between men and women that develops in long-term partnerships. He perfectly illustrates the feeling of being both an actor in a situation and an impassive observer at the same time. In ?Wednesday Afternoon?, he describes ?playing hide and seek behind my eyes? and listening to the neighbours argue on the other side of the fence.

It is hard to say how much of Edwards? lyrics are autobiographical, and how much are story telling, but unlike, say, the lyrics of Robert Forster, Holy Rose songs always have the ring of authenticity of actual lived experience.

In ?There Goes The Rain?, he succeeds in turning the most familiar occurrence into something utterly marvelous. In the song ?Lisa? he turns his attention to the experience of childhood and its secret world among suburban backyards and empty lots, inaccessible to adult eyes.

In chronicling such supposedly mundane aspects of his and our lives, without ever resorting to mawkish parochialism, Edwards truly is one of Australia’s most important songwriters and deserves to be celebrated. Still, if he were famous, he probably wouldn’t stay so damn good.
