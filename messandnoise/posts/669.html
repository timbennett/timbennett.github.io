Gotye: ?I Like A Lot Of Time To Myself

By Darren Levin 2011-08-16T14:00:00+00:00

Gotye (aka Melbourne musician Wally De Backer) tells DARREN LEVIN how the artistic indecision at the heart of third album ‘Making Mirrors’ almost cost him a number one.


Wally De Backer cuts a pretty unassuming figure for a guy who’s just topped the charts. He’s lanky and endearingly awkward (he describes himself as a ?delicate forward? in basketball terms); talks at a rate of knots; and is prone to nerding out, especially when discussing the technical intricacies of Making Mirrors, his third album under the Gotye moniker.

A lyrically rich and musically broad experience, Making Mirrors* is another triumph for the Melbourne-based solo artist and sometime member of The Basics, who’s carved out a nice little niche for himself marrying pop smarts with experimental production and sound collage. Recorded mostly in a barn on De Backer’s parents? Mornington Peninsula block – it was later mixed with composer/producer Francois Tetaz (*Wolf Creek, Sally Seltmann) – the album incorporates sounds from a variety of sources including Graeme Leak’s Winton Musical Fence (it’s sampled on first single ?Eyes Wide Open?); a Lowrey Cotillion electronic organ, which his parents gave him as a Christmas present; and an 18th-century pump harmonium that he set up in the barn.

When I met De Backer out the back of a Melbourne cafe, he’s just returned from Splendour In The Grass – ?The best festival I’ve hung out at? – and has been buoyed by news the album’s latest single, ?Somebody That I Used To Know? (a duet with Melbourne-based singer Kimbra Johnson), has just hit number one on ARIA’s Singles Chart. When Kimbra joined De Backer on stage at Splendour, the crowd response was 10 decibels shy of a jumbo jet flying 10 metres overhead.

?The audience went ballistic,? De Backer recalls. ?It was physically assaulting – even though I had in-ear monitors. It was intense – It was hard to hear what I was singing at time – just the audience and the general murmur of excitement. That’s exciting, but it makes it hard to play sometimes.?

How did you hook up with Kimbra in the first place?
Francois Tetaz, who I mix with, suggested it. But I met Kimbra a few years ago, maybe when she moved to Melbourne a year before. She was still 17 or 18. We didn’t really stay in touch very closely but she’d been working with Franc on her record. I bumped into her around the studio and we reconnected and said g’day. I’d been thinking about other vocalists and I tried it with other vocalists, and Franc just said: ?You should get Kimbra.?

And it’s proven to be a really good move.
Apart from the fortuitous aspect of her career path – it feels like a great alignment – the reason I wasn’t happy with that song up until I found Kimbra was because I wasn’t getting that core, ?This feels really great and right and raw.? She brought that to it. That was the thing that really made the song.

Is that female counterpoint something you wanted from the outset?
I didn’t know about that from the outset. I went about writing the song quite linearly. When I got to the end of the first chorus, I felt for the first time, in any song I’d written, that I’d said all I had to say. Any attempt to work an instrumental path, or bridge, back towards a repeat of the chorus felt very weak. At that point I went, ?I think it needs another story. It’s going to be a duet.? So I wrote the female part and looked for someone to bring it to life.

It’s not a specific break-up song is it?
I haven’t had that many relationships in my life – maybe three or four – but there aspects of those that come up in certain longs in the song – It’s very much a memory song for me. It literally starts with, ?Now and then I think of when we were together.? Sometimes you go into this nostalgia or sentimentality, or elements of angst or bitterness bubble up when you least expect it. I wanted to try and capture that range of things.

I guess when you make a record over three or four years, a lot of different stories from different places come up.
Some songs end up being very key to a certain time or experience, and other just amass in a way subconsciously and then come out in a lyric.

Has the success of ?Somebody?? and ?Eyes Wide Open? taken the pressure off a bit?
I felt like ?Eyes Wide Open? wasn’t that well-received, especially because it was a really hard track to finish. To tell you the complete truth, with both tracks, I still didn’t feel satisfied when I finished them. I just kinda let them go. It wasn’t about being a perfectionist. I felt like I made them the best they could be, and there wasn’t anything pragmatic or realistic I could do anymore in terms of remixing, tweaking, re-recording vocals, trying another part, or spending more money and time. I’d only make them sound worse or start disappearing in my own head – or rectum. [Laughs] In a way my feelings with both songs were a bit deflated afterwards – With distance and as I’m playing them live, maybe I’ll get to like them more.

Did those songs delay the record?
?Somebody That I Used To Know? did. It was good in a way, because it’s a song that’s set the record up and connected with people. In the interim, while it was holding the record up, I wrote ?I Feel Better?, which is another one that has a great energy. It’s a key song for me on the album.

For me, that’s the emotional turning point of the record.
That’s the arc I’m hearing, which I only realised was there after I sequenced it.

I often wonder with your records at what point you decide you’re finished.
I don’t know. I’m very intrigued to see what people make of the song ?In Your Light?. I was very close to not putting it on the album. I worked a long time on that track, rewrote the lyric a lot, mixed it a number of times – It’s vaguely Christian pop. In a way it’s on the record because enough people – from my friends to my family and management – all went, ?You can’t lose that song. It’s great. You’ve just lost perspective.? I was very close to going, ?This is too cheesy. I can’t accept it, I don’t like it, I want it off the record.? It was the one track which I put on the record because other people thought I should. I feel like it might be quite a divisive track for people.

That riff almost sounds like a sample. It’s so immediate.
It’s got a lot of immediate things on it. I worked on it so hard, and the closer I got to it being mixed, the more I started to hate it and think it was a really bad piece of music that I’d be stupid for releasing. That people would just think is shit, or that it sounds like I’m not challenging myself enough. But as a point on the record, I guess it’s the more obvious, ?Here’s a surface, here’s a moment that I ever only infrequently experience in my life.? The whole song’s about that, I guess: these tiny moments of not feeling encumbered by doubt and uncertainty and being happy for maybe a slightly longer period. Moments of pure happiness.

It makes sense that it follows a song called ?I Feel Better?.
There are a few songs on the record that are grouped in doubles. ?State of the Art? and ?Don’t Worry We’ll Be Watching You? are variations on a vaguely dub direction.

It’s interesting that you were worried about ?In Your Light?, because ?State of the Art? for me is the real out-of-the-box moment.
That’s the one I’m proudest of and I like the best.

I think you can make an entire record off of that blueprint.
I’d like to. That’s me just wanting to make a Ween record. [Laughs] I love Ween so much – It’s a daggy track in so many ways. It’s written about a Lowrey Cotillion organ that my parents gave me for a Christmas present. They bought it for $100 from a Salvation Army store. Most of the sounds on the track – all the widdly things, all the fills, the things that I’m singing about – are from the organ. It became a vehicle for me to explore my love of all these quaint synthesiser sounds on the organ, but also muse on how fascinated I am by the fact that pieces of technology like that can be state-of-the-art at a particular point in history – Technology like that, which was once quite cutting edge, can so quickly be in the bargain bin. It’s really musing on how you can get this personal relationship with technology that to other people is really quaint.

Are you a tech geek?
Yeah, I think I’m always drawn to being even more of one. I try to limit myself. Just recently my new laptop hard-drive carked it. I did a disc repair and it fucked it up – It’s laborious that stuff. I hate getting bogged down by it. I tried really hard with this record not to – even though I did some very laborious things, like multi-sampling my auto-harp note-by-note and turning it into a virtual auto-harp, which features on ?Save Me?. I stopped short of taking the easier option, just nerding out going, ?I’m just going to spend the whole week virtualising every instrument.? At the end of the week you might have a nice treasure trove of new things to play with, but I had to limit myself and go, ?Just virtualise one instrument you like and try to use it somewhere and make a musical statement with it.? You can just disappear down a rabbit hole so quickly?

It’s overwhelming.
There’s so much out there.

It’s a broad record once again. I guess that’s reflective of your musical collection?
Maybe. [Laughs*] With my last record [*Like Drawing Blood] I was actively pursuing broadening my musical horizons. Here, I didn’t have as much time to listen to music, so when I did have a chance to listen to music just for pleasure, I’m not as interested in going, ?I might check out those 40 minutes of Iranian [music] that I’m not familiar with?. It’d be more like, ?I might put on the new Bon Iver record because I really like it and I’ve only heard it four times and I want to hear it again.? The stuff I listen to is all over the shop.

Has listening to music become more of a recreational thing for you?
I still really love listening to music for pleasure. I wish I had more time to listen to music. In my early 20s I’d spend a lot of time with friends listening to records. I love that experience, but I don’t get that much of a chance to do it anymore. My girlfriend and I will listen to music in the car – that’s nice to share records there.

Do you come up with songs in the car?
More Basics songs than Gotye songs, although ?I Feel Better? was started in the car. I was listening to Martha Reeves? ?Dancing In The Street? and marvelling at the tambourine backbeat. The tambourine is ludicrously loud. I almost think of it as the hand of God just smashing this massive calf-skin tambourine. It towers over the whole mix. Fuck it’s amazing how some of these Motown tracks can be defined by a such a small percussive instrument that can be made to sound so huge. I just stopped the track and kept hearing that backbeat feel – I rapidly ran inside when I got home, found a tambourine sample, put reverb on it, made it sound really big, sat down at the piano and turned that into a song in an hour. That was a direct response to a Motown soul song.

Are records usually triggers for you?
They can be. The fairly warped vocals on ?State of the Art? are inspired by a Godley & Creme song called ?I Pity Inanimate Objects?, which is off their album Freeze Frame. It has this wild vocal effect concept, which is one of the only vocal effects I’ve never been able to work out. I looked it up online and I still don’t quite understand how they did it. It sounds intensely laborious. It was a different process I used, but I guess it inspired me.

Is it Auto-Tune?
Auto-Tune is part of it, but it doesn’t really sound like the way most people use Auto-Tune. I just tried to imagine how Godley & Creme might’ve done their vocal concept. I thought if I just tried the techniques I thought they were using, what would the end result sound like? Well, it ends up sounding like this track. [Laughs]

[De Backer nerds out while describing how he came up with the vocal technique]

That process sounds very mathematical.
Yeah, but it worked quite well. There was a lot of finicky stuff afterwards.

[More nerding out]

I’d love to see you make an animated clip for it.
I’ve made an animated clip for ?State of the Art?, which has turned out really good. Some guys from Melbourne called Rubber House did it. They have a really great ?50s animation aesthetic.

Was that the thinking behind the companion, making-of DVD. Did you want people to see the process behind the record?
I was a little worried while making these docos with my friends James Bryans that it was stripping away some of the mystery of what I do. I’m generally not someone who wants to hoard their production secrets, because I think the ideas will hopefully speak for themselves rather than the technical secrets about how it’s made. I actually had fun making these docos with James; I sometimes enjoyed it more than making parts of the record because it was totally new for me?

Did working across another medium have a positive impact on the musical side of your brain.
I think maybe when I was younger I had a greater variety of things that would do that – even playing basketball. I wonder if that varies now because I don’t exercise as much anymore.

What position did you play?
Forward, sometimes centre.

Power forward?
[Laughs] Delicate forward.

You did most of the recording for the album in a barn?
Most of it, yeah. Anybody who played instruments – my friend Lucas played bass, or Gareth Skinner, who played all these amazing cello harmonics that make the whale-like sounds on ?Eyes Wide Open? – they were all done wherever was most convenient for them. I’d just go to Lucas? loungeroom, plug my mobile system in and hit play, or go to Franc’s studio because Lucas lived around the corner. A few people came down on the barn and recorded with me, but living an hour-and-a-half out of town [Melbourne] isn’t so convenient.

Where’s the barn?
It’s down on the [Mornington] Peninsula on my parents? block.


And your dad [Frank De Backer] made all the soundproofed tubing?
He did. He’s an amazing engineer. He’s amazing to have as a co-conspirator on my stuff, because I can kinda go, ?Do you reckon you could investigate making a screen which could do this, and fit in this and meet these requirements?? A month later he’ll present me with a full brief and a prototype from China. [Laughs] Dad’s engineering and system design skills like that are amazing like that. He can research and work something out – he makes things so well.

Do you think some of that has filtered down into the way you make music?
I always think it’s child’s play, because dad has so many skills. [Laughs] He was never a soccer dad, but in Year 7 he’d give me a lot of help with my science projects. Over time it probably impressed on me to be a jack of all trades.

Did you ever want to follow him down that engineering path?
I don’t know. When I was growing up Dad was working in systems engineering and design. He was working for big banks designing mainframe software. I didn’t really understand anything about the work he did, I only vaguely got a grasp of it more recently. Dad was more of a guy who’d be making things on weekends: making chairs or fixing things. For him it was probably a big release from sitting at a computer all day during the week. Now he’s building things all the time. He’s built this barn almost singlehandedly. He’s built their house on the top of the block. He loves me giving him projects.


And obviously during that time he dabbled in art too? [The album cover was painted by Frank De Backer in the 1980s.]
Yeah, he’s actually gone back to painting recently too.

Because of the album?
No, he’s funny like that. I remember how Dad used to be a smoker, but he’d be able to quit at the drop of a hat. ?I’m not feeling very good – I’ll stop smoking.? For five years he wouldn’t smoke and then he’d start smoking again. [Laughs] He has a lot of self-control I guess like that. He’s started painting with some acrylics on chipboard recently – They’re Aboriginal dot painting type things, but it’s got his style to them. Again, it just came out of nowhere. ?Oh, you’re painting again, Dad?? He’s like, ?Yeah, I just felt like painting.?

He has a readymade audience now too?
I don’t think he does it for any other reason than to explore and satisfy himself. I think both Mum and Dad are loners on some level. They have a strong friendship relationship. They don’t really have a lot of friends they see regularly. They do their own projects, they live on the block and they grow vegetables. They’re not super social in that regard, and maybe I’m the same a bit. I like a lot of time to myself.

That isolatory aspect is really prevalent in the records you’ve made.
Well, that’s one of the core differences with The Basics. Being in a band, part of the process is being together and working together and often then working in certain timelines, where you can’t enjoy the luxury of working at your own pace or in your own headspace. Sometimes it’s a good spur, and sometimes I found it a barrier with The Basics. Sometimes I’d go, ?This is not quite feeling good yet, but we have to do it because I’m not available then, or we have to do a tour or finish mixing.? Whereas with me, half the reason my records take so long is because I go, ?Even though I’m frustrated and I would’ve liked to have released this record by now, it’s not really finished yet, so I’ll just keep working on it.?

I guess some people would question your loyalty to the Basics given the way your career has progressed.
People have mentioned that. I’ve heard people be very direct and say, ?You’re a fucking idiot. You’ve got all these opportunities people would kill for, you’ve got talent. The Basics are great, we’re sure they’re your mates, but go with what’s giving you opportunities.? I definitely felt that at times – Oh look, I don’t know. I love the guys and thought we had good music to make and record. It was a bit of an uphill battle – The reception has not kept on par with Gotye] in terms of [triple j support and the audience size and opportunities. That’s got harder and harder to balance out. On some level I wish they could’ve kept pace more, but I guess it’s hard to get through those media bottlenecks?

Has it made you appreciate the opportunities you’ve been given a bit more?
I think so. It’s made me realise how much time you have to invest to do something really well; to back it up, take those opportunities and make the most of them. I wasn’t ready to make a new Gotye record if I thought The Basics stuff was taking off. I think I would’ve followed through with that if things were going well?

I feel like I’m putting my business hat on here, but it proves how important the sense of momentum is in the music industry. A lot of people might’ve said that I was stupid for losing that [momentum] by going off the scene for three or four years. I felt like that with putting out ?Eyes Wide Open?, the momentum wasn’t forthcoming. It’s been exciting to see the new single seemingly giving a pathway for the record to do that?

?Sometimes you go into this nostalgia or sentimentality, or elements of angst or bitterness bubble up when you least expect it. I wanted to try and capture that range of things.?

Well you’re talking to me at a time when you’ve sold out four shows at The Forum [in Melbourne] and had your first number one.
That says to me that, at least with my project, it’s worth taking the time until I think I have something good together. But who knows? I could’ve finished the mix of some of these songs months earlier and people might’ve still liked it. That played on my head at different times. Franc, who I mixed it with, would always make jokes like, ?Is it going to affect the charts?? Because he’s not about commercial imperatives at all.

I don’t think you make records like that either.
No, I don’t. But I’d be getting to the fourth or fifth mix and sometimes I wouldn’t be able to express why I thought we weren’t getting it to feel right. He’d be like, ?I don’t know Wally – is it going to affect the charts?? [Laughs] It was in a supportive way: ?Maybe it’s as good as it can be, and whatever you change is not going to make any difference to how people experience the music.?

Where are you at with the record now? How do you feel about it?
It felt like quite a relief to have it mastered. [Laughs] But the manufacturing side of it has been annoying too. The product that I care most about – the vinyl with the gatefold sleeve – is not going to be finished by the release date, because the artwork was late and the first test pressing had an error in it – With vinyl it’s hard because there’s a real artistry to how it’s cut. It’s not just burning a CD.

What was the thinking behind the mini-orchestra tour?
I thought the last time I did the mini-orchestra tour in 2007-2008 was really good, but I didn’t pay much mind to how integral backing vocals are to my music. So I got this 11-piece ensemble together with a string quartet and saxes, drums and percussion and discovered that none of those people could actually sing. So I really wanted a band that could play all of my music completely live: all vocals and instruments.

So it’s the Brian Wilson approach?
Have as many people covering as many instruments and voices as possible, yeah. It’s been hard finding a great sax player who can also sing well. I had a really good moment yesterday in rehearsal where we played six or seven songs in a row and I was like, ?This sounds fucking great and it’s all live!? That feels a lot more exciting to me than being the guy who’s referencing a click track, trying to sing a lead vocal while holding down the drum part.
