Pleasure Syndrome

By Patrick 2011-10-01T00:00:00+00:00

Witch Hats has never been a band overtly obsessed with the facile excitement of the cheap pop song. Seen through the eyes of the band’s songwriting protagonist Kris Buscombe, Witch Hats offers up a world of seedy suburban characters and sociological indifference on new album Pleasure Syndrome. The irony – if it is actually irony, or in fact a logical conclusion – is that Buscombe’s characters appear to be wrestling with the inherent danger of their own forms of pleasure. ?The Bounty? – arguably the closest thing to a pristine pop song Witch Hats has yet achieved – is rich with violent imagery, its narrative either allegory for an unfulfilled emotional quest or a literal tale of amorality.

On ?Another Worthless Body Sold?, darkness clouds the otherwise beautiful act of birth; this is a world that few would be prepared to acknowledge. Ash Buscombe’s rumbling bass ushers in ?Mahoney?, possibly the tale of a child about to be force-fed a diet of wordly dysfunction, and destined to repeat the effort for the next generation. ?Combustion? is awash with a sea of confronting images, each brought to life by shards of guitar noise that inflict masochistic pleasure in your otherwise staid mind. ?In the Mortuary? opens with a distant nod to Bolan and Bowie stalking the road from folk to glam; Buscombe’s colourful lyrics offer a glint of sympathy against a backdrop of condemnation.

?Slow Eyes? evolves from what could easily be a standard blues track, with a tinge of gritty inner-urban soul. Like a Bukowski novel, there’s nothing beautiful here – this is the grim, pathetic and nasty reality of life on the margins. ?Pissing in the Snow? finds the band back in the pop territory of some of the earlier tracks on the record; in this metaphor-laden scenario, the lyrical tone is one of objection to self-gratifying pity. There’s more pain to be had in ?Model’e?, but with a distant silver lining that can barely be discerned through Tom Barry’s angry guitar attack.

Hear Martin by witchhats

It’s natural to assume ?Hear Martin? is inspired by Tasmania’s most infamous contemporary criminal. The prevailing soundtrack could be a sideshow in a travelling circus, its facade of contentment and happiness disguising the psychopath about to be released upon an unsuspecting world. Explosions of white guitar noise capture the tragedy of the moment, before the song gradually fades into memory. ?Ashley? concludes the album in a spitting hail of threats and retribution: this isn’t going to end well.

Pleasure Syndrome is – for all of its dark and disconcerting ingredients – an album replete with perverse satisfaction, enjoyment and pleasure. Most of what’s written here is probably wildly off the mark, but that doesn’t disturb the quality of the record. Within the painful tales of others lies the potential for great art – something Witch Hats do better than most.
