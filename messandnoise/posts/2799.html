Dream Cave

By Barnaby Smith 2013-08-18T00:00:00+00:00

The rather overstated significance of Cloud Control’s ?farewell? of two years ago to base themselves in London has perhaps intensified the expectation surrounding their second album, as if following up an AMP-winning debut in Bliss Release* – which, along with *Innerspeaker, was the most notable Australian album of 2010 – wasn’t enough. The fact is, artistically at least, such adventuring from the perceived periphery to the perceived centre means generally nothing in these globalised days. So the fact of their relocation can be removed from any assessment of this album.

As it happens, Dream Cave* is made up of largely the same flavours as *Bliss Release anyway, suggesting that the band’s sound is inherently within them rather than affected by any changing environment. And what an album this is, an engrossing journey through a disconcertingly beautiful landscape, of both sonic adventure and absurdly direct pop hooks. That said, while the soft and whimsical core of Cloud Control remains pervasive, there is definite development in that the vocal harmonies are more ornate and the production more drenched in electronic touches, reverb and echo akin to monoliths of thinking-man’s pop Yeasayer or Animal Collective.

With Bliss Release* and probably more so with *Dream Cave*, Cloud Control are too easily classed as ?psychedelic?. It’s true there is a sheen of, well, ?bliss? and ?dreaminess? on both albums, but on *Dream Cave* the shimmering sound is merely a frame for some wonderfully inventive playing with different genres. Hints of *Bliss Release?s anthemic indie-folk emerge on single ?Dojo Rising?, while there is a heaviness in the synths of ?Island Living? that, unlikely as it may seem, approaches Depeche Mode territory. The album’s finest achievement in terms of both arrangements and songwriting must be the delightfully weird ?Moonrabbit?, a sublime snippet of chamber pop in the mould of The Turtles? most well-known singles with a vocal hook to be savoured. ?Moonrabbit? is closely rivalled by the album’s more sprawling title track, with another dramatic, slightly gung-ho chorus. Cloud Control could never be accused of complicating things. Simplicity of melody suffices almost always.

Lyrically the album is typically fascinating, continuing a kind of autodidactic exploration of spirituality and awareness apparently informed by an unspecific Eastern influence. On Bliss Release we had ?Meditation Song #2 (Why, Oh Why)? and singing about ?Mother Ganga? on ?There’s Nothing in the Water We Can’t Fight?, and here we have more little aphorisms of the Eckhart Tolle variety with the refrain of ?I’m just a mirror for your love? on ?Promises? and a mildly esoteric thread running through both ?Dojo Rising? and ?Moonrabbit?. The abstract and even humorous way Cloud Control hint at a ?cosmic? connection is one of this record’s most attractive sources of mystery.

The downside of Dream Cave*?s quality is that – on an album packed with thoughtful, compact and brilliant pop songs – the merely average, such as ?Tombstone?, do stall the momentum. This seems an anomaly on an album that, and there is no other way to say this, has love in all of its forms at its heart. *Dream Cave?s kindness and sweetness is apparent from the startling opening harmonies on opener ?Scream Rave? to the muffled bellows of the eponymous closer.
