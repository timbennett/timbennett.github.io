Future Eaters

By timscottt 2013-03-25T00:00:00+00:00

Looks can be deceiving. At first Exhaustion don’t seem to be fast movers. Not that they are slack or lethargic, but a listen to their slowed and languid (almost sludgy) take on pub psych makes you think of a band who are comfortable taking it easy. More insouciant than indolent. But the Melbourne trio have wasted no time in releasing a debut LP just a matter of months after their first show.

Essentially a project for Duncan Blachford (Snawklor, Actual Holes), who has employed/interned bassist Jensen Tjhung (Deaf Wish, Lower Plenty) and drummer Per Bystrom (Ooga Boogas, Leather Towel), the songs are wonderful in their bleak and weirdo wackness.

Don’t Fly Right by EXHAUSTION

Whereas Blachford’s other musical paths have tended to lean more to the experimental – almost abstract – free form, Exhaustion pays more mind to the rock song. Just. Recorded by Tjhung as he drifted in and out of his self-confessed ?hate prism,? the tracks on Future Eaters are gloomy, self-loathing affairs with titles such as ?No Place for a Holiday? and ?Your Memory Don’t Want You?. Broken. Shit that people dump late at night outside St Vinnies.

The beginning of ?Old Mickey? has Blachford sounding like Lux Interior on 10mg of Nitrazepam before things turn up with Bystrom’s drums breaking out. Not exactly like a sprinter from a peloton, but adding some drama nonetheless. ?Keep the Change?, the band’s strongest point both on album and in the live setting, is positively bouncy compared to some of the more dour tracks, but even then I use the term ?bouncy? like I would to make a bad cheque seem a little more positive or bright.

Moon Out Tonight by EXHAUSTION

ZOND’s Justin Fuller steps in on power electronics punishment for the outro on the 10-minute ?No Place For a Holiday?. You know how that goes.

A good indication of where Blachford and Exhaustion are coming from is ?Moon Out Tonight?, their extension of The Capris? 1959 doo wop classic ?There’s a Moon Out Tonight?. Where in the original lines such as ?There’s a moon out tonight/Let’s go strollin?? nod to innocent romance, with Blachford’s snarl the same line takes on a more sinister tone. More Fred and Rosemary West than Joanie and Chachi.
