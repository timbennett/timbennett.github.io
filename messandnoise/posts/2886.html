Hey Daydreamer

By Anthony Carew 2014-03-30T00:00:00+00:00

It’s strange that Sally Seltmann’s second album under her own name – after two as New Buffalo – begins with her wishing to be ?back home.? Sure, Hey Daydreamer*’s title track treads familiar thematic terrain for the songwriter, given some of her truest tunes have been about long-distance longing – from New Buffalo’s ?While You’re Away? to ?Even Though I’m a Woman?, her standout jam for side-project/supergroup Seeker Lover Keeper (in which she admitted, ?I love the danger in distance,? even if it was Holly Throsby who sung it). But given that *Hey Daydreamer marks Seltmann’s first record since moving to Los Angeles – though it wasn’t recorded there – you might think it’d begin with a song about making a new home, not pining for an old one.

Of course, Hey Daydreamer* sure sounds like she’s moved to Los Angeles. Where Seltmann has long employed samples – both as central components in the rudimentary cut-ups of 2004?s New Buffalo LP *The Last Beautiful Day* to later, unexpected and inspired details like the *Breaking the Waves* church bells tolling at the close of ?Heart That’s Pounding? – here dusty records have been replaced by hot players. Curiously, it’s husband Darren – who, with The Avalanches? *Since I Left You, authored one of the definitive sample albums – aiding, as co-producer, in dressing her in a great many get-ups. There’s pedal-steel, harp, brass, woodwinds and strings, all employed in service of this being Sally’s sumptuous, sun-kissed, ?70s-singer-songwriter record.

At Hey Daydreamer*’s worst, the orchestrated outfits give off the glare of kitsch. See ?Needle in the Hay? (the album’s one real ?beginning a new start? song), which mismatches Seltmann’s airy voice to a woody, borderline-saloon sound. Or ?Holly Drive?, where she sings, awkwardly, ?people often say I’m a lot like the man from *Paris, Texas,? which marks the cue for a cheesy sting of big-hat pedal-steel. Or ?I Will Not Wear Your Wedding Ring?, whose blue-eyed funk finds Seltmann vamping unconvincingly over a big-production number boasting a backing chorus, bongos, brass, and Ron Burgundy-worthy jazz flute.

Befitting its title – and Seltmann’s back catalogue – the album is far better when it’s dreamier, gentler, more hesitant. ?Seed of Doubt? captures the universal unease of relationship uncertainty, and is striking in its sincerity. Seltmann is one of those songwriters who can make ?I love you? not sound like pop-song trope, but loaded emotion, delivering a lingering ?but? thereafter and confessing, ?I just question everything.?

It’s also better when it doesn’t try so hard to escape her past. The chiming, beautifully sung piano ballad ?The Small Hotel? features a lyrical callback to New Buffalo’s ?Misery and Mountains, Arrows and Bows? and riffs on another staple Seltmann theme: confidence-building through songwriting. ?States and Spaces? has the minimalist accompaniment familiar from her New Buffalo mode, and ?Right Back Where I Started From? the wonky synths she wears well. But, most importantly, when the latter suddenly rises up mid-bridge – harp, piano, percussion and orchestral flutters blowing in like a rainstorm – it feels like a natural phenomenon, not a shortcut to scored grandeur.

Those moments are enough to make the album nothing approaching a total misstep. They’re reminders of why Seltmann has been so beloved in Australia. But Hey Daydreamer*?s ultimate effect is of an album that feels a little too mannered, that loses the genuine intimacy of her guileless early works amidst its horn charts and string scores. A song like ?Billy? works melodically, lyrically and conceptually, its staccato parts trotting beneath a motorcycle-boy soap opera modeled on classic Shangri-Las. But delivering its character-study, Seltmann sows disbelief in the tragedy, sells it as too cutesy, too self-conscious. There’s a layer there – both in this song, and across much of *Hey Daydreamer – that’s never existed previously; the craft at work putting the emotions at a remove. Seltmann has long written songs about distance, but before this she’s never sounded distant.
