Record Reviews

Advance

By ReneSchaefer 2009-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

Huon are not your average indie group. Their members? involvements in the pop underground reads like an encyclopedia of hipster cool. Drummer David Nichols was a founding member of The Cannanes and Blairmailer; his partner Mia Schoen played in Sleepy Township, New Estate and Driving Past, and released a highly underrated solo album Champions; and Andrew Withycombe and Ellen Turner are fondly remembered for their involvement in Cats Miaow and Long Weekend, respectively. But that’s just the abridged version.

Every once in a while, these four get together at Nichols and Schoen’s house in suburban Melbourne to write and record songs as Huon. Sometimes some of their friends join in. Whenever they have enough material together, they compile it into an album of precious pop gems. Simple, really.

Huon’s songs feel as intimate and lovely as an old cardigan. They smell of dog fur and spilled beer. Maybe they’re fraying at the edges a bit and the elbows are kind of baggy and threadbare, but there’s plenty of life in them yet.

Released on vinyl in a run of only 50 copies locally (you can buy it via mail order from Knock Yr Socks Off Records), Advance is their sixth full-length album and their first in seven years. Huon don’t generally make a big hue and cry about their releases. They just suddenly appear and then disappear again. You could say everything about this band is small – even their ambition. They have no truck with the retarded game of fame or commerce, even in its most scaled-down indie pop guise. One could accuse them of being obscurantist, but that would be missing the point. Coming across a Huon release is like finding a tiny, but perfectly formed treasure.

A very long time ago I wrote a fan letter to Calvin Johnson of K Records, stupidly gushing about his band Beat Happening. Trying to describe the qualities I responded to in the wonky, homemade songs on their single ?Our Secret?, I referred to them as being about ?little feelings?. Advance is all about these too.
