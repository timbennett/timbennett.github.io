Mesozoic

By ReneSchaefer 2011-07-09T00:00:00+00:00

It’s 1966. Roky Erickson is all dressed up in tight trousers, winkle picker boots and stripey t-shirt. It’s 1967, and The Velvets blast the cobwebs off pop culture with avant garde repetition and delinquent rock. It’s 1974. Klaus Dinger and Michael Rother lay down a conveyer belt trance beat somewhere in a crumbling farmhouse in Germany. It’s 1985. William Reid leans his guitar against his amp and walks off stage while feedback reverberates around the walls. It’s 1989, and Spacemen 3 take a trip into their own reductivist minds. It’s 2011. Guitar fuzz spills from an open window of a Darlinghurst share house. The drums kick in and it’s like a homecoming.

Putting on Mesozoic by Sydney psych rock band The Laurels is a bit like falling in love. A thick, warm blanket of sound envelopes the listener, comforting yet thrilling at the same time. The rhythm is akin to a quickened heartbeat. It’s a rush and a swoon, so familiar yet brand new. It’s like imbibing a dose of a favourite drug. The chugging, repetitive riffs, the dreamy, reverbed vocals and the swirling guitar effects are all designed to immerse listeners in some kind of womb-like altered reality.

Of course, there’s nothing wrong with using music as a form of escapism – it is, after all, one of its primary functions for the majority of listeners. It’s not important then that The Laurels? songs address nothing in particular. Rather, listeners can revel in their infantile state, marvel at the swirling patterns, the inviting textures, beguiling melodies and steady pulse. Captain Beefheart often declared his intention to rouse people from their catatonic state with his rhythmically complex and tonally jarring compositions. The Laurels do quite the opposite – encouraging listeners to go further in, to head towards the centre of this catatonia, to bliss out and not break on through to the other side. Psych rock is not about mind expansion, but rather a contraction, finding a stillness in one’s own head.

The appeal of psych and shoe-gaze is exactly this other-worldliness; a dreaminess that is conveyed through a very narrowly defined set of signifiers. It can consist of nothing more than a particular vocal inflection, or it can lie somewhere in the melodic bass lines, or the way the guitars mesh in spiraling, ascending riffs. People often talk about psych’s roots in druggy ?60s rock, beginning with the likes of the 13th Floor Elevators, and this is obvious in a song like The Laurels? ?Turn On My Mind?. Really though, most of this music’s reference points lie in the ?80s and ?90s, when bands like My Bloody Valentine, Loop, Ride and Sydney’s Drop City specialised in that pre-natal, uterine bliss-pop vibe.

The Laurels – Black Cathedral by Thee Laurels

As the title of this EP implies, the evolution of The Laurels has been a slow, yet inexorable process. As with all evolutionary developments, Mesozoic builds on precedents, but what emerges is something new, fast and sleek, perfectly adapted to survival in a changing climate. Counting among them a bio-chemist, a historian, a philosopher and a cartoonist, the four-piece (three men, one woman) seem like a most unlikely bunch of rock musicians. Clearly, they are not just in this for hedonistic kicks, although who could blame them? The band has spent an awfully long time developing and crafting their sound until everything is just so. As luck would have it, their particular brand of blissed-out shoe-gaze is currently on people’s radar again. This can only be a good thing.
