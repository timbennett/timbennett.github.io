Electric Ocean

By Max Easton 2014-03-20T00:00:00+00:00

In a live setting, Scraps (the solo moniker of Brisbane’s Laura Hill) sets up her keyboard and microphone on the floor of the venue and plays below the crowd’s sightline. Crouched over her equipment, she will lean toward her microphone where appropriate and play from a position so unassuming that, if you were at the show, it’s possible that you wouldn’t even know she was there. It’s from this modest perspective that Scraps has always come from, and on her second LP she continues to mine soaring pop songs from the floorboards.

Scraps plays with a number of moods on Electric Ocean. There’s a sense of playfulness in a range of tracks, from the Kraut-esque bounce of ?Projections? featuring her own narration of the song’s effects (?It’s medieval techno I know?) to the toyish effects of ?Flying?. There are instrumental afterthoughts like ?Lonely Motorbike?, which recall surpassed technologies struggling in vain to overcome disrepair, and various cosmic pop elements that scream of the metaphysical realms. To counter it all are shallow depressions, meditative moments like the recollections of childhood on ?Electric Ocean? or the melancholic sentiment that lies under the jovial surface of ?Gone.? Like the reality of nostalgia, there’s sadness even in the pleasant associations.

One of Scraps? most affecting qualities for me has been the meeting of innocence with adult ruminations; the more child-like moments are never separated too far from depth of feeling. It reminds me of watching serious children play, the ones who have that unnerving quality of only laughing once everything has been fully processed. In a song like ?Electric Ocean?, schoolyard memories are rattled off in the present tense with an aloof, indirect air (?What’s your favourite colour/What’s your favourite number/Six, black/Six, black?) that makes them feel rapidly lost. It feels like trying to remember someone once they’ve been dead a very long time. For Scraps, memories disappear rapidly into the ether before evaporating to other realms.

Electric Ocean* and Scraps? previous releases (2011?s *Classic Shits* and a number of online-only releases) have always felt deeply personal, with a musicality befitting her status as a bedroom artist playing to a faceless reception. There are songs that feel like unsent letters written as therapy; others as jovial, electro-pop anti-hits. On *Electric Ocean it feels like there’s a more stellar sentiment than in the past, experiencing as much affection for the cosmos as for the insular realm of an unkempt bedroom. It’s less like Scraps is playing from an unwashed venue carpet and more like she’s levitating above it all.
