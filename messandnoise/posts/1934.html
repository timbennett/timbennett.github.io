You Got Ripped Off

By Patrick 2009-03-23T00:00:00+00:00

Some years ago I happened upon an academic article that narrated the attempts of the American evangelical movement to associate the Beatles with all things Satanic. Why then, the author noted wryly, did these same champions of decency, turn their focus toward the Rolling Stones, who had the irreligious audacity to release an album titled Their Satanic Majesties? Request?

What the ?50s evangelical movement would make of the extreme end of the punk movement is anybody’s guess – suffice to say, it would involve horsemen of the apocalypse, plagues of locusts, days of judgment and generally bad shit for the human race. The paradox, however, is that the hardcore punk movement tends to be – for all its rhetorical reliance on anger and anti-social behaviour – characterised by a sense of community. It’s just as, if not more, genuinely supportive than your average suburban church.

Perth band Extortion is a hardcore band and You Got Ripped Off – the 12″ reissue of their 2008 debut 7″ plus bonus tracks – is a hardcore record. The first side comprises 11 songs exploring themes typical of its genre: violence (?Mindless Violence?), retribution (?Pull the Pin?) and nihilism (?Inside?). Of those tracks, the majority are so fast and furious you’ve barely got time to remark, ?Fuck, man, these guys are intense?, before the song ends with a flurry of drums, bass, guitar and emotive vocals. The perfect example is ?Extortion?, which is so brief it makes the opening trio of songs seem like ponderous prog rock epics in comparison. Pretentious these guys certainly aren’t – if you can’t work out what ?Hate Your Guts? is all about, then you’re deserving of the journey into nowhere promised in ?Boot Ride?.

The second side opens with ?Over and Over?, the band’s ode to the monotony of everyday living, before segueing into a live set consisting of ?Pull the Pin?, ?Degenerate?, ?Repeat? and the ?Rising Tide?. There’s not a lot of discernable crowd noise, but it’s not hard to visualise the synchronised banging of heads, spiked hair and maybe the odd bit of tartan.

And that’s the beauty – and it is beautiful, albeit not in the romantic sense of the term – of hardcore punk and a band like Extortion. Within the intensity and the catharsis exists a community deserving of infinite ethnological consideration. Listen to Celine Dion and you’re liable to go postal; listen to Extortion and the anger is channelled for good, not evil.
