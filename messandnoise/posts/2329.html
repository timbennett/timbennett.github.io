Fifteen

By ReneSchaefer 2010-07-23T00:00:00+00:00

The debut album by Super Wild Horses is a delicate combination of faux naive sentiment and a knowing sophistication, writes REN? SCHAEFER.


It was clear from their earliest beginnings that Super Wild Horses had the potential to become an interesting group. Whether they were going to emerge as a superior garage rock duo, or develop their raw minimalism into more refined realms, was difficult to tell at first. As it turns out, on their debut album Fifteen, Amy Franz and Hayley McKee manage to achieve both – often in the same song.

At the heart of their method is a tremendous knack for writing melodies and classic primitive pop structures, played in the most direct way possible. SWH distill the history of primal rock’n?roll into nuggets of pure joyous noise. From the album opener ?Lock & Key?, which sounds like The Gories covering Suicide, to The Breeders-esque power pop of ?We Don’t Believe It?, ?I Want You? and ?Degrassi?, Fifteen is surprisingly varied in its use of vintage guitar tones, charming vocal harmonies and teen-beat drumming.

What really works for the duo is their deliberate combination of faux naive sentiment and a knowing sophistication. This is a tricky mix to pull off, and many other groups? attempts have fallen somewhat flat in sustaining listeners? interest beyond a novelty value. It’s what let down many potentially great bands that mined similar territory, such as Your Wedding Night, who ended up sounding one-dimensional and jokey.

If this review is peppered with references, this is not to say that SWH are derivative. Rather, they reinvent certain aspects of their predecessors? styles, whether it is the ?60s inflected C86 noise-pop of The ShopAssisstants or the Riot Grrrl stomp of Bikini Kill. They never resort to pastiches though. Invention is as important as absorbing influences. A good example is the single ?Golden Town?, which features a fuller sound and hazy shoegaze leanings, with its open-chord drone and fuzzed-out jangle. What impresses most is the strength and self-confidence of the vocal delivery with its unselfconsciously Australian accents.

??Fifteen? stands as a superior example of what can be achieved with limited means, but a great ear.?

Mikey Young’s understated, yet sympathetic production retains a garage vibe, but also gives the songs room to breathe. What sounds like a band just jamming out songs in the studio, is obviously carefully constructed and mixed. Fifteen stands as a superior example of what can be achieved with limited means, but a great ear. Being raw and immediate does not mean that music like this can only exist in the realm of lo-fi obscurity.

It was only recently, when SWH graduated to playing bigger stages and festivals, that most listeners started appreciating their sublime command of vocal harmonies. It’s surprising, given that they have only been around for a relatively short time, how quickly the group has moved on from the shoutiness of numbers like ?Enigma (You Say Go)?. A couple of these early songs are included here, but they do not detract from the overall quality of the album.

Instead, a song like ?Stranger By Day? neatly illustrates the rapid pace at which Franz and McKee have progressed towards being sophisticated songwriters and talented musicians. Maybe it’s a sign of changing mainstream sensibilities, but if clothing company Bonds can use one of SWH’s songs on one of their ads without it seeming incongruous or jarring to commercial television viewers, maybe debates over production values and the use of archaic pop and rock forms are largely irrelevant? As it is, Fifteen is a great sounding record that preserves all of the band’s noisy edges, while bringing their new-found sweetness to the fore.

+

Fifteen is out July 23 on Aarght! Records through Shock.
