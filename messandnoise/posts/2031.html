Songs for Tuesdays

By Doug Wallen 2009-07-14T00:00:00+00:00

You’d be forgiven, prior to hearing Songs For Tuesdays*, for thinking that Summer Cats are a bit one-note. The Melbourne indie poppers did, after all, release an EP titled *Scratching Post and otherwise play up the cuteness of both their band name and super-shiny songs. Their live shows have been enjoyable, but singer Scott Stephens? squeaky lead can lose its effect over the course of a set. What a shot in the arm then, this album proves to be. Three other band members step up to the mic and Summer Cats muss their overall sound with noisy garage, relentless jangle and muscular power-pop.

Cramming 14 songs into 35 minutes, the five-piece (since made six with the addition of a second guitarist) don’t rest much here, flinging infectious songs our way before we’ve recovered from the previous one. After a few seconds that sound like the start of a White Stripes tune, opener ?Let’s Go? lays on serrated guitar, robust keys and slapping tambourine. It’s an ecstatic racket that Stephens manages to cut through with an ultra-melodic delivery. The following ?Hey You? screams single, plowing into a chorus in which Stephens – formerly the singer of The Earthmen – sings with wavering innocence about his burning desire to ?mess around?. It’s cute sure, but also rowdy and harried.

While it’s tempting to single out every song, obviously the ones with different singers jump from the tight-knit pack. The Big Star-referencing ?Fulton Gurls? features the snide, throaty delivery of bassist/guitarist Hugh Owens, who also sings in a dour, blanketing tone on the Aislers Set-ish triumph ?Maybe Pile?. Keyboardist Irene Drossinos sings, fittingly, on the keys-anchored ?Christopher Wren?, and guitarist/bassist Scott Brewer takes over on ?St. Tropez?, complete with a mention of ?punching Bobby Dylan circa 1963?. On top of regular bursts of vocal harmonies, these detours help the album zip along without the doldrums setting in. Not that there’s much risk of that when songs are kept to around two minutes long.

Despite such beloved ambassadors as the Sugargliders, Even As We Speak, The Cat’s Miaow and The Lucksmiths – bands who are arguably more renowned overseas than here – indie pop has never really achieved the widespread adulation it’s deserved in this country. That’s not going to change simply because Summer Cats have chimed in with an engaging first album, but hopefully Songs For Tuesdays will inspire some converts. If nothing else, it widens the genre’s parameters and remains caffeinated for 35 minutes straight.
