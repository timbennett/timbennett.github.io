Make Your Own Mayflower

By Andy Hazel 2010-08-01T00:00:00+00:00

There was a time, a few years ago, when there was a buzz about Perth’s Institut Polaire. They won WAMI awards, got a lot of radio play and became the first Australian band signed to Popfrenzy; a band worth listening to. An interview from the time (2008) talks about this album as being ?the album we should have made a year ago?. Why their debut had such a long gestation is one thing, but the fact that Make Your Own Mayflower sounds like a band that still haven’t really defined their identity is another.

Expecting ringleader Erik Hecht to have the strength of identity and distinctiveness of sound to prevent an album with so many contributors is a bold ask, but one you could expect given the plaudits he’d already received. Inspiring commitment is a rare gift; for every Stuart Murdoch, Tim DeLaughter or Emanuel Lundgren – all band leaders (once) capable of shutting most of the band up when a song needs it – there are far more examples of a collective that dilutes vision to a bland democracy.

Hecht falls closer to the latter camp with a promising debut EP, excellent live show and obvious songwriting chops here being substituted for cluttered arrangements and a lack of inspiration and dynamics never previously hinted at. Like each of those bandleaders listed above, it’s obvious that other band members help their visions become realised and bring out the best in the chief songwriter. The same goes for Institut Polaire.

David Thirkettle-Watts arranges with flair, Ash Blakeney has a nice line in guitar fills and the band ably fill out the songs. What’s missing, however, are hooks or a memorable melody, which their EP had in droves. Make Your Own Mayflower is fine as background music, but I’m betting not one of their ?seven to nine members? moved from Perth to Melbourne to make that.

That said, there are moments that hint at what they’re capable of, most notably the title track and ?To New Holland?, which make the most of the ubiquitous mid-tempo pace this album moves along at. By not cluttering the song, working with melodies and leaving some space allows them to sound stronger and rise above The Decemberists/Arcade Fire/Beirut comparisons that have been bothering them – sounds that were so in vogue in 2008.
