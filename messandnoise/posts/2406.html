Play All Night

By Doug Wallen 2010-12-01T00:00:00+00:00

Nic Dalton has led an extraordinary life, and not only as the founder of Half A Cow and member of bands like Sneeze, Godstar, and The Lemonheads. He’s smoked pot with Harry Nilsson, gotten fan mail from a soldier in Afghanistan, and penned a proposal song for his wife three days after meeting her, all experiences documented on this shaggy album of autobiographical rambles. It’s the first album with his revolving-cast band in five years, and in that time Dalton has endured many more experiences: he moved back to Sydney, met his wife, got a day job, had two children, lost his mother, and watched his daughter recover from cancer. Most of that isn’t touched on here, as the bulk of these songs were penned earlier, but knowing it underscores the range of emotions that Dalton nails in such unflinching detail.

He has help along the way, from some 16 contributors. Those include co-writer and longtime bandmate Lucy Lehmann as well as Darren Hanlon, members of The Smallgoods, and jazz hero Bernie McGann. But while Lehmann is behind the poignant, world-weary tales ?Six Hours West?, ?Take Me As I Am?, ?And Baby Makes Two? and ?The Last Fan?, Dalton sings them and sells them like they were purely his own. His voice is unrehearsed at best, a direct outlet for musings on getting older, settling down, and kicking the grog. At the risk of putting too fine a point on it, he turns in renditions of the self-explanatory Skyhooks single ?All My Friends Are Getting Married? and Danny O’Keefe?s redemptive ?I’m Sober Now?. In a telling Dalton-esque twist, the latter includes a new middle-eight supplied to him by O’Keefe himself. Ben Lee also contributes a song, ?The Circus Clown?. While written at age 17, it has enough frayed feelings to have come from Dalton himself.

What makes Dalton’s originals stand out, however, is their free-wheeling, unabashed nature. From the reference-heavy surrender ?Okay Sydney, You Beat Me? to the Nilsson run-in told through the gently rollicking ?Harry’s Demos?, Dalton doesn’t excise much at all from his diary-like songwriting. Inspired by a Half A Cow fan stationed in Afghanistan, ?Drew’s Comfort? incorporates the soldier’s letters seemingly verbatim, at once conversational and heartbreaking. And lest Dalton ever resort to what’s expected of him, the aforementioned proposal song ?Tender, Tender? isn’t some cloying ballad, but a plucky bluegrass number steeped in the thrill of early romance. As surprises go, there’s a jazzy instrumental in album opener ?Strawberry Hills?, named for the Sydney suburb where Half A Cow is based.

In the end, though, it’s all the forthcoming words and shared experiences that reign supreme on Play All Night. While the name of Dalton’s band is accurate for the most part, his method of chasing gloom is facing it head on. That makes for an album that’s draining and uplifting in equal measure, truer to life than any music you’re likely to hear all year.
