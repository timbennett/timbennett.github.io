Don?t Let Me Down

By Doug Wallen 2014-06-25T00:00:00+00:00

Just as The Smith Street Band have struck a real chord with a beery, wordy and emotional brand of punk that’s got close cousins in rising US bands like Joyce Manor, Modern Baseball and The Menzingers, their Poison City labelmates Freak Wave have their own kindred spirits in plain view. In this case it’s more the throaty, earnest ?90s anthems of veteran US bands like Knapsack and Samiam – both of whom have had albums reissued through Poison City – that help propel Freak Wave’s debut LP. Add in the misanthropic brooding of Jason Loewenstein’s contributions to Sebadoh and the seething vocal angst of Gentlemen*-era Afghan Whigs, and you’ve got the scrappy parameters of *Don’t Let Me Down.

Of course, the fact that Freak Wave have such visible antecedents – the album cover even recalls Bad Religion’s classic Against the Grain)* – doesn’t make them unworthy of appraisal on their own strengths. And as much as the half-hour of *Don’t Let Me Down zips by in what feels like even less time, it sees the Melbourne five-piece (including Poison City owner/founder Andrew Hayden on bass) stretching out their sound in several instances. First and most obvious are the opening ?The Sleepers Must Awaken? – a warm expanse of shoegazing alt-rock complete with Neil Moog’s parting invitation, ?You could join me if you wanted? – and the closing ?Tremhello?, a crusty instrumental indulging in the titular tremolo.

There are other moments of reaching beyond mere anthem-making: ?A Period of Juvenile Prosperity? is noisier and darker in tone, threatening to pull us right under, while the centrepiece ?There’s No One Here? carves out a quieter space to reflect with wobbly acoustic guitar and effects. The jangling instrumental ?It’s So Damn Hot in This Damn City? is similarly thoughtful.

But really, Freak Wave’s bread and butter are the heartfelt, melody-laced, no-nonsense rockers like ?Tyre Kicker? (?I could stay and fight, or I could drive all night??), ?Falling Apart? and ?Shoot for the Cure?, all loaded with angular hooks and ?90s signifiers. Recorded by Michael ?Fitzy? Fitzgerald (The Smith Street Band) at The Reverence – the punk-friendly Footscray pub run by Freak Wave drummer Matt Bodiam – the album puts a premium on catchy, crunchy songs that match the urgency of Moog’s world-weary, questioning lyrics.

Crisscrossed by the sharpened, chattering guitars of Mike Hodson and Adrian Lombardi, Freak Wave have made a rewarding first album – even if they’re still finding a truly distinctive balance of the indie rock, shoegaze and melodic punk ingredients that they aim to accommodate. It’s comfort food of a very specific kind, and you should already be able to tell whether or not it’s to your taste.
