Not Meant for this World/Teen Sublimation Riffs

By Patrick 2008-12-01T00:00:00+00:00

Before there was Johnny Casino, there was John Spittles, and he played in a band called Asteroid B612. Taking their name from a Sonic’s Rendezvous Band song, Asteroid B612?s eight-cylinder motor vehicular rock sound and devil-may-care attitude owed just as much to the classic Detroit rock aesthetic of the 1960s, as appropriated and re-interpreted by Radio Birdman and its various successors and imitators.

Years out of print, and safely secreted in the collections of rock’n?roll fans from Sydney to Cleveland to Prague, Off the Hip has pre-empted the Christmas festivities by giving Asteroid B612?s AuGoGo Records releases, Not Meant for this World* and the *Teen Sublimination Riffs* their debut Australian CD release. To many Asteroid B612 fans, these record represent the band at its creative and energetic peak. The twin guitar attack of Spittles and Stewart ?Leadfinger? Cunningham is frighteningly powerful. Forget de Niro and Pacino sharing the same camera shot in *Heat, this is Bruce Lee and Chuck Norris belting the buggery out of an army of flaccid MOR rock disbelievers.

The highlights come thick and fast: Stooges riffs by the bucket-seat full in ?Destination Blues?, adolescent nihilism with a Radio Birdman flavour in ?Not Meant for This World?, Chuck Berry on ?Parramatta Road?, arrogance on ?Where Has All the Fun Gone? and ?Straight Back to You? and the countrified ?Black Diamond? stripped-back intensity of ?Undertow (Second Time Around)?. There’s plenty of bonus tracks for the true believers, including a stack of demos that embody and illustrate the natural garage state of band fighting for a rock’n?roll cause with a passion that verges on blind obsession. Longtime fan and friend Richie Ramone contributes liner notes, narrating the story of his association with the band just long enough to take a parting shot at the Cosmic Commander (Rancid Vats).

Unfortunately, like so many high energy rock bands before them, the intra-band chemistry mutated into uncontrolled internal combustion, and the Spittles-Cunningham partnership burned out on the tail end of Asteroid B612?s 1996 US tour. See the Cosmic Commander’s provocative liner notes to Greenback Blues (Off the Hip, 2004) for one perspective on that tortuous story. Fans have lamented that tragic event ever since. At least now they’ve got the opportunity to re-live the unadulterated brute force and attitude that was Asteroid B612 in full flight.
