Track: My Silken Tooth

By lawsonfletcher 2009-09-30T00:00:00+00:00

I don’t know if I can really say how this song makes me feel. Listening to it, I remember that day, and the young guy my Dad knew that played a song for the family on his resonator before the obituaries were read.

I remember looking at him, he was so nervous, straining for composure, and I was sitting there feeling for him, hoping that he would make it through. He played a subdued rendition of ?Home? by Black Pony Express, a song whose lyrics had rattled around my head that whole week, ?Circling my skull as if singing?, as Marcus Teague (aka Single Twin) puts it on ?My Silken Tooth?. I’d typed them up the night before, printed them out on a half-fold A4 sheet, facing the lyrics to the original Footscray Football Club song; the dates, her full name (I never knew the middle C stood for Clare) and a photo on the other side.

?What’s the ruckus, what’s the noise/Where’s safe harbour, where my boys?? he sung to the procession, and I remembered trying to transcribe this line. We couldn’t work who the singer was asking for in the last line, but Dad liked the sentiment so I left it like that. Indeed, I don’t even know if I’ve got the meaning right for the track I’m meant to be reviewing right now, but it’s besides the point. He faltered through this line and a few other unclear ones, but considering we’d given him the song just a few days prior and now here he was rendering it for a bunch of bereaved strangers, I couldn’t help but wish him through and applaud his composure.

Shouldn’t I have been thinking about Nan? I thought to myself, almost guiltily. But I guess his grab for self-control mirrored mine, it was just the way to sublimate the real pain. Just last until the end of the song, just last until the end of the song. The song substitutes for grief.

I get the sense that ?My Silken Tooth? – apparently written and recorded in a single night – has served a similar role for Teague. In it, he delicately layers metaphors atop one another about strange and eerie bodily metamorphosis, flowing flesh and silk teeth and everything in between, circling around the lyric in surreal, clouded fashion, obliquely laying out a more sophisticated conceit that takes some time to work out. Teague’s work – whether with his band Deloris or with his more stark and tender solo guise – is often marked by something lost, something just out of the frame, which his songs somehow manage to capture in their frozen tableaux.

I think this one is about how you find loss externalised onto your body (?I have straw now in my shoes/My fingers are in shreds?), your daily actions, and how it finally invades your thoughts. But that last place is also a place to take refuge: ?In my mind you are beside me and singing,? goes the chorus. Writing substitutes for grief. Thoughts composed into lines, into metaphors and similes, snatchings of experience built into something coherent. Something you can hold and play and sing into your tape recorder, even if you can’t really do much else to make things better. Music is that place where our loved ones come alive once more, if not in flesh then at least in memory.

The repeated chorus fades out almost imperceptibly, meeting itself again and again – like the pain of absence it’s never really fully present, but never does exactly go away – when towards the silence Teague sings: ?In my mind you are alive and singing,? quietly lifting the symbolic veil and uncovering his hoped-for duet for what it was all along: mourning.

A felt silence pervades the remainder of the track, as if the writer didn’t know what to do with himself – I dunno, it’s like nearly half a minute of nothing before he rises up to turn off the recorder, but if there ever was a palpable presence in a lack of noise then here it is. It’s that black hole, it’s where I’m no longer carried along by the chords and lines and I confront what was really there all along, where I feel it rise up in my gut and form behind my eyelids, the terrifying prospect of being alone.

But not if I get up and turn it back on: just one more song, one more time around. I’ll be OK once we’ve sung this song together, just once more.
