Happiness And Surrounding Suburbs

By Jen Peterson-Ward 2011-09-09T00:00:00+00:00

When Ball Park Music emerged in 2008, they had a tendency to exasperate as much as they engaged. For every killer single, such as the virulently infectious ?iFly?, there would be something else that felt like being smashed over the head with musical instruments by a hyperactive, though admittedly talented, toddler.

Likewise, the debut album from the triple j-championed Brisbane popsters, Happiness and Other Suburbs, is best administered in small amounts; otherwise, you run the risk of overdose. For all the abundant virtues of Ball Park Music – their energy, their vivacity, their obvious and audible love of what they do – it’s hard to sit through all of without feeling like you’re trapped in a parody of lo-fidelity, high IQ indie-dom.

That’s not to say there isn’t some genuine diamonds here – ?Happy Healthy Citizen of the Developed World? is a sherbet fizz of ?ba-ba-ba? harmonies, skipping piano and adolescent attitude, while lead single ?It’s Nice To Be Alive? wobbles between faux whimsy and charming oddness with youthful abandon. Yet as the album ploughs on, the feeling they’ve been making the same song over and over again grows. Rhythms grind, drums crash, the goofy-teenager attitude endures and excitement evaporates.

Ball Park Music aren’t afraid to wear their top-drawer influences on their sleeves, either. Happiness and Other Suburbs has the light touch and introspection of The Lemonheads and the fun-fuelled energy of Weezer. But it also reveals the extent to which the band are wrestling with themselves. Their ability to develop their own pop identity is at odds with their love of ?borrowing? sounds from just about every major American indie pop-rock band of the 1990s, with particular emphasis on Pavement’s late albums. Indeed, there are times when this could actually be one of erstwhile Pavement frontman Stephen Malkmus’s solo album: it’s in the jigsaw lyrics, huge chords and the way singer/guitarist Sam Cromack’s voice rises to a squawk on ?Glass Jar? and ?Rich People Are Stupid?.

Two things save Happiness and Other Suburbs from being utterly insufferable. The first is that many of these songs are such fun, hurtling from the speakers in a blur of fuzzy guitars, big shouty choruses and endearing girl-boy vocal jousting between Cromack and bassist/co-vocalist Jennifer Boyce. The second saving grace is that Ball Park Music have the brains to back up their smart arse attitude. For every contrived lyric like, ?I only have sex with myself? (from ?Sad Rude Future Dude?), there’s a genuinely striking image like, ?Fill my veins with anything or love/Your dilated eyes are in my mind more than usual? (from ?Birds Down Basement?).

Ultimately, though, the album’s music is too derivative to separate itself. This quirky-something sextet might aim for the joyous abandon of Los Campesinos! or Here We Go Magic, but really all they manage to communicate is a sense of self-stimulation. Yes, the bubblegum choruses and youthful exuberance make this a breezy listen, but the lack of individuality grates; an album already made countless times by countless bands.
