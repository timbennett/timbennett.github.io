Fixations

By Samson McDougall 2014-07-20T00:00:00+00:00

There was a warehouse show back in 2006 where Mikey Young of Eddy Current Suppression Ring’s guitar broke and an audience member graciously hooked him up with another in order to save the night. ECSR were scheduled openers for My Disco at the latter’s debut album launch. Also on the bill that night was Macromantics – a.k.a. Romy Hoffman, a one-woman hip-hop powerhouse – and due to the gear malfunction, the order of events was shot to hell.

An Australian rapper’s inclusion on that bill is perhaps indicative of warmer musical climes than we know today. (It should also be noted that back then Macromantics made regular appearances on stage with superb rock outfit Ground Components.) But it was, and is, the punk-rock attitude of Hoffman that enabled Macromantics to transcend these varietal divides. Regardless of what she’s doing, Hoffman is punk.

Hoffman arrived as A G E N D E R back in 2011 with album Self (En)Titled, a gloriously scuzzy, bratty, self-made mess. It wasn’t a gargantuan leap from her self-titled project Romy but it marked a pretty audible step away from the hip-hop methods of her Macromantics self. Through A G E N D E R, in a very linear way, Hoffman was capturing the essence of what made her presence as a rapper on punk and rock stages okay. She was now making synth-y punk that felt as though it’d been slapped together in the midst of a late-night frenzy of suburban bedroom aerobics. It was crazed, frantic and in some ways flippant. It felt like it was concocted on a whim. It didn’t seem likely she’d be in it for the long haul.

Now Hoffman’s back with a second A G E N D E R album, and this time she’s brought in bassist Yolanda DeRose and drummer Melissa Agate. The result is, unsurprisingly, very much a band album in comparison to the tantrum that was Self (En)Titled, and it’s a far more durable listen.

The percussive guitars of the opening bars of album starter ?Mani Pedi? could have been lifted directly from early-?90s Seattle, but as soon as the synths and drums kick in you’re in New Wave town. ?Monogamy? follows as an almost companion piece to the opener and, once into third track ?Brunch?, despite the live drum sounds this time, it’s feeling a lot like Self (En)Titled.

It’s not until the downward tempo and vocal timbre shift of ?Modern Lovers? that we get the first glimpse of the songwriting diversity that will help elevate Fixations far above the earlier release. This track, coupled with the almost-pop ?Ascertain?, brings side one (the only physical release of the album will be vinyl) to an elegant conclusion – something delightfully unexpected.

Side two blasts back into synth-punk territory with the downright sleazy ?Intimate Apparel? (?The lace, the lace, it’s all about the lace/Pin it to my palms, then attach it to my face?), and the pace remains steady for the straight-up East Coast-style punk of ?Don’t Won’t Don’t? and the shouty ?Orgasm Goddamit?. The band’s proclaimed ?spillage of sexual secrets and the purging of perversions? is in full swing now as they descend to the depths of the A G E N D E R consciousness. ?Fortune Cookie? takes the listener to the edge via a crazed monologue set to busy guitars and cymbal teases.

Closing Fixations* with a rework of *Self (En)Titled?s ?Wire/less? (this time labelled ?Wireless?) threatens to bring the whole thing unstuck. But then the bridge erupts into chorus, or next verse, or whatever it is, and slams the door on a sharp, dynamic and memorable release.
