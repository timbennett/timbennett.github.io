Thieves

By lawsonfletcher 2008-07-19T00:00:00+00:00

Thieves is most blatantly about the heady romance and excesses of youth, strewn across the borderlines of train stations, taxi rides, night clubs and airports. As someone literally leaving this time of his life, Declan Melia is understandably at pains to remain on the phone to his girlfriend, even as he hangs himself with it. That is, this is an album riddled with contradictions. For instance, dire warnings against posture and image are cast within the ?throw enough, some will stick? genre noodling of ?This Dance is Loaded?, all hi-hat yelps and indie-disco pose. Or the really tricky production, which mixes ill with the otherwise straightforward and catchy songwriting, slicking over anything that might have otherwise been a ragged hook to hang onto.

I’m trying to catch the source of this paradox. On the one hand, I think it goes back to the whole ?youth? thing. British India are desperately clinging to the easy signifiers of teenage livin? – getting wasted, partying, fucking – in order to gloss things up, but Thieves is riddled with metaphors of transition and departure – caving chests, airport tags, empty streets. ?The future is rushing at me like asphalt,? laments Melia on ?Mona Lisa Overdrive? (title? Don’t ask) and as it does, he has unwittingly slipped into nostalgia.

On the other hand, the conflict is an inevitable outcome of the band’s insertion into ?the machine? (scare quotes). Which is to say they have inadvertently become the thing they hate, even as they play to it. As the band watch themselves being inscribed into a relentless publicity juggernaut, their sense of anxiety and self-righteousness increases at equal levels, leaving things wholly unstable. Because despite all the grooming, British India are still just a bunch of guys, and the tunes they play are serviceably mediocre. One can only hope that, once they clear away the teenage rock star dreams, British India learn to explore this conflicted, elegiac quality that sits underneath Thieves, both lyrically and musically.
