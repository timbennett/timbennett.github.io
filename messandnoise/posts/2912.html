Grassed Inn

By Max Easton 2014-06-16T00:00:00+00:00

Over their previous three records, Blank Realm seemed to run with whatever form transpired from ideas thrown at the walls. On Grassed Inn everything feels a little more deliberate, as though this is the result the experiments pointed them towards. Long, precise jams are muddied by unpredictable synth lines and adventuring guitars that give murkiness to the sheen. It’s from this place that they finger the void to evoke feelings of yearning from a dark place; getting loose, with one eye ignoring the consequences.

Everything on Grassed Inn carries a sunny disposition, but that seems to come from a thematic case of denial. Displaying the glimmering surfaces of muted interiors, lines of resignation and dysfunction echo under instrumental jaunts that feel like brave-faced smiles in public. On ?Bell Tower? keys waterfall over Daniel Spencer’s sad acceptance that ?I’m just your plaything.? Meanwhile, on ?Baby I Can’t Reach You on the Phone?, some of the sunniest guitars on the record cover his awkward mutters of ?Maybe I should leave you two alone.? In different clothes, these songs would blatantly be ones of wrenching discontent. Instead, the jovial backings lead the mind astray as Blank Realm party wildly through the pain, desperately scrubbing at the darkness with trebly blares and jilted synths.

In the same way that they muddle your expectations of what the songs are about, Blank Realm’s reference points are slashed and burned. A song like ?Falling Down the Stairs? has moments that are aligned to The Go-Betweens or The Bats, but the band warp those similarities almost immediately. Likewise, ?Baby Closes the Door? recalls the creepier spectrum of Split Enz, and in all those bands they have philosophical company. These songs have elements that could make inch-perfect pop songs, but those moments are instead stretched to their limits without needlessly forcing points of convention into their manic ideas. You can file Grassed Inn awkwardly between your guitar-pop, post-punk and psych collections, but it won’t be at home anywhere you rest it.

As much as this is an evolution towards something with a more conventional sheen, the record’s certifiable oddness will be no surprise to anyone who has followed the band’s erratic tendencies in the past. It says a lot of the band that even their most structural play to date is a rambling mire of disaffected grins, vague entendre and overworked hooks. Grassed Inn may be markedly cleaner than what Blank Realm are known for, but it’s still a long way from sanitary.
