M+N Mid-Year Report: 2013

By Mess+Noise 2013-06-30T14:00:00+00:00

Because list-making is such an endangered tradition, we asked our writers to share their favourites so far this year. Behold the browser-busting results.

Darren Levin

The Ooga Boogas
?Sex in the Chillzone?

The thing I like most about Melbourne’s Ooga Boogas is that it’s never really clear when they’re taking the piss. That, dear readers, is called ?building mystique.?

Sex in the Chillzone by Mess+Noise

Dick Diver
Live at Golden Plains

Banter, sax solos, bush poetry, ace songs, a cover of ?Are You Old Enough?? – they gave the dregs of the Amphitheatre something to cheer about at 11am on Golden Plains Sunday, and that’s a very difficult thing to do indeed. Calendar Days isn’t half bad either.

The Drones
I See Seaweed

Gareth Liddiard has a way of honing in on our insecurities. Feeling nervous about the environment? ?I See Seaweed?. Worried about a Tony Abbott government? ?The Grey Leader?. Had a shit day at the office? Here’s a song about an abandoned dog that dies in space. Listening to this album doesn’t make me feel any better about my place in the world. And yet I keep returning to it. Funny, that.

Baptism of Uzi
?Stray Current?

A little bit Krauty, a little bit rock ?n? roll.

Zanzibar Chanel
Live at Big Day Out

The biggest and best fuck you to mindless patriotism/boganism/jingoism I have ever witnessed … in the Lilypad World.

Kate Hennessy

Barunga Festival, Northern Territory, June 7 – 10

Barunga Festival is Australia’s oldest and largest remote community festival. I finally made it there this year and it disassembled my idea of what a festival is and reassembled it in a new shape. It was taken over this year by Darwin’s Skinnyfish Music after it nearly collapsed last year. Skinnyfish also manage Gurrumul, who they added to the bill alongside John Butler. Butler’s name spiked white crowds but 75% of the 3,500 there were Barunga locals (a tiny community of 350 with seven languages between them), their extended families and other indigenous Australians.

I’d seen Gurrumul play at the Sydney Opera House the week before but this show was even better. ?Fidgety, over-excited indigenous kids were weaving in and out of the front few rows while their little brothers and sisters darted around in the dust, and bats were screeching from the treetops like injured piglets. Then Gurrumul starts singing and everyone calms down; his songs are like a sedative. Every singer I heard that weekend was amazing though. Best of all were the guys singing the songs for the bungul (traditional dance).

I wrote a travel feature for The Guardian UK here.

Kirin J Callinan
?Embracism? song and clip

I go to a gym in Coogee. It has windows all along the side so I can run with a fresh sea breeze. But it’s also full of bodybuilders, wannabe bodybuilders and every kind of dickhead in between, most of them on steroids. This song is for those guys. I’d like to play it at my gym one day and see what happened. Would they be repulsed, for once, by their own reflection?

Paul Kelly & Neil Finn
?Into Temptation?, Sydney Opera House

A big reason for the series of shows Kelly and Finn played together was to sing each other’s songs. The collaboration yielded one very memorable moment but rather than paraphrase myself I’m going to do something even worse and quote myself: ?When Kelly sings the Crowded House perennial ?Into Temptation? I realise the song was felled long ago by over-familiarity, its edges all worn off. Kelly sings it hands-free of a guitar, completely in the moment, drawing forth the song’s rueful tale of lust’s abyss in a way I had not heard before. Even Finn seems overcome, bereft for a moment, simply saying: ?Paul Kelly, you released it from its shackles.??

Abbe May
?Karmageddon? song and clip

I was drawn to ?Karmageddon? from the first time I saw the clip. I already said a lot in my review but all I hear is the very distinct feeling of being overcome by inevitability. It’s really cool when songs bypass the over-trafficked interstates of love, lust, bitterness and breakups (though these feature here too) and explore a side alley of emotion instead. It’s hard for music to score the subtler stuff but she does it here. Before, I didn’t know what inevitability sounded like; now I do. And I have only just now realised the connection with the concept of karma. Nice work Abbe!

The Drones
I See Seaweed

In the six-month period this column covers, The Drones released a record. Including it here is like ?including? the part of a form that’s already filled out. I haven’t been able to listen to the album much since I reviewed it. By my final edit, each time I typed the album name I typed ?I Sea Seeweed? instead, which made me feel like I might be going mad. For real. It didn’t help that my only breaks from the task were strolls along the cliffs near my house and it was a steely grey series of days – both sky and sea. ?

Lawson Fletcher

Air Max ?97
?Oops (Oh My)? remix

Probably one of the most bootlegged R&B tracks in existence; Melbourne’s Air Max ?97 furnishes perhaps the best yet – an icy blast of pure NRG.

Midnight Juggernauts
?Memorium? clip

You’d think the possibilities for the music video had just about been exhausted, but Midnight Juggernauts find a new take on the form with this doco-clip for Uncanny Valley?s best cut.

Flight Tonight
?Lines (feat. Leure)? clip

Michael Danischewski’s on-location clip perfectly captures the oneiric wanderlust of this Melbourne producer’s pristine bass track.

Zanzibar Chanel
?Big Bone Bitch? film clip

A loving tribute to the campy, lo-fi aesthetics of Melbourne’s finest public access station, Channel 31, and possible homage to those disgusting shiny curtains that used to adorn the Ding Dong stage.

Flume

From toying around with production software out of a cereal box to bro’ing down with Skrillex; dude even has his own ice! Harley Streten’s world domination: the kids are alright.

David Nichols

As everybody always says, to stop at such a random and by definition limiting number is the problematic part. San Cisco, King Tears Mortuary, Zone Out, Jen Cloher, Hot Palms. But the new Enclosures album, Claire Birchall, Milk Teddy, The Ocean Party, Courtney Barnett – all of those could as easily displace any of the aforementioned. I wish I could comfortably say it’s not a popularity contest but clearly it is.

But in any case, these are merely what sprang to mind in the first instance, and get back to me in a few hours and I can spew forth another five, because it is a very great time to be able to listen to new music, which, by the way, just reminds me once more how ludicrous the fashion is for going to see old people recreating the music they made when young in highly-paid scenarios. But I’m used to it.

Hot Palms – Benny and The Jets by Why Don’t You Believe Me?

Doug Wallen

Dick Diver
Calendar Days

Already a dear old friend.

The Drones opening for Neil Young & Crazy Horse

I See Seaweed is killer, but the band stormed their back catalogue instead at Melbourne’s Plenary Hall, criminally under-crowded at that stage of the night. Even stranded all the way back in a high and distant seat, this may be the single best set I’ve seen this year. Better, even, than three hours of Neil reconnecting with his old pals. Highlight: Kev Carmody’s show-stopping ?River of Tears?.

Yolke
Syrup EP

When they’re not bumming around North America in dubious jalopies, Yolke make groggy compositions that blur the lines between pop, electronic, ambient and psych. Like everything else on Fallopian Tunes, their second EP is all too immersive. Check out ?Golden Rings?, which sounds like The Sea and Cake gone R&B.

Yolke – Golden Rings by Mess+Noise

Doctopus
Buddies

Between the cover ?art? and non-existent price tag, Buddies could easily pass for another casualty in the Battle of Who Could Care Less. But those slacker-isms are inseparable from this debut album of rough-as-guts garage rock from Perth trio Doctopus. They also threaten to hide a rugged tunefulness and a penchant for slowing things down to almost ballads – a seriously fucked version of ballads, that is. This year’s Mining Boom.

Seja
?Call Me Wrong?

All Our Wires is a feast for synth lovers, but there’s just as much heart food too. Seja Vogel’s second album offers sighing, squelchy enjoyment across the board, while ?Call Me Wrong? hits a familiar note for anyone who’s ever been tempted to change for someone. OK, for all of us then.

CALL ME WRONG by Rice Is Nice

Jody Macgregor

Courtney Barnett
?Avant Gardener?

Everybody has this on their list, right? They bloody better.

Courtney Barnett – AVANT GARDENER by lookoutkid

We All Want To
Judith Wright Centre, Brisbane

We All Want To played an unusual gig, getting a variety of guests to take over on lead vocals. McKisko, Seja, Ed Guglielmino, Ross Hope from Iron On and a bunch of other singers got to front the band for a song, sometimes taking them in unusual directions. Having performance poet Ghostboy turn ?Ramp Up the Bleeding? into jazz-club beat poetry was ace, and so was Jeremy Neale leaping around yelping ?You Used to be Funny?, but getting everybody on stage at the end for a cover of ?Streets of Your Town? was unforgettable.

Mick Harvey
?Summertime in New York?

I’d never heard of this song before Harvey covered it. The original?s fine, but Harvey’s version is exceptional.

Dom Miller
?Gone Away?

The Johnny Cash bit.

Brendan Maclean’s Twitter account

?Stupid? is a great song, the video is ace, and he put on a top-notch show at the Judy this year. But the best thing Brendan Maclean has done for music is pour out a constant stream of wit and invective on Twitter.

Steph Kretowicz

?Sobbing, Spasms & Piss: An Alternate SXSW Experience? by Stephanie Crase

Thank Christ someone had the nerve to be honest about the international festival experience for any Australian band that isn’t Tame Impala. I haven’t enjoyed an article this much in a while. Call it schadenfreude.

Standish/Carlyon
?Industrial Resort?

Of course, Standish/Carlyon were going to be called ?gay? by an Australian site commenter as some sort of insult and, of course, it took years away from these shores to produce an album as boldly camp and creatively brilliant as Deleted Scenes. A clear departure from a rock incline into the languorous dub of Conrad Standish’s London days, it’s an intersectional opus most gracefully expressed in the swelling bass of ?Industrial Resort?.

Beaches
?Veda?

Where the guitar-based five-piece have always lent themselves to a heightened state of mind in psychedelia, ?Veda? gives explicit reference and literal realisation to their spiritual mantra and it works.

Abbe May
?Kiss My Apocalypse?

Whatever you think of the dad-joke titles and attempt at rap in ?Hurricane Heartbeat?, Abbe May has balls. The balls to sing about her own sexuality, the balls to piss away her rootsy roots and the balls to reference Madonna, Justin Timberlake and Rihanna in the first 30 seconds of her fourth album since gaining notice within pub rock. Incidentally, May doesn’t actually have balls and said figure of speech should probably change accordingly.

Lace Curtain
?Good Intentions?

Mikey Young, James Vinciguerra and David West are three of the most interesting Australian musicians working today. So it’s only fair that they should form a trio while making the logical step from hardcore to Total Control’s synth-punk and then the raw, slightly wonky electronica of Lace Curtain and ?Good Intentions?.

Max Easton

Model Citizen

Model Citizen are pretty divisive, which irks me, as they are the shit. The below clip ?Feelings,? is my favourite of theirs, a dichotomous punch to the gut and lift of the heart with Jon Hunter (The Holy Soul) singing the connection between the healing and exhaustive qualities of lust and love. Other tracks of theirs sung by Nic Warnock (Bed Wettin? Bad Boys) and Nic De Jong (Naked on the Vague) give entirely different flavours to their formula, resulting in one of the most unlikely holy trinities of pop I’ve seen.

Oily Boys, Housewives + MC Madcunt
The Old Fitz, Sydney

Gig of the year for me so far, a perfect storm of aggression directed from Sydney’s two best active punk bands and the hilarious drawls of MC Madcunt’s mock hip-hop posturing about the southwest suburbs.

Exhaustion
?Don’t Fly Right?

Everyone needs a demented fuck-you song for a fallen villain, and Exhaustion’s LP Future Eaters has a lot of stock in dancing on an enemy’s grave. ?Don’t Fly Right? is my favourite of theirs, replete with the dying-animal analogy of, ?Oh mama, you used to fly real nice/Now you’ve clipped your wings/And dug yourself a hole.?

Don’t Fly Right by EXHAUSTION

Bed Wettin? Bad Boys
Ready For Boredom

If I ever have a kid I feel like I’ll be one of those pushy dads who tries to map their kid’s development with the music I like. Suicidal Tendencies when they’re a bitter teen, The Only Ones when they get their first girlfriend and, once they’re broke and living in a tired sharehouse, looking back at their relationships as a series of ill-advised mistakes and wondering what to do with their lives – that’s when I’ll force Ready for Boredom onto them.

Dick Diver
?Calendar Days?

When I first heard this song I was convinced that Dick Diver had found the nostalgic brown sound so you could mine feelings of long-passed regret about the people you let slip away while you watch your dirty clothes and dishes pile. It frustrates me when people fob off songs and records like this as ?more jangly shit.? They always seem to be the people who could most use a long, solemn think.

Mitchell Judge

TV Colours
Purple Skies, Toxic River

After six years of waiting, TV Colours exceed all expectations with their debut record.

Doctopus
Buddies

Everything about Doctopus, from their name to their Facebook posts, indicates that they don’t take themselves seriously at all. But after this excellent album, they’ve proven that their music deserves serious attention. Keep an eye out for their follow-up Wobbegong, which is already recorded and scheduled for release later this year.

Andr?
?Mend?

Every time I watch this film clip I change my mind on whether it’s awesome or awful, but I’ve been sold on the song from the beginning. There’s something beautiful yet disturbing about Andr? calmly repeating ?My eyes are on the ceiling? as a wave of guitars tear the walls down.

Nuggets at Sydney Town Hall

Straight Arrows, The Laurels, The Gooch Palms and more of the country’s most fun bands took over Town Hall on the eve of Australia Day for a mini-festival that finished with the crowd covered in toilet paper.

Scott & Charlene’s Wedding
?Fakin? NYC?

Slacker rock with an endearingly honest and relatable story: it’s what we’ve come to expect. But after releasing one-and-a-half classic albums, there’s clearly no need for Craig Dermody to change his approach.

Scott & Charlene’s Wedding – Fakin’ NYC by FIRE RECORDS

Jagwar Ma
Howlin

The hype machine certainly went nuts over these guys. Thankfully their debut, Howlin, more than lived up to expectations.

PVT
?Homosapien?

There’s something about this screwed-up beat and crazy vocal sampling that just works, and takes the track from a typical indie thrash to something pretty interesting.

The Superjesus
The Resurrection Tour

When their killer second record Jet Age dropped in 2000, I was seven years old. Yep. Needless to say, by the time I finally got around to listening to it, The Superjesus had disappeared from the live music scene. This year, however, marked their return with a slew of shows beginning last month at the Annandale Hotel in Sydney.

Owl Eyes
?Closure?

An Australian Idol alumna, Owl Eyes has morphed from the typical cookie-cutter mould into something resembling Kylie Minogue’s disco-indie love child. Whatever, it works.

The Annandale Hotel

Respect must be paid. This grungy, falling apart building has become the microcosm of the battle for Sydney’s live music scene. Miraculously, it has survived – just.

Edward Sharp-Paul

Camperdown & Out
?Manly?

I don’t even like this song all that much. It’s pretty crummy, and yet it makes me smile so much: that stoned jangle, the not-even-close harmonising. There’s just something about the image of a lanky, crumpled inner-city mook sitting on the ferry, asking himself ?What will I do in Manly?? that makes everything seem OK again.

Oh Mercy
Northcote Social Club

I’ve always loved Oh Mercy for what they ought to be as much as for what they are. Excess, excessive restraint, and hubris have haunted their three recorded efforts, respectively, as Alex Gow has tried to find the skeleton key to the success he seems to crave. Their Australian curtain call for the immediate future was different, though. Over two nights at the Northcote Social Club, they were just a great rock band with a deep back catalogue and a blistering cover of ?Fisherman’s Blues? in their pocket.

Dick Diver
Calendar Days

Not sure what to say about this one. A brilliant album. The warm, slow realisation that you’re watching a great band develop right in front of your eyes, the thought that their next album might be better still, and knowing that you’ll be bragging about the time you walked in on Al Montfort in the loo at a house party 15 years from now.

Crime and the City Solution
?Domina?
Nestled in the middle of American Twilight, ?Domina? doesn’t initially shape up as anything in particular. Simon Bonney pulls his familiar trick of tossing out so many rhymes and alliterations that something’s bound to stick. Out of nowhere, though, a haunting, majestic chorus rises up like the bow of the Titanic. I laughed incredulously, and listened again and again. There were no clues. ?Domina? summarizes the wonder of CATCS, Bonney pulling at the eternal threads of tragedy and beauty with his grubby fingers.

Harmony
I’ll Be Your Mirror, Altona

Searching through the various messages I’d sent myself over the sweaty, attritional weekend that was I’ll Be Your Mirror, I found the following: ?They tap some ancient wellspring. Tom Lyngcoln is a motherfucker.? I’m not sure I can improve on that in terms of capturing the intensity and the beauty of that performance.

Eucalyptic Echoes

Bunch of psych-centric gigs in Brisbane. Keeping the swamp alive and weird.

The Drones

More the song than the album, though it’s a good album as well. I keep coming back to this eight-minute opener. There’s nothing about this song that’s trying to keep people at a distance. It’s just a big, epic statement of intent and you either enjoy the scale of it or you don’t.

The Rational Academy
Winter Haunts

This record actually got creepier as the months passed. Pretty sure its ghost is standing in the corner of my spare room right now, just waiting to spray blood everywhere or tell me about its ex-girlfriend. Everything about Winter Haunts is worse since the temperature dropped.

King Parrot
?Shit on the Liver? clip

Released super late in December, this didn’t make it into any of my feeds until well into 2013, probably because I don’t travel in these circles too often. ?Shit on the Liver? is funny and furious and, in a space preoccupied with benign NSFW gimmicks, this clip got over by flying its freak flag as high as possible.

Touching the Bees
Protoplanetary Disk Phase EP

New project from Jeremy Finlayson (Budd) and Nadia Markovic (Budd/Christbait). Heavy fuzz instrumentals. Have been listening to Budd for almost 20 years now so I am genetically predisposed to enjoy this EP.

Hunting Pictures
Hunting Pictures EP

This record barely exists. The band’s already over and I’d be surprised if 50 people have heard it. Pity because it was such a good start.

Anthony Carew

Misfit Mod Islands & Islands
Mmdelai II EP

Two records by two Kiwi expats that’?ve barely raised a blogospheric blip, when they deserve to be shouted about from on rooftops. Misfit Mod – the work of Auckland-born, London-based Sarah Kelleher – makes dead-zone, monotone, sullen-synth-pop mantras that find brilliance, transcendence and genuine wisdom through simplicity and repetition. Mmdelai – Christchurch-raised, Melbourne-dwellin? Annemarie Duff – hits on something more fidgety, restless, and slightly cosmic; refracting the ghosts of distant raves through glimmering synth patterns and goth-y atmospheres.

Banoffee

At an altogether memorable show on a freezing-cold Melbourne night – a crew of kids crammed into Shebeen to dance all sweaty to Oscar Key Sung, Electric Sea Spider and DJ Luke Pocock – the most memorable moment was the opening set for Banoffee, the alter ego of Martha Brown (formerly?) of Otouto. It’s essentially her turn towards R&B – all jittery thunk and slinky syncopation and vocals trending soulful – but that basic reading seems like the least interesting take on it, her mix of appropriated pop maximalism and bashful confessional scanning as borderline Grimes-y. Though her stage demeanour is filled with blushes, Brown’s music is as thoughtful and careful as it is heart-on-sleeve emotional, employing various production tricks – like Karin Dreijer-styled, downward-trending pitch-shifting, or beat-made rhythms so fidgety they scan as restless – to create an uneasiness, an anxiousness, that feels like exposing expression-of-self. Banoffee plies intimate ache into jams that still inspire dancefloor joy.

Andras
?Running Late?

From Andras Fox’s old-school-house affair, Embassy Caf?, comes this gloriously produced piece of sad-hearted, slow-footed dancefloor betrayal with Oscar Key Sung singing his soulful heart out re: relationships gone awry over a one-finger piano motif and nearly-ascetic beat-made minimalism.

Guy Blackman
Toff in Town, Melbourne

Solo gigs from the Chapter Music bossman have been scarce in recent years, but a run of supports for ex-Galaxie 500 heroes Damon & Naomi showed that not only does Blackman still have it – ?it? being, in this case, ?making straight white men feel slightly uncomfortable by confronting them with [his] renegade sexuality? – but he’s taken it to even weirder, creepier, old-man-ier places. His former ditties about pressing records and youthful comings-out have been traded in for tunes detailing dark strip-club obsessions and jerking off whilst ?camming?.

Kirin J Callinan
Sugar Mountain, Melbourne

Sugar Mountain is Melbourne’s indie-rock social event of the year: an indoor, in-the-city music jamboree free from the outdoor festival’s spectre of Southern Cross tattoos, macho guitar rock, coked-out Modular bogans, permeating smell of piss, etc. It’s a ?safe space?, as it were, which is why Kirin J Callinan’s set felt like a dropped bomb. It bombed, as situationist art action, feeling more like work of naughty, naughty schoolboy sedition – where something shocking and radical was flirted with yet quickly shied away from. We were promised transgressive performance art then treated to, sadly, just another rock show. But for anyone who laments how banal and toothless and instantly forgettable most rock shows are – especially in the confines of festivals, usually the worst place to watch anyone do anything – being presented with an instant debate-starter was like a gift from the conversational gods.

Tim Scott

Division Four – Doctors Wife by smartguy

Taken from the N-side of the Falling/Running 12? on Mexican Summer, this dirty DIY disco banger has one half of Total Control getting their clubby grime on.

You Yangs
?Tullamarine?

Aussie as. It’s called ?Tullamarine? and sounds like a mess of Dick Diver and GANGgajang playing on the side of the Western Ring Road.

Love Chants
?I Won’t Run?

Hushed, haunting and beautiful guitar explorations from their four-track EP.

I Wont Run by Love Chants

Wednesday afternoons, 4ZZZ

Hosted by Matt Kennedy of Kitchens Floor, this all-Australian radio program is an excellent mix of the new and less new. Expect brackets such as Venom P Stinger, East Link and Blank Realm. Highly recommended.
