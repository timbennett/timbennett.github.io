Not Art

By Tim Fitzpatrick 2013-07-17T00:00:00+00:00

That drum sound. Thudding away like a wet towel covering the snare after being used to clean the statue of Leapin? Leroy Loggins out the front of Brisbane Entertainment Centre. That comes later on, after the crashing cymbals and slurred rants of opener ?Hello, My Name Is?, but it hangs in the memory like that same cold, wet towel.

This makes ?Hello? a bit misleading as the opener, appearing to lay out the album’s intentions with its epic peaks and cries in a slacker drawl of ?I’m not a faceless, not a racist, not a fraction, I’m not a whore wanting more??

These are youth raiding the FM airwaves of the ?80s. They’ve got a ?big? album. Big like a Don Henley record, and Not Art fully embraces this approach, clean production and all. ?Luck Now? – with its smooth piano riffs, simple beat, odd vocals drifting up for harmonies and what sound like grunts popping up now and then on the off-beat – is a clear example of this.

These links aren’t being drawn just because there’s a song called ?Phil Collins? on the album. There’s a song called ?Phil Collins? on the album because the band are fully aware of the lineage. They ride this out and the album is solid because of this commitment.

If a band were to call a song ?Jimi Hendrix?, following a template with screaming wah, pentatonic solos and the like, it’d just come across as a wank. So how do Big Scary get away with calling a song ?Phil Collins?? Because they’re from the same generation that grew up taking the piss out of the jacket-loving drummer, watching South Park paste his face on Phil Collins Hill. Their parents probably went to see his ?Seriously, Live!? Australian tour in 1990. He’s been in their consciousness for as long as they’ve been exposed to media of some kind. They name a song called ?Phil Collins? and it’s still kind of a joke.

Singing about being taken to the river, though, is always a bit touch and go. This was probably another incentive to name the song, to give it that piss-taking halo from the start. Or maybe it isn’t a piss-taking approach. Maybe it’s the same kind of honesty as that Hendrix-loving cover band plugging away at Irish Murphy’s/Irish O’Reilly?s/Micky McGee’s Irish Tavern in any given town across Australia.

There’s a brief jump to Jeff Buckley territory on ?Belgian Blues?, soaring wails reaching the same heights as that multi-breasted sky whale floating around. Maybe if it had Puff Daddy’s cover of ?Kashmir? playing through a speaker on one of its seven nipples.

As for the honesty itself, this should be obvious from the leaf on the cover. Placed there on a plain blue background with the same impact as an avocado. Simple – not art; not meant to be art – and they’re saying so from the start.
