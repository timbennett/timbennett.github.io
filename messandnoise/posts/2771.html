Memory Night

By Luke Telford 2013-05-29T00:00:00+00:00

It’s best not to think of Chris Abrahams as a known quantity. Though most widely recognised as the pianist in The Necks, he’s been quietly active in various improv circles over the past few years. A regular performer at the NOWNow concert series, he’s also issued duo records with David Brown and Lucio Capece, acted as a foil to Dale Gorfinkel and Robbie Avenaim’s Sonic Systems Laboratory alongside prepared-piano wunderkind Cor Fuhler, and made increasing use of his DX7 synthesiser in live solo explorations.

Memory Night* is Abrahams? first solo recording since 2011?s lushly expressive *Play Scar. While the latter offered a logical progression from his expansive arpeggios with The Necks, this new record makes extensive use of his improvisational vocab. It’s a less welcoming listen, but no less rewarding.

These four tracks play more as a concise deck of windows onto palpable ambiences than discrete, carefully composed pieces. The less you can see when you hear it, the better. Each track all but challenges the listener to trust in the heightened aural awareness that comes with imposed blindness. Shorn of sight, you’re forced to let your imagination parse each of these intense headspaces and make sense of the deeply unsettling presences they imply.

?Leafer? opens with metallic coruscation, a foreboding industrial hum and dense electronic rumbling that pans almost imperceptibly across the stereo spectrum. This palpable stasis yields to what sounds like lashings of bowed metal that snarl at the edges before commanding the presence of the whole track, bellowing and flailing. A wounded creature with bared teeth, calling through the dark.

Chris Abrahams – Stabilise Ruin by ROOM40

?Bone and Teem? settles like frost in the deep of night. Its crystalline matrices gently lift the warmth from your forearms. An abstracted piano lopes over the surface of the track, carefully, for fear the ice will crack. Guitar fidgets in a panic in one channel and drones impassively in the other as the piece gradually thaws in a tense rift of texture.

?Strange Bright Fact? corrals apprehensive piano with spitting electronics before flattening it with an expansive, hollow drone and teeming clouds of bats. ?Stabilised Ruin? casts a digitally stuttering motif out into high seas to be buffeted and subsumed by the spume.

The record begs description but defies it. Its real beauty lies in its alien quality, its intangibility – none of the sound you’re presented with is immediately familiar, but all of it is charged with an intense sense of place and presence.

It’s an ambitious recording but a successful one, though it demands close attention. If you let it play quietly out of laptop speakers on a bright afternoon, it’ll seem cruelly insignificant – erroneous aural wallpaper, irrelevant, niggling, unattractive. At night, played loudly, preferably into a darkened room, it takes on its own character – the type you might expect of audio hallucinations after sitting for too long in an anechoic chamber. It’s an unassuming, disquieting piece of music.
