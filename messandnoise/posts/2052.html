Record Reviews

Intermediate

By ReneSchaefer 2009-08-01T00:00:00+00:00

I’m not a big fan of overtly technical music. I’ve never been able to embrace math-rock, post-rock, post-hardcore, and all their esoteric permutations and incomprehensible sub-categories. Something about these genres freezes my mind, makes me fidgety and ill-at-ease, like I should get it, but I’m somehow failing a test I didn’t know I was expected to study for.

Athol’s songs are all instrumental. They’re absolutely beautifully recorded by the king of that shit, Neil Thomason, at his super classy Head Gap sound factory in North Melbourne. This alone will instantly sell this record to the aficionados. The drums do that stop-start, gallop-halt thing, bass notes are shooting out all over the place like someone dropped a match in a box of firecrackers and the guitar veers from punky riffs to gratuitous jazz rock odysseys. Still, I’m wondering what it’s all for.

On track eight, the drolly named ?Mootown?, some stabby horns are thrown into the mix, emphasizing the trio’s debt to jazz and twisted funk, but the moment passes too quickly to signal any in-depth exploration. A shame really, as it’s a bit of a highlight among a bunch of tunes that deliver precious little in the way of hooks.

Sure, there is nothing wrong with music-for-music’s sake. It’s not hard to tell that these musicians are having an absolute blast creating this complex mesh of notes and rhythms. I probably would enjoy seeing them play live, as the key to the music lies in the interaction between the players in real time, in a real space. I imagine they would be sweating up a storm, pulling guitar-faces and contorting their limbs and minds to find those hard-to-reach wacky augmented chords. Athol’s fans probably stand transfixed before this spectacle, their fingers forming phantom shapes on invisible fretboards.

You know, I love the dexterity and crazy innovation of post-bebop era jazz, but at the heart of the music there was always a tune to hang sonic explorations from. It could be something as hackneyed as Coltrane’s reading of ?My Favourite Things?. Here, the tunes become lost and what I’m left with is aural wallpaper. As with loudly patterned home furnishings, whether you want to decorate your living room with them is an entirely personal choice. Beauty is in the eye (and ear) of the beholder after all.
