Hello Anxiety

By Doug Wallen 2011-02-11T00:00:00+00:00

In a familiar arc, Sydney’s Guineafowl has grown from laptop demos to a proper band, although the founder/frontman still goes strictly by the alias that is the project’s name. And the music follows suit: it’s appealing and timely on the surface, but it’s difficult to tell what exactly of substance is going on beneath. By ticking a few too many of the expected boxes, a band with all the right moves can feel prefabricated.

That’s a danger from the outset of this debut EP. The opening ?In Our Circles? carries whiffs of TV on the Radio in its chant-like vocals, Animal Collective in its reverbed shimmer, and Vampire Weekend in its faintly island melodies. In other words: a confident voice protrudes from dreamy surrounds with a vagueness of purpose. ?Little Fingers? adds the kneejerk build of Arcade Fire and dispenses indie rock clich’s about mothers, fathers and in this case, the banished planet Pluto. Also, ?revolution? and ?electrocution? are rhymed. Still, there’s a sharp span of guitar punctuating things.

IN OUR CIRCLES – Guineafowl by scarletartistmanagement

In fact, each of these tunes has one standout instrumental portion. In ?Botanist?, it’s the synth. In ?My Lonely Arms?, it’s another guitar solo. The latter song is the EP’s highlight, more rough-cut but sacrificing none of the band’s obsessive catchiness. By Guineafowl’s standards, the refrain is brazenly rebellious: ?So I’m saying that I can’t go a day/Without thinking that you’re fucking my brain.? It’s about three minutes even and feels more like a group of humans playing together, rather than a Venn diagram of the aspects required for high radio rotation and festival exposure.

The demo, ?Mothr?, is appropriately wraith-like at first, though soon it’s glossier than many indie bands? finished product. Again, it’s all about the build and well-placed signifiers. But as a send-off, it brings us no closer to grasping the soul of Guineafowl.
