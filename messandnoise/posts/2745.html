Malabar

By Anthony Carew 2013-03-12T00:00:00+00:00

When Max Doyle called his band Songs, the Sydney-based Kiwi expat was hoping for a handle so bland that no one would ever ask about it. Instead – given how few names truly thwart our Google overlords – it’s the question they’re asked most. The banality of Songs? moniker speaks to a core tenant of indie rock’s self-effacing philosophy: that the songs are just songs, man. That, contrary to a listener’s attempts to read meaning and find motivation – to create a sustained narrative – it’s all just a ?natural progression, not a conscious decision?. An analyst would call this ?resistance?, an essential refusal to engage in the psychoanalytical process. And what are critics if not mock psychoanalysts, out to form whole portraits from the fragments presented to – performed for – them? Letting the songs speak for themselves sounds great in theory, but what are they actually saying?

07 Malabar by Popfrenzy

Malabar*, as an album title, holds instant promise. After 2008?s *Songs* EP and 2009?s *Songs* LP (actually, a second *Songs* LP would’ve been truly transgressive, or at least Red House Painters-esque), having the record named after a specific Sydney suburb taps into that Waitsian philosophy that good lyrics need a place name, a location and weather. Sadly, *Malabar?s title track doesn’t have good lyrics. Instead, Doyle and foil Ela Stiles sing the mantra ?The sun rises out of the sea? six times over, confirming that Malabar is, indeed, on the east coast. ?What’s it all mean to me?/I’m still running free/It’s just a nice place to be,? they add, in singsong rhymes that feel kind of cruel to type out in black-and-white. Labelmates Camperdown & Out have turned suburban geography into a thematic metier, but here the title seems incidental and the title track’s text serves as a reminder that people need to wield Go-Betweens comparisons carefully.

All those old heroes of ?proper? indie are easy to cite when Songs barrel through jangle, the boisterous insistence and wonky vox of ?The Country? and the thrum and strum of ?Good Times? using guitars – the contrasting clangour of dissonance and the sweet peal of harmony – to create a sense of forward momentum. Wholly cut from the classic indie-rock cloth, Malabar is an album where the guitars lead the conversation and the vocals murmur mere nods of assent. ?Alone When I’m With You? opens the album with an anthemic riff rising out of squalling fuzz; there’s not a note of jangle amid ?90s revivalism so pungent it smells one part teen spirit, one part Ammonia. Stiles takes a vocal lead but her voice follows; it’s a secondary instrumental element, dowsed under reverb and obscured by a mirroring keyboard line, and it makes for a fairly potent example of Songs? songs being guitar-centric constructions.

01 Alone When I’m With You by Popfrenzy

Which isn’t to doubt the influence of keyboards on Malabar*, largely unheard on Songs? self-titled LP. Here the album’s twin try-ons of paisley psychedelia, ?Looking Without Seeing? and ?Ever Since?, are liberally ladled with Manzarek organ runs and, on the former, there’s trilling flute to boot. Like the title track, they’re exercises in repetition. And, on ?Ever Since?, something bordering on guitar heroism too. Going on comparing and contrasting further songs – like single ?Boy/Girl,? which bears the record’s best melody and builds to a wiggin? ending pleasingly, or the down-tempo ?Ringing Bells?, where the ringing strings do duly chime – gives a more rounded rendering of the record, but also paints a greater picture: *Malabar is a collection of songs, no more nor less.

If you’re fond of these songs – either as individuals or as a whole – then the lack of a central thesis statement seems of no concern. Judged as Songs, they’re swell and all; judged as Album, that’s another story.
