Stranger In My Land

By Sam 2013-04-22T00:00:00+00:00

Stranger in My Land, the first record in nine years from indigenous singer-songwriter Roger Knox, is a triumph on a number of levels. It stands as a high-water mark for Aboriginal country music; it acts as an incredibly important historical document; it almost perfectly marries the whitest of American music with indigenous Australian culture; it’s some of the finest country music you’ll hear all year. All this from the man they call the King of Koori Country – you’d expect nothing less.

Knox has been known, since his 1983 debut Give it a Go, as a shining light on the Australian country music scene. While his recorded output has been far from prolific, what he has released, along with his mesmerising live shows with the Euraba Band, has endeared him to many. Not only here at home, but recently in the US too. Perhaps he’s not reached the name status some of his contemporaries have, but Roger Knox is a force, as is evidenced here.

Stranger in My Land began a number of years ago when Chicago-based artist Jon Langford (Mekons, Waco Brothers) met Knox while in Australia. The pair connected and Langford in particular became entranced with Koori country, saying in a recent interview: ?I just thought those songs deserve to be sung, and deserve to be available.?

Roger Knox and Kelly Hogan, Blue Gums Calling Me Back Home by BSHQ

This is where the historical document comes in, as Stranger in My Land is a collection of songs by musicians and friends Knox has worked with over the years, some of which are incredibly hard to find elsewhere, and so are given new life here, a new place to shine – an Aboriginal country songbook, if you will. Langford hooked Knox up with his crack band, The Pine Valley Cosmonauts, with guest appearances from Sally Timms, Bonny ?Prince? Billy and, in his last ever recorded appearance, Charlie Louvin, amongst others. They took these songs, Knox put his stamp on them and the results are magnificent.

Perhaps the best known track is Archie Roach’s ?Took The Children Away?, to which Knox and The ?Nauts add Hammond B3 (courtesy of Dallas Good from The Sadies) and some subtle pedal steel, which along with harmony vocals from Kelly Hogan, make this as bewitching a version as you’ll ever hear.

The band bring a subtle taste of the blues to tracks like opener ?The Land Where The Crow Flies Backwards? (written by Dougie Young, who never released an album but who was recorded, Lomax-style, in the early ?60s) and the title track (from the album Vic Simms recorded live in Bathurst Gaol as a prisoner in the ?70s). Far from sticking out, the blues melds easily with the soft country sounds that make up the rest of the album, its ability to move in and out of these hallowed genres testament to Knox’s ability to read musical mood, to place things just right.

Roger Knox w/ Dave Alvin, “The Land Where The Crow Flies Backwards” by BSHQ

?Scobie’s Dream? (or ?Scobie’s Hangover, as it’s also known – also written by Young) is more in the vein of country and western, an upbeat number that precedes the Johnny Cash-esque ?Ticket to Nowhere? (on which Knox’s voice heavily resembles the aforementioned). None of these songs are simply covered; they’re lovingly recreated and turned into something new, something that pays homage to songs that may well have been lost had it not been for this album, but also something that shows the versatility not only in how Knox is able to work, but in how Koori country can stretch beyond what some consider limited borders.

Roger Know has shown here that he’s more than deserved of his King of Koori Country title. He’s recaptured a number of long-lost songs, and taken country music and made it relevant to Australian history and culture. And the album itself is a cracker. He may not be a name, but Roger Knox is a force. A formidable one indeed.
