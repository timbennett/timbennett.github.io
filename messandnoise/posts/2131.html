Ratking

By Doug Wallen 2009-10-13T00:00:00+00:00

Whether one’s goal is purism or reinvention, hip-hop is a tricky genre to try and inhabit. Fledgling Sydney rapper Belle Brooks does it with disturbing ease on her debut EP as Dirt Child, although her aim is often satirical and certainly subversive. She’s out to have fun alright, but her interest in hip-hop conventions is mostly for the sake of exploding and exploiting them. Along the way she’s willful and volatile, oozing explicit scatological and sexual details from her every postmodern pore.

Ratking alights with ?Golden Tooth?, an introduction touching on Brooks? isolated upbringing in Mudgee, NSW. ?Not a brain-dead hick at home among the gum trees,? she announces of herself. It’s more pointedly about where she stands as a rapper, however – ?I’m more troubadour than stupid whore? – and her distaste for pretty much everything around her. The insults fly fast, as does the sexual braggadocio and grotesque imagery (?Like a mining disaster I’m putting blood in your shaft?). If all of this sounds bratty or overly self-conscious, for the most part it’s engaging and entertaining in its absurdity. That said, such over-the-top antics mean some lines are bound to fall flat. Case in point: ?I’m spitting hot shit like Satan’s arsehole.?

Produced and co-written by her manager James Woods (also founder of Climbing Hills, one of the labels behind this EP), Ratking?s bank of beats and synths are light, glossy and accessible. Woods, who got his start doing A&R for the likes of The Fumes and Ash Grunwald, even raps on two tracks under the moniker Jimmy Suit. He’s clearly had a hand in guiding Brooks from her uni-born work as a sculptor to this quirky diversion. Of course, she takes to it well, nailing the complexities of her reference-heavy rhymes while establishing her own voice, complete with a rascally squeak. Some of Brooks? targets are too easy, such as the vapid nightclub scene mocked on ?Sic 101?, and she’s better when mapping out a surreal world of her own design, as on ?Slugmite Millionaire?.

The sex stuff can be tired as well as funny; ?Cremaster? is almost entirely about testicles, loaded with cartoonish violence and cheeky one-liners. But the one entry that will truly rankle – or maybe empower – listeners is ?Anthem?, a ballsy takedown of Aussie hip-hop. It ruthlessly calls out Hilltop Hoods, Figg Kid, The Herd, Kid Mad and Macromantics, although a guesting Woods half-defends Drapht and Bliss N Eso. He also sings the hook, ?This is Aussie hip-hop/So many failures there?, to which Dirt Child responds, ?You might have a hoodie but you haven’t got a Glock.? The two-pronged problem with our country’s contributions to the genre, as Brooks and Woods see it, is its dullness and its misplaced thirst for American-style street cred. That’s not altogether unfair, is it?

Dirt Child sets out to push buttons – and push buttons she does. It’s hard to take her too seriously, however, and this could just as easily be a one-off art project as the start of a music career. But in these PC times, it’s just nice to hear someone rattling the cage.
