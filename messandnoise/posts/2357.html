Midnight Remember

By DomAlessio 2010-09-01T00:00:00+00:00

Little Red has always seemed like a band older than their collective years, which worked for a record like 2008?s Listen To Little Red*, because it was imbued with such a palpable sense of fun. But on *Midnight Remember*, the Melbourne outfit has dropped the AM fidelity of their debut in favour of more sheen but less spark. Some of these songs feel so meticulously thought out, as though a certain musical alchemy will create the perfect pop song, that they’ve forgotten the one thing that’s needed for a song to connect with an audience: heart. Little Red can pull off the ebb tide of smooth yacht rock or Fleetwood Mac’s phosphorus harmonies with aplomb, and there are a handful of really catchy songs on *Midnight Remember, but it’s like all the songs are coated in Teflon – they’re shiny, but they slip right off you.

All of which makes Midnight Remember perfect fodder for radio. First single ?Rock It? is a perfect example. The lyrics rhyme so well you’ll know them after only a handful of listens, although dig a little deeper and there’s little there. The ascending/descending hook fires all the right synapses in your brain, but it’s exactly like a thousand other pop songs. All of these factors coalesce to form a song – and ultimately, an entire record – that sounds reassuringly familiar and unchallenging.

Although the album loses its momentum towards the end, the first half contains some beautiful paragons of pop music: ?Slow Motion? explodes like fireworks made of ?70s soft rock melodies, while ?Place Called Love? recalls the ?50s pop that Little Red channelled so well on their debut. But their musical touchstones are so blatant that at times the band ends up sounding like a covers act at the local RSL. ?I Can’t Wait? borrows (um, completely lifts) the synth pads from Bruce Springsteen’s ?Streets of Philadelphia?, and ?Forget About Your Man? could be a long-lost Hall & Oates number.

Midnight Remember isn’t a record that will engender passion in the listener. It won’t make people rush out to get “Little Red” tattooed on their back or inspire them to follow the band around the country like the Ribwich or The Grateful Dead. It’s pretty, sure, but it lacks the substance to make any real lasting impression.
