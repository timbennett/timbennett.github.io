X-Spurts ? The 1977 Recordings

By Patrick 2011-11-01T00:00:00+00:00

The story of X is as tragic as it is sordid. With more deaths than an Agatha Christie novel, X has been proclaimed ?the band they couldn’t kill?. Bass player Ian Rilen had famously left Rose Tattoo after writing arguably the band’s best known song, Bad Boy Love, unimpressed with the Tatts? evolving pub rock style. Rilen put together his own band, Evil Rumours, eventually recruiting a lanky hippie from Armadale, Steve Lucas, as lead vocalist, Ian Krahe on guitar and the former cop (oh, the irony) Steve Cafeiro on drums.

In 1978, with X already the scourge of the inner-city Sydney music scene and its attendant venues and booking agents, original guitarist Ian Krahe died from a heroin overdose, thereby setting the scene for X’s history of tragedy and lost opportunities. The tale of the band’s gig earlier that evening (as told by Rilen in an interview some years later) is prescient: Krahe breaks a string, while Lucas lies on his back on stage, staring at some imagined pattern in the ether. The manager of the venue confronts Rilen, demanding an explanation. Rilen, exhibiting the blunt diplomatic skills commensurate with his position in rock’n?roll folklore, retorts: ?He’s broken a string, he’s tripping, and I’m telling you to fuck off!?

Rilen, Lucas and Cafeiro soldiered on in the aftermath of Krahe’s death, initially with Geoff Holmes as replacement guitarist, and subsequently as a three-piece with Lucas assuming guitar duties. The band’s morbid tale continued with the deaths of other band members, including Cafeiro in 1988 and Rilen in 2006. Lucas has continued to breathe life into the near-dead corpse of X, with the re-incarnated version of the band variously including Cathy Green, Kim Volkman and former Cosmic Psychos drummer Bill Walsh.

Save for the odd obscure vinyl and bootleg release, the potency of the original four-piece X had been largely lost to history, until the recent recovery of the tapes from X’s first recording session in 1977. Now lovingly restored and released by local re-issue label Aztec Music, X-Spurts – The 1977 Recordings provides a glimpse into the power of X in its original guise.

Recorded originally in the lounge room of ?Irish John? on a four-track recorder, X-Spurts is rock’n?roll with attitude in the red. ?You Don’t Like Me? is both a personal and sociological assertion of isolation, while Infamy offers a prophetic observation of the band’s future. Krahe’s guitar attacks are brutal, blending with Rilen’s sociopathic bass attack and Lucas’s emphatic vocals to create a sound that set the sonic scene for a slew of successors, some of them credible, others not. The sheer brutality of ?Slash Your Wrists? condemns the efforts of many of X’s contemporaries (including those in the so-called punk scene) as vacuous and pretentious. On ?Runaway?, Lucas is a man possessed, taking Del Shannon’s original pop classic and injecting it with a nihilistic attitude so sharp it draws blood.

While X has occasionally been erroneously lumped in with punk bands of the era, in truth (and in the band’s own opinion) it was simply a rock’n?roll band – warts, excesses and all. It’s an observation borne out by the rugged intensity of ?Losing? and the slick riffage of ?Hey You?. X’s knife-wielding pop sensibility is there for the world to hear on ?TV Cabaret Role?, ?Revolution? and the original version of ?Degenerate Boy?.

On ?One More Chance?, Lucas screams like a wailing banshee, pleading for one more chance to explain his case; the original context may have been emotional, but the message has a wider resonance. X had its chances over the years and – true to its empathy with the ultimate futility of the rock’n?roll cause – pissed them all up against the wall. But that’s what make X one of the great rock’n?roll bands of history. You just don’t make this shit up.
