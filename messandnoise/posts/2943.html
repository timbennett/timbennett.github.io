Legalize Everything

By Krystal Maynard 2014-09-22T00:00:00+00:00

Dripping in reverb, lackadaisical in nature, paying tributes to the greats of the ?60s: what else did we expect from Legalize Everything*, the third album by The Frowning Clouds? Some bands commit to a sound for better or for worse, and for their own reasons. Five years on from their debut release *Listen Closelier, the Geelong outfit are still playing the kind of music they want to play, and that’s gotta warrant some respect. They’re not making music for anyone but themselves.

?Carrier Drone? opens the album with nice bright guitar tones and a staccato riff which acts as a perfect signifier for the excellent chorus – it’s mid-paced laissez-faire garage that flows effortlessly. ?Our House? follows suit in style and substance and by the third track, ?Sun Particle Mind Body Experience?, it’s worrying that the album will maintain this one relaxed jaunt throughout. Complexities start to become noticeable on ?Move It?, the first sign that the album contains a subtle depth to it. Featuring acutely accented vocal phrasing, The Kinks? Davies brothers could have easily been responsible for these pop nuances. Plus the wild outro solo, crackling with dirt, wraps the song up nicely.

?Dead Growth? excels with a galloping drumbeat, the vocals drifting above everything in an Oh Sees fashion: inaudible and floating somewhere in the dreamscape. The band step out of their reverb bubble on ?No Blues?, the vocals far more candid and to the front of the mix than on any other track. And it’s a good thing, with lyrics like ?No excuse to let it wail? and ?Life’s too easy, skin’s too pale? displaying an affable sense of humour. The twee ?60s pop of ?Leopard Print Tint? feels rather irksome after the droll commentary of the song before.

?See the Girl? is one of the cleaner cuts on the record, almost emulating the sound achieved by British ?90s bands like Blur, Ash and Oasis, who weaved pop from the ?60s into their respective sounds. Album closer ?You Do to Me, Too? ends on a investigative but optimistic pop note. The lines ?I want to be all that I want to be? and ?I want to see a light/Will a light shine down on me?? feel like the thoughts of an adolescent teen approaching the onset of adulthood with both hope and trepidation. The song is ushered out in a haze of psychedelia, the earnestness of the aforementioned lyrics balanced out by a satisfying slab of psych-rock.

There’s nothing wrong with the lazy carefree saunter that The Frowning Clouds lean towards, but it’s the tracks that break out of this mold that pique the most interest. Still drawing on classic pop bands like The Kinks, Stones and Small Faces, there’s a definite pull towards more modern-sounding garage bands like Thee Oh Sees, The Strange Boys and Ty Segall – all bands whose sound is cemented in nostalgia but are infused with the idiosyncrasies and snarling realities of the modern world. It’s when the sarcasm, wit and apathy leak out that The Frowning Clouds remind us that they’re a band just as engrossed in the now as they are in past.

Legalize Everything is an album that rolls on at its own designated pace, exuding a confidence that only a band who have been around for a while could muster. They rock the boat and ripple the waters when they feel like it, keeping you on your toes while keeping you listening.
