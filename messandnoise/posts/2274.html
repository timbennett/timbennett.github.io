Drawings

By Lucy Hearn 2010-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

Named for an unpublished story by ?poet philosopher? Soren Kierkegaard, Great Earthquake is Noah Symons, a Melbourne artist and barista and member of art-folk outfit Timothy and Wilderness and post-punk act Flag And Forget. Drawings, his first solo record, is largely instrumental, simultaneously anxious and curious with traces of post and math rock. It’s a stressed-out, wiry mess of metallic percussion, hand-claps, accordion, bass and guitar that manages to use this intensity to invoke joy as well as nervousness. In this sense, it borrows more than just a quaint moniker from Kierkegaard.

Despite its seeming preoccupation with the abstract (almost no vocals, vague song titles such as ?Create? and ?Wait Think?), Drawings* is a distinctly concrete album that makes most sense when contextualised by Symon’s hometown. Indeed, *Drawings feels like a set of sketches of the city as Symons sees it. Listening to the album is akin to watching Symons as he traces out these personal sensory experiences: the buildings, the pedestrians, old flyers in shop windows, the ragged leer of a beggar.

‘Clap! Clap!’ is a frantic walk on a cold morning at peak hour: bells chime along with a chaotic pots-?n?-pans beat. You can almost see the cars and people zipping around Symons as he tries to get a hold on his day. By contrast, ‘The Passing’ is delicate. Its delayed guitars and high-pitched bells lead seamlessly into ‘From Memory’, a track that veers in mood from nervous to melancholy. Slow, cumbersome drums plod behind a droning accordion, and it sounds as if Symons is lost and bewildered on a wet afternoon.

The only track that strays markedly from Drawings‘ music as cityscape (or vice versa) is ‘Fully Formed’. One of only two songs featuring vocals, it comes from an entirely different space than the rest of the album. It’s deliberately pretty and heightened by cute lyrics and a female singer, but feels as if it belongs somewhere else.

Listening to Drawings* requires a certain amount of trust in Symons as musical guide. Often, he leads you to unexpected places, and this can be confusing and dissatisfying, especially if you have preconceived notions about where you’re headed. Ultimately, *Drawings is not an easy record, but it’s a rewarding listen if you’re willing to take that leap of faith.
