Blue Trees

By Troy 2007-09-05T00:00:00+00:00

If, like me, you’ve been staring at Dirty Three album covers while listening to their records over the past 10 years, you’ll instantly recognise Mick Turner’s painting style. Blue Trees, a collection of Turner’s solo rarity work, carries the same semi-surrealist artwork. Now, concern yourself with Tren Brothers: because this is a half/half thing. That is, half Turner in solo mode and half Turner with drummer Jim White. For Dirty Three fans, the tones of these two together will sound instantly familiar, while somehow new in context. There is Turner’s gentle strokes of guitar, where you can hear each string being drawn down before that lovely silent second where he stops to change chord, or the way White – who surely has one of the most idiosyncratic drumming styles on offer – begins a long roll, building up speed until it breaks like a wave; his is the lovely thing about these two – it breaks right back down to where it was.

?Help Mr. Rabbit, I Can’t Get Out? has the two playing in regular time (like an actual band), the music tugging back and forth with a far off train-whistle harmonica punctuating the measure. It’s like folk-Krautrock, when it cuts out at five minutes you wish it would chug for double. ?Au Revoir Mon Petit Chou?, a lonely piece based around piano, translates as ?Farewell My Little Cabbage?, proving they aren’t taking themselves too seriously. And it’s somewhat a relief to experience perhaps the best tune, ?Swing (Parts 1 & 2)? in its entirety without having to flip the damn 45 over and lose continuity.

Tren Brothers differentiates from Turner’s solo material in its wide spaces and is further from the D3 mould in its deeply personal, more subdued mood. Never the threat here of Warren Ellis? manic violence when he really lets fly. It’s less a partnership minus Ellis than two people communicating in a language their own design. Consider that they’ve been playing together over twenty years, from Venom. P Stinger through to backing Ms. Marshall on her Moon Pix album and the language thing makes sense. Simply put, this is achingly good music.
