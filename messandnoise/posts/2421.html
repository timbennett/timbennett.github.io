Dunks

By Doug Wallen 2011-02-04T00:00:00+00:00

The second of two mini-albums from Ghoul, Dunks* is a marked progression from 2008?s A Mouthful Of Gold while still feeling like a series of sketches. The Sydney quartet’s output is restless as a rule, pinning the fragmented incantations of malleably voiced singer/producer Ivan Vizintin to the canvas with any number of textured loops and glitches. Errant percolations recur and ghost away in the background, colliding shades of post-punk and art-pop into a kind of studied chaos. Every song here not just betrays an identity crisis but thrives on it.

There’s an atmospheric architecture to it, though, built on stilted guitar loops, computer-assisted harmonies, hiccupping pulses, and stray pockets of sound. Then there’s that voice. Winning comparisons early on to Antony Hegarty, Vizintin balances that occasional likeness with the acrobatics of Dirty Projectors? Dave Longstreth. These respective thickenings and leaps mirror the unpredictability of the music, and like Thom Yorke – a determined influence on this band all around – Vizintin deals in fleeting, opaque wisdom. ?You should summon me,? he advises with appropriate foreboding on ?Ouija?, while elsewhere he tentatively spits, ?I can’t get satisfaction.?

The song with the most heft and space is ?Dreambeat?, as close to a definitive Ghoul entry as you’ll get at the moment. Highlighted by a heartburn rush of guitar and a newfound drum loop at the end, it’s the weirdly suave nexus of alternate universes. Another standout, ?The Slip?, sees Vizintin practically purring as the music invades hip-hop and Prefuse 73 territory. Like ?Idioteque? retooled for a paranoid thriller, ?3 Mark? plies chipmunked echoes of his line ?choose light? “choose life” over a curdled dancefloor feel.

Ghoul – 3Mark by inertiamusic

In a recent interview, Vizintin cited the menacing and surreal textures and effects of Liars and This Heat records as touchstones in the making of Dunks. But this is no pastiche of cool influences: Ghoul has always done murky and evocative better than most, and now the band’s inching towards something like accessibility, signing to Speak N Spell and supporting Yeasayer nationally. Due later this year, a much-anticipated first album promises equal input from and defined roles for all the members, as opposed to the more scattershot approach of the past.

That’s not to say Dunks doesn’t gel perfectly, but working with the scope of an album, Ghoul has the chance to make its most profound statement yet. As it is, this is a great precursor.
