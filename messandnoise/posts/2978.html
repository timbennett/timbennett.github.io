The Door Behind The Door

By Freya Zaknich 2015-02-24T00:00:00+00:00

Sydney duo The Black Ryder have been based in North America for the past five years while recording this, their second full-length album. The Door Behind The Door dwells in the largesse of professional nostalgia, achieving psych, shoegaze, soul and gospel descriptors with ease. As band members Aimee Nash and Scott Von Ryper were previously married, the album is the medium for the transmutation of relationship. A misleading adventure of drum loops and discordant guitar in ?Babylon? goes first. You could beg some Narnian symbolism here and dismiss it as the opening of the second door. You could.

?Seventh Moon? is a strain to decipher, but a weary line that goes something like ?weightless as we fall out of love? is one of the strongest on the album. Later ?All That We Are?, a Loveless summary sans distortion, merely fronts as a resolution. Breaking up is so very hard to do. It’s a stopover from brave intentions, however, with the bare-bones and self-assuring ?The Going Up Was Worth The Coming Down? unfortunately stretched on the rack for six minutes. ?Let Me Be Your Light? is a visitation from Nash, and much more appealing. Her whisper singing stings with control, ice-thin and sparking. The chorus blows out, dunked in break-waters of choral psychedelics. The glut of voices, strings, synths and church bells are a rousing spiritual climax, everyone high on the power of love.

In ?Santaria?, drums and a guitar lick crack the dawn, descending solemnly and very close to a Velvet Underground moment like ?I’m Set Free?. When those drums drop for a moment of lone guitar it’s a shame, but the return is a joyous kalash. It’s pure Mo Tucker, forever and ever, amen. Continuing with serious homage, this time to The Rolling Stones, is a song called ?Throwing Stones?. Nash is backed by some gorgeous extras at this point and I would kneel at the feet of that choir. Here she is steadfast and a comfort to the misty grief of the rest of the album.

While there are moments of moving composition and affective melodic collisions on The Door Behind the Door, there is also a lot that languishes. I’m not sure whether to condemn it as indulgence or respect their attempt at penance. There is 11 minutes of waiting before a fleeting, sacred string progression appears in ?The Final Sleep?. It is indicative of the entire album, then, that while the duo lie in wait of a new morning to come, there is not much to enjoy here and now.

+

####The Door Behind The Door is out now on CD, vinyl and digital through The Anti-Machine Machine/The Orchard. Read the band’s Track by Track here.
