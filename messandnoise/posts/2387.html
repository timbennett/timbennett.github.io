You Am I

By Andrew McMillen 2010-10-08T00:00:00+00:00

When discussing a new release from an act like You Am I, there’s a tendency to compare it to past works. Can it match Hi Fi Way* or *Hourly, Daily*? Was it as disappointing as *Dilettantes*? So let’s try something different. Let’s pretend it’s their first album, not their ninth. Could it be wedged comfortably between the buzz bands of 2010, and hold its own? Could You Am I find an audience with *You Am I if it were their debut?

I won’t string you along. The answer to all of the above is ?yes?, but here’s where my review concept falls apart: it’d be nigh on impossible for a rock band’s debut to emerge so fully-formed. The songs and sounds contained within You Am I could only be written by a band with 20-plus years of experience up their sleeves. Its trajectory is remarkable, as the LP seems to gather momentum and clarity as it nears the end; a rarity among musical acts of any genre, for sure. It feels as though the album’s near-perfect pacing is integral to its impact. Its 11 tracks offer disparate moods and experiences, yet they’re loosely threaded through the same rock narrative that made them endearing in the first place.

Tim Rogers soothes and croons more often than he yells, which tempers most tracks with a sense of tension and restraint. A couple of Rogers’ seeming lyrical contradictions prove more curiosity than sticking point. In opener ‘We Hardly Knew You’, he questions “How can we march so blissfully on, knowing everyone loved is on loan?”, while in the upbeat, two-minute blast of ‘Pinpricks’, he concludes “I wanna feel once again/I wanna be your man.? ‘Crime’ concocts a sense of musical foreboding unlike anything else on the record, or indeed their catalogue. Rogers intones, “You cruise, you may lose/But you may end up being the last one standing”, amid muted instrumentation threatening to boil over.

But it never quite does. This sense of restraint permeates the record, and it’s compelling; arousing, even. It’s the most enduring characteristic of You Am I*, and above all, it’s this sense of – dare I say – applied maturity has elevated Rogers et al onto the next level of songwriting craft. Their playing may not be flashy, but crucially, it’s unfalteringly memorable. *You Am I is a confident statement of intent broadcast to the band’s peers: this is how it’s done, kids.
