2010: The Year In News Pt 2

By Darren Levin 2011-01-05T13:00:00+00:00

In the final installment of our news wrap up, we take a look at the biggest stories in the last half of 2010 – from more pub closures to ARIA’s night to forget. Part one here.

July


BAM! Gets Bammed: The announcement of a ?completely new style of festival? in Queensland sends alarm bells ringing at M+N* HQ, so we send in a mole (prolific forum poster noneabove) to a compulsory artists? meeting. He uncovers, among other things, a ?unique? ticketing system, whereby performers are required to sell books of tickets to their friends. BAM! organiser Sarita Beavis responds to concerns in a follow-up interview with *M+N.

Digital Radio Un-Australian: The Australian Communications and Media Authority waives the local content quotas for digital channels, paving the way for 24-hour niche services like Pink Radio and Radio GaGa. The Australian Music Radio Airplay Project launches ?Midnight GaGa Radio? – a 24-hour mash-up of Midnight Oil’s ?Beds Are Burning? with Lady GaGa’s ?Poker Face? – in response.

Kate Nash Gets Thin: Brisbane’s The Thin Kids are picked as supports for Kate Nash’s national tour, sparking outcry from the shame cauldron. The band’s singer, notorious music writer Everett True, writes a diary of the tour.

Music Victoria Appoints CEO: Long-time Age senior music writer
Patrick Donovan is appointed CEO of Music Victoria, a newly created industry body fast-tracked by February’s SLAM Rally. Donovan discusses his plans for the organization in an interview with M+N.

A Cut Copy Comeback: Sick of playing festival DJ sets, Melbourne’s Cut Copy return with a new single ?Where I’m Going?, which they reckon sounds like ?the kind of track Brian Wilson would’ve written if he took ecstasy and hung out in ?60s London instead of California?. We reckon it sounds like ?a band seeking to divorce themselves from their party days?. Either way it bodes well for forthcoming third album, Zonoscope.

August


Splendour Passes Woodfordia Test: After a decade in Byron Bay, Splendour In The Grass makes its debut at Woodfordia in Queensland’s Sunshine Coast. About 32,000 people watch performances by The Strokes, The Pixies, LCD Soundsystem and local acts Empire of the Sun, Cloud Control, Tame Impala and Wolfmother. Andrew McMillen reports.

A Month Of Acquisitions: Two independent music distributors go under: Shock is dubiously acquired by DVD/CD manufacturing and distribution company Regency Media, while Stomp is purchased by Sydney-based company Surrealus. Meanwhile, Melbourne record store institution Missing Link incorporates Record Collectors Corner.

YAI Say Bye Bye To EMI: Despite a ?strong, affectionate, intelligent relationship? with EMI, You Am I opt to release their ninth album with fledgling Sydney indie Other Tongues. Self-titled, the record is described as their most ?sonically adventurous? to date. It’s set for release in October.

Dan Luscombe Is Not Chris Cheney: OK, so it’s hardly ?news?, but Dan Luscombe’s animation about a real-life encounter with a curious ?fan? is about the funniest thing we embed all year.

More Bad News For BAM: The writing is well and truly on the wall for the BAM! Festival after organisers issue a last-minute, 2-for-1 ticketing plea. They say they need to sell 200 more tickets or the festival is off. They don’t, and the shame cauldron revels in their stupidity.

Nick Cave Sworn In As ?PM?: A MySpace poll confirms that more people would prefer to have someone with depraved sexual fantasies about Kylie and Avril Lavigne leading the country, than John Butler or Bernard Fanning. We agree.

Planets Don’t Align For Homebake, Washington Corners The Market: Organisers of the Homebake Music, Film & Arts Festival decide to call off the 2010 event, citing some gobbledygook about the earth’s gravitational pull not being to their liking. In other words: Washington was the only suitable headliner. Speaking of Washington, our festival queen makes history in Melbourne, selling out five shows at The Corner Hotel.

World Bar Flushes Out The Pokies: In an apparent victory for live music, 15 poker machines are removed from World Bar in Sydney’s Kings Cross. Management says it’ll free up space for bands.

September


Grinding Out A Controversy: In his lengthy review of the new Grinderman album, Rene Scha’fer raises a few online eyebrows by claiming that Nick Cave wasn’t really that talented to begin with. Meanwhile, speaking to M+N from the south of France (as you do), bandmate Warren Ellis says he doesn’t care what people think. ?That’s their problem,? he says in response to detractors who think they’re nothing more than ?dirty old men?.

Modular Flexes Its Muscles: Australia’s electro-pop bad boy makes a surprising comeback, announcing a new EP of ?thumping rave tunes? called Younger & Immature*, and making a shirtless cameo in a fucked-up film clip for first single ?Girl Crazy Go?. The EP’s title is a cheeky nod to his proposed second album *Manhood. Will it ever see the light of day?

St John’s De-Canonisation: Former journalist and TV producer Ed St John steps down as president and CEO of Warner Music Australia, meaning he also has to relinquish his role as ARIA chairman. Denis Handlin from Sony Music takes over.

Awards Take The Mickey Out Of Indie: The Jagermeister Independent Music Awards cause an online flame war over: A. Nominating artists with major label ties; and B. Bringing NOVA on board as a sponsor. In response, we ask six local labels whether the definition of ?indie? has been muddied by major label subsidiaries and distribution deals.

Don’t Mess With Foreign Tobacco Companies: Twenty-four hours after they pull out of Indonesia’s Java Rockin? Land festival because of its involvement with the Gudang Garam cigarette company (and its very fine range of products indeed), Wolfmother announce they’re playing the show out of contractual obligations for the fans.

Splitting Up: In an interview with M+N, Karl Scullin (aka Kes) confirms that Mum Smokes? on-again-off-again career has been put on ice once more: ?Well, y’know, I never say never,? he says, ?but we’re not playing any live shows, that’s for sure. And there’s no recording [planned].?

October


The World According To Gaz: Andrew Ramadge finds out what it’s like to spend a weekend at a rural mansion with Gareth Liddiard and a cast of friends and hangers-ons. Liddiard is in the midst of recording debut album Strange Tourist, and Ramadge documents the experience in an online novella.

M+N Gets Roasted At The Jagers: A nerdy M+N forum poster sitting side of stage (with Dunlop Volleys and an Apple laptop no less) is MC Scott Dooley’s running gag at the Jagermeister Independent Music Awards. Other winners on the night include Dan Sultan, Cloud Control, Little Red and Midnight Juggernauts.

A Pox On The Proxy: After eight months of ?intense” negotiations, SLAM, Fair Go 4 Live Music and Music Victoria reach an agreement with the Victorian State Government to remove the link between live music and alcohol-related violence. And about time too!

It’s A Silent ?J?: Sydney’s Danimals (aka Jonti Danilewitz) changes his name to Djanimals to stave off potential legal action from a yoghurt company. True story.

Kilbey Steals The Show: I’mma let you finish, but Steve Kilbey makes the best acceptance speech of all time as The Church are inducted into ARIA’s Hall of Fame. For the first time in her professional career, host Julia Zemiro is upstaged.

Hangar To Fly No More: Short-lived, much-loved Brisbane venue Lofly Hangar announces it’ll shut its doors in December following noise complaints from neighbours.

Listomania: A new book listing the ?100 Best Australian Albums? is released, sparking a shitfight about who was omitted. Melbourne five-piece Beaches are a surprising inclusion.

Splitting Up: After a decade and three albums, Brisbane garage rock trio Vegas Kings sign off with a farewell show in Melbourne, but not before leaving us with some parting words of advice.

November


Two Venues Gone In A Week: First, it’s Melbourne’s Birmingham Hotel, which despite renovations to clear up noise-related issues, declares that live music is no longer ?a sustainable business model?. A few days later, it’s Brisbane’s much-loved Troubadour, which is derailed by an ongoing landlord dispute. The Gin Club’s Ben Salter pays tribute: ?The Troubadour’s legacy will live large.?

RIP James Freud: Just days after The Models are inducted into the ARIA Hall of Fame, the band’s former lead singer James Freud dies at the age of 51. His former bandmates cancel an upcoming tour out of respect, while his sons play ?Out of Mind, Out of Sight? at a campaign launch in tribute.

Debutt Of Our Jokes: Foreshadowing the debacle that was to come, ARIA asks independent MP and noted homophobe Bob Katter to present the award for Best Independent Release (see what they did there?). When the awards eventually take place, his ?Araria? gaffe is one of many on a shambolic night. Singer Jessica Mauboy steals the show, awkwardly hitting on swimmer Geoff Huegill and mispronouncing ?debut? twice (and apparently also in rehearsal). There are no winners on the night, and we question what the awards suggest about Australian music.

Hummingbirds Create Big Day Out Buzz: Buried somewhere in a press release is news that Sydney indie-pop icons The Hummingbirds will play their first show in 17 years at the second Sydney Big Day Out. In a follow-up interview, singer Simon Holmes remains ambivalent about future shows: ?All bets are off until after the show.?


Seabellies Bashed In Melbourne: Newcastle’s the Seabellies’ month in Melbourne comes to a bloody end when the band are set upon by a gang in Fitzroy. The attack leaves guitarist/bassist Eddie Garven with stitches to his forehead and multi-instrumentalist Steph Setz and others with bruises.

Tame Impala, The Toast Of Triple J: Tame Impala win the J Award for Australian Album of the Year for Innerspeaker at a live-to-air ceremony at Melbourne’s Ding Dong Lounge. Other winners include Washington (Music Video Of The Year for ?Sunday Best?) and Sydney five-piece Boy & Bear (Unearthed Artist of the Year).

The Punters Club Remembered: Melbourne’s music community pays tribute to the Punters Club, which was turned into a cheap pizza joint in 2002, at a pair of shows at The Corner Hotel. We talk to some of the old furniture.

December

Meredith Turns 20: Victoria’s beloved Meredith Music Festival notches up 20 years with vintage performances by The Dirty Three and Neil Finn. A.H Cayley has ?a moment? on the Ferris wheel during the latter.

Temper Trap Rake In The Bucks: Ex-General Pants employees The Temper Trap crack the Business Review Weekly‘s Top 50 Entertainers list with an estimated $2.5-million in earnings.

Paul Kelly Gets EG Hall Of Fame Nod: Paul Kelly is inducted into the EG ?Steve Waldon? Hall of Fame at a boozy ceremony at Melbourne’s Prince Bandroom. A drunken 15-minute acceptance speech is, sadly, not forthcoming.

VIVID Live Are Pav Lovas: Where do you go after Brian Eno, Lou Reed and Laurie Anderson? Stephen ?Pav? Pavlovic, of course. The Modular boss is announced as curator of next year’s VIVID Live at the Sydney Opera House.


Short Stack, Or How To Create An Online Controversy In Three Acts: M+N* is embroiled in its biggest controversy of the year when contributor Craig Mathieson accuses Short Stack bassist Andy Clemmensen of stealing lines for a guest review published on the V]Music website. Clemmensen [issues a half-arsed apology, claiming that “a friend did it?. *M+N milks the issue further, publishing a review of the new Short Stack album using nothing but plagiarised quotes.

Our Poll Stars: For the first time in M+N* history, one act (Tame Impala) dominates both the Readers and Critics polls, taking out Album of the Year for debut *Innerspeaker. Catcall’s ?Swimming Pool? is named Track of the Year.
