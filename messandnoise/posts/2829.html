Blood Of The Grove

By Freya Zaknich 2013-10-24T00:00:00+00:00

Lakes is the project of long-active Melbourne musician Sean Bailey. His fifth full-length, Blood of the Grove is the most invigorating release yet, steeped in forgotten folklore but commanding and vital even to the uninitiated.

Dynamically the album is terrific in the core sense of the term, with the aspect to impart terror. The militaristic unction typified in Lakes? blend of post-punk and neo-folk evokes the march to bloodshed, and lyrically a desperate warfare of nighttime visions.

?Blood of the Grove? opens the album, coursing with texture from the interplay of acoustic and electric strumming and (at least) triple-tracked vocals that tower prophetically. That the vastness of this delivery does not waver for the songs that follow, even in the tremulous lullaby of ?Night Lark?, makes the act of listening one of awed surrender.

LAKES – Blood of the Grove LP by AVANT! Records

Further into the album, something secret is unleashed in ?All the Waking Dreams?, like the performance of a fireside rite. All instruments gust as one voice, an elemental barrage of a harmonic minor scale from ancient Germanic influence. In twisting traditional sways of melody with valiant percussion and booming vocals, they are exhumed from what could have been dismal, nostalgic fantasy. Instead you get an epic of exposure, bleak and stunning in equal turns.

?The Oldest Place? pulses sharply with expectation to erupt in an unforgiving, tormented rage. There is a canopy of heavy boughs that feature in the funeral hymn ?Crossed With Leaves? and, again, the lone descent lingers close as they burn in ?Ashes Black?. The closer, ?Painted Wreath?, is a final recollection of doom, and those branches signify the inevitable as the terrain closes in around you.

LAKES – Blood of the Grove LP by AVANT! Records

Though European in the obvious sense of musical forebears – Of the Wand and the Moon, Death in June, Current 93 – there are frightening gestures in Blood of the Grove that would not be rendered so harshly without the insurmountable force of this continent’s landscape. With the correct survey of our surroundings, the menace is evident yet largely unexplored and ignored, almost defensively so. Thankfully elusive from the stricter definitions of punk – which are certainly irreconcilable as the band’s contemporary roots – Lakes stands alone as a hell-fiend mythology of a lost, primeval Australia.
