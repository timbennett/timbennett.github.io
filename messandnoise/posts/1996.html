Cigarettes In Paintbuckets

By Trevor 2009-06-01T00:00:00+00:00

This magnificently sloppy effort basically came out of nowhere, with very few clues as to its origin. Marf Loth? What? Is he that skinny kid on the cover? And as for the sound of it – Jesus.

There’s a reference to Alex Chilton’s 1980 solo effort Like Flies On Sherbet* on the insert, but it really doesn’t prepare you for the way it sounds. *Cigarettes In Paintbuckets* doesn’t particularly resemble *Flies sonically, but at the same time it was clearly created in the same ramshackle manner, with a very casual approach to … well, pretty much everything.

There’s a raw, boozy and loose feel to this record which is hugely direct and appealing. At first listen it seems like the song titles are out of order too: nothing gleaned from the singing seems to match anything listed. But about four songs in, everything – from the basic guitar work to the odd tempo changes – falls into place. ?City Folk? is straightforward, in the way that the early Saints were straightforward; the title track is mostly spoken, with a simple chorus over a relentless riff; while ?Abandoned? moves slowly with a weird glam feel before ending in a mess of smashed chords.

Intrigued, I checked the credits again and got in touch with the only name I recognised, drummer Owen Penglis, who also fronts Sydney garage outfit Straight Arrows. Given Straight Arrows? primal sound, it’s not surprising to find out he’s involved. Here’s what he had to say:

?Uhh, let’s see. Marf Loth is this guy called Nathan who I met around shows in Sydney – he’d just moved down from Townsville, where he’s previously released two albums. He called me up one afternoon to ask about recording an LP, and showed up at my place the next day with a case of beer and his guitar. He then asked me to play drums on it, and a guy called Al ended up playing bass. Nathan taught us the songs shortly before we recorded them.

?Al never showed up again after the first session so I ended up overdubbing myself playing bass on the tracks as we recorded and finished the album over the next two evenings. We’d run through something until we got it right, then record it straight away.

?Nathan’s a nice weird guy who really likes Alex Chilton solo records, and lives in a hallway he rents off some people in Newtown. I don’t know too much more about him – but every time he comes to visit he brings beer and pizza, so he’s always welcome.?

While Owen’s response captures the feel of the album perfectly, in a way, I wish I hadn’t asked. Keeping the veil over this little gem intact would’ve been in keeping with the way I first listened to Chilton all those years ago.
