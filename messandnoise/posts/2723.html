Symbols

By Doug Wallen 2013-01-09T00:00:00+00:00

After years spent drumming for everyone from Ella Hooper and Magic Silver White to Sophia Brous and Mountains in the Sky, Mathew Watson debuted the solo project Other Places in 2011. A self-titled first album limited him intentionally to just drums and an analogue synth from 1972. That EMS Synthi AKS took ?centre stage? during the making of follow-up Symbols, according to Watson. And yep, the opening ?Japanese Dream? is a thrumming valentine to synths, embracing their alien sheen as much as their transporting potential.

But these are instrumental pop songs more than vintage nerd-outs, steeped in considerable melody as they gurgle and tick ahead like horizon-chasing movie themes. Again self-produced, the album lodges itself less in rock drumming than its predecessor and more in the club. The start of the single ?The Sunset & You? could easily hook Daft Punk fans and, while its Kraut-y drive eases towards the end with a flitting series of melodic digressions, a rubbery pulse remains. Likewise, ?Morphology? is wonderfully locomotive synth-pop brandishing twinkling hooks, even if the threatened build and climax don’t eventuate.

Momentum isn’t constant here, but in flux. ?Moon Love? forages for funky asides and dabbles in a conversational back-and-forth, while ?Double Planet? introduces competing layers that eventually pull the track in several directions at once. More concentrated is ?Divided by One?, nicely jammy for a short track. The centrepiece is the nine-minute ?Into the Sulphuric Acid Sea?, taking the washed-out grandeur of the other tracks into uncharted territory. It feels somehow split between paranoia and serenity, using its marathon length to noodle in diffuse tangents that are largely satisfying.

As a closer, the last album’s ?Got the Zeity? is remixed by Ollie Olsen of Watson’s duo Taipan Tiger Girls. It’s decidedly more uptempo and dance-y compared to the rest of these tracks, growing increasingly madcap toward the finale in a tightening whirlpool that surrenders its form only in the final half-minute. It’s a welcome tweaking of the Other Places sound.

Through all the aforementioned collaborations, plus a spot on one of those Boredoms drum bonanzas in Japan last year, Watson has long made his versatility clear. Symbols confirms that versatility – these are streamlined synthstrumentals on the surface, but most interesting is hearing all the different routes Watson is able to choose from at every turn. The countless crossroads he faces while forging his own idiosyncratic path.
