Twirligig

By Edward Sharp-Paul 2011-10-01T00:00:00+00:00

Firstly, the facts. With Twirligig, Jonti Danilewitz has a delivered an album with nary a dull moment. Seriously, not one dull moment. He also establishes, without a shadow of a doubt, that he is the best hip-hop producer to have come out of Australia in recent memory – and yes, he was born and raised in South Africa, but let’s just let that rest for the time being. Like The Avalanches before him, Jonti has a knack for compositions that strike a balance between innovative and classic, between headphones and the dancefloor; it’s smart music that’s effortlessly enjoyable. In short, he’s vindicated those that have been singing his praises through two name changes, and he’s lived up to the hype that comes with signing to iconic US label Stones Throw.

Twirligig buzzes with invention; more than a mere sample bandit, Danilewitz draws on a wide array of sources to construct his compositions – and they actually are compositions, varying in style from the lounge-pop of The Fifth Dimension, to thugged-out Stereolab Moog-tasms, through to beats reminiscent of confessed idol J-Dilla. He deploys synth squelches, synth pads, bells, clicks, whirrs, claps, 8-bit noise, piano, electric guitar, acoustic guitar, and his own voice, among many other things.

The latter two are crucial to the album. Scratched-out acoustic guitars and high, multi-tracked voices bob up all over the album, lending it an organic element that humanises and distinguishes it. In the run of ?Firework Spraying Moon?, ?Cyclic Love? and ?Batmilk?, Danilewitz just about invents three genres in three songs. However, the album’s two major issues actually arise from this manic quality, rather than in spite of it.

Jonti – Hornet’s Nest by stonesthrow

Some of the ideas come so thick and fast that they cannot fully be taken in. At times Twirligig sounds more like the highlights reel of an eager young producer than it does a full-length release by an internationally recognised artist. Furthermore, the album’s eclecticism deprives it of an overarching character underneath the trickery; it’s almost more sizzle than steak.

Arguably, the brevity of the songs draws attention to their quality in that one never gets too much of a good thing. However, it can feel like coitus interruptus; the listener starts getting into a groove, making sense of a song’s many layers, only to have that process abruptly cut short.

It’s hard not to feel that Twirligig* could have been two great albums if Jonti had greater confidence in his ideas, allowing them to breathe. Perhaps though, this is a rock classicist’s gripe, and *Twirligig should be appreciated for the teeming, creative outpouring that it is.
