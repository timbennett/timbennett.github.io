Jane?s Lament

By Jules LeFevre 2015-02-25T00:00:00+00:00

Those familiar with Tim Jenkins? work with Parades will have a good starting point for Au.Ra, his psych-pop project with fellow Sydneysider Tom Crandles. Crandles is also no stranger to the scene, having played bass in dream-pop outfit Ghostwood back in 2007. From this history, the record they’ve created isn’t a wild redirection for either of them, but more of a gentle fusion of two likeminded people.

Jane’s Lament was created in close quarters – improvised jams grew into fully realised songs over the course of two years – and the feeling of cabin fever is powerful within the dense layers and thick effects that every note is doused in. While it’s clear that Jenkins and Crandles lost themselves down a loop-pedal rabbit hole, Jane’s Lament eschews any sense of claustrophobia; the spacious melodies and distant vocals ensure an illusion of roominess that bedroom jams rarely allow for. It results in a record that is, for the most part, an open and swerving run through the lighter parts of psych-rock.

The roominess is in part due to the fact that the songs take a while to unfurl. They stretch out in slow builds of ringing guitars and thrumming bass that only occasionally become a thrashing din (?Talk Slow?). Crandles? vocals are lazy, laconic and often slurred to great effect as they waft through the shimmering bead curtain of the rhythm guitars. On opener ?Morning?, his vocals are barely audible against the restless movement of the drums and bass, the bright licks of guitar spiking and curling over the top. It’s not a melody that moves anywhere in particular, but the heavy rhythmic textures give the song its propulsion. ?Sun? is somewhat sparser – this time you can actually hear Crandles? airy voice – and the spacious clattering of percussion allows the track to breathe under the smothering guitar loops. The onslaught continues with ?Pyramid?, the tempo upped and momentum pushing, the melody tumbling to and fro.

There’s a soft change in direction after the opening three: ?You’re on My Mind? briefly introduces synths behind a slow, thudding bass and brings them back again for a somewhat incongruous outro. Interlude ?Juki? drips the synths back over light arpeggios. The change is welcome – there is a danger of Au.Ra digging themselves too deeply into a looped, psych grave – and it is necessary before the latter half of the record seeks to flex and expand. ?Spare the Thought? is lacking in variety, the repeated mantra ?Spare the thought? quickly becoming boring when pummeled out over lashings of dreary guitars.

Much better is the delicate ?Talk Slow?, reaching out and digging in with a gritty outro to touch the six-minute mark. There’s a good deal more experimentation here: synth drones and iced harmonies pepper ?Ease?, and there’s a new looseness that inhabits the groove that was unseen in the first half. ?Ease?, in particular, is more sophisticated, the emphasis taken away from the guitar wash and placed on brief, acoustic ornamentation. The closing ?Width? is different again, a broad soundscape of laptop scratches and bleeps, with a phased and grimy synth warbling back and forth – it’s a curious, antithetic end.

Jane’s Lament is infinitely comfortable – perhaps the result of such a long gestation period, or of Jenkins and Crandles finding in each other a reflection of their own musical tastes. When the wall of sound becomes overwrought, the easy framework upon which it’s built quickly works to alleviate that; there’s a lot of great stuff in there, and luckily you don’t have to walk far to get it.
