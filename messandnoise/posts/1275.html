The Exploders: 8 Things We?ve Learnt About Being in a Band For 10 Years

By Mess+Noise 2014-10-20T13:00:00+00:00

A decade playing together in the ambitious psych-rock outfit The Exploders has taught guitarist/singer TJ ALLENDER* and bassist *PAUL DOERY quite a bit.

1. Check out your support bands; you never know who they’ll end up being

Fremantle, June 2006: opening band, Mink Mussel Creek. I fell asleep upstairs and missed it. A few members of that band went on to play in a couple of small bands you may have heard of: Tame Impala and Pond. Melbourne, August 2006: A band I hadn’t heard of, Children Collide, were main support for us. I piss-farted around getting dinner and showering and missed them. Six months later, I knew exactly who they were. Sydney, March 2007: main support, Calling All Cars. Saw them, had a chat to them; nice dudes. Missed the opener though – someone going by the moniker Ladyhawke?

2. Surround yourself with people who believe in you and what you’re all about

They will work hardest for you and with you to get you where you want to be. I’m talking managers, booking agents, record labels, etc. If there’s someone pushing you in directions that you are not interested in going to, then chances are they are not working with your best interests in mind. Don’t let them fuck it up for you.

3. Pre-gig drugs are bad, m’kay?

Well, they are when your mate found them at a bar the night before. We seriously contemplated cancelling a gig once because we all wigged out just prior to our set. Thankfully we pulled it together just in time.

4. Always play your hardest; you never know who’s watching

We played a gig in New York to a dozen or so people once. After the gig, one of those people came up, introduced himself and said he was impressed by our live show. He was some bigwig record executive at Sony whose name I can’t remember. It amounted to nothing, but it would have amounted to a lot less if we’d been sulking on stage at the lack of crowd in the room.

5. Always check and double-check the work sheet

Once we turned up to a gig in Tasmania and on arrival the house engineer asked us how many mics we’d brought with us. When we said ?none?, he told us he’d rustle some up from a neighbouring venue once we’d loaded the PA in from our hire car. And yes, he was serious.

6. Even the seemingly mundane becomes fun in a tour van

The Exploders and Even shared a tour van together to Adelaide once. We started up a word-association game where you came up with associated word that sounded out a band or artists? name and everyone just yelled out the answers. E.g. Mate Bloke = Buddy Guy. The Soaring Taco Male-Relatives = The Flying Burrito Brothers. The further we got the road, the more ridiculous the clues got and more giggles were had.

7. Being adaptable and resilient is a good thing

If your drummer doesn’t miss a beat whilst hanging upside down from the venue’s rafters, they’re a keeper. If your guitarist falls backwards down the stairs of the stage mid-solo and keeps playing without fluffing one note, they’re a keeper. (And both possibly drunk.)

8. Celebrate the small victories and don’t take anything for granted

Whether it be your mates packing out one of your gigs, selling a bunch of T-shirts, supporting a real cool band, playing gigs with mates, playing a festival, free beer, touring interstate, being on TV, being on the radio, travelling overseas or recording/releasing an album, etc. They may not come around again.

+
