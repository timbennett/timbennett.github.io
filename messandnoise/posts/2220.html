Good Gracious

By DomAlessio 2010-03-01T00:00:00+00:00

No genre is more maligned in Australia than homegrown hip-hop. The musical elitists love nothing more than taking a dump on ?skip-hop?, but I’ve never really understood why, because quite often those same people are accommodating of hip-hop from other countries. What is it about a rapper with an Australian accent that’s so repulsive to people? I think it’s a manifestation of our long-standing cultural cringe that collectively Australians are still struggling to shake off. Yes, there are a lot of bad Aussie MCs, and I’m disappointed too that the Hilltop Hoods are seen as the preeminent Aussie hip-hop group, but there are just as many horrendous indie bands as there are hip-hop outfits. Trust me, I hear them every day.

So what’s great about Mark Landon (better known as M-Phazes) and his debut record Good Gracious* is that it does the cherry-picking for you. A veritable roll call of Aussie hip-hop’s patricians, it’s indicative of the amount of kudos in the bank for M-Phazes, who has spent the past two years compiling this record, in and around his work with Illy, Spit Syndicate, Bliss N Eso and Drapht, all of whom return the favour by appearing here. Pointedly, *Good Gracious opens with ?For What It’s Worth?, with Solo from Horrorshow on vocals. Pointed because it’s a song about hip-hop’s redemptive qualities, and that’s the thing about the genre: it’s such a life-affirming venture for those who work within it. That drive and motivation that Solo raps about is evident throughout most of the album. You can hear it on ?Goodbye Gravity?, a collusion of saloon piano, big distorted guitar chords and four-to-the-floor beats, augmented by Muph’s raps and Candice Monique’s astral vocals. Delta contributes fiery vocals to ?Home?, (literally) spitting rhymes over a grime-influenced beat. M-Phazes channels ??80s hip-hop on ?Long-Winding Road?, an ebullient tune featuring Spit Syndicate and a cast of bleating funk horns. The standout for me – and one of my favourite songs of 2010 of any style – is ?Where’s Elvis??, which is Drapht’s essay on the Elvis death conspiracy. This song has it all: fantastic lyrics, a superb flow that weaves in and out of M-Phazes? beats and stellar production.

It’s a shame the big ticket collaborations are the ones that fall flat. Phrase’s feature ?Music Box? is all Guetta-esque production with deep synths and AutoTune vocals, and it sounds completely incongruous with everything else on the album. Bliss N Eso come across way too earnest on ?Walk On Clouds?, and they also contribute the most idiotic line on the record: ?I’m known for hitting that bitch like Chris Brown.? They might be talking about pot, but it’s still a fucking dumb lyric that perpetuates a long-standing stereotype of hip-hop artists as misogynists. Perhaps they should have taken a page out of Solo’s book: ?To some it might sound crazy/But music took me in, taught me and shaped me/And the life lessons learnt over break beats/Are worth way more than anyone could pay me.?

Good Gracious* is at its best when the artists involved drop the posturing, refuse to acquiesce to America’s style of pop-hop and conversely stop hamming up their ?Ozzie? personality. Thankfully it does this quite regularly, and as a result, *Good Gracious really serves as a great introduction into the stronger facets of Australian hip-hop.
