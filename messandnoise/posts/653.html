Mid-Year Report: 2011

By Darren Levin 2011-07-13T14:00:00+00:00

We asked our critics to list their top five local things of 2011 so far – from gigs to albums, singles to video clips.

Lawson Fletcher

Collarbones
?Don Juan? (video clip)

Naval officers at work and play, medical experiments and cities in rubble – Collarbones have always been rag pickers, sniffing out digital ephemera and recycling it, Dada-like, into electronic collages where the line dividing chart-pop and avant-garde is eclipsed. But when it came to making the film clip accompanying the lead track off this year’s Iconography, Travis Cook and Marcus Whale reached back into a more distant past, using colourless stock footage from the turn of the 20th-century, pock-marked and flickering with the halo of age. In doing so, an uncanny membrane opens up between now and then. Not only art and pop melt together in their beautiful textures, it seems, but time itself gets suspended, becomes strange, takes on a new aura.

Ghoul
?3 Mark? (track)

Ostensibly Ghoul’s take on dubstep, ?3 Mark? (from mini-album Dunks) actually offers something more complex than mere genre excursion. One thing that playing around with the obligatory glitch-y base beats and chipmunk vocal mirrors allows Ghoul to do is to more fully realise that enticing, schematic quality that was hinted at on *Mouthful of Gold, but with an added muscle and depth that EP sometimes lacked.

Ghoul – 3Mark by inertiamusic

Assassins 88
Kaneda (EP, Dream Damage)

This is pure death drive, boxing out your ears and with them your sense of stable self. Assassins 88’s version of punk exists past that realm where vocals need to be audible to be understood, where guitars and drums sound genuinely different and occupy separate bits of the audio channel, and where the recording is the container for the song. Beamed in from a broken neo-punk future, Kaneda exists in a state of permanent collapse, and it’s an absolute pleasure to be here!

Three Kings by Assassins 88

Parades
?Water Stories? (track)

This Sydney four-piece are currently in that unique limbo that so many bands spend the majority of their existence in – between recordings. Maybe being in a band is more about being in the middle of things than ever feeling the finality of completeness. It’s a constant state of un-finish, agonising but also creatively ripe, because a song like ?Water Stories? is exactly what can come out of this downtime. Frenetic but playful, it sounds not only like a band realising their strengths but also – liberated from the pressure of making an album – discovering new ones.

Water Stories by Parades

Seekae
+Dome (LP, Rice Is Nice)

The Sound of Trees Falling on People* was a promising debut, but the improvement between that and *+Dome is immeasurable – this is world-class electronica, sophisticated and yet still kinetic. The trio have retained the best bits of their 8-bit origins, but fleshed them out with much wider-ranging electronic and acoustic textures until something like the perfect synthesis is achieved: a nocturnal atmosphere as suited to the club as it is to the dimmed bedroom or the darkened forest. Full review here.

Seekae: Gnor by gaga-digi

Shaun Prescott

They Live
Cancel Standard (LP, Exit Records)

This record remains under the radar locally (and internationally too, for the most part), but it manages to sustain a surprising good humour despite sounding like humanity’s last hurrah, emanating from some post-apocalyptic vault. Irradiated dubstep for science fiction fans.

They Live – Cancel Standard [EXITLP007] Exit Records _LP Preview_ by stholdings

Dro Carey
Much Coke (EP, Templar Sound)

This is so goddamned ?internet? I don’t know where to start, but if you were unconvinced by Dro Carey’s juke-inspired minimalism on his Venus Knock LP, this is basically the technicolour version. Hi-res, VGA, 2.0 etc etc. Features Trim.

Much Coke by TEMPLAR SOUND

Architecture in Helsinki
Moment Bends (LP, Modular)

Allow me to get sentimental for a moment: this record contains nearly all the good feelings I associate with pop music, without ever sounding referential, or nostalgic, or cleverer-than-thou. It’s really pretty amazing. Lost for words. Full review here.

Pets With Pets
Saturday Aquatic Pixie Acid (LP, Sensory Projects)

Pets With Pets is among probably about three Melbourne bands with guitars that are worth listening to at the moment. Full review here.

Vincent Over The Sink
Dust Studies (LP, Kye)

So fragile and quiet, yet immaculately detailed, that you’ll want to hold your breath as you play through each side. Perfect staring-at-the-wall music.

Jennifer Peterson-Ward

Jack Ladder and The Dreamlanders
Hurtsville (LP, Spunk!)

Raging and delicate, complex as faith and simple as a goodnight kiss, Jack Ladder’s third – and best – album to date is an incredible summation of an already diverse career. Full review here.

HURTSVILLE by Jack Ladder & The Dreamlanders by Jack Ladder

Snowman
Absence (LP, Dot Dash/Intertia)

As the old adage says: Absence does make the heart grow fonder. Seamless in its construction, poetic in its songwriting and moving in its aesthetic impression, Snowman’s final album is as beautiful, bleak and intimate as anything 2011 is likely to throw up. Full review here.

WIM
Wim (LP, Modular)

Although it teases at the contrary for its first half, the idea that we really have no idea quite what to expect from the future of WIM is the greatest gift this nine-song breath gives us.

WIM – Something For You by modularpeople

The Middle East
I Want That You Are Always Happy (LP, Spunk!)

In a world far too concerned with backstories and far too lacking in good old dedication to craft, The Middle East’s just about as ?boring? as they come: four seven talented musicians who very quietly set out to make a fantastic record. And so they did. Full review here.

Months by themiddleeast

Guineafowl
Hello Anxiety (EP, Dew Process)

Breathlessly giddy and shamelessly trippy, Hello Anxiety sounds as if it was a blast to make. Happily, the fun doesn’t stop there. Full review here.

Matt Shea

The Grates
Live @ Ric’s Bar, Brisbane (June 15)

It was a privilege to see the new, upgraded Grates back in their native environs of Brisbane’s Fortitude Valley. A ?secret show? was a warm-up for their Secret Rituals tour, but didn’t feel like it. My eardrums will never be the same.

Oh Mercy
Great Barrier Grief (LP, Casa Del Disco/EMI)

Perhaps this was always going to be a slight disappointment after 2009?s distinctive debut, Privileged Woes, but Oh Mercy’s reserves are still better than most other artists? run-ons. Full review here.

Mike Noga
The Balladeer Hunter (LP, Other Tongues)

?Drummer makes solo album? is usually a tried-and-true cure for insomnia, but Mike Noga proves his class with this stripped down, dusty yet romantic disc. Full review here.

M’Belle by Mike Noga

The Grates
?Turn Me On? (track)

A stark but stunning statement of intent from The Grates, Patience Hodgson and John Patterson reintroducing themselves via sirens of guitar, synth and fuzzed-out bass. The video ain’t half bad, either.

Liam Finn
FOMO (LP, Liberation Music)

Liam Finn’s off-the-cuff approach to his craft is something to be celebrated, particularly when it leads to the kind of tunes packed onto this pocket rocket of a record. Full review here.

The Struggle by Liam Finn

Steph Kretowicz

Snowman
Absence (LP, Dot Dash)

In perpetuating the myth of the tortured artist, a premature death and a parting opus from Snowman offers musical catharsis for those left devastated. Full review here.

Hyena by SNOWMAN

The UV Race
Homo (LP, Aarght!)

There’s one place where it’s safe to release songs devoted to torturing animals, people with disabilities and fun Nazi wordplay without getting your arse kicked. The UV Race have found it somewhere on the brilliantly messy, tone-deaf, punk rock margins. Full review here.

Total Control
?Pyre Island?/?Mind Shaft? (7?, Hustle Muscle)

Following the malignant electrophilia of last year’s ?Paranoid Video?, Total Control abandon their respective punk rock backgrounds completely for even more primitive techno beats.

Total Control – Pyre Island by smartguy

Golden Staph
Golden Staph (LP, R.I.P. Society)

Golden Staph is more than a serious disease. It’s also a band that doesn’t mind offending your sensibilities with songs about sweat and spinal injuries drenched in rapid-fire drumming and brash organ lines. Full review here.

Golden Staph – Last Caesar by R.I.P SOCIETY RECORDS

Lakes
Winters Blade (LP, Inverted Crux)

It’s unclear whether Sean Bailey is really an occultist but the attachment to mysticism is always there. Chanting vocals weave through a collection of rhythmic bric-a-brac to create a space of hidden wisdom. Full review here.

5. Winters Blade by IRX

Darren Levin

Adalita
Adalita (LP, Liberation)

An album informed by tragedy, which ends up transcending it. Beautiful, brave, intimate, stark, confronting; a perfectly executed vision. Her performance of these songs at our Lunchbox gig in Melbourne was truly something to behold. Album review here.

Melodie Nelson
Meditations On The Sun (LP, Other Tongues)

An experimental artiste’s take on ?60s girl-group music that makes you yearn for the summer more than this shitty July weather.

Teeth & Tongue
?Unfamiliar Skirts? (track)

I could’ve just as easily said the whole album (Tambourine), but I wanted to make special mention of this track, because as Jess Cornelius herself puts it, it’s nasty and vindictive. As an aside, can people stop comparing her to PJ Harvey now?

YIS
Kingdom Of Fuzz (LP, independent)

Melbourne’s best pub rock band. Bar none. Get it? Full review here.

YIS – (I Feel) Repulsed by Mess+Noise

Dereb The Ambassador
Dereb The Ambassador (LP, Other Tongues)

Fuck ?world music?, this is the sound of Australia right now: an Ethiopian ?migr? leading a musically and ethnically diverse backing band through a set of songs that take in ethio-jazz, soul, deep funk and psychedelic rock. That’s what you call a melting pot.

Dereb The Ambassador- Etu Gela by Other Tongues

Caitlin Welsh

Dads
Man of Leisure (LP, independent)

This is still very new, and I might not be quite so enamoured of it at the end of the year. But Tom Iansek’s casual little solo record is cosy, clammy, heartfelt bedroom rock, and doesn’t insist upon itself at all. It’s quietly wonderful, and seductive precisely because it sounds like an afterthought. (Iansek is also half of Melbourne’s excellent Big Scary – I’m excited to see if their debut long-player, out later this year, can top this.)

Life, Oh Life by musicyourdadsmake

Liam Finn/Jack Ladder
?Cold Feet?/?Cold Feet? (tracks)

Two of my favourite songs of this year happen to be called the same thing (and produced by Burke Reid). Finn’s is a deceptively humble love anthem, distracting you with its schoolboy shyness and shuffling feet while the sunny melody gleefully burrows into your brain for all eternity. Ladder’s is languid and wintry and blanketed, crucially, in Kirin J Callinan’s infinite guitars and (literally) three storeys of reverb.

Oscar + Martin
For You (LP, Two Bright Lakes/Remote Control)

When most other acts do this wonky, childlike shit it sounds calculated and cloying. O+M get up on stage in Cosby sweaters looking like hungover 12-year-olds, and they offer up their earnest, ramshackle skeleton of R&B and everyone in the room falls in love. Full review here.

Do the right thing by 00remotecontrol

Belles Will Ring
Crystal Theatre (LP, Dot Dash/Remote Control)

If Cloud Control’s Bliss Release* is that friendly, guitar-strumming guy at school who talks earnestly about the warmth of vinyl and sometimes has weed, *Crystal Theatre* is his brooding, clever, drug-fucked big brother who’s really into Dario Argento and *always has weed. Full review here.

Belles Will Ring – Street Lamp Stomp by The Workers Club

Doug Wallen

Architecture in Helsinki
Moment Bends (LP, Co-Op/Modular)

From the level of songwriting right down to the production, this is indie pop dreaming itself into a universal language. Perfect songs given exhilarating clarity. Full review here.

Jane Badler with Sir
Tears Again (LP, Remote Control)

What happens to a star when the spotlight is long gone? In the exaggerated world of Jane Badler and her collaborators, she sits awash in men and drink, wondering where the time went and whether or not she left the house today. Full review here.

Jane Badler with Sir – Men Who Lie by 00remotecontrol

Collarbones
Iconography (LP Two Bright Lakes/Remote Control)

A similar strain of glitch-y R&B-infected releases by Ghoul and Oscar + Martin, but Collarbones managed something just that bit more consuming. And live, they’re like two hopped-up kids ambushing an older generation’s party.

The Middle East
Live @ Karova Lounge, Ballarat (June 13)

Anticipation ran head-on into unwieldiness when The Middle East’s second album finally surfaced this year. But live the band became reborn, finally comfortable in their skins and rewarding everyone who stuck by them.

Teeth & Tongue
Tambourine (Dot Dash/Remote Control)

Guitars meander and whip as a drum machine keeps the beat and Jess Cornelius finds her own voice in a husky, world-weary drawl saturated with regret. These songs reveal spooky new truths with each and every visitation. Full review here.

Tim Scott

Native Cats
Process Praise (LP, Rough Skies/Ride the Snake)

Lower Plenty
Mean Tape (cassette)

Woollen Kits
?Maths? (track/clip)

Homo (LP, Aarght!)

Andrew McMillen

Adalita
‘Hot Air’ (track)

The most emotionally devastating song in recent memory. I wouldn’t change a thing about ‘Hot Air’. It’s perfect.

Nova Scotia
‘The World Is Not Enough’ (track)

If I could only listen to one bassline for the rest of my life, it’d be this one. (Or Ride’s ‘Leave Them All Behind’).

10 The World is not enough by Nova Scotia

Faux Pas
‘Renfield’s Dream’ (track)

I love that one man can sit in a room with a computer and create something as beautiful and rich as this.

No Anchor
‘Gatton Bohemia’ (track)

The only 17 minute-long song released so far this year where you’ll find 50 seconds devoted to two bass guitars alternating feedback solos. If you turn it up loud enough, it feels like your skin is peeling off.

Mr. Maps
‘Nice Fights’ (track)

Listening to this track feels makes me feel like I’m a dragon-slayer in some mythical land far, far away from Brisbane. It’s a nice feeling, which is probably why I keep returning to this song. (Technically this was released in 2010 but it appeared on their 2011 album, so I’m counting it.)

Mr. Maps – Nice Fights by lofly recordings

Ren? Schaefer

Harmony
Harmony (LP, Casa Del Disco/Other Tongues)

The most stunningly original band to stand out from the morass of endlessly recycled rock cliches, by virtue of having a completely self-contained vision and executing it with a fierce determination that has nothing to do with any musical fashions. Full review here.

Woollen Kits
?Maths? (track/clip)

Possibly the most infectious pop song you’ll hear this year, with a home-spun video to match. ‘Maths’ recalls the best of the NZ ?80s underground, while the vocals make Calvin Johnson sound like he’s auditioning for the Vienna Boys’ Choir.

Various Artists
Ladyz In Noyz, Australia (LP, Corpus Callosum Distro)

This compilation and series of events around the country demonstrated once of for all that experimental noise isn’t the exclusive domain of socially mal-adjusted weird-beards with a fetish for the acrid smell of overloading circuit boards. Full review here.

44 – Bonnie Mercer by Lars Von Onion

Pop Singles
Live @ The Old Bar, Melbourne (January 29)

Another band that takes some of its cues from the Flying Nun school of jangle and drone pop and mixes it up with some brightly chiming psychedelic guitar, but unlike some of their contemporaries, Pop Singles avoid sounding either derivative or twee – just completely infectious.

Spinning Rooms
Live @ The Old Bar, Melbourne (June 24)

This new-ish Melbourne band, fronted by ex-Deaf Wish guitarist Pete Dickinson, doesn’t play all that often, but when they do, they invariably blow people’s heads off with noisy guitar, restless saxophone improvisations and relentlessly churning, Moodists-like bass lines.
