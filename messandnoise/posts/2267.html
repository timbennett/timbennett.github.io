Escape From Algebra

By ReneSchaefer 2010-05-01T00:00:00+00:00

Stu Thomas is the embodiment of the journeyman musician. He has served a lengthy apprenticeship as sideman to luminaries such as Kim Salmon and Dave Graney; he has performed session work with the likes of Spencer P Jones, Mike Noga, Penny Ikinger and Jane Dust; and he has intermittently fronted his own bands The Brass Bed and The Stu Thomas Paradox.

The fact that recognition for his own songwriting has not quite reached the same levels as those of his mentors, has not deterred him. He diligently plies his trade, acquiring the skills that will one day see him take over the mantle of the masters. Surely, this day is approaching rapidly, if his new album Escape from Algebra is any indication.

Much like Salmon in his Surrealists? guise, Thomas favours variety on his third solo release. His approach to recording is very direct. One gets the sense of a band playing in the studio, unconcerned with presenting a definitive version of a song, but experimenting with different feels and playing with a sense of immediacy. Having musicians of the calibre of Surrealists Drummer Phil Collings and ex-Ferrets main man Billy Miller on hand, ensures a high level of taste and chemistry. While Thomas is a very adept writer and arranger, he steers clear of ostentatious arrangements, and delivers his songs in pure, barebones form. Generally, this works extremely well, recalling classic Surrealists records like Essence, in its stripping bare of pop music conceits.

The first three songs display a Beatles-like take on psychedelic pop, reminding me of Ross McLennan’s old band Snout, with their stuttering bounce and clipped, understated guitar tones. Thomas? trademark, surf rock-inflected Burns baritone guitar only starts coming to the fore on third track ?Love Charm?, which could be a long lost vintage Lime Spiders or Stems classic. Structuring the track list alphabetically on a whim (apparently a nod to the surrealist practice of Automatism, or leaving things up to chance), may have been ill-advised, with some of the strongest songs buried somewhere in the middle.

Still, there are plenty of gems to discover. ?Only Friend? is a dark narrative about everyday psychosis, set to a great garage-psych tune. ?Shake Your Derriere?, while centering around a throw-away rhyme (which doesn’t even bear repeating in print), is a corker of a track that deserves the same dancefloor hit status that Dave Graney’s ?I’m Gonna Release Your Soul? had in 1994. ?So Happy? borders on XTC whimsy, while album closer ?Woman Of Steel? should be the title song to the next James Bond movie, where the role of the titular heroine is played by a man-eating woman. Casting suggestions welcome.
