Time On Earth

By Ben 2007-06-20T12:00:00+00:00

Crowded House have always had a warmly familiar style – one all-too-easily dismissed for its easy-on-the-ear, radio-groomed quality. What such a simplistic dismissal would miss is Neil Finn’s way of shoehorning an unexpected approach into familiar forms: on Time on Earth, their decade-later returning salvo, ?She Called Up? is all keyboard stabs and jabbed vocal harmonies, as melodically freewheeling as anything Split Enz ever did, but delivered with a consummate ease – doused in honey to make the medicine go down.

With Time on Earth, you hear the knack of old hands intuitively moving over keys, frets and drums; Crowded House yields to neither the stultifying ?professionalism? of many reunions, nor flashily announces itself ?contemporary? with a set of electro-post-punk. Like the best artists, this is a band who knows their genre well enough to twist and turn inside its box – alternately surprising and delivering on expectations. In the best possible way, it’s like they never went away: the songs are too present and fresh here to be the bearers of sentimentalism or remembrance.

Time on Earth sees the band plough through a remarkable set of ballads, pop songs and bittersweet, mid-tempo delights that just feel right. The pathos at its core – grieving the death of the band’s longtime class clown, the drum-rolling joker up the back – makes it all the richer; the closing trio of songs, starkly shaded in charcoal, is particularly affecting in this context. The entire album is veiled by a minor-key sorrow but never burdened by it. Finn’s ability to find levity – and thus listenability – in the gravest of matters is his songwriting strength. So while there have been any number of indie-pop and power-pop bands come and go in the lost decade of Crowded House, none of them put out an album this deeply satisfying.
