Lands

By JP Hammond 2010-04-01T00:00:00+00:00

It’s hard to conceive of a less forgiving audience than a mortgage of Carlton North families (that’s a proper noun by now, isn’t it?) attending a local school fete. At the least, it’s a nasty venue for an experimental art rock band to play. At midday. On a Sunday.

Yet this is where TTT graced the stage a month ago, at the Lee Street School fete. Motive was difficult to discern, beyond perhaps a certain medieval desire for public flagellation via indifferent crowd response. But although TTT played their set to a single old man, sitting in a bank of four rows of plastic chairs, there was a celebratory death-dance enthusiasm to the disaster – some kind of ridiculous fuck-it-all embrace of the sublime finality of a gig apocalypse.

It’s also possible TTT were entirely comfortable with the conditions of the experiment. Lands, the second album from the band formerly known as Tic Toc Tokyo, is a record focused on the reactions between the insensate ugliness of post-punk and the sterile clarity of melodic pop sheen. Chaos wins.

TTT are at their best when these precipitating elements collide and rebound in constant flux. Opener ?Change? hits the mark immediately, chiming guitars giving away to murky, serpentine bass moving into a brutish, mechanical flow that repeats, relentlessly. It’s music for empty freeways at night, the thrilling clutch of acceleration matched by the dull emptiness of an endless road.

The best moments on Lands clone this feeling. Excellent single ?Animal? ultimately proves anomalous, hinting at a Talking Heads influence that is, at best, a minor element of the band’s palette here. More important, and more satisfying, is the decision the band have taken to push their default songwriting approach (find a Gang of Four song, and sound like it) not by changing it, but by augmenting it by any means possible.

This bionic grafting is best represented by ?Traps?, which starts with 30 seconds of whistling, to which the band add incessant, multiplying drums the type of which Pornography-era Cure drummer Lol Tolhurst would be proud. If it all sounds like standard ?80s stomp, guitars surgically intersect about two minutes in, pushing the song somewhere into the necropolis where post punk, goth and the mechanical hell My Disco trawl collectively reside. ?Hinterlands? and ?Compass Point?, which follow and use the same template, create a thrilling mid-album triumvirate of tracks. Here, lyrics are little more than apocalyptic homing beacons, subsumed into the sonic morass: ?Nobility, humiliation, anarchy? proceeds one repetitive sonar depth-charge.

The downside is that these moments of invention highlight the stagnation that still dogs the band. Lands* is an imperfect album. For every ?Change?, there’s a ?Tyranny? to follow, which is probably the worst, most redundant song TTT have recorded. One listen to Gang of Four’s *Entertainment !, and you’ve heard it. ?The River? just proves that loving ?One Hundred Years? by The Cure a whole lot isn’t a good enough reason to write a song, particularly if you can’t be bothered concealing the guitar part.

?States? is the worst offender, an ultimately middling song that sits in a land-locked groove for three minutes of its life-span, before suddenly, positively shifting into something closer to the supernova of Jane’s Addiction. Brass cascades from the background, percussive intensity (digital and otherwise) escalates and the guitars, and the song, open up. It’s full of stars, but only briefly.

?Where’s the scene?? is how singer Simon Gibbs opens the record. It’s a good question, and a fair one to pose to a band seemingly too late for hype and now too idiosyncratic for hope. Fortunately, Gibbs has his own, immediate rejoinder: ?I’ll set it.? It’s worth noting that, for all the hellish incongruity of the fete experience, the little two-year-old girl I attended with danced by the end, won over by the endurance experiment. Listeners willing to take a chance, and work through some missteps, will surely feel the same way.

Fittingly, TTT close Lands with the strongest song of the set, ?At Land?. Built around a churning, delayed guitar part and the album’s simplest, most effective rhythmic lock, Gibbs breaks out of his self-imposed signal to cry, desperately, and clearly at last, ?Fire, fire, at land.? It’s a simple representation of the achievement of TTT on this record. Their success here emerges from a singular, hermetic universe, where the beating heart is the focus, imperfect as always.
