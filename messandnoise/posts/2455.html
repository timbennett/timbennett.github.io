Avalon Cassettes

By Shaun 2011-04-15T00:00:00+00:00

It’s common for respected songwriters to gradually fall into statesmanship, to become eminently and tiresomely worthy, to rest on their laurels. Jamie Hutchings is probably entitled to that mantle now, but Avalon Cassettes* doesn’t come with the whiff of the casually tossed off, even while it trades in a brand of domestic sentimentalism that can devalue artists of his calibre through sounding too easy. Following the colourfully orchestrated *His Imaginary Choir*, *Avalon Cassettes is as stripped back as its name suggests, recorded straight to eight-track in the Sydney beachside suburb of Avalon with the help of family and friends.

Jamie Hutchings :: When It’ll Blow by AICEntertainment

There is, and always has been, something quintessentially ?Sydney? about Hutchings? songwriting. His is an idealised beachside suburbia that seems irretrievably lost now, a humbleness and universalism that’s been priced out of our means. It’s difficult to listen to Bluebottle Kiss – or anything with Hutchings vocals attached – without being taken to this place, no matter the subject matter at hand. ?Slack Magic? riffs directly on the city’s changes both directly and metaphorically, evoking through his references a sense of maturation and accelerated development. He sings of the impossibly bustling Taylor Square (?near impossible to fit into a spot there?) and the face of Australian cultural cache Geoffrey Rush, as if they were signals of Sydney’s (and the country’s) conflicted modernity, its aspiring worldliness. His songwriting with Bluebottle Kiss often depicted Sydney through a prism of backyard, provincial sentimentality, but Hutchings seems displaced by this locale now, as much as some of his other characters throughout Avalon Cassettes seem suddenly at odds with their natural environment.

Jamie Hutchings :: Ned Kelly Indoors by AICEntertainment

This sense of infeasible trajectory is captured in ?New Vagabond?, where an aging protagonist is confronted by a vagrant he once loved – a semblance of a girl ?who’d filled me with such lust?. She claims that ?My heart it’s given out/I thought there was nobody left?. ?Cicada Symphony? sees Hutchings reflecting on ?the Rocks and the Quay? and ?that blurry wretched bridge?, imploring the former to ?dig me up a little history?, which is perhaps a desire for some obscured essence. There’s a looseness to this latter track, proceeding as it does like some loving incantation: light drums spatter a bed of wordless choral flourishes, while idyllic flute asserts itself against the melee, and you can almost see the quay flooded with solemn apparitions. It’s among the most baroque moments on a record marked by a very carefully maintained quietness, a reliance on voice and song that may well be the mark of a songwriter relatively comfortable with his tools. It works because Jamie Hutchings’ art has an air of authority and purpose, a sense of space that is quintessentially his. It’s always a pleasure to be reminded.
