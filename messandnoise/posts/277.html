Track By Track: Grand Salvo

By Darren Levin 2009-07-26T14:00:00+00:00

Grand Salvo (aka Paddy Mann) gives us an insight into the 10 tracks that comprise album number five, the quietly beautiful ‘Soil Creatures’ – he even includes some nifty diagrams.

?Storm?

Mixed with the guitars out and only being heard through the bleed in the vocal mics. Refers to some extent to the state of mind that comes with the song trance.

?Needles?

This one is a kind of ramble or dirge. Very simple musical material: three chords repeated ad nauseam with a single repeated vocal line. The vocal line and the chord progression DO repeat over a different length though?

Vocal:
(---- ?---- ?----? ----/----? ---- ?----? ----/---- ?---- ?----?----)

Chords:
(---- ?----? ----/---- ?---- ?----/---- ?---- ?----/----? ---- ?----)

… so there IS a level of complexity that makes what is pretty boring in itself, listenable. The harp also shifts at times from a straight on-the-beat playing, to a triplet pattern, enforcing this underlying musical idea.

Lyrically, I wanted a kind of stream-of-nonsense opening to slowly shift into form and meaning but only when seen from outside the process, with every snippet functioning on its own, but also giving an overall impression when combined, like a picture emerging from smaller pictures.

Sometimes you hit on a chord progression or repeated phrase that sets you off in a kind of trance and you stay there for a time staring into whatever you’re facing and your imagination aroaming until – sometimes a long time later – something slaps you back home. Usually, when it’s time to work the doodle into a presentable form you end up breaking it and adding traditional western song structures like choruses or things like that – or at least movement in dynamic stasis – and when you do, whatever you had goes. I thought for this one I’d leave it and let it roll. The whole album was originally going to be a few slight variations on this idea (long, repetitive, super simple, lyric-based ideas) but I couldn’t pull it off. This song was the starting point of the album with all its themes and imagery forming the direction and material for the rest of the record.

I recorded so many takes of this one, changing the lyrics every now and then, that I forgot a line and only noticed during mixing. It was very important because it included the word ?genitals?, so I added it to the liner notes.

?Ship?

This is an idyllic image of youth born from the mind of a sheltered middle-class child with a loving family and a happy home life. I wrote it as I recorded it, from the bass line, which is how I wrote songs when I was 17.

?Flowers?

How do I get away from these chords? They are SOOO over used. Fuck it! I’ll use them again.

?Father?

Very difficult to record this one (the overdubs that is; the guitar and vocals were cut in the almost perfect environment of Chris Townend’s studio/shack in the hills above Hobart). I never use a click track and I like to slow and quicken in my own damn time, so the basic track (guitar, vocals) was all the string trio had to play over. It’s also very soft and my equipment very makeshift and the room very hot and dark and the time very short (as were the leads on the headphones) and the timing very fluid. Anyway, hats off to the string players.

A kind of analogy for the death of squandered youth. Also, about projecting yourself into the future in order to reminisce about the now so you can extract something from your present that can only be seen by a nostalgic eye. An approach I often use and is probably giving me a skewed world view.

?Chimney?

This is one of those songs that probably isn’t meant to be played live. The opposite in character to the former song. It’s the mid-point of the album and marks a change in mood.

?Road?

Another tedious analogy outlining the journey from birth to death. Had no time to flesh-out the multiple string parts that come in after the violins – it’s the first arrangement I’ve done from theory. It needs clarinet or horns, or pedal steel or something in the outro.

?Rain?

This song is the companion to ?Flowers?. It has the same chordal material and also uses the nylon string guitar/bass xylophone coupling. Both songs have lyrics that were written well into the process and act like threads to bind all the imagery.

?Brother?

Inspired by a horrible nightmare.

?Sea?

This song is the twin to ?Needles? and is the only one I kept from the original discarded idea for the record. It uses the same overlap technique?

Vocal:
(----?----?----?----/----?----?----?----/----?----?----?----)

Chords:
(----?----?----?----?----?----/----?----?----?----?----?----)

? and the vocal line is the same, but upside down – not quite though because the end of the line didn’t work. (So what? No one gives a shit).

The accompanying line that repeats in various guises throughout the song follows the chords rather than the vocals, enforcing the 3/4 feel. This line is also folded over itself, firstly in the form of a round, then expanding into a six-part canon in the outro. At the end we find ourselves at the beginning.

+

Soil Creatures will be launched in Sydney at the Paddington Uniting Church on August 8 and in Melbourne at the Arts Centre’s Fairfax Studio on August 15.
