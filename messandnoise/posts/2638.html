The Brightest Light

By Doug Wallen 2012-06-01T00:00:00+00:00

King Cannons don’t shy away from cliches. In fact, they seize them and make them an advantage. When singer-guitarist Luke Yeoward announces, ?There’s something about a midsummer’s Friday night/The smell of the grass and gasoline? on the title track of debut album The Brightest Light, he’s tapping into our shared experience for emotional resonance. The same goes for the music of this New Zealand-formed Melbourne six-piece, which unabashedly channels influences like The Clash and Bruce Springsteen into rousing working-class anthems that are instantly familiar.

That approach can feel a bit calculated: every detail on this album, from harmonica solo to vocal phrase, is positioned precisely to make us feel something. There’s nothing wrong with that in itself, but there’s hardly an unfamiliar note or lyric to be found in this band. Every subject, setting and sentiment is already well-trodden.

The band’s saving grace, besides a wealth of urgency, is an unshakable earnestness and conviction that comes from Yeoward having lived what he’s singing about. He started his first band at 13 and busked to save up for his first electric guitar and amp, dropping out of school to haunt music venues while working alongside his father at a sawmill by day. So when he sings about small-town escape and factory life on ?Too Young?, it’s more diary than fantasy. A songwriter doesn’t need to have inhabited his or her subject matter that way to prove vital, but knowing Yeoward’s backstory helps us take the universal themes of a song like ?Too Young? to heart.

King Cannons – Too Young by theinsound

Stylistically, King Cannons take a lot on board for one record – especially a debut. ?Stand Right Up? dabbles in gospel (at least via the rock tradition), while other tracks are as likely to adopt a reggae crackle (?Charlie O?) as a Johnny Cash country vibe (?On Our Own?) or a socially aware, Billy Bragg-like stance (?Everyman’s Tale?). If ?Shot to Kill? and ?The Cool Change? are thick with platitudes, these guys know their way around an anthem too well to be sunk so easily. Besides, their everyman appeal is part of the band’s DNA – from the Bob Seger-isms of ?Too Hot to Handle? to the total Tom Petty chorus of the name-checking tour of New York City that is ?Call for Help?.

Produced mostly by Shihad drummer Tom Larkin, who’s playing in King Cannons on their current launch dates, The Brightest Light is a sturdy thing indeed. It may be gushing in its hero worship – Yeoward met guitarist Rob Ting and bassist Jonno Smith at a screening of a Joe Strummer documentary – and overladen with hand drums on several tracks, but the band endear themselves to us pretty strongly. What’s more, they’ve laid a solid foundation for something more distinctive next time.
