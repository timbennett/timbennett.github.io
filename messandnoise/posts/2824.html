Are You Life

By Jules LeFevre 2013-10-13T00:00:00+00:00

Brisbane town has a hankering for seriously weird pop. Push aside The Grates for a moment, and remember that this humble city produced the warped indie rock of George, the power opera of Kate Miller-Heidke and, you know, Regurgitator. Recently, the more sculpted, sophisticated indie of The Trouble With Templeton and Last Dinosaurs can be heard carving up the airwaves.

Four-piece outfit Little Scout have sprung up from some fertile ground, and it’s George and their record Polyserena* that cast the biggest shadow over *Are You Life. It’s imprinted in the percussive muscle of the drums, the elastic vocals of Melissa Tickle and the dedication to constantly changing course. Most of all, though, it’s the overarching feeling that Little Scout are aiming for immersion rather than impression.

If you wanted to pin them down further, perhaps imagine what Sigur Ros would sound like if fronted by Karen O. But heady influences aside, Little Scout both stutter and dream their way through their second LP, falling back heavily on the scattered, interlocking drum rhythms. Tickle’s voice is mostly trapped in an echo haze, which can either annoy (?Go Quietly?) or soothe (?Don’t Teach Me to Sing?).

Little Scout in general seem caught between needing to rush forward but at the same time trying to get you to relax. The drums are urgent, relentlessly pushing against the wafting guitars and vocals that skim across the top. It’s an odd combination, and occasionally grates when it starts to feel as the song is being cleaved in two (?Flash in a Pan?.) When it does work (check the lilting ?Ten Taxis? or the shifting ?Heard It All Before?), the dislocation becomes utterly beguiling. Even better is the full-steam-ahead clatter of ?March Over to Me?, which pulls off a garish rhythm change in a decidedly George-inspired move.

Lyrically, it’s also a scythe or butter-knife dichotomy. Tickle sings of the soulless and ?hate-filled sirens,? but also of old friends coming home and how, at large, she feels like ?a monster on speed dial.? The harshness in the lyrics just doesn’t cut through the jumble of indie sugar that encases it.

Despite the lack of direction, the ebb and sway of Are You Life, coupled with its ingrained darkness, elevates it above the standard indie mix. Brisbane’s reputation is thus maintained.
