Arrows At The Sun

By Doug Wallen 2009-11-13T00:00:00+00:00

Recorded on eight-track and later computer at his home studio, Martin Craft’s second solo album is a hazy thing of ripe atmosphere and fleeting embellishment. Once of ?90s mainstays Sidewinder, the now London-based Craft has constructed these 10 songs from soft piano melodies, an assortment of skeletal sounds and the occasional warm extras elicited from guests. The result is a sort of shadow version of an adult-contemporary record that sees Craft tempering the proudly cheesy music with his dark lyrical smarts.

?The Beautiful Future? achieves a wonderful tension from the start with two looped-sounding piano parts, one plinking and one shuddering. That tension, which strongly recalls Lou Reed’s ?Street Hassle?, is maintained throughout the song. Craft’s low, conversational voice sounds as if it was recorded at the opposite end of the room, while his lyrics zoom out from an isolated jungle tribe to the unreal technology humming away in the world at large. By the third and final verse, there’s quite a bit of scope to it. It’s certainly a strong opening, and could be the best song here.

Most of the other tracks are more intimate and less ambitious, though just as likeable. ?Mexico? is utterly drowsy, jumbling squishy synths with off-the-cuff percussion and honeyed violin before saxophone enters to crank up the cheese factor. (Not that I’m complaining.) ?Young And In Love? opens like either a classic Bacharach number or some ?70s TV theme, and the snappy ?Thunder? casually tosses a sassy backup singer, a bouncy drum loop, escalating synths, and handclaps. ?Sparrows? could almost pass for a Crayon Fields song, while the ephemeral ?Little Rider? ends the record on a quiet note.

There’s an ongoing contrast between Craft’s wistful lyrics – he conjures London as a landscape of grey, perpetual autumn – and the nifty, airy music he’s dreamed up. The only song to edge into full-blown earnestness is ?He’s Never Coming Home?, which follows a woman in the wake of a departed lover, but even it’s a well-drawn character study. If at times it can feel insubstantial, Arrows At The Sun* proves a graceful follow-up to Craft’s solo debut, *Silver And Fire.
