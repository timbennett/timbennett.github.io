Whereabouts

By Christopher Lewis 2013-01-23T00:00:00+00:00

?All Angles? opens this album bristling with slacker charm. The guitars twang and it is immediately engaging in a breezy, effortless way. Unfortunately, the opening drum roll is the terminal velocity the drum kit reaches on the album and it rarely ever matches the promise of the opening track.

Whereabouts* is The Frowning Clouds? addition to the dolewave canon. Carved out with DIY minimalism and produced as if ?Please Please Me? and ?A Hard Day’s Night? have been on repeat, The Frowning Clouds have followed up their debut album *Listen Closelier with more of the same nonchalance.

A discussion for another day is whether we need another album in this tradition, especially following the brilliant Calendar Days* by Dick Diver last year. But it would be unfair to not judge *Whereabouts on its own merits, and they seem self-aware enough, singing on ?Shoe Suede Blues?: ?Copycats start having fun.?

There are some stellar moments on this album; moments that are reverb-soaked, blissed-out and carefree. ?Much Too Much Too Soon? is fantastic – sharp, jangly and friendly – but its biggest problem is that it takes 37 minutes to get to it. The album is overlong and has quite a lot of forgettable, under-realised filler.

The tone oscillates between romantic ?60s-tinged nostalgia and bratty swagger. ?3 O’clock Habit? echoes The Byrds with harmonies that are obviously the band’s fort?, while ?Human Being, Human Doing, Human Going? has a middle finger in the air that harks back to The Replacements. What captures the mood of the latter is its energy; if only we felt it more often, as Whereabouts is often held back by its heavy-handed lethargy. On the odd moment when a song does kick to life, the energy is infectious. But too often, like on ?Into the Ground?, it drones along with grinded teeth.

They embrace melodic simplicity on ?Submarine? to brilliant effect, and the du-du-du-dudu vocals on the previously released ?Propellers? still make it a feel-good highlight. Lyrically, though, the simplicity across the album wears thin. There are no proper characters in these songs, so there are no stories to tell; these are just catchy songs with words that go along with the melody.

THE FROWNING CLOUDS – Propellers Owen by Saturno Records

Throughout the album, you can almost feel the fatigue from consecutive 40-degree days and, at their worst, The Frowning Clouds minimalise their songs to the point of boredom, barely holding together with a metronomic kick drum. At their best they are rollicking and have all the best shades of The Kinks.

The Frowning Clouds are not dolewave pretenders, but they have not crafted a unique niche either, which leaves them in a conspicuous middle ground. And when one of their best songs, ?Heaps Deep?, sounds like a note-for-note cover of Twerps, you have to wonder how much they are doing right and how much they are treading stale water.
