This Is Bat Country

By Darren Levin 2010-10-01T00:00:00+00:00


This is music for Saturday morning sport, fast food drive-throughs, shopping centres, touring car telecasts and daily commutes. Short Stack frontman Shaun Diviney plays all kinds of scary creatures on the NSW Central Coast trio’s second album: hungry vampire, desperate lover, carnivorous werewolf, vengeful killer; he makes Placebo’s Brian Molko sound like the shy boy next door.

Across the album, he relives the more harrowing aspects of a deep personal bond, pinging from bitterness (“Heartbreak made me a killer baby/But I was born for this/But I was born for this,” he sings over gloriously spare electro-pop on ?Heartbreak Made Me A Killer?), to confusion (?I don’t know how to talk to you/And where did you come from?? he head-scratches on the hymn-like ?Planets?, a masterclass in modern vocal arrangement), to, um, melodramatic transmogrification (?And we’re going to hell/Just because we’re werewolves?). Surrounding himself with music that expertly balances between over-orchestrated and chaotic, Diviney elevates his pedestrian travails about love and lust into legendary myths in which he’s rarely the hero.

That bad attitude suits this synth-laced album, which plays like a love letter to all the juvie-hall grads and Ritalin rats still waiting for someone to build a bomb big enough to blow all the pop bands off the airwaves. Songs like ?We Dance To A Different Disco, Honey?
and ‘Sweet December’ are some of the biggest pop songs Short Stack have ever written, but that isn’t to say they are cheesy. They are great, unique, well crafted songs; Diviney’s voice is always aggressively sexual (?I want to do some horrid things with you?) and the introduction of heavy pads and sampling is proof they’re keen to keep up with the times. Songs engage with a fierce pop sensibility, all the while throwing in a mesmerising diorama of sounds and musical instruments.

Love them or hate them, these boys are remarkably talented at marketing themselves. Springing from obscurity almost five years ago, they’ve quickly won over thousands of fans from across the globe. And when you listen to their music, it’s not entirely confusing why. Short Stack have mastered the art of pop rock that’s innocent enough to please parents, but still a little rebellious to select teenage ears.

And This Is Bat Country is the album they were born to make. It gives us all the things that punk has never been able to provide: romance, sex, the adventure of the open road and sheer nihilism-banishing energy. The quality of music here, and the sense of invention and design behind that music, is phenomenal.

by Darren Levin
