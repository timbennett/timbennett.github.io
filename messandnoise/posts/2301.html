We Are Born

By DomAlessio 2010-06-22T00:00:00+00:00

After the disappointing down-tempo ‘Some People Have Real Problems’, Adelaide’s Sia Furler returns with an album that showcases her pop credentials, writes DOM ALESSIO.


There are many elements that make Sia Furler an unlikely pop star: up until this point, none of her records have contained an overly-commercial sound; she doesn’t possess model-esque looks; and she’s from Adelaide. But it’s precisely this ?Girl Next Door? appeal that makes Sia the perfect pop star. In interviews she comes across free of pretention; a natural-born raconteur with a penchant for the quirky and wry. She’s the girl everyone wants to be BFFs with.

But her effervescent personality had lain predominately dormant over her past two releases: 2004?s Colour The Small One* and its follow-up, *Some People Have Real Problems*. It wasn’t an issue with the former because it was a ridiculously sublime record, layered to the hilt with gorgeous melodies and luscious textures. *Some People Have Real Problems, by contrast, never peaked like its predecessor did. There were too many songs that sounded like discarded Zero 7 tunes, the downtempo UK electronica act Sia joined after immigrating to England in the early Noughties.

We’ve subsequently learned during Sia’s latest interviews promoting We Are Born* that it was, in fact, this record that was supposed to proceed *Colour The Small One*. Her record label at the time, Universal, thought it would be career suicide to release an up-tempo dance album, considering she’d just found an international audience with ?Breathe Me?, a hauntingly intimate tune that soundtracked the final scene of *Six Feet Under.

Maybe the planets have aligned correctly now, or maybe she’s just on a new record label, because We Are Born has finally seen the light of day, almost four years after she started work on it with Greg Kurstin, one half of synth-pop darlings The Bird And The Bee and a man who’s worked with pop juggernauts (Kylie, Ke$ha, Lily Allen), indie royalty (The Flaming Lips, Peaches) and bands we’d rather forget (Geggy Tah).

?She’s a wonderful singer who’s been channelling Billie Holliday and Etta James since Amy Winehouse and Duffy were in high school.?

We Are Born* is Sia’s most overtly positive record to date, mainly inspired by her relationship with Le Tigre’s J.D. Samson. It manifests itself in music that yokes mainstream pop melodies, disco tempos and glossy synth-pop patinas. Starting with children’s voices singing the album’s title, Sia invites you to reclaim the abandonment of youth. The album’s opening line, ?We are born/Without a care,? is as much a statement of intent as it is an introductory message. The arrangements on *We Are Born are largely unfettered. The grandiose string sections and tinkling accoutrements have been discarded in favour of a more economic lineup; guitar or keyboard takes the lead melody, anchored by an insistent rhythm section. It’s all the name of immediacy, the trademark of a good pop song.

In this respect, We Are Born* is a winner. If you take Sia’s advice and listen to her new album ?without a care?, it’s a very easy album to like. But unlike some of her previous material, the album is destined to have a short shelf-life. What Sia did so beautifully in the past was focus on the minutiae of everyday situations (?Sweet Potato? from *Colour The Small One* or ?Little Black Sandles? off *Some People Have Real Problems*). On *We Are Born*, she recycles ideas and observations that have been applied by songwriters ad nauseam. ?Clap Your Hands? comes across as a simulacrum of a pop song, with contrite lyrics like ?Clap your hands/Turn the lights on my nights this is life/And we only get one chance.? We’ve heard this kind of ?party like it’s your last night? bunkum before. In fact, the first four tracks off *We Are Born are pretty much interchangeable. They adhere to the same formula and almost the same tempo.

It’s not until you get to the business end of the record that you find its stronger songs, and evidence of Sia’s excellent songwriting abilities. On ?Hurting Me Now?, she juxtaposes sparkling instrumentation with lyrics about feeling underappreciated. ?The Co-Dependent? benefits from a fantastic wandering bass line that dances around the hand-claps and busy percussion that constitutes the rhythm section. Sia’s got a proven track record of writing down-tempo numbers, so unsurprisingly these showcase her strengths, especially vocally. She’s a wonderful singer who’s been channelling Billie Holliday and Etta James since Amy Winehouse and Duffy were in high school. Songs like ?I’m In Here? and ?Be Good To Me? demonstrate how Sia picks the odd notes in a musical scale, which piques your interest because it’s not what your brain expects to hear. I just wish she’d done this on her more unabashed dance-pop numbers.

Ultimately, We Are Born is a sugar rush record: it’ll put you on a high during the moment, but the effect wears off minutes later. If the rumours are true, and this is going to be Sia’s final album, it’ll be a shame because she’s undoubtedly a talented songwriter who’s still learning a new idiom. Even if this album misses the mark on occasion, the music world needs a pop star like Sia. She’s real, and that’s more than you can say about most of her contemporaries these days.

+

Sia’s We Are Born is out now through Monkey Puzzle/Inertia.
