Umpire

By JP Hammond 2009-04-01T00:00:00+00:00

How easily can you forgive the sins of a band with a rich pedigree? Umpire are one such band, comprising the Sandgroper talents of uber-producer Simon Struthers and fellow Adam Said Galore acolytes Geoff Symons and Michael Lake. Their debut self-titled release comprises seven tracks (I’ll call it an album under instruction from their bio) and taps a rich vein of late ?80s/early ?90s American underground guitar workouts.

Opener ?Streamers? is an act of subterfuge, an undulating pop song propelled by stuttering, shifting chords and a shimmering lead line followed by gentle backing vocals. If it’s unlike anything else on the record that’s no problem – instead it’s an explanation for the fact that, however dour and downbeat their remaining songs may end up, they inevitably revert to the comfort of strong melodies (and are all the better for it).

M+N?s Tim Scott was compelled to draw links to Squirrel Bait for his Late Arvo Sons review a few weeks ago, but here the reference skips back to the older, more mournful Brian McMahan of Slint. Umpire aren’t as corrosive (nor anywhere near as good) but what they take from Slint and bands of their ilk is a deliberate complication in their songwriting; a keen refusal to accede to the easy melody that first catches your ear. You can hear it most prominently in ?Feed Swans Chocolate?, where the churning drudge of the opening palm-mute chords breaks into a blossoming guitar line before spectacularly jumping into a staccato crescendo. The song makes the strategic move from ugly discordance to memorable melody sound willfully easy – as though there are other, better hooks they are yet to reveal.

This pattern recurs over three more excellent, downbeat tracks; the sum total of which renders the final two numbers confusing. ?Paddlesteamers? lasts less than a minute, during which it manages to summarily take the chorus from opener ?Streamers? and put some cheap keys and a decidedly dodgy 808 beat behind it. Radiohead’s ?Morning Bell? it isn’t. Worse still is ?Vapours Wrecked Your Knives?, which shows that the physical reaction to the ?honest to blog? moment in Juno can be replicated in lyrical form. Both tracks are awkward, unnecessary additions to a collection of great songs.

What’s more disappointing about ?Vapours? is that it ruins some of the lyrical mystery of the band. On the preceding five songs, vocalist Geoff Symons exploits his lilting falsetto to full effect, managing to sound desperately involved in lyrics that may not amount to much. ?Feed Swans Chocolate?, for example, sounds like a disturbing metaphor for the onset of mental illness – even if the subsequent lyrics don’t totally secure that narrative to the song.

This enigmatic approach certainly isn’t a bad thing, particularly combined with their trenchant refusal to anchor a song to a simple structure. What makes ?Vapours? problematic is the repeated invocation of the title as the chorus (?If you continue to rise/You’ll find vapours wrecked your knives?), delivered with po-faced sincerity. It throws the rest of the CD into a starker, more critical relief and the picture it paints isn’t flattering. You begin to second-guess Symons as a lyricist and the crude missteps he makes at other, earlier points on the record begin to seem less charming and more like a recurring pattern of poor editorial judgement.

But despite these extraneous additions, there’s much to recommend here – and there’s better to come, for sure.
