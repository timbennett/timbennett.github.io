A fool who?ll

By Hannah Brooks 2011-09-03T00:00:00+00:00

Something happened to Laura Jean in the country. After the release of her acclaimed second album Eden Land in 2008, Laura Jean relocated to a cottage in the Victorian highlands to work on material for her next record. With clean air, a couple of kelpies and a reputation as one of Australia’s most promising folk voices, Laura Jean’s third album could have easily become an introspective, Vashti Bunyan in the Outer Hebrides-style paean to animals and elements. Yet what was intended to be an acoustic album turned into quite a different beast when a garish yellow electric guitar won in a songwriting competition was dubiously dragged out from under the bed.

The album’s press release suggests, ?Laura Jean went to the country and came back with a rock record?. That’s true to a degree but more than that she returned with an attitude found through a process of transformative amplification. It seems that electricity not only sharpened Laura Jean’s edges but also flushed out any of the navel-gazing that can so often be the result of a quiet, isolated existence coupled with an acoustic guitar.

A fool who’ll* shows a very different artist to the one introduced on her first two albums. The cover shows Laura Jean defiantly stooped, her long hair obscuring her face like a veil, in what looks like a decaying shed. Her now infamous SG is cast to one side but still at arms length, like a sidekick who knows their place. With her dark hair and dark vibes, Laura Jean almost looks like some kind of metal god. It’s a world away from the leafy greenness of her 2006 debut *Our Swan Song and testament to the fact that something did indeed happen to Laura Jean in the country.

However, for all the rain-slicked doom and gloom, the album is actually very friendly. It’s simple yet rich, with its rock elements balanced out by the use of viola, piano accordion, saxophone, clarinet, violin, cello and trumpet, courtesy of bandmates Biddy Connor and Jen Sholakis and a host of other guests. The record follows the way paved by countless singer-songwriters before her but even recycled ideas, in Laura Jean’s now-electrified hands, become something new: simply because we’ve never heard Laura Jean do it.

Beginning with a trio of unashamed love songs: ?So Happy?, ?Missing You? and ?Valenteen?, the record dances around familiar territory and some awkward lyrics before finding its soul on fourth track ?No’l?. A distinctive song of biblical and primeval intensity, the album reveals its guts from here, in powerfully bent lyrics including the album’s golden line: ?You did not prepare lost lamb.? Songs such as the aching duet ?Spring? and the sleepily playful ?My Song?, which features harmonies from Magic Silver White’s Jojo Petrina and Monica Sonand, show that Laura Jean’s voice is an undeniably beautiful sound. Yet this album finds her vocals catapulted into darker spheres. Her range is remarkable and she allows herself room on most tracks to leap from hushed tones to explosions of emotional fireworks.

Laura Jean – Missing You by ChapterMusic

The album focuses on personal themes of love, longing and not knowing what to do with that strange, fleeting feeling known as happiness. Then there’s ?Australia?, a song that takes a stab at the socio-political. While it’s an ambitious howl at a Bambi-nation still unsteady on its feet, it feels more contrived than the other songs: it lingers to point a finger at the problem while the other tracks simply kick the fence in and walk all over it.

Laura Jean – Australia by ChapterMusic

Unlike the country she slaps the face of, Laura Jean isn’t facing an identity crisis. A fool who’ll is a strong statement of existence from a woman who has found her voice and knows what to do with it. And while Laura Jean recently bemoaned in the press that whenever she tries to summon her influences she always ends up sounding like herself, that’s far from a bad thing. If anything, it’s a mark of authenticity achieved through metamorphosis – as painful and exhilarating as that can be.
