Unicycles

By Ian Rogers 2012-06-06T00:00:00+00:00

I’ve heard a lot of this sort of music lately. There’s a tremendous amount of ?twinkling? music around at the moment. Strings are plucked one at a time, vocals glimmer in the distance, drums pitter-patter, synths bleep on and off like Christmas lights. It’s not something that worries me a great deal; this version of Australian indie pop is entirely less awkward than its ?90s equivalents. Indie now has absolutely nothing to do with DIY punk’s ideals of separatism. Nothing at all. Good.

The album Tiger Choir have made in Unicycles* tills this twinkling musical ground thoroughly. There’s nothing startlingly original about it. If you’ve heard Animal Collective or the bleepy parts of The Flaming Lips or the other Beach-Boys-meets-computer bands that dot almost every music scene in Australia at the moment, you’ve heard this as well. And while my pointing this out isn’t exactly helpful to the Hobart trio, all their borrowing and rehashing doesn’t really diminish my enjoyment of the album. *Unicycles is very pleasant. It’s a very personable, enjoyable album. You’d have to be a bit of a grump to dismiss it. Maybe this upbeat, agreeable vibe of Tiger Choir’s (and their contemporaries) is how this music skates by. Yet no one ever seems overly keen to point out that a lot of unpopular music sounds the same as well. Have you ever heard a hardcore band that sounds completely dissimilar to Black Flag or Minor Threat? Similarly, as a fan of grindcore and ambient music, I can’t possibly pooh-pooh all these new indie bands for the consistency of their sound.

With all that clearly noted, I’d be remiss if I didn’t have a bit of a whinge. After repeated listens to the album, I do yearn for a little more of the occasional weirdness and a little less of Tiger Choir’s stock motifs. There are a lot of multi-tracked vocals and entirely too much shimmering guitar for my tastes, which tend towards guitars never shimmering.

Tiger Choir – Dancer by Tiger Choir

I much prefer some of the deeper cuts, things like ?Cotton Claws? with its hurdy-gurdy vocals and the darker-hued loops of ?Dancer?. In these moments, I find myself thinking that this band could benefit from shaking off some of the pleasantries. The prospect of a looser, grittier and noisier Tiger Choir is enticing and wouldn’t run entirely counter to their pop tendencies. They could have both. So while Unicycles works well here and now, I’m much more buoyed by the sense that they’re capable of something more interesting. In the future, hinting at it won’t be enough. But today I’m happy to give them a little leeway. Something about them makes me think they deserve it.
