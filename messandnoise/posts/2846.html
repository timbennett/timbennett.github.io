A History Of Hygiene

By Doug Wallen 2013-11-27T00:00:00+00:00

The latest in a line of short-and-cryptic DIY songwriting that runs from early Wire through to Guided By Voices, The Stevens follow up their six-song and 13-minute debut EP – which Chapter Music’s Guy Blackman has called Australia’s version of the Buzzcocks? Spiral Scratch EP – with a debut album squeezing 24 songs into 44 minutes. That constant brevity could either be a selling point or a deal-breaker, depending on your opinion of channel-surfing.

The cessation of songs just as they get interesting can be frustrating, as can the overall feeling of underachievement, but there’s also a great friction when one song suddenly gives way to a new one that changes the mood in a heartbeat. Oddly enough, my favourite moments here aren’t what I expected. They’re not the more focussed pop songs, although ?Turpins Falls? and the single ?Hindsight? are wildly catchy, but the times when the quartet jam out the songs for a bit of instrumental free-wheeling.

That usually happens at the end of a song, and usually hinges on an interesting change in guitar sound, whether on ?Red Ribbon? or ?Worse Everyday? or the near-ballad ?Come Outside?. The album’s longest entry, ?Travelator?, is purely instrumental for its final two minutes, savouring the bristling potential of electric guitar. (In a similar vein, the frazzled guitar in ?Elpho Beach? recalls Westing-era Pavement.) The ?80s-tinged ?True Tales of Half Time? is all instrumental, playing like incidental film music that keeps jamming after the film has finished.

That haphazard quality is essential to this band, who began when guitarists Alex Macfarlane and Travis MacDonald merged their individual recording projects. Now rounded out by bassist Gus Lord (Boomgates) and drummer Matt Harkin, they cruise along on jittery vigour and buzzing guitar leads while populating this record mostly with one-take recordings mostly helmed by Mikey Young. Right from the wobbly tone and off harmonising of ?From Puberty to Success? and the scrappy singing of ?Scared of Other Men?, the appeal of the unpolished is clear. Behold the psych sogginess of ?Fascinator Preparations? and the GBV-style muddied vocals and splintered guitar of the closing ?Lucky Scores a Ride?.

The lyrics, though, add to the songs? staying power, partly because they’re hard to figure out – which, in turn, keeps us trying. There’s nice wordplay in ?Blind in One Ear? (?the lowest common dominator?) and the rhyming of ?suffragette? and ?cigarette? in ?College Theme? – both courtesy of MacDonald – while his ?Skeleton Vs. Silicon? drops not just the album’s title phrase but the line ?What’s a poor boy to do but grow his hair and learn irony??, in a sort of Twitter-era updating of the classic lyric from ?Street Fighting Man?. ?Challenger? seems to be at least somewhat about the 1986 shuttle disaster, and ?The Long Vacation? belies any slacker airs with the line ?We woke up to the morning news/An embassy had been bombed.? And Macfarlane offers a lovely bit of insight in ?Limbo?: ?I’ll just push them away with everything I say/Maybe I try to push them away.?

The Flying Nun stable is obviously a touchstone here – MacDonald was in a band that covered The Clean at age 13 – but more so it’s the wider strain of guitar bands that aren’t afraid to be shabby and shambling. The phrase ?anxiety pop? gets mentioned in the presser, and that just about nails what The Stevens are going for. Not just short songs that thumb their noses at gloss, but catchy little oddities that tap into fragmented imagery and a general uncertainty. It’s pop music made abstract (and abstracted) and comfortably uncomfortable.
