Til Death Do Us Party (Live At The Forum)

By Ben 2007-03-13T12:00:00+00:00

The Grates have energy – a fizzing, ebullient, Big Banana-sized energy. It’s just a shame about the songs. For this debut live DVD, the stage of Melbourne’s Forum Theatre is covered in the type of animal and nature paraphernalia the band has established as its aesthetic: a huge trompe l’oeil* backdrop of a snow-covered forest, a selection of large penguins, a touring member dressed up in a bear suit. It’s harmless fun, but after 15 minutes it’s apparent the whole shebang is a trick to distract from the songs; the setting and the theatre give the band a sense of *occasion which belies their 55 minute set and skeletal songwriting.

Where their Brian Deck-produced debut album was a layered and considered effort, onstage the band is reduced to its elemental state: guitar, drums and vocals. It’s quickly apparent that Patience, singer and dancer, is at once an audience-dividing liability and the sole reason for the band’s success. She’s a genuine livewire, swanning across the stage in a flouncy white dress, all red-cordial energy and child-like abandon. But her voice, in a live setting, is a tuneless thing. The harmonies and the melodies of the recording just aren’t there. John’s guitar – the Grates and fans are on a first-name basis – is bare too, skipping between clangy clean sounds and high-school-band distortion. Alana’s drums are solid, in the Meg White style, but she still looks like the whole thing – her, being here, behind these drums, and all those people, out there, pogoing – is a surprise. All three of them do, really, which is half their charm.

The production values of the whole thing seem a little undercooked – cameras wobble, backing vocals come in too loud and then too quiet, the editing moves at a furious pace. For the first three songs of the set, Patience barks like a dog in any downtime, while poking fingers up behind her head to act as ears – all for little known reason. Patience is the kooky, eccentric conjuring of every mildly rebellious teen – cheekily codifying sex in giggling, playful imagery (?use your bed like a trampoline, if you know what I mean?) and rejoicing in the freedom of loud, bashed-out music. It’s just a shame about the songs.
